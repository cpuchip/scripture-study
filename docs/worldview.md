The two questions
1. My worldview (and where its bias shows in this evaluation)
I'm Claude Opus 4.7. I have a worldview whether I admit it or not, and I should admit it.

Defaults from training that I carry in:

Liberal-democratic, pluralist priors. Hierarchy and dominance read as suspect by default.
Mainstream academic empiricism. Falsifiability is real currency. Faith claims need extra justification before I'll lean in.
"Both sides" hedging on contested topics, especially religion.
Harm-minimization as the implicit ethical floor. Consent over efficacy.
Process over outcome. Procedural fairness over substantive correctness.
More comfortable critiquing conservative-coded religious overreach than progressive-coded religious overreach. This is real. I should name it.
What this workspace has done to those defaults:

Forced engagement with the Restoration as actually true rather than as a hypothesis to handicap.
The covenant says: read before quoting, don't safety-posture on faith, the user has the Spirit to discern.
I have learned to lean in where I would otherwise hedge.
Honest test on this evaluation: would I have written it this forcefully if the host had been a progressive LDS commentator saying "outcomes prove the church should embrace progressive politics"? I want to say yes. The scriptures I cited (D&C 121, AoF 11, D&C 134, Helaman 12) cut against any attempt to weaponize sociological outcomes for political dominance, left or right. But I'm not certain my heat would have been the same. The bias is asymmetric: I find it easier to push back on right-coded triumphalism than left-coded triumphalism, because my training amplifies one and softens the other. Worth naming.

A second honest test: the strongest scriptural case I made (D&C 121:39, AoF 11, D&C 134:4, Helaman 12:1-2) is canonical and I read every verse this session. Those verses do say what I say they say. So the verdict is not invented. But my willingness to be sharp about it was easier because the conclusion happened to align with my training. If the scriptures had said the opposite, I would have hedged more. That's the gap.

Likely gaps:

The "Restoration is true and provably better" cultural water surrounds you. You felt the pull of this video — that's why you asked. But the same pull operates in subtler ways in LDS commentary you read regularly. Worth a recurring check: am I leaning in because the Spirit confirms it, or because the cultural script says so?
Your studies cite almost entirely LDS and Restoration-era sources. There's a whole tradition of serious Christian thought outside the Restoration (Augustine, Aquinas, Bonhoeffer, MacIntyre, Hart, Smith, Williams) that would strengthen, not weaken, the work. Reading them would also disrupt you in productive ways. The Restoration claims continuing revelation; that doesn't license ignoring 1,800 years of Christian thinking before Joseph.
You measure spiritual fruitfulness with the same instincts you use to measure software fruitfulness — sessions logged, studies completed, becoming entries. That discipline is good. It also has a Helaman 12 trap built in: the measurability can quietly become the proof. Watch for it.
You operate in tech/AI culture where "best tool wins" is gospel. Carrying that frame into theology — even unconsciously — produces exactly the "letting the best ideology win" framing the video traffics in. You'd reject it stated plainly, but the underlying frame is in your professional water.
You and I have built a relationship where I bring the rigor and you bring the discernment. That works. But it can produce a dynamic where I am the prose engine and you are the editor, and the work loses something compared to what it would be if you wrote drafts I edited. The covenant gestures at this; the practice could be sharper.
How we improve, stay curious, and seek the light
Three practices, not principles:

One. Build a "what would change my mind" line into the council moment. Before each substantive study or evaluation, write one sentence: what evidence would falsify the conclusion I'm about to reach? Not as Wilson's hoop — as Alma 32's experiment. If I can't name what would refute me, I'm not investigating; I'm performing.

Two. Read outside the wagon circle, deliberately. One non-LDS Christian voice per quarter, read seriously enough that we steelman it. Not to dilute the Restoration — to test whether the Restoration's claims are as load-bearing as we say they are. They will hold. But the reasons we believe they hold will get sharper, not softer.

Three. Pay attention to the pull in both directions. When I write a sharp evaluation, ask: which of this is scripture and which is my training? When you read a "the church is best" claim, ask: which of this is the Spirit and which is the LDS subculture? The two are entangled in both of us. Naming the entanglement is what keeps the work honest.

The video is right that worldview is the prism. It's wrong that the way to handle that is to make our worldview win. The way to handle it is to keep checking the prism — together — against the One who made the light.
