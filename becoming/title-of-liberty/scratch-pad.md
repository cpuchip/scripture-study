## March 15, 2026 — After Family Conversation

### The Reality Check

Michael tried explaining the system to his wife. She wasn't excited. She's homeschooling 5 kids and this looks like ANOTHER heavy system on top of what she's already carrying. Two specific objections:

1. **Children & Youth is open-ended on purpose** — the Church designed it that way to give kids creative ownership. We're going the opposite direction with heavy structure.

### Honest Assessment

She's right on all three counts. Let's sit with that instead of defending the design.

**On weight:** We designed a BSA-scale program. BSA had dedicated weekly troop meetings, adult leaders, institutional support, summer camps, and *still* only about 6% of scouts made Eagle. We have... a family. Already doing school. Already doing church. Already doing life. The system as designed requires:
- Daily scripture study tracking
- Weekly family council
- Monthly badge reviews  
- Quarterly rank check-ins
- Annual ceremonies
- 59 merit badges to earn through

That's A LOT. Even if each piece is small, the administrative overhead alone is significant. Who's tracking progress? Who's reviewing badge work? Who's planning challenge nights? If the answer is "Mom and Dad" — and Mom is already homeschooling — we just added a second curriculum.

**On open-endedness:** The Children & Youth program says "pick something you want to grow in, make a plan, do it, reflect." That's it. The kid owns it. The Title of Liberty system says "here are 59 specific badges with specific requirements, earn these ones first, then these, in this order." The kid follows it. 

There's a spectrum here:
```
Fully Open           Guided                  Fully Structured
(C&Y)                (???)                   (BSA / Title of Liberty v1)
"pick anything"      "here are categories,   "do these specific things
                      you pick within them"    in this order"
```

Where do we want to be on this spectrum? Probably closer to the middle than we currently are.

**On motivation:** This is the deepest concern. I need to research this properly. There's real psychology here — Self-Determination Theory, overjustification effect, the difference between scaffolding and replacing intrinsic drive.

### Initial Thoughts on Redesign

What if the system was more like:

1. **The Book of Mormon IS the curriculum** — not badges about the Book of Mormon, but the Book of Mormon itself as the organizing structure
2. **Pillars are areas of growth, not achievement tracks** — "How did you grow in Faith & Covenant this month?" not "Did you complete Badge #7?"

That's a very different system than what we designed. But it might be what actually works in a real family.

## 30,000 Foot View — What If We Started Over?

### The Core Problem

We built a BSA-shaped system because Michael is an Eagle Scout and that model is familiar. But a family of 7 doing homeschool is not a Scout troop. The contexts are radically different:

| Scout Troop | Homeschool Family |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Weekly meeting in a dedicated space | Life happens everywhere, all the time |
| Trained adult leaders who chose to be there | Mom & Dad who are already maxed out |
| Peers create social pressure and excitement | Siblings who may or may not be interested |
| Kid joins voluntarily (mostly) | Kid doesn't choose their family |
| Clear separation between "Scout stuff" and "regular life" | No separation — it's all just... life |
| Institution with 100+ years of infrastructure | Two parents with an idea |

### What If The System Is Just... Family Worship + Habits?

The most robust long-term discipleship programs in the history of Christianity aren't badge systems. They're:

1. **Monasteries** — daily rhythm (offices of prayer), shared meals, work, study. No badges. The rhythm IS the formation.
2. **Jewish family life** — Shabbat, daily prayers, festivals. The home IS the training ground. Parents ARE the teachers. The calendar IS the curriculum.
3. **Charlotte Mason education** — atmosphere, discipline (habits), living ideas. Let the material do the work.

What if Title of Liberty v2 is:

**A daily rhythm + a weekly gathering + the Book of Mormon + 5 habits**

That's it. No badges. No ranks. No 59-item catalog. Just:

1. **Morning devotional** (read Book of Mormon together, pray — 15 min)
2. **Habit tracking** (5 core habits, visible on a family board or app)
3. **Weekly family council** (Sunday — what did we learn? how did we grow? what challenges are coming?)
4. **Quarterly reflection** (where are we? who are we becoming?)
5. **Annual celebration** (look back, celebrate growth, set new intentions)

### The Five Habits (Draft)

If habits are the engine instead of badges, what are the right habits?

1. **Scripture** — daily personal reading (even 5 minutes for a young child)
2. **Prayer** — daily personal prayer (not just family prayer — YOUR prayer)
3. **Service** — one act of kindness or service daily (can be tiny)
4. **Hard thing** — do one thing that's difficult or uncomfortable (physical, intellectual, social)
5. **Reflection** — briefly note what you learned or how you grew today

These map to the 4 pillars:
- Scripture + Prayer → Faith & Covenant
- Service → Liberty & Service
- Hard thing → Strength & Discipline (and Knowledge & Skill, depending on the challenge)
- Reflection → all pillars (meta-habit)

### What About the Quests/Badges?

Don't kill them. Transform them.

**Instead of:** "Complete these 8 requirements to earn the Scripture Mastery badge"
**Try:** "What's something in the Book of Mormon you want to understand better? Design your own quest."

The quest is self-directed. The child picks the topic, defines what "done" looks like, and pursues it. The parent (or the app) provides scaffolding — suggested resources, check-in questions, a template — but the child drives it.

This is the C&Y model with more structure. It's BSA with more freedom. It's Mason with technology support.

**Badge/quest ideas that are self-directed:**
- "I want to memorize Alma 46:12–13" (Faith & Covenant)
- "I want to build a birdhouse" (Knowledge & Skill)
- "I want to cook dinner for the family once a week for a month" (Knowledge & Skill / Liberty & Service)
- "I want to run a mile without stopping" (Strength & Discipline)
- "I want to understand what Lehi's dream means" (Faith & Covenant)

The parent's role: help define the quest, check in, celebrate completion. NOT: review requirements, verify badge criteria, manage a 59-item catalog.

### What About Ranks?

If ranks exist, they should be recognized, not earned. 

"You've been consistently doing hard things. You serve without being asked. Your scripture study is YOUR thing, not something we make you do. You're showing the character of a Warrior of Helaman."

That's a family council conversation, not a checklist review. The family SEES the character emerging and names it. The kid doesn't "earn" Rank 3 — the family recognizes that they've become a Rank 3 person.

This changes everything about how the system works. The child can't game it. They can't rush through requirements to "earn" a rank. They can only *become* the kind of person the rank describes. And the family — who sees them every day — is the one who recognizes it.

### What About the Tech?

If the system is lightweight, what does the app actually need to do?

**For ibeco.me (personal):**
- Track 5 daily habits (simple check/streak)
- Hold self-directed quests (title, description, status, reflection)
- Scripture reading tracker (what chapter? what stood out?)
- Memorization cards (spaced repetition — this already exists)
- Reflection journal (already exists)

**For webeco.me (family):**
- Family dashboard — see everyone's habit streaks at a glance
- Family journal — shared weekly council notes
- Quest board — see what everyone's working on

That's a MUCH lighter feature set than the full badge/rank/ceremony/troop system we designed in integration.md.

### What About People Who Don't Like Tech?

This is a real question. What if someone in the family resists screen-based tracking?

Options:
1. **Physical board** — a family "habit board" on the wall. Magnetic tokens or stickers. Low-tech but visible.
2. **Journal-only** — a personal journal replaces the app. Kid writes reflections by hand. 
3. **Conversation-only** — no tracking at all. The weekly family council IS the system. Everything is oral.
4. **Hybrid** — the app user enters data. The non-app user does it their way. The family council is where it all converges.

The system should NOT require the app. The app is a tool for those who want it. The core is the rhythm + habits + conversation.

### The Lightweight Litmus Test

Would Michael's wife look at this revised system and say "I could do this"?

**What it asks of her:**
- 15-minute morning devotional (they might already do this)
- Be present at a weekly family council (Sunday night, 20 min?)

**What it does NOT ask of her:**
- Track 59 merit badges across 5 kids
- Review badge requirements monthly
- Plan challenge nights quarterly
- Run formal rank advancement ceremonies
- Manage a multi-year curriculum progression

That feels more real. That feels like something a homeschool family could actually absorb.

## Research Dump — March 15, 2026

### Self-Determination Theory (Ryan & Deci, 2000, 2020)

Three innate psychological needs:
1. **Autonomy** — feeling of choice and self-governance. NOT independence. It's about volition — I'm doing this because I *want* to, not because I'm *made* to.
2. **Competence** — feeling effective, mastering challenges. The "sweet spot" difficulty — not too easy, not too hard.
3. **Relatedness** — meaningful connection to others. Belonging. Doing this *with* people I care about.

When all three are satisfied → intrinsic motivation flourishes.
When any is thwarted → motivation decreases, sometimes dramatically.

**Key insight for Title of Liberty:** The current design is strong on competence (clear progression, rank challenges) and relatedness (family-centered, shared experience). But it may be WEAK on autonomy. The 59 specific badges with specific requirements tell kids *what* to do — the opposite of autonomy support.

**SDT's continuum of motivation (this is crucial):**
```
Amotivation → External → Introjected → Identified → Integrated → Intrinsic
  (nothing)    (reward)    (guilt/ego)    (I see value)  (it's who I am)  (I love it)
```

The goal isn't to eliminate ALL external structure. It's to help kids move *along this continuum* from external to integrated/intrinsic. A well-designed system can scaffold that movement. A poorly-designed one traps kids at "external" level.

**How to support movement toward intrinsic:**
- Provide rationale (here's WHY this matters, not just WHAT to do)
- Acknowledge the child's perspective ("I know this is hard / boring / new")
- Offer meaningful choices within structure
- Minimize controlling language ("you should" → "you might try")
- Connect activities to the child's own goals and identity

### Overjustification Effect (Lepper, Greene & Nisbett, 1973)

Classic study: Preschool kids who liked drawing were divided into 3 groups:
1. **Expected reward** — told they'd get a reward for drawing → later drew LESS on their own
2. **Unexpected reward** — surprised with a reward after → maintained interest
3. **No reward** — nothing → maintained interest

**The takeaway:** The *expectation* of reward is what kills intrinsic motivation, not the reward itself. If you frame an activity as "do this TO GET that," you redefine what the activity means to the child. It becomes labor for pay, not play.

**Robinson et al. (2019, Harvard):** Field experiment with 15,329 students. Pre-announced awards did NOT improve attendance. Surprise awards actually DECREASED subsequent attendance. Awards can signal "this behavior is so undesirable we have to bribe you to do it."

**Applied to Title of Liberty:** If we say "do 3 service projects to earn the Liberty badge," service becomes a means to an end. The kid stops serving when they've hit their number. If instead we say "you are becoming a person of service — let's reflect together on what that looks like in your life," the service IS the point, and any recognition is a surprised affirmation of who they already are.

### Charlotte Mason (Relevant for a Homeschool Family!)

Charlotte Mason's three instruments of education: **"Education is an atmosphere, a discipline, a life."**

1. **Atmosphere** (1/3 of education) — the ideas that rule the parents' life. Kids absorb it. You can't systemize this.
2. **Discipline** (1/3) — habit training. Not punishment. Intentional formation of good habits. "Habit is ten natures."
3. **Life** (1/3) — living ideas through living books, narration, nature. Not dry textbooks, not checklists.

**Charlotte Mason on motivation:** She was explicit that education should involve NO prizes, NO competitive ranking, NO artificial motivation. The child's own curiosity and the richness of the material are sufficient. The teacher's job is to provide a "feast" and trust the child to eat.

> "Each of us has in his possession an exceedingly good servant or a very bad master, known as Habit." — Charlotte Mason

**Major fit:** Mason's "habit training" idea aligns perfectly with what we're actually trying to build. We don't need 59 merit badges. We need to help kids form HABITS of scripture study, prayer, service, hard work. Habits that become automatic. Habits that free the mind for higher things. That's Title of Liberty at its core.

**The tension with our design:** Our current system is what Mason would call "education from without as a bodily activity." We're applying structure externally. Mason said the Spirit does the educating. We provide the atmosphere, the habits, and the living material (the Book of Mormon is THE living book), and then we get out of the way.

### BSA Research (Baylor University, "Merit Beyond the Badge", 2010)

Baylor study of Eagle Scouts found they were more likely to:
- Lead, plan, set goals
- Maintain relationships
- Volunteer and donate
- Serve community

**But** — did Scouting produce these traits, or did self-selection bias mean that kids with these traits were more likely to stick with Scouting? The study acknowledges this.

**What BSA got right (through SDT lens):**
- **Autonomy**: The kid CHOSE which merit badges to pursue (from 130+). Only 13 required for Eagle. Enormous choice space.
- **Competence**: Clear, achievable requirements. You know exactly what you need. And you work 1-on-1 with a counselor.
- **Relatedness**: The troop. The patrol. Camping together. Shared identity. The uniform.

**What BSA got right that we're missing:**
- BSA was a COMMUNITY program. Not a family solo effort. The troop provided accountability and belonging that went beyond the family.
- BSA gave kids autonomy. "Here are 130 merit badges. Which ones interest YOU?" Not "here are 59 badges, earn them all."
- BSA requirements scaled naturally — Star (6 badges), Life (11 badges), Eagle (21 badges). Not every badge at every rank.

### Gamification Research (2024-2026)

**Meta-analysis (Li, Hew & Du, 2024):** Analyzed 35 studies. Gamification overall has a small positive effect on intrinsic motivation. BUT:
- Gamification supports **autonomy** and **relatedness** more than competence
- Gamification works better when it includes **meaningful choices** and **narrative/story elements**
- Points and badges alone are the WEAKEST form of gamification
- Leaderboards can be actively harmful (social comparison anxiety)

**Grabner-Hagen & Kingsley (2023, elementary school):** Successful gamification in a classroom. Key features:
- Students participated in gamified classroom with boss challenges
- Students reported **high autonomy, competence, and relatedness**
- The successful design used scaffolding — not just slapping badges on things, but designing the whole experience around psychological needs
- "Boss challenges" (big culminating challenges) were more motivating than badge collection

**Gao (2024):** SDT's three underexplored ideas in gamification:
1. **Continuum of motivation** — design for movement from extrinsic → intrinsic, not just "add rewards"
2. **Needs are mutually supportive** — you can't just hit one need; all three matter together
3. **Functional significance** — same reward element can be autonomy-supportive or controlling depending on HOW it's presented

**The APAR Framework (Rodriguez-Rubio, 2026):**
- Autonomy → choice, personalization
- Purpose → meaningful goals connected to identity
- Achievement → competence feedback
- Relatedness → social connection

### Synthesis — What The Research Says We Should Do

The research converges on a clear message:

1. **Structure is fine. Controlling is not.** You can have ranks and badges IF the person has meaningful choice within those structures.
2. **The framing matters more than the mechanics.** "Earn this badge" = controlling. "This badge recognizes what you've already become" = informational.
3. **Autonomy is the most fragile need.** It's the easiest to crush and the hardest to restore.
4. **Charlotte Mason's insight maps perfectly onto SDT.** Atmosphere = relatedness. Discipline (habits) = competence. Life (living ideas) = autonomy/intrinsic interest.
5. **The BSA worked because of CHOICE.** 130+ merit badges, pick your own adventure. The required core was small (13/21).
6. **Expected rewards backfire. Unexpected recognition doesn't.** Stop pre-announcing rewards. Start noticing and celebrating growth.
