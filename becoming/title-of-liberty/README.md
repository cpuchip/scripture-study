# Title of Liberty

**A Family Discipleship Rhythm Built on the Book of Mormon**

---

## Why This Exists

The Lord told us plainly:

> "And your minds in times past have been darkened because of unbelief, and because you have treated lightly the things you have received — Which vanity and unbelief have brought the whole church under condemnation. And this condemnation resteth upon the children of Zion, even all. And they shall remain under this condemnation until they repent and remember the new covenant, even the Book of Mormon and the former commandments which I have given them, not only to say, but to **do** according to that which I have written" — [D&C 84:54–57](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/84.md)

We are under condemnation for treating the Book of Mormon lightly. Not someone else. Us. The children of Zion, even all. The way out isn't just reading it. It's *doing* according to what's written. Becoming the kind of people who live what it teaches.

Title of Liberty is a family discipleship rhythm designed to take the Book of Mormon off the shelf and into the bones. It is simple enough that a homeschool family of seven can sustain it. It asks real things of real people. It runs on habits, not badges. On character recognized, not achievements earned.

The standard we're aiming at:

> "They were exceedingly valiant for courage, and also for strength and activity; but behold, this was not all — they were men who were true at all times in whatsoever thing they were entrusted. Yea, they were men of truth and soberness, for they had been taught to keep the commandments of God and to walk uprightly before him." — [Alma 53:20–21](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/53.md)

Those young men weren't products of a badge system. They were products of mothers who lived the covenant and a culture that formed character through daily life. That's what we're building.

## The Gospel Framework

Before a single habit or structure, the gospel itself establishes the design constraints:

> "For behold, it is not meet that I should command in all things; for he that is compelled in all things, the same is a slothful and not a wise servant ... Verily I say, men should be anxiously engaged in a good cause, and do many things of their own free will, and bring to pass much righteousness; For the power is in them, wherein they are agents unto themselves." — [D&C 58:26–29](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/58.md)

The power is in them. Not in the system. Not in the tracking. In the person.

> "For behold, God hath said a man being evil cannot do that which is good; for if he offereth a gift, or prayeth unto God, except he shall do it with real intent it profiteth him nothing." — [Moroni 7:6](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md)

A child who does 10 service hours to earn a badge is not the same as a child who serves because they've become a person of service. The intent is what matters. If a system produces people who act only to earn, it produces [Moroni 7:8](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md) people.

> "And because that they are redeemed from the fall they have become free forever, knowing good from evil; to act for themselves and not to be acted upon." — [2 Nephi 2:26](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)

The test for everything we design: **does it produce agents, or does it produce the acted-upon?**

## Design Principles

These emerged from motivation research (SDT, overjustification effect, Charlotte Mason, BSA analysis, gamification studies) filtered through the gospel framework above. The full research synthesis is in [motivation-systems.md](motivation-systems.md).

1. **Framework tight, content loose.** The pillars, the rhythm, the family council are the structure. What goes *into* those containers is chosen by the person, not prescribed by the system.

2. **Recognition, not reward.** Don't pre-announce what kids will earn. Notice what they're becoming. "I've seen you reading your scriptures every morning without being asked. That's character." Recognition after the fact is informational. Reward before the fact is controlling.

3. **Habits over badges.** Charlotte Mason was right: habit training is more powerful than achievement tracking. A child who has the *habit* of daily scripture study doesn't need a badge to motivate it. The habit does the work.

4. **The Book of Mormon IS the curriculum.** Don't build a system *about* the Book of Mormon. Read the Book of Mormon together and let it do the work. The book is alive. Trust it.

5. **Choice is the fuel.** When growth projects exist, the choice space should be enormous and the requirements small. "Pick something you want to grow in" not "complete these 8 specific requirements."

6. **Parent burden near zero.** If the system requires Mom to track progress, review badges, plan challenge nights, and run ceremonies, it will die. Technology handles tracking. Family council is conversation, not administration.

7. **Start with atmosphere, not curriculum.** The parents' own Book of Mormon practice is a third of the education (Charlotte Mason's "atmosphere"). Before any system launches, the adults should be living it first. The kids absorb what the parents model.

## The Four Pillars

Four areas of attention for growth. These emerge from scripture and align with the Church's Children and Youth framework ([Luke 2:52](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/2.md)) and the Becoming app's default pillars (Spiritual, Social, Intellectual, Physical). Same pillars, Book of Mormon framing.

| Pillar | Emoji | Scripture Foundation | What It Grows |
|--------|-------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Faith & Covenant** | ⚔️ | [Alma 53:21](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/53.md) — "men of truth and soberness ... taught to keep the commandments" | Prayer, scripture, ordinances, personal revelation, covenant keeping |
| **Liberty & Service** | 🛡️ | [Alma 46:12](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/46.md) — "in memory of our God, our religion, and freedom" | Service, leadership, community building, defending truth |
| **Knowledge & Skill** | 🏗️ | [Abraham 1:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/1.md) — "desiring also to be one who possessed great knowledge" | Practical skills, deep study, creative work, wisdom |
| **Strength & Discipline** | 💪 | [Alma 53:20](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/53.md) — "exceedingly valiant for courage, and also for strength and activity" | Physical fitness, endurance, self-discipline, doing hard things |

The pillars are not categories to fill. They're lenses for reflection: *How am I growing in each of these areas? Where am I strong? Where have I been coasting?*

## The Five Habits

If habits are the engine instead of badges, these are the right habits. They map to the pillars, but more importantly they map to the kind of person the Book of Mormon describes.

| Habit | What It Looks Like | Pillar |
|-------|--------------------|--------|
| **Scripture** | Daily personal reading, even 5 minutes for a young child | ⚔️ Faith & Covenant |
| **Prayer** | Daily personal prayer — your prayer, not just family prayer | ⚔️ Faith & Covenant |
| **Service** | One act of kindness or service daily (can be small) | 🛡️ Liberty & Service |
| **Hard Thing** | Do one thing that's difficult or uncomfortable — physical, intellectual, social | 💪 Strength & Discipline / 🏗️ Knowledge & Skill |
| **Reflection** | Briefly note what you learned or how you grew today | All four |

Five habits. Every day. That's the core of the program.

A 4-year-old's "scripture" might be a parent reading aloud for 3 minutes. A teenager's "hard thing" might be a cold shower, a difficult conversation, or finishing a project they've been avoiding. The habits scale naturally by age without requiring a degree matrix.

Reflection is the meta-habit. It's how you move along the motivation continuum from "I do this because Dad says so" toward "I do this because it's who I am." Without reflection, the other four habits are just motions. With it, they become material for becoming.

## Self-Directed Quests

Beyond the daily habits, growth happens through quests — self-directed projects that the person chooses, defines, and pursues.

**Instead of:** "Complete these 8 requirements to earn the Scripture Mastery badge."

**Try:** "What's something in the Book of Mormon you want to understand better? What would it look like to really dig into that?"

The person picks the topic, defines what "done" looks like, and pursues it. The parent provides scaffolding — suggested resources, check-in questions, encouragement — but the person drives it.

**Examples:**
- "I want to memorize [Alma 46:12–13](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/46.md)" (Faith & Covenant)
- "I want to build a birdhouse" (Knowledge & Skill)
- "I want to cook dinner for the family once a week for a month" (Knowledge & Skill / Liberty & Service)
- "I want to run a mile without stopping" (Strength & Discipline)
- "I want to understand what Lehi's dream means" (Faith & Covenant)
- "I want to learn first aid" (Liberty & Service / Knowledge & Skill)

The parent's role: help define the quest, check in, celebrate completion. Not: review requirements, verify badge criteria, manage a catalog.

A quarterly rhythm works well for quests. At family council: "What do you want to work on this quarter?" Pick one or two. Not ten.

## Recognized Milestones

If the family wants to name stages of growth, those milestones should be *recognized*, not earned. The family sees character emerging and names it. The person doesn't "earn" a rank by checking boxes. The family recognizes that they've become the kind of person the milestone describes.

| Milestone | Named For | Character Recognized |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Apprentice of Lehi** | [1 Nephi 2:2–3](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/2.md) — Lehi obeyed when called | Willing to begin. Shows up. Tries. |
| **Scout of Nephi** | [1 Nephi 3:7](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/3.md) — "I will go and do" | Does hard things. Doesn't quit when it's difficult. |
| **Warrior of Helaman** | [Alma 53:20–21](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/53.md) — "true at all times" | Trustworthy. Keeps promises. Reliable without being watched. |
| **Captain of Moroni** | [Alma 48:11–13](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/48.md) — "firm in the faith of Christ" | Defends what matters. Leads by example. Prepares others. |
| **Builder of Zion** | [Moses 7:18](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/7.md) — "one heart and one mind" | Creates refuge for others. Builds up instead of tearing down. |

"You've been consistently doing hard things. You serve without being asked. Your scripture study is *your* thing now, not something we make you do. You're showing the character of a Warrior of Helaman."

That's a family council conversation, not a checklist review. The child can't game it. They can't rush through requirements. They can only *become* the kind of person the milestone describes. And the family — who sees them every day — is the one who recognizes it.

Milestones are optional. Some families will use them. Some won't. The habits and quests work with or without named milestones.

## Family Rhythm

### Daily (~15 minutes structured)
- **Morning devotional:** Read Book of Mormon together, pray. Even 10 minutes.
- **Five habits:** Each person tracks their own (app, journal, or just a conversation at dinner).

### Weekly
- **Family council** (Sunday, ~20 minutes): What are we reading in the Book of Mormon? What stood out? How did our habits go? What quests are in progress? Celebrate growth you've noticed.

### Quarterly
- **Reflection:** Where are we? Who are we becoming? What quests do we want to pursue next? Are the habits working? What needs to change?
- **Milestone recognition** (if the family uses milestones): Has anyone's character growth reached a point worth naming?

### Annually
- **Celebration:** Look back over the year. What did we learn? How did we grow? Renew commitments.

That's the whole structure. No monthly badge reviews. No quarterly rank check-ins. No formal advancement ceremonies unless the family wants them. The rhythm is light enough that a busy homeschool family can absorb it instead of stacking it on top of an already full schedule.

## Who It's For

**Right now: our family.** Five kids, ages 4–15, two parents who participate alongside them. Charlotte Mason's "atmosphere" principle says the parents' own practice is a third of the education. We don't run this from above. We live it together.

**Eventually: other families.** The system is designed to be shareable through webeco.me. But the first job is to live it ourselves and see what works.

**Anyone who wants it.** Adults, individuals, missionaries. The habits and quests don't require a family context. The family rhythm is just one way to structure them.

## The Book of Mormon as Living Text

The one structured element: reading the Book of Mormon together as a family.

Everything else grows from what you're reading. Alma 53 sparks a conversation about reliability. 1 Nephi 3 sparks a conversation about doing hard things. The book itself generates the topics for quests, the material for reflection, the scripture for memorization.

This is Charlotte Mason's "living books" principle applied to scripture. Don't build a curriculum *about* the Book of Mormon. Read the Book of Mormon and let it teach. It was written for our day ([Mormon 8:34–35](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/morm/8.md)). It knows what we need.

## What About the App?

Title of Liberty can run with or without the Becoming app. The core is the rhythm, the habits, and the conversation. The app is a tool for those who want it.

**For ibeco.me (personal):**
- Track 5 daily habits (streaks, consistency)
- Hold self-directed quests (title, description, status, reflection)
- Scripture reading tracker (what chapter, what stood out)
- Memorization cards (spaced repetition — already exists)
- Reflection journal (already exists)

**For webeco.me (family):**
- Family dashboard — see everyone's habit streaks at a glance
- Family journal — shared weekly council notes
- Quest board — see what everyone's working on

**For families who don't want screens:**
- Physical habit board on the wall (magnetic tokens or stickers)
- Personal journal instead of the app
- Conversation-only — no tracking at all; the weekly family council IS the system
- Hybrid — app users enter data, non-app users do it their way, family council is where it converges

The system does not require the app. The app is a convenience, not a dependency.

See [integration.md](integration.md) for the technical mapping to Becoming app features.

## What Makes This Different

| Comparison | How Title of Liberty Differs |
|------------|------------------------------|
| **BSA / Scouting** | Family-centered, not troop-centered. Book of Mormon, not generic outdoors. Habits over badges. No institutional dependency. |
| **Children & Youth (LDS)** | More daily structure (5 habits, family devotional). Same self-directed spirit for quests. Book of Mormon as the explicit center. |
| **Charlotte Mason** | Aligned philosophically (atmosphere, discipline, life). Title of Liberty is Mason's principles applied specifically to Book of Mormon discipleship with optional digital support. |
| **Generic habit trackers** | Gospel framework. Four pillars. Family rhythm. The Book of Mormon as the living text, not just "read more." |

## The Covenant Thread

This program isn't about achievement. It's about covenant identity.

The stripling warriors weren't remarkable because they were strong fighters. They were remarkable because "they had been taught by their mothers, that if they did not doubt, God would deliver them" ([Alma 56:47](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/56.md)). That faith came from parents who lived it.

We face our own version of those challenges. Our children are growing up in a world that treats lightly everything the Book of Mormon teaches. This is our family's answer: we will not treat it lightly. We will read it, live it, and build the kind of character it describes.

Not through a 59-badge catalog. Through daily habits, honest conversation, self-directed growth, and a book that was written for exactly this moment.
