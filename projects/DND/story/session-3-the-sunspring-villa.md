# The Sunspring Villa — Session Three

## Part I: The Letter

---

Daggerford smelled like woodsmoke and river water. The Boar's Tooth smelled like ale and old wood. Remi smelled like trouble.

He was waiting at their usual table when they walked in. He had not moved. He had not eaten. He had been watching the door since they left for the tower, and he had not blinked.

"Seal #1," he said. He did not ask.

"Stabilized," Elara said. She sat down. Her armor was scuffed, her hammer was notched, her eyes were tired. "We chose to stabilize, not extract."

"Vell lives?"

"Trapped. Alive."

Remi nodded. He did not smile. He had been counting on them, and he did not celebrate easy victories. "Good. That buys time. Time is the one thing we're running out of."

He reached into his vest and pulled out a scroll. He placed it on the table. It was sealed with black wax, stamped with a hammer — Bane's symbol.

"The goblin boss's letter," Elara said. She had carried it since the road, unopened.

"Open it now."

She broke the seal.

---

> *Brother Malakor,*
>
> *The siphon failed at the tower. The dwarves were there — all five of them, as the prophecy said. They stabilized the mechanism. I hold it, but not for long. Ten days, perhaps less.*
>
> *But the villa holds. The warm wells grow stronger. The second seal weakens with each passing day. When it breaks, the network will follow. The keystone will turn, and the coast will be free.*
>
> *Do not send more goblins. They are useless against what these five carry. Send word instead. Tell me what you find beneath the villa. Tell me what the old scholars hid in the basement.*
>
> *— V.*

---

Remi read over Elara's shoulder. When he finished, he looked up.

"Vell is alive," he said quietly. "But that's not Vell from Candlekeep. That's Vell the goblin. Two different people, same name. The goblin is writing to someone at the villa."

"Malakor," Tilly said. She had been reading the letter too. "He's the cult leader. The warm wells — he's using them to weaken the seal."

"The letter says the villa holds," Lyra said. "Which means the cult is strong there. Stronger than the goblins were at the tower."

Malachi's orb pulsed — faint, like a heartbeat. "Bane," he said. "The hammer symbol. My patron knows that name. Bane is a god of tyranny, of conquest. The cult does not worship him out of devotion. They worship him because he gives them power."

Finn plucked a chord on Whisper. The note was low, uncertain. "The prophecy said five travelers. But the goblin said five, too. They know we're coming."

Remi stood up. "Then we don't wait. The villa is south, past Baldur's Gate. We move tonight."

---

## Part II: The Risen Road

The Risen Road was older than the Way of the Lion. It ran east from Baldur's Gate, through Baldur's Mountains, and down the slopes toward the coast. The road was paved, cracked, and uneven, and the stones were warm beneath their feet — not from the sun, but from something deeper.

Tilly noticed first. She knelt, pressed her palm to the stone, and pulled back a glove. "The road itself is affected," she said. "Trace arcane residue, same as the river. The villa's magic is leaking into the land."

They traveled in the dark, moving fast. The road was empty — no caravans, no merchants, no travelers. The cult's influence had cleared the way.

By dawn, they reached the Lastwall Gate.

The symbols on the rock faces were glowing — faint, blue, pulsing. The same symbols from the tower, from the Cormyrean Milestone, from Candlekeep's Restricted Wing. The seals were connected, and the network was alive.

Kaelen placed his hand on one of the symbols. His eyes closed. He did not speak for a long time. When he opened them, his voice was low.

"This stone remembers," he said. "It remembers the hand that carved it. The hand was afraid."

"Of what?" Elara asked.

"Of what lies beneath."

They passed through the gate. The symbols dimmed behind them.

---

## Part III: Baldur's Gate

The city was a maze. Not the kind you get lost in — the kind that gets lost in you. Streets narrow and winding, buildings tall and leaning, people everywhere, all of them moving, all of them talking, all of them hiding something.

The Flaming Fist patrolled the streets. They wore red, they carried spears, and they did not smile.

Elara walked at the front. Her armor bore the mark of the Fist — a red gauntlet, stamped on her shield. She did not need to explain who she was. The Fist recognized their own.

"Marshal Ravengard," she said to the first patrol they met. "I need an audience."

The captain looked at her. He looked at the party behind her — the dwarf, the elf, the warlock, the bard, the gnome, and the Harper who had not removed his hood.

"You'll wait," he said.

They waited.

---

### While They Waited

The Underground found them before the Fist did.

The man was thin, dark-eyed, dressed in clothes that cost more than they looked. He approached Finn first — bards were easy to talk to, and Finn was the easiest of all.

"Friend," the man said. His smile was too wide. "I hear you're heading south. Toward the Sunspring Villa."

Finn smiled back. "We're heading south."

"Good. I have information about the villa. Layout, guard rotations, the warm wells. But I need a favor first. Smuggle a package into Candlekeep. Nothing dangerous. Just... documents."

He offered a small box, wrapped in cloth. Tilly's goggles caught the faint glow through the fabric — the same glow as the river, the road, the tower.

She did not move. She did not speak. But her hand went to her tinker's tools, and her eyes narrowed.

Finn looked at the box. He looked at the man. He looked at Tilly, and he saw what she saw.

"No," he said. "I don't smuggle packages."

The man's smile tightened. "You'll regret that."

"Maybe," Finn said. "But I play my own songs."

The man turned and walked away. He did not look back.

Tilly exhaled. "That was a siphon device," she said quietly. "Same technology as the goblins. The Underground isn't just criminal. They're connected to the cult."

"Connected how?" Malachi asked.

Tilly shook her head. "I don't know yet. But the same hand built both."

---

### The Audience

Marshal Ravengard was a large man, broad-shouldered, gray-haired, with eyes like flint. He sat behind a desk that had seen too many wars, and he did not stand when they entered.

"Elara Sunstrider," he said. "Former Fist. You left under bad terms."

"I left under orders," Elara said. "There's a difference."

Ravengard's eyes flicked to Remi. "And you are?"

Remi did not remove his hood. "A friend."

Ravengard's jaw tightened. He knew what a Harper was, and he did not like them. But he was a pragmatist, and pragmatists listened.

"The villa," he said. "I know about it. The warm wells, the basement activity, the growing number of recruits. The Fist has been watching."

He paused. His voice dropped.

"But I cannot act openly. The villa is on private land. The owner died, no heirs, and the legal proceedings would take months. By then, whatever they're doing down there will be done."

He pulled a letter from his desk and slid it across. It was sealed with the Fist's mark.

"I'm giving you authority," he said. "A letter of marque. Enter the villa, search the premises, arrest suspects. I cannot accompany you. The Fist is stretched thin."

He leaned forward. His voice was low, almost a whisper.

"The cult is not just worshiping Bane. They're using the villa's basement for something else. Something older. Be careful what you find down there."

Elara took the letter. She did not open it. She would not. Not yet.

---

## Part IV: The Villa at Dusk

The Sunspring Villa was not what they expected.

They had expected a fortress. What they found was a ruin — walls crumbling, gardens wild, vines climbing every surface like green fingers. The estate had been beautiful once. Now it was beautiful in the way a corpse is beautiful — preserved, pale, waiting.

But the basement windows glowed. Faint, warm light, like a hearth fire deep in the earth.

They approached at dusk. The light was easier to see in the fading sun.

Four figures stood at the gate. Cultists. Robed, hooded, faces hidden. They carried daggers, not swords. They were not warriors, but they were devoted, and devotion is its own kind of armor.

Kaelen stopped. His eyes narrowed. "Four," he said. "Not enough to hold a fortress. Enough to hold a gate."

"Options?" Elara asked.

Finn stepped forward first. He had been practicing this.

---

### The Disguise

The melody Finn played was simple — a tavern song, light and easy, the kind you hum while you drink. The cultists did not react to the music. They reacted to the voices.

"We're recruits," Finn said, his tone bright, confident, the voice of a man who belonged. "The word spread. The wells call to those who hear them."

The lead cultist tilted his head. "Who sent you?"

"Vell," Finn said. The goblin's name, used without knowing what it meant. A gamble.

The cultist hesitated. One second. Two. Three.

"Pass," he said.

Elara's eyes met Finn's. She did not smile. She did not need to. The gamble had worked.

They walked through the gate.

---

## Part V: The Ground Floor

The villa's interior was abandoned but not empty. Dust covered everything. Furniture rotted. The air smelled of copper and old stone. But the walls — the walls were marked with symbols.

The same symbols from the tower. From the Milestone. From the road.

Tilly's goggles caught the light. Her mind was racing. "They're not decorations," she said. "They're a map."

She stepped closer, tracing the lines with her eyes. The symbols formed a pattern — a pentagram, five points connected by lines. The Shattered Tower at the top. The villa at the second point. A forest at the third. A mountain beneath Waterdeep at the fourth. And at the center —

"The Cormyrean Milestone," she whispered. "It's the keystone. If it fails, all five fail."

Malachi's orb flared. "A network," he said. "Not five separate seals. One system. Breaking one accelerates the others."

Kaelen's hand went to his hammer. "Which means the cult here is not just attacking this seal. They're attacking the whole coast."

Elara's eyes were sharp. "Ravengard warned us. Something older."

Tilly traced the lines on the wall, her fingers light, her mind working. "The lines form a chain. Tower to villa. Villa to forest. Forest to mountain. Mountain to milestone. If the chain breaks at the keystone —"

"The coast falls," Lyra finished. Her seeds pulsed in her pouch, warm and urgent. The Neverwood was Seal #3, and she could feel it — the Blight spreading, the forest dying, the roots reaching for water that was no longer there.

They moved toward the basement stairs.

---

### The Test

The floor had pressure plates. Tilly recognized the pattern immediately — blue tiles safe, red tiles dangerous. The same pattern from the tower.

"The tower taught us," she said. "The villa is testing whether we learned."

They stepped carefully. Blue. Blue. Blue. No traps triggered. No magic fired. The villa was watching, and it was satisfied.

Malachi paused. "It's weaker than the tower. Testing, not punishing."

"Good," Elara said. "We don't need punishment. We need answers."

---

## Part VI: The Basement

The stairs were intact. The door was locked. The warm light grew brighter as they descended, and the air grew hot, thick, hard to breathe.

Tilly worked quickly. Her tinker's tools came out, her hands moving fast, her mind focused. She crafted cooling amulets — small, crude, but functional. She handed them out, one for each.

"Wear these," she said. "The heat is magical. The amulets won't stop it, but they'll help."

They put them on. The warmth was still intense, but bearable.

At the bottom of the stairs, the basement opened.

---

### The Warm Wells

Three wells stood in the room — stone circles, water glowing faintly orange. The warmth came from below, rising through cracks in the floor, filling the air with steam and the smell of copper.

Beyond the wells, a circle of twelve cultists stood around a pit. The pit led deeper, into the rock, and the warm water flowed from below. The cultists were chanting, low and steady, their voices rising and falling like a tide.

In the center of the circle, a man stood. Human. Older. His robes were dark, his face lined, his eyes bright. He was not shouting. He was not gesturing. He was speaking, and the cultists listened.

"You think the seals protect the coast," the man said. His voice carried, clear and calm. "They do not. They imprison it. The pressure they hold back is not a threat — it is a gift. The old scholars were afraid of power they could not control, so they built cages and called them shields. I am freeing what they imprisoned."

He turned. His eyes met theirs.

"You are early," he said. "But you are here. Good. Let me show you what lies beneath."

---

### Malakor

His name was Malakor. Former priest of Helm. His temple had been destroyed, and he had turned to Bane in the aftermath. He was not a madman. He was a man who had been hurt, and he had found a god who told him his pain had purpose.

"You match the prophecy," he said. "Five travelers. The Harper makes six. The prophecy says you will save the coast or doom it. I believe you will doom it. Not out of malice. Out of necessity."

He spread his arms. The cultists did not move. The chanting continued.

"The seals were built by people who feared magic. Who wanted control. Who did not trust the land. I am giving the coast its strength back."

Malachi stepped forward. His orb was dark, his voice low. "Bane does not care about the coast. He uses you. When the seal breaks, he will discard you like tools."

Malakor smiled. "I know. I do not care."

Elara raised her hammer. Her oath to Tyr burned bright. "You're wrong," she said. "The seals protect. I've seen what happens when they crack. The land shakes. The magic leaks. People die."

"People die when the old order falls," Malakor said. "New order rises. That is how the world works."

Finn plucked a chord on Whisper. The note was sharp, clear, cutting through the chanting. The cultists faltered. Their voices wavered.

"That's enough," Elara said.

---

### The Ritual

The cultists moved first.

They were not warriors, but they were devoted, and devotion makes cowards brave. They rushed the party, daggers drawn, eyes bright, voices raised in Bane's name.

Kaelen met them. His hammer swung, his chain mail turned blows, his dwarf legs were short but strong. He did not fight to kill. He fought to hold.

Lyra moved to the treeline — no, there was no treeline here. She moved to the edge of the room, her bow drawn, her aim true. She did not shoot to kill. She shot to disable.

Malachi cast. The words came easily — *burning hands*, *hellish rebuke*, his orb flaring with each spell. He did not hold back. The cult was attacking the seals, and his patron hated what Bane stood for.

Elara stood at the front, shield raised, hammer striking. Her *Sacred Edict* Channel Divinity blazed — a command to the cultists, clear and loud: *Choose. Me or him.*

Three cultists hesitated. Their eyes flicked to Malakor, then to Elara, then to the pit. They lowered their daggers. They stepped back.

Finn played. The melody was not a tavern song this time. It was deeper, older, the melody from the dreams — the one about five travelers, a tower, and a villa with warm wells. The music reached the cultists, and they remembered. They remembered who they were before the cult. Before Bane. Before the chanting.

Two more stepped back.

Tilly worked fast. She did not fight. She calculated. The ritual was channeling magic into the pit, weakening the seal. Each round the ritual continued, the seal weakened further. She had six rounds to disrupt it.

She had three.

"Malakor!" she shouted. "The seal reaches critical in three rounds. You know this. You've been counting."

Malakor did not stop chanting. "Let it break. The coast will be free."

"Free of what?" Lyra asked. She had lowered her bow. Her voice was quiet, but it carried. "You talk about freeing the land. But the Blight is spreading. The forest is dying. The Neverwood — it's my home. What happens to it when the seal breaks?"

Malakor's eyes flicked to her. For a moment, something passed through them — doubt, maybe, or memory. But it was gone quickly.

"The old world dies," he said. "The new one grows."

Elara struck. Not Malakor. The pit.

Her hammer hit the stone edge, and the stone cracked. The warm water surged, then receded. The chanting faltered.

Malakor turned to her. His face was calm. "You think you are saving the coast. You are only delaying the inevitable."

"Maybe," Elara said. "But I'm here now."

---

## Part VII: The Choice

The ritual was broken. The pit sealed temporarily, the warm water receded, and the basement went dark. The cultists stood still, uncertain, their leader's voice silenced.

Malakor looked at them. His eyes were tired now. The fanaticism had burned out, and what remained was a man who had believed too hard in something that was wrong.

"You have won this round," he said. "But the seals were built by people who feared the truth. The truth is coming. And when it does, your choices will not matter."

Elara lowered her hammer. She looked at the party.

"We have a choice," she said.

Kaelen nodded. "Destroy the villa. Collapse the basement, seal the pit permanently. The cult is trapped. The seal is secure. But the secrets are lost."

"Or seal the villa," Malachi said. "Lock the basement, trap the cult alive. Buy thirty days. But they survive."

"Or convert them," Finn said. His voice was soft, but his eyes were bright. "Malakor is tired. The cultists are uncertain. We can turn them. It's harder. But if we succeed —"

"They become allies," Tilly finished. "Informants. Malakor knows the seals. His knowledge is invaluable."

Elara looked at Malakor. "What do you say?"

Malakor smiled. It was not a bitter smile. It was not a kind one. It was honest.

"I say you cannot save all five seals. You can save three. The other two will fall. And when they do, the coast will remember your names."

He was not entirely wrong.

---

### The Decision

Elara looked at each of them.

Kaelen: the dwarf who survived Mithral Hall, who carried guilt like armor. He wanted to destroy. Clean. Final. No secrets left to haunt him.

Lyra: the elf who ran from her forest, who could feel the Blight spreading. She wanted to seal. Buy time. The Neverwood needed time.

Malachi: the warlock who had made a pact to save his mother, who knew what fiends wanted. He wanted to convert. Knowledge was power, and power was control.

Finn: the bard who played music to connect, who dreamed of five travelers. He wanted to convert. Music was persuasion, and persuasion was his song.

Tilly: the gnome who had lost Oren, who calculated every outcome. She wanted to seal. Thirty days was data, and data was survival.

Remi: the Harper who had orchestrated this, who watched from the shadows. He said nothing. He let them choose.

Elara closed her eyes. Her oath to Tyr burned bright. She was a shield for those who could not protect themselves.

"We convert them," she said.

Kaelen nodded. He did not argue. He trusted her.

Lyra closed her eyes. She trusted the forest, and the forest trusted her.

Malachi's orb went dark. He trusted the words, and the words were truth.

Finn smiled. He trusted the music, and the music was ready.

Tilly adjusted her goggles. She trusted the math, and the math said thirty days was enough.

Remi nodded. He had been waiting for this.

---

### The Persuasion

It was not easy. DC 20 Charisma (Persuasion), and all six had to contribute.

Elara spoke first. Her voice was clear, her oath was bright, her hammer was steady. "You were Helm's priests once. Helm protects. Bane conquers. Which god do you serve?"

Three cultists looked away. Not convinced, but uncertain.

Finn played. The melody was not sharp this time. It was soft, warm, the kind of music you hear when you're home. The cultists remembered their families, their homes, their lives before the villa.

Two more looked away.

Malachi spoke Infernal. Not a spell. A name. The name of the fiend who had approached him, the name of the patron who had saved his mother. "Bane uses you," he said. "I know. He will discard you when the seal breaks. I know what it costs."

Two more lowered their heads.

Tilly showed them the math. She drew the pentagram in the dust, traced the lines, showed them how breaking one seal accelerated the others. "The coast falls if the keystone turns," she said. "You know this. Malakor knows this. He just doesn't care."

Malakor did not answer. But he did not stop her.

Lyra placed a seed on the floor. It glowed, then sank into the stone. A small thing, but it was life — growth, roots, water. The opposite of the Blight. The opposite of what the seals were failing to stop.

Kaelen struck the floor with his hammer. Not a blow. A tap. The stone rang like a bell, clear and true. "This stone remembers," he said. "It remembers the hand that carved it. The hand was afraid. But it was not wrong to be afraid."

Six voices. Six contributions. All of them true.

The cultists lowered their daggers.

Malakor stood alone.

---

## Part VIII: What the Villa Revealed

The basement went quiet. The warm wells dimmed. The pit sealed, not permanently, but enough. Thirty days.

The cultists did not fight. They did not flee. They stood, uncertain, waiting.

Elara turned to them. "You have a choice," she said. "Walk away, or walk with us."

Some walked away. Some walked with them.

Malakor stayed. He did not join them. He did not leave. He sat by the pit, his eyes closed, his voice quiet. He was praying, or thinking, or both.

Tilly worked quickly. She searched the basement, her goggles catching every detail, her mind cataloging everything.

She found letters. Correspondence between Malakor and someone in Undermountain. The letters mentioned "the deep seal" and "the pressure beneath Waterdeep." Seal #4. Connected to the cult.

She found a map. Five seals, marked. And a sixth location — the Cormyrean Milestone. The keystone. The inscription said: *"Do not approach until the other five are addressed."*

She found a journal. Malakor's personal records, detailing his research. He believed the seals were built to suppress the Sword Coast's natural magic. He was wrong about the outcome. He was right about the origin.

And she found a warning. Carved into the basement wall, in the third language — the same language from the Milestone. Tilly could not read it yet. But she recognized the style.

*"When the keystone turns, the network fails. Protect the center at all costs."*

She showed the warning to Remi. His eyes narrowed.

"The Harper network knew," he said quietly. "The keystone is the priority. The other four are supports. If the center falls —"

"The coast falls," Elara said.

Remi nodded. "We need to find the Milestone. And we need to protect it."

---

## Part IX: The Return

They left the villa at dawn. The cultists who had joined them walked behind, uncertain but willing. The ones who had walked away were gone, scattered into the morning mist.

Malakor did not move. He sat by the pit, his eyes closed, his voice quiet. Elara looked back once. He did not look up.

The Risen Road was different now.

The Lastwall Gate's symbols glowed faintly, the seal in the mountains affected by the villa's stabilization. The road felt steadier, the arcane residue thinner. The network was healing, slowly.

At Chionthar's Bend, the river bandits were gone. Their boats were burned, their camp abandoned. The cult at the villa had been supporting them, and without that support, they had scattered.

They passed the Cormyrean Milestone. The inscription glowed brighter than before. The third language was almost readable. Tilly stopped, her goggles catching the light, her mind straining.

She made out one word.

*"Soon."*

She did not tell the others. Not yet.

---

## Part X: The Intelligence

They reached Daggerford by dusk. The Boar's Tooth was waiting. Remi was waiting.

He had news.

"Undermountain," he said. "Seal #4 is stable but threatened. The cult has contacts deep in the mountain. Skullport, the undercity, the tunnels beneath Waterdeep. They're preparing."

He pulled a map from his vest. It showed the five seals, connected by lines. The pentagram was clear now, and the keystone was at the center.

"Seal #3 is the Neverwood," he said. "The Blight is spreading. Sixty days until it reaches critical. Lyra, you feel it, don't you?"

Lyra nodded. Her seeds were warm, pulsing. The forest was calling, and the call was urgent.

"Seal #5 is the Milestone," Remi continued. "The keystone. We don't know where it is, exactly. But we know this: if it fails, all five fail. The party cannot save all five seals. They must choose which three to prioritize."

He looked at each of them.

"The road ahead leads north. Neverwinter. The Neverwood. The Blight. Seal #3 is the next target."

He paused.

"But Undermountain is the cult's heart. If we strike there, we cut the head off the snake. Seal #4 becomes secure, and the cult loses its network."

Elara looked at the map. She looked at the party.

"Two targets," she said. "Three seals can be saved. Two must be abandoned."

Kaelen's voice was low. "We choose wisely."

Finn plucked a chord on Whisper. The melody was uncertain, searching. The prophecy had said five travelers would save the coast or doom it. They had stabilized two seals. Three remained.

And the keystone was hidden.

Remi stood up. "Rest tonight. We move at dawn."

He did not say where. Not yet.

The map was on the table. The pentagram was clear. The keystone was at the center, veiled in fog.

And the word on the Milestone burned in Tilly's mind.

*Soon.*
