# The Coast Breaks — Session Five

## Part I: The Sky Thins

The dragonward did not fall all at once. It thinned. 

From the Trade Way, it looked like oil on water—a shimmer above Waterdeep catching the dawn light, pulling taut, and then tearing. The sound didn't reach the road, but the vibrations traveled through the bedrock. Kaelen stopped mid-stride. His jaw locked, and his grip on Dawnbreaker tightened until the leather creaked. He didn't say he was terrified; he felt the tremor in his teeth and looked at the sky.

"That's not surface magic," Kaelen said, his voice a low gravel rumble.

Remi pulled his hood further down, though the sun wasn't in his eyes. "Undermountain. The deep seals are buckling. Both of them."

When they reached the gates, no one checked their papers. A fruit merchant had abandoned a cart of winter apples in the center of the thoroughfare; people were rushing past it, crushing the fruit into the cobblestones. The smell of bruised apples and ozone hung over the city.

Laeral Silverhand did not meet them in a throne room. The Open Lord of Waterdeep was standing on a balcony overlooking the chaos, her hands braced on the stone rail. Her silver hair was tied back, but strands had escaped, clinging to the sweat on her neck. She didn't look like a legend. She looked like a woman who had been awake for three days holding a door shut.

"Neverember," Elara said, stepping onto the balcony. Her hand rested on the pommel of her hammer—anchoring herself.

Laeral didn't turn around. "He’s in the deep chamber. The network is unraveling in sequence. If the Cormyrean Milestone turns before the pressure is vented—" 

"The coast fractures," Tilly said. She pushed her goggles up, pulling them down immediately when she realized she’d done it. "In theory. Well, mathematically certain, not just theory."

Laeral finally turned. "I cannot send the Watchwarriors. The city is panicking. If I pull the guard, Waterdeep burns before the magic even hits us." She met Elara’s amber eyes. "I need you to descend. Stop him. Not for the Alliance. For the people currently crushing apples in my streets."

## Part II: The Price of the Seals

The Castle Ward war room felt too small for the six people inside it. 

Malakor sat at the edge of the table. The cult leader looked ancient in the morning light. He rubbed his left knee slowly, rhythmically, the damp chill of the impending magical storm aggravating old joint pain. He wasn't menacing. He was just a man with bad knees who had believed suffering was the only honest teacher. 

"The cult is fractured," Malakor said softly, not looking up from his knee. "If you vent the pressure through the Sunspring Villa, the well floods. Bane's influence dilutes. My people will scatter."

"Good," Kaelen said abruptly. 

Malakor stopped rubbing his knee. "Yes. It is."

Remi spread a map, flattening the curled edges with two daggers. "The Milestone is the keystone. That is non-negotiable. You can restore three seals. You must release one to drop the pressure. The released seal shatters, and whatever is housed there takes the brunt."

Tilly traced the lines on the map. "Undermountain is Neverember's engine. That's seal four. We secure that. The Neverwood is healing. The Tower is stable. The math leaves the Villa. If we release seal two..."

"The cult’s power breaks permanently," Elara said. "And the pressure drops enough for the network to hold."

"It's a gamble," an Emerald Enclave Warden muttered from the corner. "If Neverember has already tapped the deep seal, you'll be walking into an oven."

Finn strummed a discordant chord on Whisper, letting the sour note hang in the room. "Friends. Have we walked into anything else lately?"

Elara picked up her shield. "We descend. Now."

## Part III: Descent

They did not fight their way through Undermountain. They endured it. 

The descent took six hours, but time warped the further down they went. In the first hour, the air grew stale. By the third, gravity felt inconsistent—a heavy, cloying pressure that made lungs burn and ears pop. Finn hummed the song of the Boar's Tooth, anchoring them to a rhythm that didn't belong to the dungeon. Malachi’s obsidian orb pulsed hot against his hip, his patron hissing static into his mind. 

They bypassed random shifts in reality—a hallway of glass, a staircase that bled—navigating purely by Tilly's frantic, muttered calculations and Kaelen's instinct for stone. 

When they reached the final corridor before the deep chamber, Tilly stopped so fast Kaelen nearly knocked her over. 

"Tiles," she whispered. 

The floor was a mosaic of shifting red and blue. Spores drifted lazily above the red. 

"The Neverwood blight," Lyra breathed. She didn't draw her bow. She put a hand to her chest, feeling the phantom ache of the corrupted forest. 

*"You cannot stop the inevitable,"* a voice echoed from the walls. Neverember. *"The Alliance is a rotting corpse. I am the fire that clears the brush. Turn back."*

Elara didn't hesitate. "He's deflecting. If it was inevitable, he wouldn't be talking. Blue tiles. Move."

They crossed the threshold. 

## Part IV: The Core Lie

The deep chamber smelled of burning copper and old dust. The seal crystal floated in the center, spider-webbed with cracks that glowed blinding white. 

Dagult Neverember stood before it. He wasn't casting. He was writing in a small, leather-bound accounting ledger. He closed the book deliberately, placed a charcoal pencil in the spine, and set it on a stone plinth. 

He didn't look like a madman. He looked tired. 

"You tracked mud on the teleportation circle, Elara," Neverember said, finally turning. His armor was immaculate. 

"You're under arrest by order of the Open Lord," Elara said. Her voice didn't shake, but her hand gripped her warhammer tight enough that her knuckles popped. 

Neverember smiled—a small, sad expression. "Laeral is a bureaucrat holding an umbrella in a hurricane. Without me in absolute control, the coast dies, Elara. You know this. The Alliance debates while people starve. I act."

"You act for yourself." 

"I carry the burden no one else has the stomach for!" Neverember’s voice finally broke its calm, echoing off the runes. Elite guards stepped from the shadows behind him, raising halberds. 

Elara looked at him, and for a terrifying second, she saw herself. The absolute certainty that she knew best. The willingness to make the hard choice. It made her stomach turn. 

"Take the guards," Elara said to the party. She didn't wait to see if they followed. She charged the Lord of Neverwinter.

The clash was deafening. Kaelen caught two halberds on his shield, his boots sliding back an inch on the stone. He dropped his center of gravity. "Thardir taught me that," he grunted, swinging his own hammer low to break a guard's knee. "Weight beats speed."

Malachi cast *Hellish Rebuke*, the flames illuminating the cavern, but his eyes never left Neverember. Finn’s music wasn't a battle march; it was a dirge, slow and heartbreaking, meant to sow doubt in the guards' minds. 

Neverember parried Elara’s strike. His blade moved with brutal efficiency. "You swore an oath to the Alliance," he snarled, locking their weapons. "You are protecting your own cage."

"Oaths aren't cages," Elara said, her breath ragged. "They're anchors."

Neverember shoved her back and reached for the crystal. "Then sink with them."

He channeled magic directly into the cracked stone. The light turned blinding.

"He's overriding it!" Tilly screamed over the roar of magic. "If it shatters, Waterdeep falls!"

Elara had a choice. Strike the man, or strike the crystal to forcibly stabilize the matrix. Striking the crystal meant breaking the very artifact she had sworn to protect. It was blasphemy for an Oath of the Crown. 

She didn't look at Neverember. She looked at the crystal. She swung *Justice's Weight* with both hands. 

The hammer hit the crystal. The resulting shockwave threw Elara backward. She heard the sickening *crack* of her own radius bone snapping in her left arm as she hit the floor. Her pristine shield, strapped to her broken arm, shattered down the center. 

The crystal chimed—a pure, resonant note. The searing light collapsed into a steady, dim pulse. 

Neverember fell to his knees. The magical feedback had drained him entirely. He looked at the ledger on the plinth, then at his empty hands. He didn't try to stand back up.

"Chain him," Elara gasped, clutching her ruined arm against her chest. "Let Laeral deal with the paperwork."

## Part V: The Milestone

It took them four days to reach the Cormyrean Milestone on the Risen Road. They didn't speak of the battle. Kaelen set Elara's arm the first night. She hadn't complained, but her jaw had locked tight enough to crack a tooth.

The Milestone stood tall in the plains, its ancient runes glowing a faint, expectant amber. Tilly stood before it for a long time. She took her goggles off, wiped them on her sleeve, and put them back on. They didn't help her read the third language any better, but the ritual seemed necessary.

"It knows we're here," Tilly murmured. "The network is waiting for the input."

She read the runes aloud, her voice trembling slightly. 
*"When five travelers stand at the keystone, each carrying a piece of the truth, the network can be restored—not by force, but by choice."*

Remi emerged from the tall grass. He never seemed to travel; he simply arrived. "Five dials," the Harper said. "You lock three. You vent one."

They moved to the stone together. 

Kaelen turned the dial for the Tower. The stone shifted heavily. 
Lyra turned the dial for the Neverwood. She imagined roots taking hold.
Malachi turned the dial for Undermountain. His patron went terrifyingly quiet.
Finn placed a hand on the dial for the Sunspring Villa. He looked at the others, then turned it counter-clockwise. The vent. The release. 
Tilly stepped up to the final dial. The Keystone. She turned it until it locked with a sound like a vault door shutting.

The ground did not shake. The sky did not crack.

Instead, the air simply felt... lighter. 

A vision washed over them, not as a booming voice, but as a quiet understanding. They saw the Shattered Tower glowing blue. The Neverwood drinking clean water. Undermountain locked in silence. And the Sunspring Villa, far away, crumbling as the warm wells boiled over, washing the cult's legacy out into the dirt. 

The pressure was gone. The Sword Coast breathed.

## Part VI: The Empty Chair

It was raining when they walked back into the Boar's Tooth in Daggerford. 

The tavern was exactly as they had left it months ago. A man in the corner was loudly complaining about the price of imported grain. The fire in the hearth snapped and hissed. The mundane world had persisted, completely ignorant of how close it had come to an end. 

They took their usual table near the back. 

Kaelen unstrapped his hammer. He placed it on the table. It was notched deeply near the head, the dark metal scarred white. He looked at it, then deliberately pulled his hand away and rested it on his knee. He didn't pick the hammer back up.

Lyra sat down, her hand instinctively brushing the pouch at her belt. It was flat. Empty. The seeds were in the Neverwood. Her fingers hovered over the leather for a moment, tracing the absent shapes, before she let her hand drop to her lap.

Elara’s left arm was bound in a sling across her chest. The shattered pieces of her shield had been left on the road. The sigil of the Lords' Alliance was gone. She adjusted the sling, her face betraying a wince, but she didn't reach for her holy symbol to heal it. Some breaks needed to heal the slow way. 

Finn took Whisper off his back and leaned it against the wall. The tavern was noisy, begging for a song, but he didn't reach for the strings. He just stared at the worn wood of the neck.

Tilly folded her goggles and set them lens-down on the sticky table. She closed her eyes and massaged the bridge of her nose.

There was one empty chair at the table. Remi had vanished into the rain before they entered Daggerford. Nobody moved to take his seat, and no one pulled it out of the way. It sat there, speaking louder than the tavern noise.

The bartender set down six mugs of ale without being asked. He didn't look at them closely. He wiped down the adjacent table and moved on. 

Finn wrapped his hand around his mug. The clay was cold. He looked up, meeting the eyes of the people he had bled with.

"To the road," Finn said softly. 

They raised their mugs. Nobody cheered. Nobody smiled. 

Outside, the rain kept falling on the muddy streets of Daggerford. The network was secure. The world had survived.

They drank the ale in silence. It was bitter. It always had been.

And the fire burned on.
