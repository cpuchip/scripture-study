# The Blight Unbound — Session Four

## Part I: Neverwinter

---

The city was holding its breath.

Neverwinter stood on the edge of the dying forest, its walls tall, its gates guarded, its people moving fast and quiet. The Blight was visible from the road — a gray-green haze at the edge of the Neverwood, creeping closer each day like a stain on a white shirt.

Lyra stopped at the edge of the city. Her seeds pulsed — hot now, urgent, almost painful. She pressed her hand to her pouch and closed her eyes.

"It's worse," she said. "The forest is screaming."

Kaelen looked at the walls. "Those are reinforcements," he said. "New battlements. Reinforced gates. They're bracing for something."

"Not something," Elara said. She had been watching the guards. "Someone. They're expecting an attack."

Remi did not remove his hood. "The Blight is the attack. But it's not the only one."

They entered the city.

---

### The General

General Esvele Greycrone found them before they found her. She was tall, sharp-eyed, dressed in armor that had seen more battles than most men see winters. She did not wait for introductions.

"The Blight is moving faster," she said. "The Wardens at the forest edge say it's accelerating. Forty days, perhaps less, before it reaches the city walls."

She paused. Her voice dropped.

"But that's not why I found you. Neverember's agents are still in the city. They've been sabotaging the defenses — weakening the walls, spreading panic, hoarding supplies. They think the Blight will do their work for them."

"Neverember is dead," Elara said. "He died in the Icefox Tower."

"Did he?" Greycone's eyes were cold. "Because the people I've been tracking sure don't act like they're working for a ghost."

She handed Elara a scroll. It was a map of the Warrens — the tunnels beneath Neverwinter — with three locations marked.

"They meet tonight," Greycrone said. "An abandoned warehouse near the lower docks. I need you inside."

Finn tilted his head. "And the Blight?"

"The Wardens can handle the forest," Greycrone said. "For now. But I need those agents exposed before they weaken the walls further. If the Blight reaches the city and the walls are gone —"

"We die," Malachi said. "Straightforward."

"Practical," Greycrone said. "I like practical."

Elara looked at the map. She looked at the party.

"We split," she said. "Kaelen, Malachi, Finn — you're with me in the Warrens. Lyra, Tilly, Remi — you go to the Wardens. Find out what the Blight really is."

Tilly adjusted her goggles. "Two problems, two teams. Efficient."

"Risky," Malachi said. But he did not argue. His orb was dark, and darkness meant he was listening to something the others couldn't hear.

Elara nodded. "Move."

---

## Part II: The Warrens

The tunnels were narrow, wet, and smelled like old stone and older things. Kaelen led, his hammer low, his eyes scanning the ceiling for loose rock. Elara walked beside him, her shield ready. Malachi walked behind them, his orb pulsing faintly — not with magic, but with warning.

Finn walked last. He hummed, low and steady, and the echo told him what was ahead — open space, narrow passages, the sound of water dripping from stone. The tunnels were alive with sound, and he listened to every note.

They reached the sewer entrance first. The air was thick, poisonous, heavy with spores. Malachi held up his hand.

"Constitution save," he muttered. "All of us."

Kaelen breathed through his nose. Elara held her breath. Finn plugged his ears with his fingers and prayed to Liriel. Malachi whispered a word to his patron, and the word held.

They passed.

The tunnel opened into a larger passage, and the passage opened into the warehouse.

---

### The Warehouse

The warehouse was large, empty, echoing. Neverember's agents were gathered in the center — six of them, robed, hooded, faces hidden. They were not cultists. They were soldiers. Organized. Disciplined.

Finn pressed himself against the wall. He raised Whisper and played a single note — soft, low, almost inaudible. The note carried through the warehouse, and the agents did not react. Good.

He played another note. A third. He was mapping the room through sound, learning where the walls were, where the people stood, where the exits were.

Kaelen caught his eye and pointed. Three agents near the front. Three near the back. A table in the center, covered with maps and scrolls.

Elara nodded. She stepped into the warehouse, her armor gleaming in the dim light, her hammer raised.

"By order of General Greycrone," she said. Her voice was clear, loud, authoritative. The voice of the Fist. "You are under arrest."

The agents turned. They did not panic. They did not run. They reached for their weapons, slow and deliberate, trained.

Kaelen met the front line. His hammer swung, his chain mail turned blows, his dwarf legs planted firm. He did not fight to kill. He fought to pin.

Elara was beside him, shield raised, hammer striking. Her oath to Tyr burned bright, and the agents felt it — the weight of a god who judged, who condemned, who did not forgive.

Two agents broke. They dropped their weapons and raised their hands.

Malachi cast. *Hold person.* The words came easy, the magic older than the warehouse, older than the city, older than the Blight. Three agents froze, their eyes wide, their bodies still.

Finn played. The melody was not a song of war. It was a song of surrender — soft, slow, the kind of music you hear when the fighting is done and the world is quiet again. The last two agents lowered their weapons. They did not fight. They did not run. They stood, uncertain, waiting.

Silence fell over the warehouse.

---

### The Evidence

Tilly was not there to see it, but she would have recognized the pattern. The maps on the table showed the five seals, connected by lines. The scrolls were letters — correspondence between Neverember's agents and the goblin boss, between the agents and the cult at the villa, between the agents and someone in Undermountain.

Neverember was not dead. He was hiding. And he was pulling strings.

Kaelen picked up a scroll. His eyes scanned the text, and his jaw tightened. "He knows about the seals," he said. "He's been funding the goblins, the cult, everything. He's accelerating the failures."

"Why?" Elara asked.

Kaelen did not answer. He did not need to. The answer was on the map — the pentagram, the keystone, the network. Neverember wanted the pressure to release. He wanted the power.

Malachi's orb flared. "My patron knows this hunger," he said quietly. "It is the same hunger that drives fiends. Power for its own sake. Control for its own sake. It does not care what breaks in the process."

Finn lowered Whisper. His face was pale. "He's not just attacking the seals. He's attacking the coast."

Elara rolled up the scrolls. "Greycrone needs to see this." She looked at Kaelen. "We hold these agents. The rest of the party needs to reach the Wardens."

Kaelen nodded. "We split was right."

---

## Part III: The Wardens

Lyra walked at the edge of the Neverwood, and the forest greeted her.

Not with welcome. With warning.

The trees were thin, their leaves gray-green, their branches twisted like arthritic fingers. The ground was soft, spongy, wet with spores that rose in clouds with every step. The air was thick, hard to breathe, heavy with the smell of decay.

But Lyra had the ward stone. It was warm in her hand, carved with runes older than the forest, older than the Blight. It pulsed with the same rhythm as her seeds, and the rhythm was steady.

Tilly walked beside her, her goggles catching the spores, her tinker's tools already out. She was working — crafting filters, blending herbs, calculating concentrations. Her hands moved fast, her mind faster.

Remi walked behind them, his hood still up, his eyes scanning the treeline. He did not trust the forest. He did not trust the Blight. He trusted the party, and that was enough.

The Wardens were waiting at the edge of the Middle zone.

Three druids. Tall, lean, their skin marked with bark-like patterns, their eyes bright and ancient. They did not smile. They did not bow. They watched.

The eldest spoke. His voice was rough, like stone grinding against stone.

"Lyra Nightbreeze. You returned."

"I had to," Lyra said. Her voice was quiet, but it carried. "The forest is dying."

"The forest is being killed," the Warden said. "There is a difference."

He stepped forward. His eyes were sharp, scanning the party. "The Blight was seeded sixty days ago. At the heart of the forest. A device — mechanical, magical, ancient. It was placed by someone who knew exactly what they were doing."

Tilly's goggles caught his every word. "A device? Not a spell? Not a curse?"

"Worse," the Warden said. "A machine. Built by people who understood both magic and steel. It is part of the seal network — corrupted, repurposed, turned against the forest."

He turned to Lyra. His voice softened, just a fraction.

"The wards protect the device. Old wards, older than us, older than the Neverwood. We cannot penetrate them without triggering the Blight. But you — you carry the seeds. The seeds were meant to replace the device. To restore the forest naturally."

He placed the ward stone in Lyra's hand. It was warm, heavier than it looked, carved with runes that matched the device's description.

"This will protect you," he said. "And it will guide you to the heart. But you must go alone. We cannot enter the wards. Our presence would trigger the Blight."

Lyra closed her fingers around the stone. She could feel the forest through it — the roots, the water, the life that was still there, struggling, fighting.

"I won't fail you," she said.

The Warden's eyes were sad. "Many have said that. Few have kept the word."

He turned to Tilly. "You can craft filters. They will help at the edge. But deeper in, the Blight does not care about filters. It cares about will."

Tilly nodded. "I know."

Remi stepped forward. "And the device? Who built it? Who activated it?"

The Warden's voice dropped. "We do not know who built it. But we know who activated it. Someone left a marker at the seed point. A name, carved into the stone."

He paused.

"Neverember."

---

## Part IV: Into the Neverwood

They entered at dawn.

The Outer zone was poison. Spores filled the air, thick and gray, and every breath burned. Tilly's filters helped — advantage on the save, she had said — but the air was still heavy, still thick, still killing.

Kaelen had rejoined them by then. The warehouse was secured, Greycrone had the evidence, and the agents were in chains. He walked at the front, his hammer resting easy on his shoulder, his eyes scanning the treeline.

Elara walked beside him, her shield raised, her hammer ready. She did not trust the forest. She trusted the party, and that was enough.

Malachi walked in the center, his orb dark. He did not speak. He did not need to. The Blight was feeding him information — whispers, fragments, the shape of what waited ahead.

Finn walked behind them, humming. The melody was different now — darker, deeper, matching the forest and the Blight and the stone. Whisper hummed in response, its willow wood warm against his back.

Lyra walked at the edge, her hand on the ward stone. She could feel the forest — the roots, the water, the life. She could feel the Blight too — the corruption, the decay, the machine at the heart. She did not flinch.

They reached the Middle zone by midday.

The Blight fought back.

---

### The Tree of Names

The oak was massive. Its trunk was wide as a house, its branches thick as pillars, its bark covered in names — hundreds of them, carved by generations of druids. The tree did not move. It did not need to. It blocked the path, and the path did not go around.

Tilly stepped forward, her goggles catching the light, her mind working. "It's a ward," she said. "Old. Older than the current druids. The names — they're not decorations. They're keys."

Lyra placed her hand on the bark. The runes glowed faintly, warm against her palm. "It wants a name," she said. "Not yours. Someone you carry."

She stepped back. She looked at the party.

Kaelen went first. His voice was low, rough, the voice of a man who carried his father like armor.

"Thardir," he said.

The tree's leaves rustled. The path opened an inch.

Lyra closed her eyes. "Elandil," she whispered. Her brother. The one she had not been brave enough to save.

The tree's leaves rustled again. The path opened wider.

Malachi's orb pulsed. "Elara," he said. His mother. The name coincided with the paladin's, and she looked at him — surprised, maybe, but she did not speak.

The tree opened wider.

Elara's hand went to her hammer. Her voice was steady. "Tomas," she said. The recruit she had left behind. The one she had not been fast enough to save.

The tree opened wider.

Finn's voice was soft. "Lirael," he said. His mentor. The one who had taught him to listen.

The tree opened wider.

Tilly's voice was quiet. "Oren," she said. Her friend. The one who had died because of her.

The tree opened.

Its leaves whispered, and the words carried through the forest:

*"You carry the dead with you. That is why you will succeed where others failed."*

The path was clear.

They did not stop to celebrate. They did not stop to mourn. They moved forward, and the Blight moved with them.

---

### The Spore Mines

The ground was covered in them — invisible clusters of spores, waiting, patient, hungry. The pattern was the same as the tower, the villa — blue zones safe, red zones trapped.

But the Blight had learned.

Tilly stopped. Her goggles caught the glow, her mind racing. "The pattern shifted," she said. "Some blue zones are trapped. Some red zones are safe. The Blight adapted."

She knelt, pressing her hand to the ground, her tinker's tools out. She tested — a pebble here, a twig there, her mind calculating the new pattern.

"DC 16 Arcana," she muttered. "Let's see if I can read this."

She rolled her mind, her intelligence sharp, her focus absolute.

She saw it.

"Blue is still mostly safe," she said. "But every third blue zone is trapped. Red is mostly dangerous, but the ones near the roots are clear. The Blight uses the trees as anchors."

She stood. "Follow me. Carefully."

They moved, tile by tile, zone by zone, learning the pattern as they went. The first spore cluster was a warning — Finn stepped on it, and the spores erupted, poison filling the air. He stumbled, his face pale, his breath ragged.

"DC 14 Con save," Malachi said. He cast *lesser restoration*, and the poison faded from Finn's system.

"Note to self," Finn gasped. "Every third blue is bad."

"Learned," Tilly said. "Moving on."

They did not trigger another cluster. The Blight had tested them, and they had learned. The third trap — if they had failed — would have been a punishment.

They did not fail.

---

## Part V: The Blight Heart

The Inner zone was where reality broke down.

The air shimmered, the ground pulsed, and the trees were gone — replaced by gray-green stalks that twisted and writhed like serpents. The Blight was not just corruption here. It was alive.

And at the center, embedded in the heart of the ancient forest, was the device.

It was a sphere of black metal, covered in runes, pulsing with gray-green light. It was not natural. It was not magical. It was technological — built by a civilization that understood both magic and machinery, long before the current age.

Tilly's breath caught. "That technology," she whispered. "It matches the symbols from the Milestone, the tower, the villa. It's part of the seal network."

Malachi's orb flared. "The runes," he said. "They're Infernal. But older. Deeper. More fundamental than anything my patron uses. This was built before the planes were named."

Lyra felt her seeds pulse — not with fear, but with recognition. They were responding to the device, and the response was clear.

"The seeds were meant to replace it," she said. "The device was the old system. The seeds are the new one. But someone activated the device first, and the seeds have been waiting ever since."

Kaelen's eyes narrowed. "Neverember."

"Yes," Lyra said. "He seeded the Blight. He corrupted the device. He's been feeding it."

Tilly stepped closer, her goggles catching every detail, her mind cataloging everything. "I can read it," she said. "The runes, the mechanisms, the energy flow. This device is part of the seal network — but it's been repurposed. Instead of stabilizing the seal, it's draining it. The Blight is the exhaust."

Elara's hand went to her hammer. "Can we stop it?"

Tilly paused. Her voice was quiet.

"We have choices."

---

### The Choice

"Destroy it," Kaelen said. "Shatter the sphere. Stop the Blight now."

"The seal breaks," Tilly said. "The Blight stops, but Seal #3 is lost. The coast loses a support."

"Replace it," Lyra said. Her seeds were hot in her pouch, pulsing, urgent. "I plant the seeds in the device's housing. They absorb the corruption. It takes time — an hour, maybe — but the seal is restored. The forest heals."

"The seeds are consumed," Tilly said. "Lyra loses her connection to the old forest."

Lyra did not hesitate. "Worth it."

"Or repurpose it," Tilly said. Her eyes were bright, her mind racing. "I reprogram the device. Reverse its function. It's harder — DC 18 Arcana, and all six of us need to contribute — but it saves the seal, heals the forest, and preserves the device as a tool for future emergencies."

Elara looked at each of them.

Kaelen: the dwarf who survived Mithral Hall, who carried guilt like armor. He wanted to destroy. Clean. Final.

Lyra: the elf who ran from her forest, who could feel the Blight spreading. She wanted to replace. The seeds were her gift, and she was ready to give them.

Malachi: the warlock who had made a pact to save his mother, who knew what ancient power cost. He wanted to repurpose. Knowledge was power, and power was control.

Finn: the bard who played music to connect, who dreamed of five travelers. He wanted to repurpose. Music was harmony, and harmony was repair.

Tilly: the gnome who had lost Oren, who calculated every outcome. She wanted to repurpose. The math said it was possible, and possibility was hope.

Elara: the paladin who had sworn an oath to Tyr, who carried Tomas like a wound. She wanted to save what could be saved.

"We repurpose it," she said.

---

### The Work

Tilly went to work first. Her tinker's tools came out, her hands moving fast, her mind focused. She traced the runes, mapped the mechanisms, calculated the energy flow.

"It's like the tower," she said. "Like the villa. The seals respond to the party — to each of us. We need to channel our power into the device, together."

Kaelen struck the device with Dawnbreaker. Not a blow — a tap. The stone rang like a bell, and the runes flared blue.

Lyra placed her seeds on the device's surface. They glowed, warm and bright, and the gray-green light flickered. The Blight fought back — poison spores erupted, reality warped, the ground shook — but the seeds held.

Malachi spoke a word of power. Not Infernal this time. Primordial. The language of creation, older than fiends, older than the planes. The runes responded, and the device's pulse slowed.

Elara raised her hammer and spoke an oath to Tyr. The light was white, bright, pure — the light of judgment, of order, of a god who did not tolerate corruption. The Blight recoiled.

Finn played. The melody was not a tavern song. It was not a song of war. It was a song of healing — soft, slow, the kind of music you hear when the wound is closing and the body is remembering how to be whole. The device responded, its pulse matching the melody, its light shifting from gray-green to gold.

Tilly channeled magic through her crystal focus. The runes aligned, the mechanisms shifted, the energy flow reversed. The device was no longer draining the seal. It was feeding it.

The Blight fought back.

Waves of corruption rolled through the Inner zone — poison spores, reality warping, Blight creatures spawning from the stalks. The party held their ground, fighting, casting, healing, working.

Round after round, they pushed. The device resisted, then yielded, then reversed. The gray-green light faded, replaced by gold. The stalks withered, replaced by green. The air cleared, the ground steadied, the forest remembered how to breathe.

And then — silence.

The device was still. The runes were blue. The seal was stable.

Seal #3 was secure.

---

## Part VI: The Discovery

Tilly was the first to notice.

Embedded in the device's housing, behind a panel of black metal, was a journal page. It was old — sixty days, maybe older — but the ink was still legible.

She pulled it out. She read it aloud.

> *"The device is active. The Blight spreads. The seals weaken. When the coast breaks, the pressure will release — and I will be there to control it. The power of the ancient network will be mine, and with it, I will reclaim what was stolen from me. Let the scholars keep their books. Let the druids keep their trees. I will keep the coast."*

> *— Dagult Neverember*

---

Elara's eyes narrowed. "He's not dead."

"He never was," Malachi said. His orb was dark, his voice low. "My patron knows this kind of hunger. It does not die. It waits."

Kaelen's jaw tightened. "He's been pulling strings the whole time. The goblins. The cult. The Blight. All of it."

Finn lowered Whisper. His face was pale. "He's not just attacking the seals. He's attacking the coast. He wants the power when the network collapses."

Tilly's goggles caught the light. "The letters from the villa mentioned Undermountain. The deep seal — Seal #4. He's going after that next."

Lyra's seeds were quiet now. The forest was healing, and the seeds were at peace. But her eyes were sharp. "The keystone," she said. "Seal #5. The Cormyrean Milestone. If Neverember reaches it —"

"The network fails," Remi said. He had been quiet, listening, watching. Now he spoke, his voice low. "All five seals. The coast falls."

Elara looked at the journal page. She looked at the party.

"Neverember is moving," she said. "Fast. He knows we're coming, and he knows what we'll do. He's heading south — Waterdeep, Undermountain, the Milestone."

Kaelen's hand went to his hammer. "Then we don't wait."

---

## Part VII: The Return

They left the Neverwood at dusk. The forest was quiet behind them, healing, the Blight receding. The Wardens were waiting at the edge.

The eldest Warden bowed to Lyra. His voice was rough, but it carried warmth.

"The forest remembers," he said. "You have earned the right to carry its name."

Lyra smiled. It was small, but it was real. "The forest survives."

"The forest sings," the Warden said. "And your names are in the song."

They did not stop to celebrate. They did not stop to rest. The road ahead led south, toward Waterdeep and Undermountain and the Milestone and the keystone and the man who wanted to break the coast.

On the road, a messenger intercepted them — a Flaming Fist courier, breathless, his horse spent.

"Neverember has fled Neverwinter," he gasped. "Heading south. Toward Waterdeep. His final message —"

He handed Elara a scroll. She broke the seal.

> *"The seals will break. The pressure will release. And I will be waiting."*

---

Elara rolled up the scroll. She looked at the party.

"Waterdeep," she said. "Undermountain. The Milestone. We move at dawn."

Kaelen nodded. His hammer was ready.

Lyra closed her eyes. The forest was behind her, but it was alive, and that was enough.

Malachi's orb pulsed — faint, steady, certain. The words were coming, and he was ready.

Finn plucked a chord on Whisper. The melody was new — sharper, faster, the rhythm of a race. He did not know the ending yet. He would learn.

Tilly adjusted her goggles. Her mind was already working, calculating, planning. The math said they could win, and possibility was hope.

Remi did not remove his hood. He did not need to. The Harper network was moving, and the network always knew.

The road was long. The enemy was fast. The keystone was hidden.

And the coast was breaking.
