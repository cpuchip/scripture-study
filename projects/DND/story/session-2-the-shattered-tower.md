# The Shattered Tower — Session Two

## Part I: The Way of the Lion

---

The road east from Daggerford was not kind.

It was dirt and stone and the occasional rut deep enough to swallow a horse's hoof. The High Forest loomed to their left, its canopy so dense that sunlight reached the forest floor in fragments — thin, pale, uncertain. To their right, the Serpent Hills rose in jagged teeth, their slopes bare rock and the occasional shrug of scrub brush that looked more like desperation than vegetation.

Between them, the Way of the Lion was a ribbon of dust and determination.

Kaelen walked at the front, his hammer resting easy on his shoulder. He did not speak. He did not need to. The road told him what he needed to know — where the stone was solid, where it was cracked, where the earth had shifted in ways that should not have shifted. His dwarf eyes, trained on Mithral Hall's deep places, saw patterns in the rock that the others missed.

Lyra walked at the edge of the forest, her hand resting on her bow. She could hear the trees breathing — not metaphorically, but literally. The High Forest was alive, and it was watching them. She did not know if it was friendly yet. She would learn.

Malachi walked in the center, his orb dark but warm against his hip. He had not opened his book since they left Daggerford. He did not need to. The road was writing its own story, and he was reading it in the dust, the wind, the way the light fell through the trees.

Elara walked beside Kaelen, her shield on her back, her hammer in her hand. She had not spoken of Tomas since the letter. She would not. But her eyes were sharp, her stance ready, and her oath to Tyr burned bright in her chest. She was a shield for those who could not protect themselves. She would not fail them again.

Finn walked behind them all, humming. The melody had changed since Daggerford — it was darker now, deeper, with a rhythm that matched the road and the forest and the stone. Whisper hummed in response, its willow wood warm against his back. He did not know what the song was yet. He would learn.

Tilly walked wherever she wanted, which was everywhere. She stopped to examine a rock formation that looked like a broken tooth. She stopped to collect a sample of soil that smelled like copper. She stopped to argue with a spider about the structural integrity of its web. She did not stop for long. The road was moving, and she was moving with it.

They reached the Winding Water Crossing by midday.

---

## Part II: The Crossing

The river was high. Not flood-high, but high enough that the water ran fast and cold and angry, cutting through the stone like a blade. The ford was narrow, the current vicious, and the horses of the world would have turned back.

The party did not have horses.

"I'll go first," Elara said. Her plate armor was heavy, but her footing was sure. She stepped into the water, and it rose to her waist, cold enough to steal breath, fast enough to pull at her legs. She did not falter. She crossed. She stood on the far bank and waited.

Kaelen followed, his dwarf legs short but strong, his chain mail anchoring him against the current. He crossed without speaking. He did not need to.

Lyra crossed like water — smooth, effortless, her leather armor light, her balance perfect. She did not fight the river. She moved with it, and it let her pass.

Malachi crossed with the grace of a man who had learned that elegance was its own form of armor. His robes did not slow him. His orb did not weigh him down. He crossed, and he did not look back.

Finn crossed singing. The river did not care about his song, but the party did, and that was enough.

Tilly crossed last. She stopped in the middle of the ford, waded to her knees in the current, and held up a handful of water. "Fascinating," she murmured. "The mineral content is off. There's trace amounts of arcane residue in the water itself. This river has been touched by magic."

She finished crossing. She dried her goggles. She did not mention the residue again. Not yet.

Beyond the ford, the road narrowed. The forest grew denser. The light grew thinner.

And then they saw them.

Goblins.

Not many — six, maybe eight — crouched in the trees, watching, waiting. They did not attack. They watched. Their eyes were bright, their weapons crude but functional, and their leader — a smaller goblin, thinner, with eyes that burned like coals — held something in his hands that made Tilly's breath catch.

A crystal. Glowing. Pulsing. The same color as the residue in the river.

The goblins did not move. The party did not move. The forest held its breath.

Then the goblin leader raised the crystal, and the goblins vanished into the trees, silent as smoke.

Tilly stared at the space where they had been. "That was not a goblin," she said quietly. "That was a sorcerer. And that crystal was a siphon — designed to drain magical energy from a source and store it."

"From what source?" Elara asked.

Tilly did not answer. She did not need to. They all knew.

The Tower.

---

## Part III: The Outer Ruins

The Shattered Tower rose from the earth like a broken fist.

Its walls were cracked, its stones scattered, its entrance half-buried in rubble. But the magic — the magic was intact. It crackled in the air, visible as faint blue arcs that danced between the stones like lightning trapped in stone. The air hummed. The ground vibrated. The tower was alive, and it was waiting.

Kaelen stopped at the edge of the ruins. His eyes narrowed. His hand went to his hammer. "This stone," he said. "It's old. Older than Mithral Hall. Older than the dwarven holds. This was built by someone who understood the earth."

"Can you read it?" Tilly asked.

"I can feel it," Kaelen said. "That's enough."

They approached the entrance.

The first ten feet of the passage were littered with stones that hummed when touched. Tilly noticed immediately — her goggles caught the faint glow, her ears caught the frequency. "Don't touch them," she said. "Those are glyphs. Warding glyphs. Touch one, and you'll regret it."

Finn reached out anyway.

He stopped an inch from the stone. He could feel the magic — warm, sharp, hungry. He pulled his hand back. "Noted."

They passed the glyphs carefully. The entrance sealed behind them with a sound like a door closing in a tomb.

Beyond it, a doorway. Beyond the doorway, three floating stones, each inscribed with a symbol:

- A hammer striking an anvil
- A tree with roots reaching into water
- An eye watching through flame

Kaelen's breath caught. "That hammer," he whispered. "It's the Ironheart sigil. But older. Much older."

Lyra's hand went to her pouch. The seeds pulsed — warm, bright, urgent. "The tree," she said. "It's the Neverwood. Or what the Neverwood was before the Blight."

Malachi's orb flared. "The eye," he said. "That's Infernal. My patron knows that symbol."

Tilly stepped forward, her goggles catching the light, her mind working. "The inscription on the wall," she said, reading the carved text. *"First the earth, then the stone, then the watch."*

She turned to the party. "Tree. Hammer. Eye. Land, structure, activation. That's the order."

Finn plucked a chord on Whisper — a minor chord, low and resonant. The tree stone glowed faintly in response.

"Music," Tilly said. "The stones respond to music."

Finn smiled. "Then let's introduce ourselves."

He played. Lyra touched the tree stone. Kaelen touched the hammer stone. Malachi touched the eye stone.

The stones sank into the floor. The doorway opened.

A voice spoke.

*"Welcome, travelers. You are early."*

It was warm. It was kind. It was wrong.

Elara's hand went to her hammer. "Remi warned us," she said. "Do not trust the first voice."

The voice did not answer. The doorway was open. The tower was waiting.

---

## Part IV: The Hall of Echoes

The corridor was long. The walls were intact. The floor was cracked. And every sound they made echoed three times — but the echoes were not identical. They were slightly different, as if the corridor were rewriting what was said, bending their words into something new.

Kaelen's boots struck the stone. *Clank. Clank. Clonk.* The third echo was wrong.

Lyra's breath fogged in the cold air. *Hiss. Hiss. Hissss.* The third echo lingered too long.

Tilly stopped. "The floor tiles," she said. "They're marked. Blue, red, green, purple. The colors match the schools of magic — abjuration, evocation, conjuration, and something I don't recognize."

She studied the tiles, her mind racing. "Blue is safe. Protection. Red is dangerous. Evocation — damage. Green is slow. Conjuration — delay. Purple is unknown."

"Can you read the pattern?" Elara asked.

Tilly pushed her goggles up her nose. "I can learn it. But I need to test it."

She stepped on a blue tile. Nothing happened. She stepped on a red tile.

*Magic missile.* Three darts of pure force struck her, unsaveable, unavoidable. She staggered, her HP dropping, her goggles cracking slightly. "Noted," she gasped. "Red is bad."

She stepped on a green tile. The air thickened. Her movement slowed. "Green is slow. Literally."

She stepped on a purple tile.

Nothing happened.

"Interesting," she murmured. "Purple is safe. For now."

The party followed her lead, carefully, tile by tile, learning the pattern as they went. The corridor tested them, and they learned. The first trap was a warning. The second was a lesson. The third — if they had failed — would have been a punishment.

They did not fail.

At the end of the corridor, a door with no handle. Above it, an inscription:

*"Speak your truth, and the door will open. Speak a lie, and the door will close. Speak nothing, and the door will wait."*

The voice returned. *"You do not need to do this. I can open the door for you. Just trust me."*

Elara's eyes narrowed. "It's lying."

"It's helping," Malachi said. "But it's lying about why."

Finn stepped forward. "I'll go first."

He closed his eyes. He placed his hand on the door. And he spoke.

"I play music to connect with people," he said. "But I'm afraid no one is really listening. That I'm just... noise. In a world that's too loud to hear me."

The door shuddered. The echoes grew louder. But it did not close.

His truth was true enough.

Kaelen stepped forward. "I survived Mithral Hall," he said. His voice was low, rough, the voice of a man who carried guilt like armor. "And I don't deserve to have lived."

The door did not close.

Lyra: "I ran from my forest. And I haven't been brave since."

Malachi: "I made a pact to save my mother. And I don't know if it was worth it."

Tilly: "Oren died because of me. And I don't know how to forgive myself."

Elara stepped forward last. Her hand rested on her hammer. Her eyes were bright. Her voice was steady.

"Tomas," she said. "I left him behind. And I don't know if staying would have saved him."

The door opened.

The echoes fell silent. The corridor was still. And beyond the door, the tower's heart waited.

---

## Part V: The Seal Chamber

The room was circular. The walls were lined with runes. The floor was marked with a pentagram. And in the center, on a pedestal of black stone, sat a crystal — the seal's core.

It was cracked.

Light leaked from the fractures like blood from a wound, and the air hummed with power that was old and deep and failing. The seal was dying, and the room felt it.

But they were not alone.

Trapped in the corner of the chamber, bound by ropes of light, was a gnome. Old. Weakened. His eyes were bright, his mind sharp, and his hands clutched a piece of the seal's mechanism like a man clutching a drowning child.

"Professor Vell," Tilly breathed. "From Candlekeep. You're alive."

Vell smiled weakly. "Tilly Cogsworth. I wondered if you would come." He coughed. "You're too late. The seal is failing. The mechanism — I hold it, but I cannot release it. If I do, the seal breaks. If I don't, I die here, and the seal breaks anyway."

Kaelen stepped forward. "What are your choices?"

Vell's eyes were sharp. "You stabilize the seal. All six of you activate the runes around the chamber. I let go of the mechanism. I am trapped here until the seal is fully repaired — weeks, possibly months. Or..." He paused. "You extract me. The seal continues to weaken. You buy time — ten days, maybe — but you do not solve the problem."

Elara looked at Kaelen. Kaelen looked at Lyra. Lyra looked at Malachi. Malachi looked at Finn. Finn looked at Tilly. Tilly looked at Vell.

And they chose.

---

### The Choice

*"We stabilize the seal,"* Elara said.

Kaelen nodded. Lyra closed her eyes. Malachi's orb went dark. Finn strummed a single chord on Whisper. Tilly unpacked her tools.

Vell smiled. He released the mechanism. The ropes of light faded. He slumped to the floor, weak but alive.

"Thank you," he whispered. "The coast will remember."

The party moved to the runes.

**Kaelen** struck the hammer rune with Dawnbreaker. The stone rang like a bell.

**Lyra** placed a seed from her pouch on the tree rune. The seed glowed, then sank into the stone.

**Malachi** spoke a word of power in Infernal. The eye rune flared red, then blue.

**Elara** raised her hammer and spoke an oath to Tyr. The shield rune blazed white.

**Finn** played a melody on Whisper — the melody from the road, from the tavern, from the dreams. The song rune sang in response.

**Tilly** channeled magic through her crystal focus. The crystal rune pulsed, then stabilized.

The runes activated in sequence — tree, hammer, shield, song, eye, crystal. Land, structure, protection, harmony, awareness, activation.

The crystal sealed.

The light stopped leaking. The hum faded. The tower stopped shaking.

Seal #1 was secure.

For now.

---

## Part VI: The Vision

They received the vision as they left the tower.

The sky above them shifted — not darkened, but thinned, as if the world were becoming transparent. And through the transparency, they saw:

A map of the Sword Coast. Five points of light, glowing faintly, connected by lines that formed a pentagram.

- **Seal #1** — the Tower. Stable. Their work.
- **Seal #2** — a villa they did not know, south on the Risen Road. Weakened. Cult activity visible.
- **Seal #3** — the Neverwood. Active spread. The Blight consuming the forest.
- **Seal #4** — deep beneath Waterdeep. Stable but threatened. Shadows moving in the dark.
- **Seal #5** — a milestone on the Risen Road. Unknown. Hidden behind a veil of fog.

A voice spoke — not the first voice, but a different one. Older. Warmer. Satisfied.

*"You have seen the seals. You have made your choice. The coast depends on what you do next. Remember: you cannot save all five. Choose wisely, for the two you abandon will remember your name."*

The vision ended.

The sun was rising. The road ahead was long.

And the tower stood behind them, silent, sealed, waiting.

---

## Part VII: The Return

They did not walk silently on the return journey.

The goblins attacked twenty miles from Daggerford — eight of them, two sorcerers, a boss who carried a siphon device and would not die easily. They fought with the desperation of creatures whose magic was failing, whose master was losing control, whose time was running out.

Kaelen held the front line, his hammer swinging, his chain mail turning blows that would have killed lesser men. Lyra rained arrows from the treeline, her aim true, her breath steady. Malachi cast *burning hands* and *hellish rebuke*, his orb flaring with each spell. Elara stood beside Kaelen, her shield raised, her hammer striking true. Finn played a melody that bolstered their spirits and weakened their enemies. Tilly cast *shield* and *magic missile*, her goggles catching every angle, her mind calculating every outcome.

They won.

The goblin boss dropped a letter as he fled — addressed to someone in the Sunspring Villa, mentioning "the warm wells" and "the second seal."

Elara picked it up. She did not open it. She would not. Not yet.

They reached Daggerford at dusk.

The Boar's Tooth was waiting.

Remi was waiting.

And the road ahead led south, toward Baldur's Gate and the villa and the wells and the cult and the second seal.

The coast was breaking.

And they were the only thing standing in its way.
