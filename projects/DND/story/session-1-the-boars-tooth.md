# The Boar's Tooth — Session One

## Part I: Six Strangers, One Tavern

---

The Boar's Tooth was not a grand establishment. It sat on the edge of Daggerford's Market Ward, its timber walls leaning slightly to the left as if the building had decided years ago that standing perfectly straight was beneath it. The sign above the door showed a boar's head, painted in red that had faded to the color of dried blood. Inside, the air smelled of ale, roasted meat, and the faint metallic tang of the road that ran past the city — the Trade Way, the spine of the Sword Coast.

It was evening. The lamps were lit. The travelers were arriving.

---

### Kaelen

The dwarf entered first.

He did not so much walk into the tavern as arrive in it — boots heavy on the floorboards, shoulders filling the doorway, chain mail clinking softly with each step. His beard was braided with copper wire, his eyes the color of storm clouds, and the scar that ran from his left temple to his jaw caught the lamplight like a blade.

He ordered a mug of ale, took a stool at the bar, and said nothing. His hammer sat at his belt, wrapped in cloth. He did not draw it. He did not need to. The people of the tavern knew what a hammer looked like. They knew what it meant when a dwarf carried one like it was an extension of his arm.

Kaelen had tracked a shipment of mithral ore — the kind used in Mithral Hall, the kind that had not been mined in decades — moving through Daggerford. The caravan carrying it had been robbed two nights ago. He had followed the trail of coin and rumor to this city, and he would follow it further if he had to.

He drank his ale. He watched the door. He waited.

---

### Lyra

She came in an hour later, moving like smoke through the doorway — not because she tried to be invisible, but because the tavern had already taught her that stillness was louder than speed.

She was tall for an elf, lean in the way of those who ran rather than rode, and her hair was the color of birch bark, pulled back in a practical braid that escaped at the ends. Her eyes were green — the particular green of new leaves in spring — and they missed nothing. She tilted her head as she entered, listening to the room the way a bird listens for movement in the underbrush.

She wore no jewelry. Her armor was leather, stained by use and treated with oils that made it smell faintly of pine. Her cloak was her own handiwork — bark, moss, and woven grass that made her nearly invisible in the forest. In the tavern, she looked like a traveler. In the wild, she disappeared entirely.

She took a table in the corner, ordered water, and placed her hand on the pouch at her belt. The seeds inside pulsed faintly, warm against her palm. They had been pulsing stronger since she crossed into Daggerford's territory. The river water here tasted wrong — metallic, faintly bitter — and the burial mounds beyond the Farms had been humming in her dreams for three nights.

She drank her water. She watched the dwarf. She wondered if he felt it too.

---

### Malachi

He entered like a man who knew he was being watched and did not care.

His skin was the deep amber of burnished copper, his eyes burned with an inner fire that was not entirely his own, and his horns curved back from his temples like a crown of obsidian. He dressed well — too well for a warlock, some might say — in robes of dark crimson that seemed to drink the light around them. The fabric moved like liquid, and those who watched carefully might notice that the shadows cast by his clothing did not quite match the shape of his body.

He ordered wine. He took a table near the fire. He opened a book and began to read, as if the tavern were a library and the noise were merely background.

The obsidian orb at his belt pulsed. Not brightly — just enough that he felt it, a warmth against his hip, a reminder that his patron's influence was strong here. He had followed the trail of fiendish magic from Waterdeep to Daggerford, and the trail had led him to this tavern. He did not know why. He only knew that the orb had not pulsed this strongly since he made the pact as a boy.

He turned a page. He watched the room from the corner of his eye. He smiled, rarely genuine but always effective.

---

### Elara

She arrived with the kind of presence that made rooms feel smaller.

Her scales were the color of polished brass, warm and luminous in the lamplight, and her horns curved back from her brow like a warrior's crest. Her eyes were amber, sharp and unblinking, and when she looked at you, you felt seen — not judged, but seen. She stood well over seven feet, her build lean muscle rather than bulk, and she moved with the economy of motion that came from years of military training.

Her plate armor was well-maintained, polished to a mirror shine. The sigil of the Lords' Alliance was emblazoned on her shield, and the amulet of Tyr hung from her neck, hidden beneath her gorget. Her warhammer, "Justice's Weight," was balanced perfectly in her grip.

She carried a letter of marque from the Duke of Daggerford, authorizing her to investigate the caravan robberies on the Trade Way. She had not yet opened the letter. She would not until she had assessed the room, the people in it, and the threats they might pose.

She took a table near the center of the tavern, ordered stew, and placed her shield on the floor beside her chair. She did not sit with her back to the door. She did not sit with her back to anyone.

She ate her stew. She watched the dwarf, the elf, the tiefling. She wondered what had brought them all to Daggerford on the same night.

---

### Finn

He came in singing.

Not loudly — just enough that the tavern turned its head. His voice was warm, clear, and slightly off-key in a way that made it endearing rather than annoying. He stood at four feet of pure, unadulterated charm, with sandy hair that fell in soft waves around his face and eyes the color of honey caught in sunlight. His smile was wide, genuine, and slightly mischievous — the kind of smile that made you feel like you were in on a joke you hadn't heard yet.

He wore a tunic of deep green, a vest of rust red, and trousers the shade of autumn leaves. His boots were well-worn but clean, and his fingers were calloused from years of plucking strings and tapping drums. Around his neck hung a small wooden flute, carved by his grandmother, and slung across his back was a lute he called "Whisper."

"Evening, friends!" he called, his voice carrying over the murmur of conversation. "What's good here? What's strong? What's the tale of the road tonight?"

He took a table near Elara's, close enough to join her conversation if she wanted it, far enough to give her space if she did not. He ordered ale, ordered bread, and began to hum a melody under his breath — a melody he had been hearing in his dreams for weeks, a melody that pulled him south, toward Daggerford and beyond.

Whisper hummed in response.

---

### Tilly

She arrived last, jingling.

Not unpleasantly — more like a walking treasure chest of knowledge. She stood three feet tall, which she compensated for with an attitude that filled rooms twice her size. Her hair was a wild explosion of silver curls that defied every hairpin, comb, and spell she had tried to contain them, and her eyes were bright blue behind a pair of goggles she built herself — brass-rimmed, multi-lensed, and perpetually smudged with grease or soot or something she refused to identify.

She dressed in practical clothing — a tunic of sturdy brown cloth, reinforced at the elbows and knees, with pockets sewn into every available inch. Those pockets contained tools, scrolls, crystals, gears, and at least three types of screwdriver. When she walked, her pockets announced her.

She pushed her goggles up her nose, scanned the room, and took a stool next to Kaelen at the bar. She did not ask if it was taken. She simply sat.

"Evening," she said to the dwarf. "You look like a man who knows his way around stone. I'm looking for someone who does. Candlekeep sent me."

Kaelen did not look at her. He finished his ale. He said nothing.

Tilly nodded, as if his silence were an answer she had expected. She ordered cider. She began to unpack a screwdriver and examine the hinge on the tavern door, murmuring to herself about torque and tension and the curious way that wood warped when exposed to magic.

She did not notice the seeds in Lyra's pouch pulse. She did not notice the orb at Malachi's belt flare. She did not notice that Elara's eyes had narrowed slightly when she entered.

She was too busy fixing the door.

---

## Part II: The Messenger

The door opened again.

This time, there was no song. There was no jingle. There was only the sound of a body hitting the floorboards, heavy and final, and the smell of blood that cut through the ale and the meat and the woodsmoke like a knife.

A rider. Or what was left of one.

He had stumbled through the doors on a dead horse, his armor torn, his cloak soaked with blood, his hand clutching a sealed letter to his chest. He collapsed at the bar, between Kaelen and Tilly, and whispered a single word before his eyes went dark:

*"Tower."*

Then he was still.

---

The tavern went quiet. Not the quiet of peace — the quiet of people who had just realized that violence had found them, and they were not sure if it was finished.

Elara was on her feet first. She crossed the room in three strides, dropped to her knees beside the body, and pressed two fingers to his neck. "Dead," she said. "But recently. His heart stopped within the last minute."

Finn's lute went still. His smile faded. "What happened to him?"

Tilly was already examining the body, her goggles catching the lamplight as she leaned close. "Arrow wound to the shoulder. Blunt force trauma to the ribs. Someone didn't want him talking. Or they didn't want him living." She paused. "Or both."

Malachi did not move from his table, but his eyes were on the body now, sharp and calculating. "He carried something," he said. "A letter. Sealed."

Kaelen reached down and took the letter from the dead man's hand. The wax seal bore a symbol he did not recognize — a circle, divided into five sections, each marked with a different glyph. He turned the letter over in his hands, feeling its weight, its texture, the faint warmth that came from within the parchment.

"Who was he?" Finn asked.

"No one," Elara said. "Or someone who did not want to be found."

Lyra stood. She had not spoken since she entered the tavern, but now her voice cut through the silence like a blade. "He said 'Tower.'"

"Tower?" Tilly pushed her goggles up her nose. "What tower?"

Lyra did not answer. She was looking at the pouch at her belt, at the seeds that were pulsing now, bright and warm, responding to something in the room that she could not name. She could feel it — a pull, a resonance, a connection between the dead man and the seeds and the tower he had named with his last breath.

Kaelen broke the seal.

---

## Part III: The Letter

The letter read:

> *"To those who carry the marks of the old road:*
>
> *You have been chosen, though you do not yet know it. The Sword Coast is built upon seals — five of them, placed by scholars and warriors long dead. They hold back something that should not wake.*
>
> *The first seal is failing. It lies in the Shattered Tower, on the Way of the Lion. The goblins are not raiding by chance. They are being directed. The magic in the stones is not residual. It is alive.*
>
> *I cannot go. My handlers would stop me. But you — you are not bound by factions, not yet. Go to the Tower. Learn what is buried there. And when you are ready, come find me.*
>
> *Look for the blue candle.*
>
> *— R.H."*

Kaelen folded the letter. He did not share it. He did not need to. The others had heard enough.

"Seals," Tilly said. "Five of them. That's... that's a network. A containment system. Someone built something beneath the coast, and it's failing."

"The Shattered Tower," Lyra said. "On the Way of the Lion. That's east of here. Through the High Forest."

Malachi's orb pulsed again, brighter this time. "The magic in the stones is alive," he repeated. "That's not a metaphor. Whoever wrote this knows what they're talking about."

Elara stood. "The Duke has authorized me to investigate the caravan robberies on the Trade Way. If this Tower is connected to those robberies, it falls under my mandate."

Finn strummed a single chord on Whisper. "And if it's not?"

"Then we have found a different mystery," Elara said. "One that may be more important."

Kaelen finally spoke. His voice was low, measured, the voice of a man who did not waste words. "Mithral Hall fell because the stone beneath it was breached. If there are seals beneath the coast, and they are failing..." He did not finish the sentence. He did not need to.

Tilly was already packing her tools. "I've studied the Weave. I've mapped magical anomalies across the Sword Coast. If there's a network of seals, I want to understand how it works." She looked at Kaelen. "And you, dwarf — you said you know stone. I could use someone who does."

Kaelen looked at her. He looked at the letter. He looked at the dead man on the floor.

"I know stone," he said. "And I know when it's about to break."

---

## Part IV: The Blue Candle

They left the tavern at dawn.

The body had been taken — by whom, they did not ask. The letter was folded in Kaelen's cloak. The seeds in Lyra's pouch had quieted, but they were still warm. The orb at Malachi's belt had gone dark. Elara's mandate was clear. Finn's melody had a new verse. Tilly's goggles were clean.

They searched Daggerford for "the blue candle."

It was not hard to find. Not because the clue was obvious, but because the Harper network was efficient, and efficiency left traces.

The candle was sold at a shop in the Market Ward — the one that also sold herbal remedies. Sister Alinthe's temple supplied them, and the candle was marked with a Harper symbol, visible only in moonlight. Finn bought it with a joke and a smile. Elara bought it with a letter of introduction. Tilly bought it with a question about the shopkeeper's lock mechanism.

The candle glowed faintly blue in Tilly's hand.

"Interesting," she murmured. "The wax contains trace amounts of arcane residue. It's not just a candle — it's a homing device."

They followed it through the Market Ward, past stalls and merchants and the occasional Watchwarrior who asked questions they did not answer. The candle burned brighter as they approached the safehouse, its flame steady and blue and unwavering.

The safehouse was behind a bookshelf in a rented townhouse. The bookshelf opened with a pressure plate — Tilly found it on the second try, muttering about poor craftsmanship — and beyond it was a room filled with maps, supplies, and a woman waiting.

Her name was Remi Haventree. She was a Harper agent, widow of the late Masked Lord Arthagast Ulbrinter, and she had been watching all six of them since they arrived in Daggerford.

"Sit," she said. "We have much to discuss, and little time to discuss it."

---

## Part V: The Road Ahead

Remi gave them supplies. She gave them a map. She gave them a warning.

*"The Tower will test you. Not with monsters, but with choices. It was built to keep people out, but also to keep something in. Do not trust the first voice you hear inside."*

They left Daggerford at dawn the next day, heading east on the Way of the Lion. The road was dirt and determination, a path carved by scholars, merchants, and fools. The High Forest loomed to the north, ancient and dense and alive with magic. The Serpent Hills rose to the south, rocky and hot and full of creatures that did not welcome visitors.

Between them, the Way of the Lion was a ribbon of dirt and resolve.

Kaelen walked at the front, his hammer at his belt, his eyes on the road. Lyra walked at the edge of the forest, her hand on her bow, her ears open to the wind. Malachi walked in the center, his orb dark, his book closed, his mind working. Elara walked beside Kaelen, her shield on her back, her mandate in her cloak. Finn walked behind them all, humming a melody that had no name yet, his lute silent but ready. Tilly walked wherever she wanted, her goggles catching the light, her pockets full of tools and questions.

They were six strangers. They were six seekers. They were six pieces of a puzzle that none of them understood yet.

The road ahead was long. The Tower was waiting.

And somewhere beneath the stone, something old was stirring.
