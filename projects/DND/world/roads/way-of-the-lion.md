# The Way of the Lion — Daggerford to Candlekeep

## The Route

The Way of the Lion is the shortest road between Daggerford and Candlekeep, but shortest does not mean easiest. The route cuts east from the Trade Way, through the edge of the High Forest, across the Serpent Hills, and down to the headland where the library sits. The road is not paved — it is a dirt track in most places, maintained by travelers rather than cities, and it shows the wear of centuries.

The landscape is wild. The High Forest looms to the north, ancient and dense and alive with magic. The Serpent Hills rise to the south, rocky and hot and full of creatures that do not welcome visitors. Between them, the Way of the Lion is a ribbon of dirt and determination, a path carved by scholars, merchants, and fools.

This is not a road for the impatient. It is not a road for the unprepared. It is a road for those who have a reason to reach Candlekeep that is stronger than their desire to turn back.

## Along the Way

**The Winding Water Crossing (30 miles from Daggerford)** — The road crosses the Winding Water here, a fast river that cuts through the foothills. The bridge was washed out last autumn, and the replacement has not been funded. Travelers ford the river, wading through water that is cold, deep, and swift. Horses hate it. Carriages survive, barely. Pack animals do better. The crossing is safe in dry weather, but after rain, the river rises fast and the current turns vicious. There is no guard here. There is no help here. There is only the water, and the road on the other side.

**Elventree (80 miles from Daggerford)** — A village on the edge of the High Forest, built by half-elves who wanted the benefits of civilization without surrendering their connection to the wild. The buildings are wooden, curved, and beautiful — grown more than built, shaped by hands that understand both timber and magic. The people here are skilled herbalists, craftsmen, and healers. They sell remedies that work, weapons that sing, and advice that stings. They do not trust city folk, but they respect travelers who earn their way. The inn here is called The Root and Branch, and its proprietor, a half-elf named Sylva, remembers every face that walks through her door. She has been running the inn for forty years. She does not look a day over thirty.

**The Shattered Tower (130 miles from Daggerford)** — A watchtower that collapsed centuries ago, its ruins scattered across a hillside like broken teeth. The tower was part of an ancient defense network, built to monitor the High Forest and warn of invasions. It was abandoned when the network failed, and it was forgotten when the world moved on. But the magic in the stones has not faded. Spells echo in the ruins, walls shift when no one is looking, and the air hums with residual power. Scholars come here to study. Adventurers come here to loot. Both leave changed. The tower does not give up its secrets easily. It does not give up its visitors either.

**Serpent Hills Pass (175 miles from Daggerford)** — The road climbs through the Serpent Hills here, a narrow track carved into the rock, exposed on both sides to wind and drop. The hills are named for the snakes that nest in the crevices — venomous, camouflaged, patient. They do not attack unless provoked, but a misplaced foot, a startled horse, a dropped tool can be enough. The pass is beautiful — the view stretches for miles, the sky is vast, the air is thin and clean. It is also dangerous, and beauty does not stop fangs. Travelers are advised to move carefully, watch their steps, and respect the silence.

**The Scholar's Rest (200 miles from Daggerford)** — The last inn before Candlekeep, run by a retired scribe named Master Havel who has filled every wall with books, every shelf with scrolls, and every conversation with rumors. The inn is small, warm, and full of the kind of people who travel to Candlekeep with purpose — scholars, adventurers, pilgrims, and the occasional madman. Havel serves good stew, decent ale, and excellent information. He knows everyone who passes through, he remembers every story they tell, and he files it all away in a ledger that is longer than the road itself. He will sell you a copy of any entry. He will not tell you which ones are true.

## Dangers

The Way of the Lion is the most dangerous road on the Sword Coast, and it earns that title daily.

Goblin raids near the Shattered Tower are frequent and unpredictable. The goblins are not a large tribe, but they are clever, and they target magical items — wands, scrolls, foci, anything that hums with power. They strike at night, fast and quiet, and they disappear into the forest before help can arrive. The ruins of the tower provide cover, and the residual magic confuses trackers. The militia does not patrol this route. The travelers defend themselves.

Snake pits in the Serpent Hills are camouflaged, venomous, and patient. The snakes do not attack unless threatened, but the hills are narrow, the track is rough, and a misstep is easy. Antidote is available at Elventree, but it is expensive, and it does not work if you wait too long to use it. Travelers are advised to watch their steps, travel at midday (when the snakes are less active), and carry rope to lower themselves past the worst sections.

Bandits operate along the entire route, preying on isolated travelers who think the lack of militia means the lack of danger. They are not organized like the bandits on the Trade Way — they are smaller, faster, more desperate. They strike without warning, take what they can carry, and run. They are not brave. They are hungry, and hunger makes people reckless.

Magical anomalies near the Shattered Tower are unpredictable. Spells fire without casters, walls move without reason, and the air crackles with residual power. Scholars study these phenomena carefully. Adventurers study them poorly. The tower is not cursed. It is not haunted. It is charged — saturated with magic that has no source, no direction, no end. Those who enter the ruins should bring wards, warnings, and a plan to leave.

## Rumors

- The goblins near the Shattered Tower are not acting alone. Someone is training them, equipping them, directing their raids. The magical items they steal disappear — not sold, not used, but removed. The destination is unknown.
- Sylva at The Root and Branch has been receiving visitors from the High Forest — elves who do not speak Common, who carry gifts of herbs and wood, who leave before dawn. She does not discuss them. She does not need to. The gifts appear in her shop the next day, priced fairly and labeled honestly.
- The Shattered Tower was not abandoned. It was sealed — from the inside. Something was trapped in the lower levels, and the magic that holds it is weakening. The scholars who study the tower know this. They have not reported it, because reporting it would mean someone acting, and no one wants to be the one who opens what's inside.
- Master Havel's ledger contains an entry about five travelers — the same prophecy that appeared at Beregost's temple and in Candlekeep's archives. He has not shared the entry. He has not hidden it either. It sits on his desk, open, waiting for the right person to read it. The party's table is the one closest to it.
