# The Risen Road — Baldur's Gate to Candlekeep

## The Route

The Risen Road runs south from Baldur's Gate through the Lastwall Mountains, following the River Chionthar as it curves toward the coast. It is older than the Trade Way, narrower, and less traveled — scholars and pilgrims prefer it for the direct route to Candlekeep, while merchants take the longer, safer path through Daggerford. The road is cobblestone in sections, dirt in others, and sheer cliff in places where the builders ran out of patience and prayed to the mountains.

The landscape is dramatic. The Lastwall Mountains rise steeply from the Gate, their peaks jagged and their passes narrow. The Chionthar carves through the valley below, its waters clear and fast and cold. Beyond the mountains, the road descends into coastal plains, dotted with ruins, farmland, and the occasional village that has forgotten its own name.

The Risen Road is named for a legend — that the road itself rises at night, shifting its course, hiding its turns, protecting Candlekeep from those who would approach with ill intent. Most travelers dismiss the story. Most travelers have not walked the road at midnight in fog.

## Along the Way

**The Lastwall Gate (10 miles from Baldur's Gate)** — The mountain pass that marks the beginning of the real journey. The road narrows here, winding through rock that towers on both sides, and the guard-post that once monitored traffic is abandoned — the soldiers left years ago, citing "unrest" that never came. The pass is safe in summer, dangerous in spring (rockfalls) and winter (snow). The rock faces are carved with symbols — old symbols, older than the road, older than the cities at either end. No one can read them anymore. No one tries.

**Chionthar's Bend (40 miles from Baldur's Gate)** — Where the river curves and the valley opens, this is the most peaceful stretch of the Risen Road. The farmland here is fertile, the air is clean, and the villages are small but content. The people grow wheat, raise goats, and celebrate harvest festivals that draw travelers off the road and into their homes. The food is simple, the hospitality is genuine, and the stories are old — stories about the river, the mountains, the things that sleep beneath both. The farmers do not believe in monsters. They believe in the land, and they know the land has moods.

**The Sunspring Villa (70 miles from Baldur's Gate)** — A noble estate abandoned decades ago, its gardens overgrown, its walls crumbling, its basement intact. The villa was the home of a family that vanished overnight — no bodies, no signs of struggle, just empty rooms and cold hearths. The wells in the courtyard run warm, even in winter, and the water tastes faintly of copper. Cultists have been using the basement as a meeting place — followers of Bane, gathering in the dark, whispering prayers to a god who does not answer. They are few, but they are growing. They are watching the road. They are watching travelers. They are waiting for the right moment to recruit — or to sacrifice.

**Cormyrean Milestone (95 miles from Baldur's Gate)** — An ancient boundary marker, carved from a single block of stone and inscribed in three languages: Common, Elvish, and a third that no one recognizes. The milestone marks the edge of old Cormyrean territory, land that was claimed, contested, and abandoned centuries ago. The inscription glows faintly at midnight — a soft blue light, like a candle seen through water. Scholars from Candlekeep have studied the milestone, measured it, copied its text, and argued about its meaning. They have not agreed on anything. Emergency rations are cached here, left by Candlekeep scholars for travelers in need. The rations are always fresh. No one restocks them.

## Dangers

The Risen Road is not the busiest road on the Sword Coast, but it is not the safest either. The dangers here are older than banditry, deeper than politics.

Mountain rockfalls at the Lastwall Gate are seasonal but lethal. The spring thaw loosens the stone, and chunks the size of carriages fall without warning. The guard-post that once warned travelers is empty now. The road markers are faded. The mountains do not care about schedules. Travelers are advised to pass through quickly, in summer, with eyes on the sky and ears open for the sound of falling stone.

Cultists operate near the Sunspring Villa, using the abandoned basement as a sanctuary. They are followers of Bane — the god of tyranny, the god of chains, the god of order enforced through fear. They are not numerous, but they are organized, and they are recruiting. They target the desperate, the angry, the lost — people who have nothing to lose and everything to hate. They offer purpose. They offer belonging. They offer a knife and a name to write on a wall. The Flaming Fist knows about the cult. They have not acted, because acting would mean entering the villa, and no one who enters the villa's basement returns the same.

River bandits operate at Chionthar's Bend, ambushing travelers from the water — fast boats, masked raiders, clean strikes. They take coin, supplies, and sometimes hostages. They do not usually kill, but they do not hesitate either. The farmers in the valley know about the bandits. They do not report them, because reporting means the Flaming Fist comes, and the Flaming Fist does not distinguish between bandits and farmers when things go wrong. The bandits know this too. They are patient. They have the river, and the river is on their side.

The road itself is dangerous. It curves sharply, drops suddenly, and disappears into fog that rolls in from the coast at dusk. Travelers who ignore the warnings and push on in the dark have lost horses, missed turns, and walked off the road entirely. The legend says the road moves. The road does not need to move. It only needs to be forgotten.

## Rumors

- The symbols carved into the Lastwall Gate are not decorative. They are a seal — an ancient barrier, warded by magic older than the road, designed to keep something in the mountains from escaping. The seal is cracking. The stones are shifting. The guard-post was not abandoned. It was evacuated.
- The warm water in the Sunspring Villa's wells is not natural. It comes from beneath the basement, from a place that should not be warm, should not be wet, should not exist. The cultists know this. They pray to the warmth, as if it were a god. It is not a god. It is something else. Something older. Something that does not need prayers. It needs fuel.
- The Cormyrean Milestone's third language is not lost. It is spoken — by one person, in one place, at one time. The High Avowed of Candlekeep knows this. He has not shared the knowledge. He is waiting for the right traveler to read the milestone, to hear the third language, to understand what it says. The inscription is a warning. It is also an invitation.
- The rations cached at the milestone are not just rations. They are marked — each pack bears a symbol, faint but deliberate, the same symbol found in the Shattered Tower's ruins, the Sunspring Villa's basement, and the restricted wing of Candlekeep. The symbol means nothing to most travelers. It means everything to those who know. The party has seen it before. They did not recognize it. They will.
