# The Coast Way — Neverwinter to Waterdeep

## The Route

The Coast Way runs south from Neverwinter along the edge of the Neverwood, hugging the coastline for most of its length. The road is well-maintained — cobblestone laid by generations of laborers, repaired by generations after them — and it is one of the busiest trade routes on the Sword Coast. Caravans, merchants, pilgrims, and adventurers all use it, and the rhythm of traffic is steady: northbound in the morning, southbound in the afternoon.

The landscape changes as you travel. Near Neverwinter, the road climbs through volcanic hills, the air warm and the ground firm. By mid-journey, the terrain flattens into coastal plains, dotted with farms, orchards, and the occasional village. Near Waterdeep, the road rises again, climbing through the hills that separate the countryside from the city's outer wards.

The sea is always visible to the west — a constant presence, gray in the morning, blue at noon, purple at dusk. Sailors say the Coast Way follows the same curve as the coast itself, and they are right. If you lose the road, follow the sound of the waves. You will find it again.

## Along the Way

**High Hill Fortress (15 miles from Neverwinter)** — The ruins of a military fortress built centuries ago to defend the road from raiders. The walls still stand, the gate is still intact, and the interior is occupied by a family of squatters who claim descent from the original garrison. They are poor, proud, and protective of their home. They will sell you water, answer your questions, and warn you about bandits — but they will not let you sleep inside the walls without paying, and they consider a night's lodging worth five silver pieces. Fair, given the alternative.

**The Stone Bowl Tavern (45 miles from Neverwinter)** — A real tavern, not a ruin pretending to be one. The building is round — hence the name — built of stone with a thatched roof, and it sits in a small clearing where the road crosses a stream. The proprietor, a half-orc named Grelga, runs the place with efficiency and warmth. The ale is good, the food is hearty, and the beds are clean. Grelga knows everyone who travels the Coast Way, and she remembers faces. If you have done something worth remembering, she will know about it. She will not judge you. She will just mention it when you order your second pint.

**Ashwood Thicket (70 miles from Neverwinter)** — The road narrows here, cutting through a dense stretch of forest where the trees grow close and the light is thin. This is where bandits operate — not a large group, but a skilled one. They strike at dusk, when travelers are tired and the shadows are long. They take coin, supplies, and sometimes horses. They do not usually kill, unless they are provoked. The local militia has tried to root them out, twice. Both attempts failed. The bandits know the thicket better than anyone, and they use it well. Travelers are advised to pass through at midday, in groups, with weapons drawn and patience short.

**Lathander's Rest Shrine (85 miles from Neverwinter)** — A small shrine to Lathander, god of dawn, built into a hillside overlooking the road. The shrine is well-maintained — the statue is clean, the flowers are fresh, and the spring beside it runs clear. Pilgrims stop here to pray, travelers stop here to drink, and the occasional lost soul stops here to cry. The priest who tends the shrine is an old man named Brother Elandor, and he gives blessings freely. He has not aged in twenty years. People ask him about it. He smiles and changes the subject.

## Dangers

The Coast Way is not a deadly road, but it is not a safe one either. The dangers are predictable — bandits, weather, terrain — but predictable dangers kill just as reliably as unpredictable ones.

Bandits in the Ashwood Thicket are the primary threat. They are organized, patient, and selective. They do not attack every caravan. They wait for the right target — one that looks valuable but poorly defended. They have been known to let groups pass, watching, learning, waiting for a better opportunity. If you travel at midday, in numbers, with visible weapons, you will likely pass unnoticed. If you travel at dusk, alone, with a loaded pack mule, you will not.

Wild boar herds cross the road at dusk, moving from the forest to the fields. They are not aggressive unless cornered, but a startled horse can cause as much damage as a hostile one. Travelers are advised to slow down, make noise, and give the herds space.

Fog rolls in from the sea in the early morning, particularly in spring and fall. It reduces visibility to a few feet, mutes sound, and makes the road feel longer than it is. Travelers who insist on riding through the fog have lost horses, missed turns, and walked straight into the stream at Stone Bowl. Wait it out. The fog burns off by midday.

## Rumors

- The squatters at High Hill Fortress have found something in the lower levels — a sealed door, a symbol no one recognizes, a draft of cold air that should not exist. They have not opened the door. They are waiting for someone who can tell them what it means.
- Grelga at the Stone Bowl has been receiving letters from someone in Waterdeep. She does not open them in public. She burns them after reading, and the ashes disappear into the stream.
- The bandits in Ashwood Thicket are not working alone. Someone is feeding them information — caravan schedules, guard rotations, the contents of certain shipments. The source is unknown, but the pattern is clear.
- Brother Elandor at Lathander's Rest has been seen talking to travelers who were not there — standing alone in the shrine, gesturing, nodding, as if in conversation. The spring water beside the shrine glows faintly at dawn. No one has mentioned this to the priest. No one has mentioned this to anyone.
