# Candlekeep — The Great Library

## Appearance

Candlekeep clings to a headland above the Sea of Swords, a fortress-library built into the cliff face like a hive of stone. From the sea, it looks impenetrable — walls rise from the water, towers catch the wind, and the gates are narrow and guarded. From the land, it looks different — inviting, almost. The path winds up the hill, past gardens and courtyards, to the great doors that bear the symbol of Oghma.

The air smells of parchment, ink, and salt. The sound of the keep is the sound of pages turning, quills scratching, and the occasional shout from the Galaudar when a scholar tries to leave a candle burning in a restricted section. Inside the library, it is quiet — reverent, almost — as if the books themselves demand silence.

Candlekeep does not care about your title, your wealth, or your reputation. It cares about one thing: what you can give to the collection. Entry is earned, not bought. You bring a book — a real book, something not already in the archives — and you are welcomed. You bring nothing, and you turn back. This rule has not been broken in a thousand years.

## History

Candlekeep was founded centuries ago by a group of scholars who believed that knowledge was the most powerful force in the world — more powerful than armies, more powerful than magic, more powerful than gods. They built the keep on a headland, fortified it against invasion, and filled it with books. Over the centuries, the collection grew — donated, purchased, stolen, rescued from burning buildings and sinking ships.

The Avowed are the keep's scholars, its rulers, its guardians. They are chosen for their knowledge, their dedication, and their ability to resist the temptation of power. The High Avowed leads the council, serving for life or until they step down — whichever comes first. The current High Avowed is an elf named Cadderly, who has held the position for three hundred years and shows no sign of stopping.

The Galaudar are the keep's warriors, sworn to protect the library and its scholars. They are drawn from adventurers who have donated something extraordinary to the collection and chosen to stay. They are few in number, highly trained, and deeply respected. They do not carry weapons inside the library. They do not need to.

## Districts

**The Library** — The heart of Candlekeep. The main halls are vast, their shelves stretching from floor to ceiling, their aisles narrow and labyrinthine. The books here are organized by subject, language, and age, and finding what you need requires patience, skill, and sometimes a favor from a librarian. The air is cool, dry, and still — preserved by magic and by discipline. This is where scholars spend their days, and some spend their lives.

**The Towers** — The scholar quarters, a cluster of towers built into the cliff face. Each tower houses a dozen or so scholars, their rooms small but comfortable, their views magnificent. The towers are connected by bridges, stairs, and tunnels, and the sound of arguments — scholarly, heated, passionate — carries on the wind. This is where ideas are born, where theories are tested, and where friendships are forged over shared obsession.

**The Gates** — The entrance to Candlekeep, guarded by the Galaudar and overseen by the Avowed. Here, newcomers present their donations — books, scrolls, artifacts — and are judged on their worth. The process is not cruel, but it is not kind either. The keep does not accept duplicates, forgeries, or trivialities. Those who pass are welcomed. Those who fail are turned away, and the decision is final.

**The Galaudar Barracks** — The warriors' quarters, separate from the scholars but close enough to respond to any threat. The barracks are sparse, functional, and clean. The Galaudar do not need luxury. They need readiness. Their training grounds are on the cliffs, where they practice with weapons, test their endurance, and prepare for the day when the keep is threatened. That day has come before. It will come again.

**The Archives** — The restricted sections, accessible only to the Avowed and those they authorize. The books here are dangerous — not because they are cursed, but because the knowledge they contain can be weaponized. Spells that can unmake reality, histories that can topple kingdoms, prophecies that can drive people mad. The Archives are warded, guarded, and sealed. They should remain that way.

**The Cliffs** — The outer defenses, sheer drops on three sides and a wall on the fourth. The cliffs are Candlekeep's greatest defense — no army can scale them, no siege engine can reach them, and no magic can easily penetrate them. The scholars who live here have come to love the view — the sea below, the sky above, the horizon beyond. It is a reminder that knowledge, like the sea, has no boundaries.

## Notable NPCs

- **High Avowed Cadderly** — An elf who has led Candlekeep for three centuries. He is wise, patient, and deeply committed to the keep's mission. He does not tolerate theft, destruction, or the weaponization of knowledge. He has invited the party to visit, offering access to the Archives in exchange for a favor — he will not specify what the favor is, not yet.
- **Master Galaudar Dorn** — The commander of the keep's warriors, a human veteran who has defended Candlekeep from invaders, thieves, and madmen. He is gruff, honorable, and deeply suspicious of adventurers. He has assessed the party's combat ability and found it adequate. That is high praise from him.
- **Scholar Veyla** — A half-elf researcher who specializes in ancient prophecies. She is brilliant, obsessive, and dangerously curious. She has been studying a text that mentions the party — not by name, but by description. She believes the prophecy is about them, and she wants proof. She has been watching them, from a distance.
- **The Scribe** — An anonymous figure who copies books for the collection, working day and night in a room no one has seen. No one knows their name, their age, or their origin. They produce flawless copies, faster than any mortal should be able to. The Avowed tolerate them. The Galaudar watch them. The scholars ignore them. Everyone is wrong to do so.

## Sights & Sounds

Candlekeep wakes with the sun. The first light catches the towers, turns the stone gold, and fills the library with warmth. The scholars rise, light their candles (carefully, responsibly), and begin their work. The sound of the keep is the sound of pages, of quills, of quiet conversation — the murmur of minds at work, of ideas taking shape, of knowledge growing.

The food here is simple — bread, cheese, fruit, stew — but it is good, prepared by cooks who understand that scholars need fuel, not feast. The wine is moderate, the ale is light, and the tea is excellent. The keep does not encourage excess. It encourages focus.

At night, the candles are trimmed, the doors are locked, and the library sleeps. But the books do not. They wait, they whisper, they promise. And those who listen — truly listen — hear them.

## Secrets

The Restricted Wing contains a text that should not exist — a book written in a language that predates Common, Elvish, Dwarvish, and every other tongue. The Avowed cannot read it. They cannot translate it. They cannot even agree on what it is about. Some believe it is a record of the world before the current age. Some believe it is a warning. Some believe it is a key. The book has been sealed for a century. The seal is weakening.

Alaundo's Prophecy Chamber holds the original prophecies of the seer Alaundo, writings that predicted the founding of Candlekeep, the fall of empires, and the coming of a new age. The chamber is sealed, accessible only to the High Avowed, and guarded by magic that has not been tested in centuries. The current prophecies mention five travelers — five strangers who will arrive at the keep, each carrying a piece of a puzzle that, when assembled, will reveal the fate of the Sword Coast. The party matches the description. The High Avowed knows this. He is waiting to see what they do.

The Scribe is not human. No one knows what they are, but they are not mortal. They appear when the keep needs them, copy books with impossible speed, and vanish when their work is done. The Avowed have debated their nature for decades. Some believe they are a servant of Oghma. Some believe they are something else — something older, something that uses the keep for its own purposes. The Galaudar have searched for them, once. They found an empty room, a finished book, and a single candle burning low.
