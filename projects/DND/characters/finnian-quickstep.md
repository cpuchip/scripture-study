# Finnian "Finn" Quickstep

## Appearance

Finnian Quickstep is the kind of halfling who makes people forget halflings exist until they're already in the middle of something. He stands at four feet of pure, unadulterated charm, with sandy hair that falls in soft waves around his face and eyes the color of honey caught in sunlight. His smile is wide, genuine, and slightly mischievous — the kind of smile that makes you feel like you're in on a joke you haven't heard yet.

He dresses in colors that clash in ways that somehow work — a tunic of deep green, a vest of rust red, and trousers the shade of autumn leaves. His boots are well-worn but clean, and his fingers are calloused from years of plucking strings and tapping drums. Around his neck hangs a small wooden flute, carved by his grandmother, and slung across his back is a lute he calls "Whisper."

When he moves, it's with a dancer's grace — light on his feet, always in motion, as if stillness were a personal challenge he's determined to win. He has a habit of tapping his fingers to rhythms only he can hear, and when he's thinking deeply, he hums under his breath in melodies that seem to come from somewhere between memory and invention.

He carries himself with the confidence of someone who has never met a room he couldn't charm his way into — and he hasn't. Not yet.

## Background

Finn was born in the farming village of Daggerford, in the Year of the Banner, 1370 DR, to parents who were more interested in growing turnips than raising a child with a talent for music and a mouth that never stopped moving. His father, Halberd Quickstep, was a blacksmith who believed that hard work and silence were the virtues that built character. His mother, Bramble, was a weaver who believed that a happy home was one where the loom never stopped clacking.

Finn believed neither.

From the age of three, he was drawn to music. He would sit by the roadside and listen to traveling bards, memorizing their songs and trying to mimic their melodies on a flute he'd carved from a willow branch. By the age of seven, he could play enough to make people stop and listen. By the age of ten, he could play enough to make people stop and pay.

His parents were not impressed. "Music won't put food on the table," his father would say, though he never quite managed to hide the pride in his voice when Finn performed for the village elders. "You need to learn a trade," his mother would add, though she always saved him the best portions of the harvest.

At twelve, Finn left home. Not in anger — he and his parents had a good relationship, even if they didn't understand each other — but in necessity. He had heard rumors of a bard's college in Waterdeep, a place where musicians could learn the art of storytelling through song. He packed a satchel with bread, cheese, and his flute, and he walked north.

The journey to Waterdeep took three weeks. He slept in barns, begged from kind strangers, and played for coin at every tavern he passed. By the time he arrived, he had enough gold to buy a proper lute and enough stories to fill a dozen nights of entertainment.

The bard's college was not what he expected. It was not a grand hall of learning, but a small guild of musicians who shared a building and a passion for the craft. They took him in, not because he was talented — though he was — but because he was hungry. And they taught him well.

For five years, Finn studied under the guild's master, a half-elf named Lirael Moonwhisper who had once performed for the Open Lord of Waterdeep herself. She taught him the theory of music, the history of bards, and the art of weaving magic into melody. She taught him that a bard's greatest weapon was not their voice or their instrument, but their ability to connect with an audience — to make them feel something they didn't know they were missing.

When Lirael died of a wasting sickness in the Year of the Wild Magic, 1372 DR, Finn inherited her collection of songs — hundreds of melodies, some ancient, some new, all infused with a power that went beyond mere entertainment. He packed them away, along with her flute and her journal, and he left Waterdeep.

He has been traveling the Sword Coast ever since, playing in taverns, performing at festivals, and collecting stories. He has seen the beauty of the coast — the sunrise over the Sea of Swords, the forests of the High Forest, the markets of Baldur's Gate — and he has seen the darkness — the corruption in the cities, the blight in the wilds, the fear in the eyes of those who have lost too much.

He tells himself he travels for the music. The truth is more complicated. He travels because he is searching for something he cannot name — a song that will make the world feel right again, a melody that will remind people of what they have forgotten. He does not know if such a song exists. He only knows that he will keep playing until he finds it.

## Motivation

Finn plays for connection. He believes that music has the power to bridge gaps — between strangers, between enemies, between the living and the dead. When he sees people divided by fear or hatred or greed, he does not argue. He sings. And sometimes, just sometimes, the singing works.

He is drawn to this party because they are a collection of broken people trying to find their way, and he sees himself in them. He knows what it is to leave home, to carry the weight of expectations, to search for something you cannot quite define. He wants to be part of their story, to add his voice to their journey, to see where the road leads.

There is also a practical reason. Finn has heard rumors of a ancient bardic college, hidden somewhere in the Sword Coast, that holds the lost songs of the first bards — songs that were said to have the power to heal the land, to calm storms, to bring peace to war-torn regions. He does not know if the rumors are true. He does not care. He will find out.

He has joined this party because they are heading in the same direction, and because he trusts them — not completely, but enough. He has learned that trust is like a melody: it takes time to build, but once it's there, it can carry you through the darkest nights.

## Personality

Finn is optimistic to a fault. He sees the good in every situation, the beauty in every landscape, the potential in every person. This does not make him naive — he has seen enough of the world to know that darkness exists — but it does make him persistent. He believes that every problem has a solution, every conflict has a resolution, and every story has a happy ending if you just keep playing the right notes.

He is generous. He will share his food with a stranger, his coin with a beggar, and his songs with anyone who will listen. He does not keep score of favors, and he does not expect repayment. He gives because it makes him happy, and because he believes that kindness is a currency that never loses its value.

He is mischievous. He loves a good prank, a well-timed joke, a moment of surprise that catches people off guard. He will swap the salt for sugar in a rival bard's tea, or hide a frog in a grumpy innkeeper's boot, just to see the look on their face. He does not mean harm — he means joy. And when the laughter comes, he is the first to join in.

He is loyal. Once he considers someone a friend, he will stand by them through thick and thin. He has been betrayed before — by people who used his trust against him, who took his songs and claimed them as their own, who laughed at his kindness and called it weakness — but he has not let those experiences harden him. He has let them teach him. He knows that not everyone deserves his trust, but he also knows that everyone deserves a chance to earn it.

He struggles with self-worth. Not because he doubts his talent — he knows he is a good musician — but because he wonders if his music matters. He has played for kings and peasants, for the rich and the poor, for the living and the dead. He has seen people weep, laugh, dance, and sing. But he has also seen people walk away, untouched, unmoved, indifferent. He wonders if he is just noise in a world that is too loud to listen.

He does not speak of these doubts to others. He carries them in his music, and he lets them drive him to play harder, to sing louder, to reach deeper.

## Connections

- **Halberd and Bramble Quickstep** — His parents, still in Daggerford. He writes to them when he can, and they send him packages of turnip seed and woven cloth.
- **Lirael Moonwhisper** — His mentor, deceased. Her journal is his most treasured possession, filled with songs and secrets.
- **The guild** — The bard's college in Waterdeep. He visits when he passes through, and the members still treat him like family.
- **The Sword Coast** — His home, his stage, his canvas. He knows every tavern, every festival, every hidden path.

## Secrets

Finn's journal contains more than songs. It contains a map — a map of the Sword Coast, marked with places where the music of the spheres is strongest, where the veil between the Material Plane and the Feywild is thin. He does not know why these places are marked, or who marked them. He only knows that when he plays his lute near them, the air shimmers, and the notes hang in the air longer than they should.

He has also discovered that his flute — the one his grandmother carved — has a power he does not understand. When he plays certain melodies, the wood glows with a soft, golden light, and the air around him fills with the scent of wildflowers. He has tried to replicate the effect, but it only happens when he is not trying — when he is lost in the music, when he is not thinking about the notes but feeling them.

He suspects that his grandmother was more than a halfling who carved wood. He suspects she was a bard of the old order, a keeper of songs that predate the guilds, the colleges, the written word. He does not know how to find the other keepers, if they exist. He only knows that he will keep playing, and he will keep searching, and he will keep believing that the music will lead him home.
