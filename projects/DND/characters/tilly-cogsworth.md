# Tilly Cogsworth

## Appearance

Tilly Cogsworth stands three feet tall, which she compensates for with an attitude that fills rooms twice her size. Her hair is a wild explosion of silver curls that defy every hairpin, comb, and spell she has tried to contain them, and her eyes are bright blue behind a pair of goggles she built herself — brass-rimmed, multi-lensed, and perpetually smudged with grease or soot or something she refuses to identify.

She dresses in practical clothing — a tunic of sturdy brown cloth, reinforced at the elbows and knees, with pockets sewn into every available inch. Those pockets contain tools, scrolls, crystals, gears, and at least three types of screwdriver. When she walks, she jingles. Not unpleasantly — more like a walking treasure chest of knowledge.

Her hands are stained with ink and oil, her nails are short and practical, and her fingers move with the dexterity of someone who has spent a lifetime assembling things that should not work but do. She has a habit of pushing her goggles up her nose when she's thinking, and when she's excited — which is often — she bounces on the balls of her feet like a child on the eve of a festival.

She does not care about appearances. She has been called many things in her life — eccentric, brilliant, annoying, indispensable — and she has accepted them all with equal grace. The only thing she cares about is that her goggles stay clean and her spellbook stays dry. Everything else is negotiable.

## Background

Tilly was born in the mining town of Mirabar, in the Year of the Shield, 1368 DR, to parents who were more interested in extracting gold from the earth than understanding the magic that made the world turn. Her father, Glimmer Cogsworth, was a miner who believed that anything that couldn't be weighed and sold was not worth the time. Her mother, Petal, was a herbalist who believed that nature provided all the answers anyone needed.

Tilly believed neither.

From the age of four, she was obsessed with how things worked. She would take apart her father's mining tools, study their mechanisms, and put them back together — usually with improvements. She would mix her mother's herbs with crystals she found in the mines, creating potions that did things no one could predict. Sometimes they worked. Sometimes they didn't. Once, she turned the family cat purple for a week. The cat did not appreciate the experience, and neither did her mother.

At eight, she discovered magic. Not the kind her mother practiced — the gentle, natural magic of herbs and healing — but the kind that came from books and spells and the raw power of the Weave. She found a spellbook in the attic of her family's home, hidden beneath a pile of old mining maps, and she devoured it. She did not understand all of it, but she understood enough to know that this was what she was meant to do.

Her parents were not supportive. "Magic is for fools and dreamers," her father would say. "You need to learn a real trade." Her mother was kinder, but no less firm. "Tilly, darling, magic is dangerous. You could hurt yourself."

Tilly did not listen. She practiced in secret, in the basement of their home, casting spells by the light of a single candle. She started with cantrips — lights, mending, message — simple spells that were safe and easy to control. But she was hungry for more, and she was not content to stop at the basics.

At twelve, she cast her first real spell. It was a simple shield spell, meant to protect her from a falling rock in the mines where her father worked. The spell worked — too well. The shield expanded beyond her control, engulfing the entire mine entrance and trapping twelve miners inside for six hours. No one was hurt, but the incident made her infamous in Mirabar. She was known as the girl who almost killed her father's workers, and the whispers followed her for years.

She left home at thirteen, determined to find a place where her talents would be valued rather than feared. She traveled south, following rumors of Candlekeep — the great library-fortress on the Sword Coast, where scholars of all races were welcomed and knowledge was revered above all else.

The journey to Candlekeep took a month. She slept in barns, begged from kind strangers, and cast spells to protect herself from bandits and beasts. By the time she arrived, she was exhausted, hungry, and covered in dirt. But she was also determined.

The Avowed of Candlekeep did not turn her away. They saw her potential, and they took her in. For five years, Tilly studied under the keep's greatest scholars, learning the theory of magic, the history of the Weave, and the art of abjuration — the school of magic focused on protection and defense.

She excelled. Her mind was sharp, her focus was unbreakable, and her dedication to the craft was unmatched. She learned to cast spells with precision, to weave magic into barriers that could withstand the mightiest attacks, and to understand the underlying principles that made magic work.

But Candlekeep was not enough. The books were vast, the knowledge was deep, but Tilly felt a pull toward the world beyond the library's walls. She wanted to see magic in action, to test her spells against real threats, to prove to herself — and to the world — that she was more than the girl who almost killed her father's workers.

She left Candlekeep in the Year of the Wild Magic, 1372 DR, with a spellbook full of spells, a head full of knowledge, and a heart full of curiosity. She has been traveling the Sword Coast ever since, seeking knowledge, protecting the innocent, and inventing things that should not work but do.

## Motivation

Tilly studies magic because she believes it is the key to understanding the world. Not just the surface world — the rocks and the trees and the rivers — but the deeper world, the one that exists beneath the visible, the one that holds the secrets of creation. She wants to know how magic works, why it works, and what it means for the people who live in a world where the Weave is real and the planes are close.

She is drawn to this party because they are heading into danger, and she believes that magic — her magic — can help them survive. She does not see herself as a warrior, but she sees herself as a protector. Her abjuration spells are not just tools for defense; they are expressions of her belief that no one should have to face the darkness alone.

She has joined this party because the road ahead runs through places where magic is wild and unpredictable — places where the boundaries between the Material Plane and the Feywild are thin, where ancient spells still echo in the stone, and where the Weave itself seems to breathe. She wants to study these places, to understand them, to map the magical currents that flow beneath the surface.

There is also a personal reason. Tilly carries a guilt that she cannot shake. When she left Candlekeep, she left behind a friend — a young scholar named Oren who had shared her passion for magic and her love of discovery. Oren had asked her to stay, to continue their research together, to build something new in the safety of the library. She had said no. She had chosen the road, the adventure, the unknown.

Three months after she left, Oren died. Not from danger — from a spell gone wrong. He had been experimenting with a ritual she had taught him, and he had made a mistake. A small mistake, the kind that anyone could make, but one that cost him his life.

Tilly tells herself that she could not have prevented it. She tells herself that Oren was an adult, capable of making his own choices. But in the quiet hours of the night, when the campfire burns low and the spellbook closes, she wonders if she should have stayed. She wonders if her presence would have made a difference. She wonders if she is the kind of person who can lead others, or if she is just a scholar who runs from responsibility.

She does not have the answers yet. But she will keep studying, and she will keep protecting, and she will keep inventing — because that is what she does, and that is who she is.

## Personality

Tilly is curious. She asks questions constantly — about magic, about people, about the world. She does not accept answers at face value; she digs deeper, probes further, seeks the underlying truth. This can be exhausting for those around her, but it is also what makes her brilliant. She sees connections that others miss, patterns that others overlook, and possibilities that others dismiss.

She is protective. She does not tolerate danger lightly, and she will not stand by while others are harmed. Her abjuration spells are not just tools for self-defense; they are expressions of her belief that everyone deserves to be safe. When she casts a shield spell, she is not just protecting herself — she is protecting the group, the mission, the future.

She is inventive. She loves to build things, to tinker with mechanisms, to create gadgets that solve problems in unexpected ways. Her goggles are just the beginning — she has crafted a portable spell amplifier, a magical compass that points toward sources of arcane energy, and a device that can detect lies by measuring the subtle shifts in a person's magical aura. She does not share all her inventions with others, but she is always working on something new.

She is patient. She understands that knowledge takes time, that magic cannot be rushed, and that the best solutions are often the ones that require the most preparation. She will spend hours studying a spell, testing its effects, refining its casting — and when she is ready, she will be ready. She does not waste energy on haste.

She is kind. She does not belittle those who do not understand magic, and she does not mock those who make mistakes. She believes that everyone has the potential to learn, to grow, to discover something new about themselves and the world. She will spend hours teaching a novice the basics of cantrips, or explaining the theory behind a complex ritual, or simply listening to someone's story. She does not judge. She listens.

She struggles with confidence. Not about her abilities — she knows she is a skilled wizard — but about her worth. She wonders if her inventions matter, if her spells make a difference, if her presence in the world is enough. She wonders if she will ever be as great as the scholars she studied under, as powerful as the wizards she has read about, as respected as the mages she has met.

She does not speak of these doubts to others. She carries them in her work, and she lets them drive her to study harder, to invent bolder, to reach further.

## Connections

- **Glimmer and Petal Cogsworth** — Her parents, still in Mirabar. She writes to them when she can, and they send her packages of mining tools and herbal remedies.
- **Oren** — Her friend at Candlekeep, deceased. His memory drives her to be more careful, more thoughtful, more present.
- **Candlekeep** — Her home, her school, her sanctuary. She visits when she can, and the Avowed still treat her like family.
- **The Sword Coast** — Her laboratory, her testing ground, her canvas. She knows every magical anomaly, every hidden current, every secret path.

## Secrets

Tilly's spellbook contains more than spells. It contains a theory — a theory about the nature of the Weave, about the source of magic, about the connection between the Material Plane and the other planes. She believes that magic is not just a force but a living thing, a consciousness that exists beyond the planes, beyond time, beyond understanding. She has not shared this theory with anyone, not because she is afraid of their reaction, but because she is not sure it is complete.

She has also discovered that her goggles have a power she does not fully understand. When she looks through them at certain magical artifacts, she sees not just the aura but the history — flashes of the past, echoes of the hands that have touched the object, whispers of the spells that have been cast. She does not know why this happens, or how to control it. She only knows that it is real, and it is growing stronger.

She suspects that her father's mining tools — the ones she took apart as a child — contained more than metal and wood. She suspects they contained fragments of ancient magic, magic that had been trapped in the stone for centuries, waiting for someone to release it. She does not know how to find the other fragments, if they exist. She only knows that she will keep searching, and she will keep studying, and she will keep believing that the answers are out there, waiting to be discovered.
