# Malachi Vane

## Appearance

Malachi is handsome in the way a blade is handsome — sharp, precise, and dangerous to anyone who mistakes beauty for harmlessness. His skin is the deep amber of burnished copper, his eyes burn with an inner fire that is not entirely his own, and his horns curve back from his temples like a crown of obsidian. They are not large, but they are unmistakable, and he has never bothered to hide them.

His hair is black as a moonless night, usually slicked back with some unguent that costs more than most people's monthly rent. He dresses well — too well for a warlock, some might say — in robes of dark crimson that seem to drink the light around them. The fabric moves like liquid, and those who watch carefully might notice that the shadows cast by his clothing do not quite match the shape of his body.

He carries himself with the posture of someone who has been taught that every gesture is a statement. His voice is low and measured, each word chosen with care, as if speaking carelessly were a sin. When he smiles, it is rarely genuine — but it is always effective.

The obsidian orb at his belt is his arcane focus, and it hums with a warmth that is almost comforting. Those who have seen it glow in the darkness say it looks like a dying star, or a demon's eye watching from within the stone.

## Background

Malachi was born in Waterdeep, the City of Splendors, to a human mother named Elara Vane and a father whose name he does not know — and does not wish to know. His mother was a scribe in the service of one of the Noble Families, a woman of quiet dignity who tried to shield her son from the prejudice that followed a tiefling through the streets of even the most cosmopolitan city.

She succeeded for a time. Malachi grew up in the upper wards, where the guards looked the other way and the merchants accepted his mother's coin without question. He was bright, curious, and insatiable in his hunger for knowledge. He read every book he could find, memorized every scroll he could reach, and asked questions that made even the most patient scholars uncomfortable.

When he was twelve, his mother fell ill. The physicians could not diagnose her condition, and the temples offered only prayers. Malachi, desperate, began to search for answers in places he should not have looked. He found a book in the restricted section of a temple archive — a book bound in leather that felt too much like skin, written in a language that shifted when he tried to read it aloud.

The book spoke of power. Not the power of gold or position, but the power that lay beneath the world, in the spaces between the planes. It spoke of patrons who could grant their followers abilities beyond mortal comprehension, in exchange for service. It spoke of a bargain that could save his mother, if only he was willing to pay the price.

He was twelve. He did not understand the price. He only understood that his mother was dying, and that this book offered a way to save her.

He made the pact in the temple cellar, speaking words he did not understand to a presence he could not see. The power came immediately — a rush of heat through his veins, a clarity of mind that made the world seem sharper, brighter, more alive. He ran to his mother's bedside and spoke a healing word he had never learned, and she opened her eyes.

She lived for three more years. When she died, it was peacefully, in her sleep, with Malachi holding her hand. She never knew what he had done to save her. She never needed to know.

After her death, Malachi withdrew from the world. He retreated to a small cottage in the Hills of Hermitage, north of Waterdeep, where he spent years studying the nature of his patron, the limits of his power, and the true cost of the bargain he had made as a boy. He learned that the patron was not a single entity but a faction — a court of fiends who saw him as a useful tool and nothing more.

He accepted this. Tools can be sharp, and sharp things can cut both ways.

## Motivation

Malachi is driven by a single, consuming goal: to understand his patron completely. Not to serve them, not to rebel against them — to understand them. He believes that knowledge is power, and that if he can comprehend the full nature of the fiendish court that holds his soul in thrall, he can find a way to turn their own rules against them.

He is not naive. He knows that his patron will not give him the answers he seeks willingly. He knows that every piece of information he gains comes at a cost. But he also knows that he is smarter than they think, and that he has been playing a longer game than they realize.

He has joined this party because the road ahead leads through places where his patron's influence is strong — places where the boundaries between the Material Plane and the Nine Hells are thin. He wants to see what lies on the other side of those boundaries, and he wants to map the territory before his patron can use it against him.

There is also a quieter motivation, one he rarely admits even to himself. He is lonely. The years in the hills taught him how to be alone, but they did not teach him how to be content with it. He watches the other members of this party and sees something he has never had — genuine connection, loyalty that is not bought or bargained for. He does not know how to ask for it, so he does not ask. But he notices.

## Personality

Malachi is calculating. He thinks three steps ahead in every conversation, every encounter, every decision. He is not cruel — cruelty is wasteful, and he does not waste — but he is not kind either. Kindness is a vulnerability, and he has spent his life building walls against vulnerability.

He is intellectually honest. When he does not know something, he says so. When he is wrong, he admits it — not because he is humble, but because pretending to be right when you are wrong is inefficient. He values precision in language, in thought, and in action.

He has a dark sense of humor that catches people off guard. He will make a joke about death in the middle of a battle, or comment on the irony of a situation with a dryness that makes even the most stoic among them pause. He does not do this to be provocative. He does it because humor is a way of maintaining perspective, and perspective is a weapon.

He does not trust easily, and he does not forgive readily. But when he does commit to someone — when he decides that a person is worth his loyalty — he is unwavering. He has learned that promises mean nothing unless they are kept, and he keeps his promises.

He struggles with the nature of his own soul. He knows he is not good, and he does not pretend to be. But he also knows that he is not evil in the way his patron is evil. He is something else — something that exists in the space between the light and the dark, and he is still trying to understand what that means.

## Connections

- **Elara Vane** — His mother, deceased. Her memory is the one thing his patron cannot touch.
- **The Fiendish Court** — His patron, a faction of devils and demons who see him as a tool. He is learning to see them the same way.
- **Waterdeep** — His birthplace. He has not returned since leaving the Hills of Hermitage.
- **The cottage** — Where he spent his years of study. It still stands, though he suspects his patron has eyes there now.

## Secrets

Malachi's pact has a clause he has not yet triggered. The book he read as a boy mentioned a "final accounting" — a moment when the patron would collect what was owed. He does not know when that moment will come, or what it will cost. He only knows that when it does, he will be ready.

He has been preparing for it since he left the hills. Every piece of knowledge he has gained, every ally he has made, every spell he has mastered — it is all part of a plan. He has not shared this plan with anyone. Not yet. But when the time comes, he will need this party. And they will need him.

The obsidian orb is not just an arcane focus. It is a prison. Inside it, trapped in stone, is a fragment of his patron's power — a piece of the fiend that he severed during his years of study. It is small, but it is real. And it is growing.
