# Ward Council — General Handbook ch. 4 (training share)

**For:** today's General Handbook training for ward council.
**Source (read this session):** [General Handbook 4 — Leadership and Councils in the Church of Jesus Christ](../gospel-library/eng/manual/general-handbook/4-leadership-in-the-church-of-jesus-christ.md).
**Shape:** three points, ~5 minutes each. Each ties a handbook section to a principle I
actually practiced this week, so I can teach it from lived experience, not just theory.

> Framing line to open with: *Councils aren't a meeting format — they're how Heaven works.*
> "Councils existed before the world was created. Each of us participated in these councils
> before coming to earth" ([4.3](../gospel-library/eng/manual/general-handbook/4-leadership-in-the-church-of-jesus-christ.md); D&C 121:32; Abraham 3:22–28).

---

## Point 1 — Counsel together, and listen more than you talk (~5 min)

**Handbook 4.4.3 (Discussion and Decisions).** The Lord's pattern: *"Let one speak at a time
and let all listen unto his sayings, that when all have spoken that all may be edified of
all, and that every man may have an equal privilege"* ([D&C 88:122](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/88.md)).
And the line that should change how a leader runs a council:

> "A leader guides the council's discussions. However, he or she should listen more than
> talk. When a council leader shares his or her perspective too early, it can inhibit the
> contributions of others." — 4.4.3

The promise: *"The council process helps bring about inspired decisions that go beyond a
leader's own best judgment."* The council isn't a vote and it isn't a rubber stamp — it's a
revelation surface. The bishop who walks in with the answer and hands out tasks gets his own
best judgment. The bishop who opens the floor gets more than that.

**Ward application:** the agenda item gets *explained*, then the leader asks and waits.
Women and men both — "different perspectives that provide needed balance" (4.4.3). The hard
discipline is the silence after the question.

**Discussion question to pose:** *When was the last time a council changed your mind — and
what made that possible?*

**What I lived this week:** the most useful thing my AI does for me isn't answering — it's
surfacing the tension I hadn't considered before I commit. Every time I let it counsel
before I decided, the decision got better than my first instinct. Same principle, small
scale: the counsel goes beyond my own best judgment.

---

## Point 2 — Delegate by teaching and trusting, then hold accountable (~5 min)

**Handbook 4.2.5 (Delegate Responsibility and Ensure Accountability).** First the warning:
*"If you try to do too much, you will 'surely wear away'"* ([Exodus 18:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md) — Jethro to Moses). Then the heart of it:

> "Delegating is more than giving an assignment. It also includes teaching and trusting
> another person to fulfill the assignment." — 4.2.5

The four elements the handbook lists: **invite** and help them understand the purpose;
**counsel together** about how and when; **trust** them and let them seek their own
inspiration ("Show your trust and help the person succeed"); **ask them to report**, and
"accept the person's best efforts." Delegation isn't offloading and it isn't
micromanaging — it's handing over the *doing* while staying close enough to support.

**Ward application:** the goal of an assignment is partly the work and partly the *growth of
the person*. "Sometimes the same people are called repeatedly… this can overburden them and
take opportunities away from others" (4.2.6). Spread it; trust it; follow up on it.

**Discussion question to pose:** *Is there an assignment you're holding that someone else
should be growing into?*

**What I lived this week:** I spent the week handing real work to systems and helpers and
learning where the line is — delegate the *execution*, but keep the *judgment* that's mine
to make. When I trusted the doing and stayed responsible for the discernment, it worked. When
I tried to do all of it myself, I started to wear away — Jethro was right.

---

## Point 3 — The work happens before and after the meeting; follow up, in unity (~5 min)

**Handbook 4.4.5 (Action and Accountability) + 4.4.4 (Unity).**

> "Council members do most of their work before and after council meetings… Council members
> report on their assignments. Progress usually requires sustained attention and follow-up
> assignments." — 4.4.5

The meeting is the hinge, not the work. A decision with no follow-through is just a
conversation. The handbook is blunt that progress needs *sustained attention* — you watch the
thing through until it's done, and you ask for the report. And it's held together by unity:
*"be one"* ([D&C 38:27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/38.md)), avoiding
"contention, unrighteous judgment, and gossip" (4.4.4).

**Ward application:** close every council with *who, what, by when* — and open the next one by
*asking how it went*. The follow-up is where ministering actually reaches the family you
discussed.

**Discussion question to pose:** *What's one assignment from a past council we never closed
the loop on — and who could pick it back up?*

**What I lived this week:** my hardest rule in my own work is that "it should work" isn't done
— I have to watch it actually work, reproduce the result, confirm it. Every time I skipped the
follow-through I paid for it. A council is the same: the decision isn't the fruit; the
watching-it-through is.

---

## Close (~30 sec)

Three things, all from chapter 4: **counsel together** (listen more than you talk),
**delegate by trusting** (and don't wear yourself away), **follow through in unity** (the work
is before and after). The thread: a council that counsels, trusts, and watches gets decisions
"beyond a leader's own best judgment" — which is the whole point of Heaven giving us councils
in the first place.
