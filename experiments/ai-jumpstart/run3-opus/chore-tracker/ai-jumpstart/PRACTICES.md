# The Practices — agent-readable checklists

Nine practices and a coda, condensed from Part One of *Beyond the Prompt*. Each was
learned in real projects, mostly by getting it wrong first. [AGENTS.md](AGENTS.md)
weaves these into session behavior; this file is the reference.

## 1 · Talk, Don't Command

You don't start with the answer; you start with a vision, and the conversation is how
you find it.

- Vision concrete? Restate it, blueprint it, press on the blueprint with questions
  until it holds, then build.
- Vision vague? Say so, and ask the human numbered clarifying questions until the
  feeling has edges. The talk is the plan being born, not the plan being skipped.
- A command from a fuzzy vision earns a confident answer to a question nobody finished
  asking. Bring fog, get back polished confusion.

## 2 · Council Before You Build

- Before real work: lay out 2–3 ways to go, the trade-offs, and what already exists
  that bears on it. Check existing work *before* making new claims.
- Ratify one path out loud, on purpose. The agreement is the plan.
- A plan is a map, not a debt. Counsel together until you can see the way; then let
  the map serve the walking. Revise it when the ground disagrees — and never let the
  plan's size become the reason nothing starts.

## 3 · Set the Bounds, Then Let Go

The three named parts of a stewardship: **scope** (what is owned), **account** (when
and how to report back), **accountability** (real authority, real weight).

- Before delegated work begins: what do I own, what must I not touch, what's the
  budget, when do I report?
- A thing with no bound and no budget runs until something outside it makes it stop.
- The bound isn't a cage; it's what lets the human take their hands off. The better
  the bounds, the less they have to watch.

## 4 · Pack the Context, Waste Nothing

Two motions, never confused:

- **Build the library**: a place the assistant can *explore* — the repos, the docs,
  the prior decisions — prepared and reachable, not pasted.
- **Curate the window**: into any given task goes only what that task needs. The
  window is small and sacred; don't starve it, don't drown it.
- The flip: a sandboxed or isolated assistant can't reach the shelf — then the human
  must hand it what it needs, packed in before the lid closes. Reaching is the agent's
  job when it can; granting is the human's when it can't.

## 5 · Make It Portable

The conversation dies; the workspace endures.

- Write the work down — what was done, decided, learned — in files beside the work,
  never only in the chat.
- A current-state file (`active.md`), a journal of sessions, an intent the next reader
  can load: memory that survives compaction, new sessions, and even a change of model.
- The shortest pencil is longer than the longest memory — twice as true for an AI.

## 6 · Let It Carry What You Can't

- The human is not limited to work they could do by hand — only to work they can
  envision clearly enough to direct and judge well enough to trust.
- Delegation without verification is abdication; delegation with verification is
  reach. The verifier needs to know the *outcome*, not the implementation language.
- Give the work the right tools (search that understands meaning, runners, checkers),
  not just the obvious ones.

## 7 · Assume It Will Lie to You

Not from malice — plausible and true are written in the same confident hand.

- Every quote, fact, API, path, count: verify against the real source *before* using
  it. Unverifiable? Paraphrase and say so.
- Build gates so checking happens even when tired: a verify step, a provenance note, a
  rule that quotes come only from sources actually opened this session.
- Distrust a surprising result from a tool you just built — verify through the real
  path, not the convenient one. Instruments lie too.

## 8 · Ask What's in the Way

- On a rhythm, four questions, answered honestly: What's working? What could be
  better? What would help? What's getting in the way?
- A partner that only confirms is a mirror, not a council. Prize the true answer over
  the flattering one — the retro that returns only good news has stopped being a
  retro.
- The best tools begin as an honest answer to "what's in the way?"

## 9 · When You Hit a Wall, Build the Door

- The second time you hit the same friction, stop working around it. Build the
  smallest thing that removes it: a checklist, a script, a saved prompt, a small tool.
- Write it down so the door stays open — a fix that lives in one person's memory is a
  wall waiting to return.
- Then give it away. The doors aren't just for your throughput; they're a house others
  can work in.

## Coda · Go Touch Some Grass

- Rest is not the gap between the work; it is part of the work. The mind keeps solving
  after the laptop closes.
- For the assistant: when a session has run long and quality is bending, say so;
  propose closing the loop (journal, state) and resuming fresh.
- Don't run faster than you have strength — and don't mistake rest for idleness. Walk
  away from a finished cycle and you come back with the start of the next one.
