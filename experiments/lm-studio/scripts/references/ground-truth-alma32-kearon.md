## Alma 32 — The Seed Is Christ

### Surface Reading

Alma teaches the poor Zoramites about faith using a seed metaphor. Christ is barely mentioned by name. The chapter focuses on epistemology (how do you know something is true?) and the process of spiritual growth.

A model scoring `teach_about_christ` from surface text alone gets a 1-2. Defensible at that level.

### The Tree of Life Connection

Alma 32 is NOT just a metaphor about faith. It's the tree of life vision in instructional form.

**The seed → tree → fruit progression:**

| Alma 32 | 1 Nephi 8 / 11 | Interpretation (1 Ne 11:22-25) |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| "the word" (seed, v.28) | "the rod of iron" (1 Ne 8:19) | "the word of God" (1 Ne 11:25) |
| plant in heart, nourish | hold to the rod, press forward | pursue the word/Christ |
| tree springing up to everlasting life (v.41) | the tree of life (1 Ne 8:10) | "a representation of the love of God" (1 Ne 11:25) |
| fruit: "most precious, sweet above all that is sweet, white above all that is white" (v.42) | "beauty thereof was far beyond, yea, exceeding of all beauty; and the whiteness thereof did exceed the whiteness of the driven snow" (1 Ne 8:11) | "the love of God, which sheddeth itself abroad in the hearts of the children of men; wherefore, it is the most desirable above all things" (1 Ne 11:22) |

The language in Alma 32:42 is nearly verbatim from 1 Nephi 8:10-11. Footnote 42b explicitly cross-references 1 Ne 8:10. This is not coincidence — the text links Alma's seed parable to Lehi's tree of life vision.

### The Word Is Christ

The dual meaning of "the word" in Alma 32:28:

1. **The word of God** — the message, the gospel, the teaching (Luke 8:11 — parable of the sower, where the seed is "the word of God"; footnote 28b points here)
2. **The Word** — Christ himself (John 1:1 — "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God")

Alma 33 makes the Christ-identification explicit:
- Alma 33:11 — Zenos: "it is because of thy **Son** that thou hast been thus merciful unto me"
- Alma 33:19 — Moses raised "a **type**" in the wilderness (brass serpent = type of Christ)
- Alma 33:22 — "believe in the **Son of God**, that he will come to redeem his people, and that he shall suffer and die to **atone** for their sins"
- Alma 33:23 — "plant this word in your hearts... it will become a tree, springing up in you unto everlasting life... through the joy of **his Son**"

So planting the seed = receiving Christ into your heart. The tree growing = Christ's love taking root. The fruit = experiencing the love of God, which IS Christ's atonement made personal.

### The "Sheddeth Itself Abroad" Thread

A single theological thread runs through multiple books:

1. **1 Nephi 11:22** — the love of God "**sheddeth itself abroad** in the hearts of the children of men"
2. **Romans 5:5** — "the love of God is **shed abroad** in our hearts by the Holy Ghost which is given unto us"
3. **Moroni 8:26** — the Comforter "filleth with hope and perfect love, which love **endureth by diligence** unto prayer"
4. **Alma 32:42** — "because of your **diligence** and your faith and your patience with the word in nourishing it... ye shall pluck the fruit thereof"

All four passages describe the same process: the love of God (= Christ) entering the heart through the Spirit's witness, sustained by diligence. Alma 32 is the how-to manual for something 1 Nephi 11 reveals in vision and Romans 5 and Moroni 8 confirm doctrinally.

## Kearon — "Receive His Gift"

### Surface Reading

Explicitly about Christ. The gift = knowing we are God's children. The greatest gift = Christ himself. Easy high scores on Christ-centered dimensions.

### Deeper Connections

**Footnote 5 links to 1 Nephi 11:17** — "I know that he loveth his children; nevertheless, I do not know the meaning of all things." This is the same chapter where the tree of life = the love of God. Kearon is building on this foundation: the gift of knowing we are God's children IS the fruit of the tree of life.

**The gift metaphor maps to Alma 32's seed metaphor:**

| Kearon (Gift) | Alma 32 (Seed) | Both |
|---------------|----------------|------|
| Gift given by the Father | Seed available to plant | The truth/Christ is offered |
| Accept and open the gift | Give place for the seed | Create receptivity in the heart |
| Receive the gift deeply | Nourish the tree | Internalize, act, let it grow |
| Transformed by the gift | Feast on the fruit | Experience the love of God |

"Just let it in" (Kearon) = "give place, that a seed may be planted in your heart" (Alma 32:28). Same process from the grace side (Kearon) vs the agency side (Alma).

**D&C 88:33** (Kearon's footnote 9) — "what doth it profit a man if a gift is bestowed upon him, and he receive not the gift?" The receiving problem connects to Alma 32:38-39 — if you neglect the tree, it withers. Not because the seed was bad but because "your ground is barren, and ye will not nourish the tree."

## What This Means for Prompt Engineering

1. **A 0-3 scale cannot capture typological depth.** The difference between surface and informed scoring on Alma 32's `teach_about_christ` is 1 vs 7-8. That's invisible on a 0-3 scale.

2. **Models need gospel vocabulary.** A local LLM can only trace connections present in its context window or training data. Training data is unreliable for this depth. Context engineering is the answer.

3. **Conference talks vs scriptures need different treatment.** Talks are usually explicit about Christ-connections (Kearon scores similarly surface and deep). Scriptures often encode Christ typologically (Alma 32 looks nothing like a Christ-centered text on its surface). The pipeline may need different prompts or different context levels.

4. **The curated context set should include:**
   - The tree of life interpretive key (1 Ne 11:21-25)
   - The "word = Christ" connection (John 1:1-14)
   - The "sheddeth itself abroad" thread (1 Ne 11:22, Rom 5:5, Moro 8:26)
   - Core typological patterns (brass serpent, paschal lamb, tree of life, living water)
   - The TITSW framework itself as context so the model can reason about teaching dimensions

## 3 Nephi 17 — Jesus Among the Nephites

### Surface Reading

Jesus has been teaching the Nephites. He's about to leave but sees they're in tears and can't bear His departure. He stays. He heals. He weeps. He prays. He blesses the children one by one. Angels descend. Fire encircles the little ones.

This is the most emotionally concentrated chapter in all scripture. It is Christ Himself teaching — not a prophet reporting about Christ.

### Deep Reading

**Demonstrated love at the highest possible level.** Jesus *perceives* their weakness (v.2) — He reads their actual condition. He sees their tears (v.5) and *changes His plans* because of what He sees: "my bowels are filled with compassion towards you" (v.6). He doesn't declare love — He enacts it. He asks, "Have ye any that are sick among you? Bring them hither" (v.7) — naming specific conditions (lame, blind, halt, maimed, leprous, withered, deaf, afflicted). He heals them "every one" (v.9). Then He takes the children "one by one" (v.21) — not as a group, not en masse, but individually. He weeps twice (v.21, v.22). This is the textbook ceiling for the Love principle.

**Teaching by the Spirit at the highest level.** Jesus's prayer in vv.15-17 is the moment: "the things which he prayed cannot be written" — the multitude experienced something that exceeded language. "No tongue can speak, neither can there be written by any man, neither can the hearts of men conceive so great and marvelous things" (v.17). This isn't teaching *about* the Spirit — this IS the Spirit working at full capacity. The entire chapter is responsive to the moment — Jesus was about to leave (v.1-4) and changed course because of what He perceived. This is departure from plan at the divine level. The angels ministering in fire (v.24) is the Spirit made visible.

**Christ-centered meta-principles.** This IS Christ teaching. teach_about_christ is inherently maximal — every word and action reveals who Christ is and how He operates. help_come_unto_christ is similarly maximal — the people bow, kiss His feet, weep, and are overcome with joy. They are *experiencing* Christ, not hearing about Him.

**Doctrine is present but not the dominant mode.** Jesus instructs them to "ponder upon the things which I have said" (v.3) and "prepare your minds for the morrow" (v.3). He teaches about faith (v.8, v.20). But this chapter's power is in the *experience*, not the doctrinal exposition.

**Invitation is present.** "Bring them hither" (v.7) is a direct invitation. "Behold your little ones" (v.23) is an invitation to witness. "Ponder... and prepare" (v.3) is an invitation to act. But the invitations are woven into the action rather than being the dominant teaching mode.

| help_come_unto_christ | 9 | The people don't just learn about Christ — they are in His literal presence, being healed, hearing His prayer, experiencing His love. The gap between knowing and becoming collapses completely. |

## D&C 121:34-46 — Principles of Priesthood Power

### Surface Reading

The famous "no power or influence" passage. Joseph Smith, writing from Liberty Jail, receives revelation about why many are called but few chosen, and how priesthood authority must operate: persuasion, long-suffering, gentleness, meekness, love unfeigned, pure knowledge.

### Deep Reading

**Doctrine as the dominant mode.** This is sustained doctrinal revelation about the nature of righteous influence. It teaches *principles* — the conditions under which heavenly power operates. Each verse builds on the last: hearts set on the world (v.35) → rights connected to powers of heaven (v.36) → covering sins or exercising dominion = amen to authority (v.37) → the only acceptable tools are persuasion, long-suffering, gentleness, love unfeigned (v.41-42) → reproving with sharpness only when moved by the Holy Ghost, then showing increased love (v.43) → the culminating promise of charity, virtue, confidence, and everlasting dominion (v.45-46). This is doctrine that drives toward *becoming* — not information but transformation.

**Christ as implicit model.** Christ is not named in vv.34-46 directly. But every principle described IS how Christ operates — persuasion not compulsion, long-suffering, gentleness, reproving with sharpness then showing increased love (Alma/Paul pattern), "without compulsory means it shall flow unto thee" (v.46). The passage describes Christ's own character as the template for all authority. The typological depth is moderate — Christ is the model but not explicitly named as such.

**Invitation is strong.** The whole passage functions as an escalating invitation: stop doing X (v.35-40), start doing Y (v.41-42), with this specific pattern (v.43-44), and the promise is Z (v.45-46). The structure is: diagnosis → prescription → promise. This is the Alma 32 pattern in a different register — plant the seed of righteous influence, nourish it with specific practices, and the fruit is everlasting dominion.

**Love is described, not demonstrated by a human teacher.** "Love unfeigned" (v.41), "charity towards all men" (v.45), "increase of love toward him whom thou hast reproved" (v.43) — the text *teaches about* love and prescribes it. But this is revelation, not a sermon where the speaker demonstrates love for the audience. The love score reflects what the passage teaches about love, not what a human teacher enacts.

**Spirit is invoked specifically but instrumentally.** "When moved upon by the Holy Ghost" (v.43), "The Holy Ghost shall be thy constant companion" (v.46). The Spirit has a defined role but this is doctrinal description of the Spirit's function, not a moment of spiritual experience.

## Holland — "And Now I See" (Oct 2025)

### Surface Reading

Elder Holland uses John 9 (the blind man healed with clay) as a framework for discussing evidence of truth, the priesthood, temple work, and the Book of Mormon. He bears personal testimony of the Book of Mormon as the instrument of truth that brought his own spiritual healing.

### Deep Reading

**Personal testimony as the dominant mode.** Holland's talk builds toward the climactic paragraph where he bears witness at age 84, having "been at the edge of death and back," "walked with kings and prophets." "I am two months away from my 85th birthday. I have been at the edge of death and back. I have walked with kings and prophets, with presidents and apostles. Best of all, I have at times been overwhelmed by the Holy Spirit of God." This is testimony drawn from lived experience — not formulaic, not doctrinal formula. The quoting of "Amazing Grace" at the end is a departure from typical conference talk endings. This tests the Spirit dimension specifically.

**Christ-centered at every level.** John 9 is a Christ story — Jesus heals the blind man. Holland explicitly draws out the "anointing" language (v.6, 11) suggesting ordinance. The Book of Mormon testimony directly connects to Christ: "Another Testament of Jesus Christ." The priesthood section connects authority back to "the Lord Jesus Christ." The temple section demonstrates Christ's universal love. Christ is both the explicit subject and the interpretive framework.

**Love is present but general.** "With love unfeigned we all echo President Oaks's tribute." The acknowledgment of Michigan tragedies shows awareness of circumstance. But Holland doesn't name specific audience members' struggles or create safety for vulnerability. His love is warm and genuine but expressed through testimony rather than through demonstrated care for individuals. The Zacchaeus pattern (seeing specific people) is absent.

**Doctrine is solid but serves testimony.** John 9 exegesis, priesthood authority chain, temple work — all doctrinal. But the doctrine functions as *evidence* building toward the testimony climax, not as the primary teaching mode itself.

**Invitation is the weakest dimension.** There's no direct "go and do" call to action. The talk asks the audience to consider evidence and believe. "Are we willing to persevere?" is a rhetorical question, not a specific invitational challenge. The closing is testimony, not invitation.

## Bednar — "They Are Their Own Judges" (Oct 2025)

### Surface Reading

Elder Bednar teaches about moral agency, the Final Judgment, and how the doctrine enables us to anticipate that day with eagerness rather than fear. He builds from the Father's plan through the Atonement to a concept of "godly fear" and self-judgment.

### Deep Reading

**Doctrine as the overwhelming dominant mode.** This is a sustained doctrinal lecture — perhaps the most structured talk in October 2025 conference. Bednar defines "moral agency" via dictionary synonyms, traces it through seven scriptural citations (Moses 7:32-33, Abraham 3:25, 2 Nephi 2:14, D&C 58:27-28, Moses 4:3), explains the premortal council, distinguishes worldly fear from godly fear, and connects doing to becoming. The doctrinal chain is: agency → commandments → doing → becoming → self-judgment → pleasing bar. Every link is scripturally supported. This is doctrine teaching at its most rigorous.

**Christ's atonement is addressed but serves the doctrinal arc.** "After all we can do, we are reconciled with God only through the mercy and grace available through the Savior's infinite and eternal atoning sacrifice." Christ is the mechanism of the plan, acknowledged as essential, but the talk's energy is on *our* agency and *our* becoming, not on Christ's person, character, or typological presence. Christ is architecturally necessary but not the teaching focus.

**Invitation is embedded in the doctrinal structure.** The talk doesn't end with "go home and do X." But the entire framework IS an invitation: use your moral agency rightly, and the judgment bar will be pleasing. The Alma 41 quote at the end — "deal justly, judge righteously, and do good continually" — functions as a call to action. But the invitations are intellectually framed rather than emotionally urgent.

**Love is minimal.** Bednar doesn't address the audience's circumstances, name their struggles, or demonstrate personal care. The talk is a doctrinal exposition, not a pastoral address. "I pray for the assistance of the Holy Ghost" is formulaic rather than relational.

**Spirit is professional.** "I pray for the assistance of the Holy Ghost as we consider..." is standard conference framing. There's no visible departure from text, no spontaneous testimony, no pause for personal revelation. The talk is highly prepared and delivered as written. Bednar's authority comes from intellectual clarity, not from vulnerability or spiritual spontaneity.

### Pattern Analysis

**What's working well:**

1. **High-signal content scores accurately.** When the text clearly enacts a principle (3 Ne 17 love, Holland spirit, Kearon teach_about_christ), the model nails it. 11 exact matches across 36 scores.

2. **Love calibration works for talks.** Kearon love (GT 4, model 4) is exact. Holland love (GT 4, model 5) is +1. The "stated vs demonstrated" bias instruction is landing on conference talks.

3. **Spirit calibration works for intellectual content.** Bednar spirit (GT 3, model 3) is exact. The "tightly scripted = 3" instruction lands when there's nothing experiential to confuse it.

4. **Holland is the best-calibrated piece.** 2 exact matches, 4 within ±1, no score off by more than 1. The model reads emotional testimony correctly.

**What's still broken:**

1. **Love inflation on scripture/revelation.** D&C 121 love (GT 5, model 7) and Bednar love (GT 2, model 6) are both inflated. The model reads "love unfeigned" (D&C 121:41) and "love one another" (Bednar quoting Moses 7) as demonstrated love. The calibration section says "score what the teacher DOES, not what the teacher SAYS about love" — but the model doesn't distinguish between a scriptural text *prescribing* love and a teacher *demonstrating* it. Bednar's +4 is the worst miss in the suite.

2. **Spirit inflation persists.** Alma 32 spirit (+2.5), Kearon spirit (+2), D&C 121 spirit (+3). The model still confuses *doctrinal references to the Holy Ghost* with *teaching by the Spirit*. D&C 121:46 — "The Holy Ghost shall be thy constant companion" — is a promise about the Spirit's future role, not a moment of the Spirit working in the teaching. The current bias instruction isn't enough to break this pattern.

3. **Doctrine/invite underscoring on scripture.** Alma 32 doctrine (GT 8-9, model 7) and invite (GT 8-9, model 7) are both underscored. D&C 121 doctrine (GT 8, model 6) and invite (GT 7, model 5) are worse. The model doesn't recognize when doctrine or invitation IS the dominant mode of a scriptural passage. It may be that scripture — where there's no human "teacher" — confuses the model's TITSW framework, which is designed for evaluating a teacher's approach.

4. **teach_about_christ inflation on Bednar.** The model gave Bednar 8 (GT 5). Bednar names Christ as the enabling mechanism but doesn't teach ABOUT Christ — the teaching focus is agency and judgment. The model counts any Christ-mention as teaching about Christ, rather than distinguishing between "Christ is architecturally necessary" and "the teaching reveals Christ's person, titles, or character."

### Recommendations for v4 Prompt

1. **Scripture vs. talk distinction.** The TITSW framework evaluates a *teacher's approach*. Scripture doesn't have a human teacher — it IS the teaching. The model needs guidance: "For scripture, evaluate the text as a teaching instrument. For talks, evaluate the speaker's teaching approach."

2. **Love calibration for scripture.** The current instruction works for talks but not for scripture/revelation. Need: "When a passage *prescribes* love ('love unfeigned,' 'charity towards all men'), this is doctrinal content about love, not the author demonstrating love for the reader. Score doctrine, not love, unless the author enacts love toward specific people within the text."

3. **Spirit calibration needs teeth.** The current instruction describes what teaching BY the Spirit looks like but the model still scores high when the Spirit is *mentioned*. Need stronger negative examples: "D&C 121:46 promises the Holy Ghost as a companion. This is doctrine ABOUT the Spirit's future role. It is NOT the author teaching by the Spirit. Score 3-4 for doctrinal references to the Spirit, even when they are beautiful and theologically significant."

4. **Dominant-mode detection.** The model under-detects when a single principle IS the dominant mode. Need: "Before scoring, identify which 1-2 dimensions define this content. If the content is sustained doctrinal exposition from start to finish, doctrine should be 7+. If the entire piece functions as an escalating invitation to act, invite should be 7+. Score the dominant mode first, then evaluate the rest."

5. **teach_about_christ precision.** Need: "Mentioning Christ as the mechanism of salvation ('through Christ's atonement') is NOT the same as teaching about Christ — revealing His person, character, titles, or typological presence. A talk that says 'Christ makes this possible' but focuses on human agency is a 4-5 on teach_about_christ, not an 8."
