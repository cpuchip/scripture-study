# Teaching in the Savior's Way — Scoring Framework

This document defines what each TITSW principle looks like in practice, with score-level anchors that distinguish surface presence from defining quality.

The manual organizes Christlike teaching into two layers: **what** we teach (Meta-Principles A and B) and **how** we teach (Principles 1–4). The meta-principles frame every dimension — all four principles serve the goal of teaching about Christ and helping people come unto Him.

## Meta-Principle A: Teach About Jesus Christ

"All of these are branches of the same tree, for they all have one purpose: to help all people come unto Christ and be perfected in Him" (TITSW, Part 1). "All things are created and made to bear record of me" (Moses 6:63).

Three sub-dimensions: (1) emphasize Christ's example — "we don't just follow principles, we follow Jesus Christ"; (2) teach His titles, roles, and attributes — "go beyond what He said and did to who He is"; (3) look for symbols that testify of Him — parallels in prophets' lives, bread, water, light, the brass serpent, the tree of life.

**The key differentiator is depth of Christ-connection:**
- **Score 3:** Mentions Jesus Christ or quotes Him. Christ is named but not integrated into the teaching's structure.
- **Score 7:** Reveals how the topic IS about Christ even when Christ isn't named. Traces types, symbols, or theological connections to Christ's person, mission, or atonement. The teaching makes visible what was hidden — the way Alma 32's seed metaphor encodes the doctrine of receiving Christ into the heart, or the way a commandment reveals something about the Lawgiver's character.

## Meta-Principle B: Help Learners Come Unto Christ

"Nothing you do as a teacher will bless learners more than helping them know Heavenly Father and Jesus Christ and feel Their love" (TITSW, Part 1). The manual distinguishes knowing *about* Christ from *experiencing* His power: "Learning about the experiences of Daniel is incomplete if it doesn't inspire us to trust the Lord when we face our own figurative den of lions."

Three sub-dimensions: (1) help learners recognize the Lord's love, power, and mercy in their own lives — move from narrative to personal; (2) help strengthen their relationship with Heavenly Father and Christ — through covenants, prayer, repentance; (3) help them intentionally strive to be more like Christ — "becoming like Him happens only as we act in faith."

**The key differentiator is the gap between knowing and becoming:**
- **Score 3:** Mentions the importance of coming to Christ or following Him. Stays at the level of exhortation.
- **Score 7:** Actively closes the gap between knowing about Christ and experiencing His power. The teaching moves the listener from information to transformation — not just "Christ loves you" but "here is how to receive that love, and here is what changes when you do." Includes the covenant path or specific mechanisms of grace.

## Principle 1: Love Those You Teach

"Everything the Savior did throughout His earthly ministry was motivated by love" (TITSW, Part 2). Love in the TITSW framework is not sentiment — it is seeing as God sees and responding to what you see.

Five sub-dimensions: (1) see divine potential — Jesus saw a son of Abraham in Zacchaeus where others saw a corrupt publican; (2) know their circumstances — the Samaritan woman's thirst was more than physical, and Jesus connected her physical need to "living water"; (3) pray for them by name — "No one can conceive of the joy which filled our souls at the time we heard Him pray for us unto the Father" (3 Nephi 17:17); (4) create safety — Jesus ate with sinners, made the accused woman feel safe enough to repent; (5) express love — stayed longer with the Nephites because He saw their need (3 Nephi 17:5–6).

**The key differentiator is stated love vs. demonstrated love:**
- **Score 3:** States that God loves people, or that we should love one another. Love is declared but not enacted.
- **Score 7:** Demonstrates Christlike love through specificity — naming people's circumstances, seeing potential others miss, creating safety for the vulnerable, altering plans because someone needs something. The Samaritan woman pattern: knowing what the person actually thirsts for, not just announcing that living water exists.

## Principle 2: Teach by the Spirit

"The Holy Ghost is the true teacher. No mortal teacher, no matter how skilled or experienced, can replace His role" (TITSW, Part 2). The teacher's role is instrumental — making room for the Spirit to work, not replacing Him.

Five sub-dimensions: (1) prepare spiritually — Jesus treasured up "words of life" for the "very hour" He needed them (D&C 84:85); (2) respond to needs in the moment — sensing the woman who touched His clothes amid the crowd (Luke 8:41–48); (3) create space for the Spirit — "Whom say ye that I am?" invited Peter's witness to come from "my Father which is in heaven," not from flesh and blood (Matthew 16:13–17); (4) help seek and recognize revelation — "Did I not speak peace to your mind?" to Oliver (D&C 6:23); (5) bear testimony — Jesus's witness to Martha prompted her own: "I know that he shall rise again" (John 11:23–27).

**The key differentiator is teaching ABOUT the Spirit vs. teaching BY the Spirit:**
- **Score 3:** Mentions the Holy Ghost, quotes scriptures about the Spirit, or discusses revelation doctrinally. The Spirit is a topic.
- **Score 7:** The teaching itself becomes an instrument of the Spirit. It creates space for personal witness — pauses, questions that invite inner searching, vulnerability that opens hearts, responsiveness to what learners actually need rather than rigid adherence to a plan. The teacher's own testimony is direct and heartfelt, not formulaic.

## Principle 3: Teach the Doctrine

"Doctrine is eternal truth — found in the scriptures and the words of latter-day prophets — that shows us the way to become like our Father in Heaven and return to Him" (TITSW, Part 2). The Road to Emmaus is the archetype: "Did not our heart burn within us, while he talked with us by the way, and while he opened to us the scriptures?" (Luke 24:32).

Five sub-dimensions: (1) learn the doctrine yourself — personal depth precedes teaching power; (2) teach from the scriptures — not drifting from God's word; (3) help seek and understand truths — the Good Samaritan parable method, where Jesus guided the lawyer to answer his own question; (4) focus on conversion, not just information — the Pharisees were doctrinally correct about the Sabbath but "looking beyond the mark" (Jacob 4:14), missing that the Lawgiver Himself stood before them; (5) help find personal relevance — the lost sheep, lost coin, and prodigal son each met the Pharisees where they were.

**The key differentiator is opening scriptures vs. quoting scriptures:**
- **Score 3:** Cites scriptures or teaches doctrinal facts. The doctrine is present but functions as information.
- **Score 7:** Opens the scriptures — makes hearts burn. The doctrine connects to the listener's actual life, drives toward conversion rather than knowledge, and reveals the Savior behind the commandment or principle. Weaves scripture into the argument rather than appending proof-texts. Avoids the Pharisaic pattern of doctrinal correctness without spiritual power.

## Principle 4: Invite Diligent Learning

"'Come.' 'Come and see.' 'Come, follow me.' 'Go, and do thou likewise'" (TITSW, Part 2). Learning in the Savior's framework is not passive — "it's also changing, repenting, and progressing. ... Faith includes acting for ourselves, not simply being acted upon" (2 Nephi 2:26). "Our intent ought not to be 'What do I tell them?' Instead ... 'What can I invite them to do?'" (Elder Bednar).

Five sub-dimensions: (1) help take responsibility — the brother of Jared was given the problem of lighting the barges, not the solution; (2) encourage daily scripture study — "It is my voice which speaketh" (D&C 18:35–36); (3) invite preparation — the parable of the sower: even precious doctrine cannot change an unprepared heart; (4) encourage sharing — Enoch was slow of speech, but "I will give thee utterance" (Moses 6:31–32); (5) invite to live it — "Whosoever heareth these sayings of mine, and doeth them" (Matthew 7:24).

**The key differentiator is generic exhortation vs. specific, escalating invitation:**
- **Score 3:** Encourages people generally to do better, pray, or read scriptures. The invitation is present but vague.
- **Score 7:** Extends specific, agency-respecting invitations tied to the doctrine just taught. The pattern escalates: reflect → act → choose → commit. Peter walking on water is the archetype — a specific invitation ("Come"), requiring personal faith, resulting in personal experience. The invitation asks the listener to DO something concrete with what they just learned.

## Scoring Calibration

These definitions should help CALIBRATE scores, not inflate them. Most content will score in the 3–5 range on most dimensions. A score of 7+ means the content could serve as a teaching exemplar for that principle. Reserve 8–9 for content that is genuinely exceptional — content where the principle is not just present but defining.

The presence of a keyword (love, Spirit, doctrine, invitation) does NOT determine the score. What matters is whether the principle is enacted, not whether it is named.
