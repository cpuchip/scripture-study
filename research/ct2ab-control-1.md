# Bacteriopolis: Winogradsky Column Living Biology Exhibit

## 1. Story / Example

Every spring in rural Missouri, farm ponds stir to life. Cattle wade to the edge, grandkids toss rocks, and the mud at the bottom begins to bubble with an invisible workforce. A Winogradsky column takes that same pond mud, seals it in glass, and turns the hidden world into a slow-moving painting. The story begins with a jar of mud from a local stock pond—say, the one behind the courthouse or down the road from the fairgrounds. Mixed with shredded newspaper, a crushed eggshell for sulfur, and a pinch of garden lime, the mud looks like brown soup for the first week. Then a thin green film appears at the surface. By week four, a stripe of purple rises from the bottom, followed by rust-red and inky black layers. Visitors who farm or fish will recognize the sulfur smell of anaerobic mud, but they will not have seen it organized into color-coded neighborhoods. The column becomes a conversation piece between generations: the grandparent knows the smell of pond muck, the child sees a "rainbow in a jar," and both can point to the same band and ask, "What is living right there?"

## 2. Real-World Application

The same gradients that paint the column drive critical processes in rural Missouri and beyond. Anaerobic digesters on dairy operations and swine farms rely on the same sulfate-reducing and methanogenic bacteria to break down waste. Wastewater treatment lagoons—common in unsewered rural areas—depend on microbial sulfur and nitrogen cycling to remove contaminants before discharge. In the southeastern Missouri Lead Belt, acid mine drainage is mediated by sulfur-oxidizing bacteria that convert sulfide minerals to sulfuric acid; understanding their redox chemistry is essential for remediation. On the education side, the Exploratorium has published a "Science Snack" called Bacteriopolis that walks visitors through building a home column, treating the device as accessible public science rather than a specialized lab technique [Exploratorium Snacks](https://www.exploratorium.edu/snacks/bacteriopolis). NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory has also used Winogradsky columns in astrobiology outreach as an accessible model of how closed microbial ecosystems might function in space habitats, though this is an educational analogy rather than active life-support research [NASA JPL Astrobiology](https://astrobiology.nasa.gov/education/exhibit-assets/winogradsky-columns/). The column is not merely a classroom novelty; it is a miniature working model of anaerobic biogeochemistry that appears in agriculture, sanitation, mining remediation, and space-ecosystem education.

## 3. Museum Demo

**Physical Setup.** Two vertical glass columns (approximately 7–8 cm diameter, 30–45 cm tall) sit side-by-side in a ventilated, transparent acrylic display case that is *not* sealed—air must circulate. One column is enriched with egg yolk and cellulose (shredded newspaper); the other serves as a lower-nutrient control using only mud and sand. Both rest on a wooden stand painted black to hide plumbing. A full-spectrum LED gooseneck grow lamp on a 14-hour timer illuminates the columns from one side, while the back of the stand is covered with matte black poster board to create a dark backdrop that makes the color bands pop.

**What Visitors See.** Within two to four weeks, distinct color zones become visible against the glass. The surface water turns blue-green from cyanobacteria and algae. Just below, a rust-orange or pink layer indicates purple non-sulfur bacteria. Beneath that, purple sulfur bacteria form a magenta stripe, while green sulfur bacteria appear as an olive band. At the very bottom, sulfate-reducing bacteria produce black iron-sulfide sediment. Tiny bubbles of methane or carbon dioxide occasionally rise through the mud. A laminated cross-section diagram mounted beside the columns labels each zone.

**What Visitors Do.** Because the columns are live cultures with a strong odor and potential gas emissions, there is no direct contact. Interactivity is provided by:
- A toggle switch that turns on a backlight (LED strip behind the columns) so visitors can silhouette the bubble trails and density layers.
- A touchscreen or printed time-lapse photo series showing the column's progression from day 0 to week 8.
- A "predict the color" dial quiz that asks visitors to match metabolic functions (e.g., "eats hydrogen sulfide in the dark") to the colored band.

**What Visitors Hear/Touch.** The display is silent; a small printed notebook invites visitors to sketch what they see and compare drawings across weeks. Hand sanitizer is provided nearby as a courtesy.

## 4. Underlying Science

A Winogradsky column is an artificial redox tower. Oxygen diffuses downward from the surface water, while hydrogen sulfide diffuses upward from the anaerobic bottom. The two gradients create a continuous spectrum of ecological niches in a single tube.

At the very bottom, oxygen is absent. Anaerobic *Desulfovibrio* and related sulfate-reducing bacteria respire sulfate instead of oxygen, producing hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) that reacts with iron in the mud to form black iron sulfide. The University of Delaware's World Wide Winogradsky Project notes that an egg yolk amendment produces the darker coloration at depth: "areas of darker green, purple, and/or black coloring develop over time near the bottom—these colorings could be groups of certain anaerobic bacteria: green sulfur bacteria, purple sulfur bacteria, and sulfate-reducing bacteria, respectively" [Science Buddies / Univ. of Delaware](https://www.sciencebuddies.org/science-fair-projects/project-ideas/MicroBio_p026/microbiology/build-a-winogradsky-column). Green sulfur bacteria such as *Chlorobium* tolerate high H₂S and use light to fix carbon. Just above them, purple sulfur bacteria such as *Chromatium* also photosynthesize using sulfide, producing the distinctive magenta layer. Higher still, where oxygen is scarce but present, purple non-sulfur bacteria (*Rhodopseudomonas*, *Rhodospirillum*) generate a rust-colored zone. The surface is dominated by oxygenic cyanobacteria and algae that release oxygen, reinforcing the aerobic top layer and completing the chemical circuit.

The carbon cycle runs in parallel. Cellulose from paper ferments to organic acids, CO₂, and methane in the deep anoxic zones. Those acids feed the photoheterotrophs, while the CO₂ feeds the autotrophs. The column is therefore a closed sulfur-carbon loop visible to the naked eye.

**For the deeper reader:** The physics is fundamentally about electrochemical potential. The Biofilm Hypertextbook explains that "the column contains at least two steep gradients (oxygen and hydrogen sulfide)" that create "a continuous range of habitats selective for a variety of microorganisms" [Biofilm Hypertextbook](https://biofilmbook.hypertextbookshop.com/public_version/contents/appendices/appendix002/pages/page019.html). The depth at which purple sulfur bacteria appear corresponds to the intersection of the downward light gradient and the upward sulfide gradient—an interface that can be predicted by the Nernst equation for redox potential in sediments. In practice, the banding is messy and dynamic; the diagram is an idealization, and the real column fluctuates with temperature, light intensity, and the exhaustion of the original cellulose substrate.

## 5. History

Sergey Nikolayevich Winogradsky (born Sept. 1, 1856, Kiev, Russian Empire [now in Ukraine]—died Feb. 25, 1953, Brie-Comte-Robert, France) was a Russian microbiologist whose work established the physiology of soil bacteria. After studying at the University of St. Petersburg in 1881, he moved to Strasbourg, Germany, in 1885. According to Encyclopaedia Britannica, "In 1887 he established the specific physiology of sulfur bacteria," and "discovered (1889–90) the microbial agents responsible for nitrification," establishing the genera *Nitrosomonas* and *Nitrobacter* [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sergey-Nikolayevich-Winogradsky). He also discovered the anaerobic nitrogen-fixing bacterium *Clostridium pasteurianum*; the isolation was reported in his 1895 publications on anaerobic nitrogen fixation, building on work from 1893 onward [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sergey-Nikolayevich-Winogradsky). Forced out of Russia by the October Revolution of 1917, he resumed his career in 1922 at the Pasteur Institute in Paris, remaining until a second retirement in 1940.

The column itself is an outgrowth of Winogradsky's selective enrichment methods from the late 1880s. Rather than plating bacteria on agar, he modeled a natural pond cross-section in glass, using mud, cellulose, and sulfur sources to favor the organisms he wanted to study. The MiSAC educational guide notes that "the Winogradsky Column illustrates the wide diversity of different types of physiological activity that is found in the microbial world. It was devised in the 19th century by the Russian soil microbiologist Sergei Winogradsky (1856-1953)" [MiSAC](https://misac.org.uk/PDFs/MISAC-Activities-3-Winogradsky-Web-(4).pdf). The device was not patented or formally christened on a single date; it evolved as a laboratory practice throughout the 1880s and 1890s and was later named in his honor. Today it serves simultaneously as a teaching tool, an art installation, and a model ecosystem in microbiology research.

### Step-by-Step Assembly

1. **Collect sediment.** Gather mud and water from a local freshwater pond or slow-moving creek. The University of Delaware protocol specifies: "water—either from the place you got the mud or distilled (not tap!) water for soils" [Univ. of Delaware](https://sites.udel.edu/winogradsky/the-columns/build-a-column/). If tap water is the only option, dechlorinate it by leaving it open to air for several days; note that chloramines will not dissipate this way and may inhibit growth.

2. **Prepare the base mixture.** In a bucket, combine roughly 2 parts mud to 1 part plaster of Paris and 1 part calcium carbonate by volume. Mix in shredded paper and a crushed egg yolk. Add distilled (or pond) water until the consistency is "thick, sludge-like (similar to dense cake batter)" [Illinois ProjectMicrobe](https://publish.illinois.edu/projectmicrobe/files/2015/05/U9_L4_Resource_WinogradskyColumnProtocol.pdf). Check pH; aim for 7–8. Adjust with small amounts of baking soda or vinegar.

3. **Pack the columns.** Line the bottom of each glass cylinder with a thin layer of wet shredded paper to exclude air bubbles. Spoon in the mud mixture slowly, tamping to remove voids. Fill to within 5 cm of the rim. Add a water layer on top, leaving 2–3 cm of headspace.

4. **Cap loosely.** The Illinois protocol warns explicitly: "DO NOT tighten the lids! (Build up of gas can cause an explosion if the column is tightly covered.)" [Illinois ProjectMicrobe](https://publish.illinois.edu/projectmicrobe/files/2015/05/U9_L4_Resource_WinogradskyColumnProtocol.pdf). Use a loose cap, a cork with a vent hole, or plastic wrap with a slit cut in it so gases can escape. The Biofilm Hypertextbook agrees: "Be sure to [leave] a slit or [hole] in the plastic wrap or parafilm to permit gas [escape]" [Biofilm Hypertextbook](https://biofilmbook.hypertextbookshop.com/public_version/contents/appendices/appendix002/pages/page019.html).

5. **Light and placement.** Position columns under the LED lamp set to 14 hours on / 10 hours off. Place them "near a window or tungsten lamp in a well ventilated room. They can smell until they stabilize" [Biofilm Hypertextbook](https://biofilmbook.hypertextbookshop.com/public_version/contents/appendices/appendix002/pages/page019.html). Ensure the lamp does not heat the glass above room temperature.

6. **Observation timeline.** Growth should appear in 2–4 weeks and continue changing for 6–8 weeks before stabilizing. The MiSAC guide advises: "Examine weekly for a month or more; can then be maintained as a permanent museum specimen" [MiSAC](https://misac.org.uk/PDFs/MISAC-Activities-3-Winogradsky-Web-(4).pdf). Maintain by adding dechlorinated water periodically so a thin water layer always covers the sediment. The Illinois protocol confirms: "Once constructed, the Winogradsky columns can be kept for months to years... To maintain the columns and prevent them from drying out, periodically add dechlorinated water so there is a layer of water on top of the sediment mixture" [Illinois ProjectMicrobe](https://publish.illinois.edu/projectmicrobe/files/2015/05/U9_L4_Resource_WinogradskyColumnProtocol.pdf).

### Signage Suggestions

- **Primary panel:** Cross-section diagram showing color bands and their bacterial names.
- **Safety note:** "Live bacteria at work. Please do not shake or open. Smells like science."
- **Observation log:** "Draw what you see today. Come back in two weeks and compare."
- **Local connection:** "This mud came from [Named Local Pond]. The same organisms cycle sulfur in Missouri farm ponds and wastewater lagoons."

### Safety Considerations

- **Gas explosion:** Never seal the column airtight. Methane and hydrogen sulfide produced by anaerobic metabolism are flammable. The Lipson Lab at SDSU documented a sealed plastic Winogradsky tube that doubled in diameter and produced demonstrably flammable gas [Lipson Lab, SDSU](https://sci.sdsu.edu/lipsonlab/microbial-knot/). Venting is non-negotiable.
- **Hydrogen sulfide odor:** H₂S has a rotten-egg smell and can be nauseating in an enclosed gallery. The Lipson Lab reported that H₂S diffused through plastic tubing and forced removal of their art installation from the Oceanside Museum of Art: "We initially installed this at OMA, but the smell was starting to offend people... H₂S was diffusing through the walls of the plastic tubing. So we were screwed. Next time, use glass!" [Lipson Lab, SDSU](https://sci.sdsu.edu/lipsonlab/microbial-knot/). Use glass columns, not plastic, and ensure the display case is ventilated, not sealed.
- **Handling:** Wear gloves and eye protection when building or maintaining. The MiSAC guide notes: "Disposal: contents can be poured onto a garden. Wear eye protection and gloves. The contents have a putrid smell" [MiSAC](https://misac.org.uk/PDFs/MISAC-Activities-3-Winogradsky-Web-(4).pdf). The Illinois protocol adds: "Do not breathe in directly over an uncovered column" and recommends gloves when handling sediment [Illinois ProjectMicrobe](https://publish.illinois.edu/projectmicrobe/files/2015/05/U9_L4_Resource_WinogradskyColumnProtocol.pdf).
- **Pathogen caution:** The vast majority of environmental microbes are non-pathogenic, but the Biofilm Hypertextbook warns: "There is no easy way to determine if the mud is contaminated with sewage, farm waste, heavy metals or other toxic materials. Wear latex or vinyl gloves when mixing materials and packing the column" [Biofilm Hypertextbook](https://biofilmbook.hypertextbookshop.com/public_version/contents/appendices/appendix002/pages/page019.html). Avoid collecting near septic drainfields or known industrial runoff.

### Known Failure Modes

1. **Gas-blowout or explosion from over-tightened lids.** Remediation: loose cap or vented seal only; inspect weekly.
2. **Odor permeation through plastic walls.** Remediation: use borosilicate or soda-lime glass, not acrylic or polycarbonate, for the column itself.
3. **Drying out.** Remediation: top off with dechlorinated/distilled water every 1–2 weeks.
4. **Overheating from lamp.** Remediation: LED only; keep lamp >30 cm from column surface; monitor temperature.
5. **No growth.** Common causes: chlorinated water, pH too far from neutral, or mud collected in winter dormancy. Remediation: use pond water, verify pH 7–8, collect in late summer/early fall when microbial biomass is highest [Illinois ProjectMicrobe](https://publish.illinois.edu/projectmicrobe/files/2015/05/U9_L4_Resource_WinogradskyColumnProtocol.pdf).
6. **End-of-life decay.** After 1–3 years, substrate exhaustion or algal overgrowth may collapse the banding. Dispose of contents in an outdoor compost bin or garden bed, wearing gloves and eye protection. Replace with a fresh column built from the same recipe.
