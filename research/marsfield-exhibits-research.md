# Low-Cost, Immersive, Rotating STEM Exhibits for the Mars-Field Science Center

**Binding question:** How can a new science center showcase the latest physics, STEM, and AI advancements through simple, low-cost, immersive exhibits that are easy to build and rotate—keeping visitors returning without the budget of a major metropolitan institution?

---

## Headlines

### 1. Modular architecture is the non-negotiable foundation
Every credible source—vendors, designers, and industry observers—agrees that rotating exhibits cheaply requires designing for swapability from day one. [Flexhibit](https://flexhibit.com/) sells a portable "FlexWall" system whose core claim is that exhibits "can be swapped out in hours for NEW exhibits" rather than weeks. [CoMotion Exhibits](https://comotionblog.blogspot.com/2025/04/modular-exhibits-by-comotion-exhibits.html) builds on the same principle: "engineered to break down quickly and fit into standard transport cases," minimizing setup time for organizations that rotate regularly. [Canopy Creative](https://www.canopycreative.design/all-industries/education-and-science-centres) reinforces the operational side: "steel frames, replaceable panels and off-the-shelf parts keep exhibits robust," while "modular exhibits and shared components reduce long-term cost."

The implication for Mars-field: whichever wall or framing system you choose, it must be a standard substrate that future exhibits plug into. A custom-built beautiful display that can't be unbolted is a future liability.

### 2. AI is the single biggest literacy gap—and the biggest opportunity
The [Association of Science and Technology Centers](https://www.astc.org/ai/) reports that "nearly three-quarters of Americans say it is extremely or very important for people to understand what AI is," while roughly 60% want more control over how AI is used in their lives. ASTC frames science centers as "well-positioned to help bridge this gap" because they offer trusted spaces where the public can both understand AI and shape its future.

[Futurist Thomas Frey](https://www.impactlab.com/2025/11/12/the-cultural-infrastructure-gap-why-we-need-museums-for-the-ai-age/) sharpens the point: "When electricity transformed civilization in the late 1800s, we built science museums to help people understand it... Now we're living through changes more rapid and profound than anything in history—AI, robotics, autonomous systems, quantum computing, synthetic biology. And we have almost no cultural institutions helping people understand them."

This is a gap you can own. A regional science center that makes AI tactile—training a simple model, watching it fail, experiencing facial recognition or generative art—fills a void that even major cities have left open.

### 3. Cutting-edge physics can be demonstrated for under $100 with commodity parts
Two independent open-source projects prove that "low cost" and "latest advancements" are not mutually exclusive.

[DIYnamics](https://diynamics.github.io/blog/technics-table-2020.html), out of Columbia University's Lamont-Doherty Earth Observatory, documents the "Technics Table": an $80 rotating tank platform built from LEGO Technics pieces and a kitchen turntable. It demonstrates fluid dynamics, atmospheric vortices, and planetary rotation at outreach events. Per their field report: "The Technics Table can be purchased in its entirety for $80... Prior inconveniences with the LEGO Table, such as the arm falling off or the LEGO assembly shifting, have abated to non-issues."

[BoxQubit](https://boxqubit.co.uk/teaching-quantum-with-lego-challenges-classroom-competition-) maps quantum computing onto LEGO bricks and 3D-printed connectors. A basic kit per three teams runs on "150–300 generic LEGO bricks and plates" plus printable cardstock flags. Add a sub-$250 entry-level 3D printer for latch and rotor connectors, and teams physically act out qubit states, Hadamard gates, and entanglement. A pilot with a Year 12 physics class reported that "95% of students reported they 'understood entanglement better' after doing a Bell-pair challenge"—though this is self-reported and unaudited.

These are not stripped-down demos. They are rigorous enough for university open houses and quantum-classroom competitions. Mars-field can deploy them as permanent maker stations or seasonal challenges.

### 4. Traveling vendor exhibits let a small center compete immediately—if you know the licensing math
The [Science Center of Iowa](https://innovationia.com/2026/02/19/new-science-center-exhibit-invites-visitors-to-explore-how-ai-works-in-everyday-life/) is currently hosting "Artificial Intelligence: Your Mind & The Machine," created by the Relayer Group. The exhibit covers generative AI, machine translation, self-driving cars, and large language models through hands-on experiences. Per the reporting, it is "one of the first major museum exhibitions to focus on the real-world relevance of AI."

This is competitive intelligence: peer regional institutions are already leasing AI-focused traveling exhibitions. The open question is cost. None of the sources provide licensing fees for traveling shows. Flexhibit and CoMotion publish product philosophies but not price lists. For Mars-field, the strategic move is to treat traveling exhibits as a short-term gap-filler while building a library of low-cost, in-house modules that plug into the same modular wall system.

### 5. "Immersive" does not require projection domes—it requires interaction
ASTC's [framework for AI public engagement](https://www.astc.org/ai/) lists "create opportunities for exploration and hands-on learning about AI" as Role A1—the starting point for every science center. Frey argues that the Exploratorium's revolution was making science about "doing, not just looking." Visitors didn't read about optics; they manipulated light and shadow.

The common thread across the credible sources is that immersion comes from physical manipulation, not capital-intensive AV. The $80 rotating tank is immersive because the visitor sets the spin rate and drops dye into water. The LEGO quantum challenge is immersive because teams race to entangle. Even the Relayer Group AI exhibit relies on "facial recognition technology," "AI-generated art," and direct comparison of machine learning to the human brain.

For a low-cost center, this is liberating. Immersion is a design problem, not a budget problem.

## Notable

- **ASTC's 13-role framework** gives a practical roadmap for AI engagement, from hands-on literacy (Group A) to public input into AI governance (Group B) to creative expression (Group D). It is designed as a "living document" that evolves as the field changes. If Mars-field commits to AI as a signature topic, this framework is a ready-made strategic plan.
- **Durability through commodity parts.** Canopy Creative explicitly designs with "replaceable components and commodity parts to withstand high footfall while remaining cost effective over time." This matters because school traffic destroys bespoke exhibits. Using off-the-shelf motors, standard steel framing, and printable replacement panels changes the maintenance model from "call the fabricator" to "swap the part from inventory."
- **Sustainability by reuse.** CoMotion notes that "modular components reduce waste by allowing reuse across multiple exhibitions." For a center with a rotating mission, this lowers the marginal cost of each new exhibition.
- **Competition format drives retention.** The BoxQubit pilot used timed team challenges (5–25 minute rounds) with scoring rubrics. The physical-to-digital bridge—mapping the LEGO circuit to a Qiskit simulator afterward—creates a "powerful loop: physical intuition → simulation → back to physical debugging." This format is directly transferable to public exhibit design.

## Skeptical Takes

- **Vendor affordability claims are unverified.** Flexhibit says its system enables switching "in a highly affordable manner," and CoMotion calls modular exhibits "an excellent choice for organizations that rotate exhibits regularly." Neither provides itemized cost data or independent case studies. "Affordable" is a marketing claim until you have a quote in hand.
- **Self-reported educational outcomes should be treated as directional, not proof.** BoxQubit's claim that 95% of students understood entanglement better after the Bell-pair challenge is promising but unaudited, from a single mixed-ability Year 12 class, with no control group. It justifies a pilot, not a curriculum overhaul.
- **Frey's essay is framing, not engineering.** The Impact Lab piece is valuable for articulating the cultural infrastructure gap, but it is an opinion essay by a futurist, not a facilities plan. The specific venue concepts (Robotics Arena, Unicorn Hall of Fame, Future Careers Discovery Center) are aspirational sketches. Do not budget around them.
- **Traveling exhibits carry hidden costs.** Innovation Iowa's article on the Relayer Group show describes the *content* but does not mention shipping, insurance, floor space requirements, or per-square-foot licensing fees. A "traveling" exhibit that requires three weeks of install and a dedicated climate-controlled bay may cost more in labor than a locally built module.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the actual all-in cost of a FlexWall or equivalent modular framing system per linear foot, including mounting hardware?** The sources describe the architecture but do not publish price lists. A direct quote request is needed before the modular strategy can be budgeted.
2. **What do traveling AI exhibit licenses actually cost for a regional institution?** The Science Center of Iowa's deployment suggests the product exists, but the financial model (flat fee? per-square-foot? revenue share?) is undocumented in the gathered sources.
3. **What is the break-even math between building in-house (DIYnamics/BoxQubit style) and renting traveling modules?** If a $80 rotating tank lasts three years with minor parts replacement, it is almost certainly cheaper than any traveling show. But for high-production topics like self-driving cars or large language models, the capital cost of an in-house build may exceed a short-term rental. The crossover point depends on attendance, ticket price, and exhibit lifespan—none of which the sources address.
4. **Which topic beyond AI and quantum has the largest exhibit gap?** Frey lists robotics, autonomous systems, and synthetic biology alongside AI and quantum. Of these, which has accessible, safe, low-cost hands-on demonstrations that can survive unsupervised public interaction? Robotics (small programmable rovers, maze-running competitions) looks immediately viable; synthetic biology does not.
5. **How much educator staffing is required for interactive exhibits to work?** Canopy Creative mentions "timed educator rotations" as part of their operational model. The DIYnamics table and BoxQubit challenges both assume a facilitator. If Mars-field aims for low recurring cost, the exhibit design must either be robust enough for unguided use or the staffing model must be explicit.
