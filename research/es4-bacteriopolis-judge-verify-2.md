# Bacteriopolis: Winogradsky Column Living Biology Exhibit Brief

## Story / Example

On a Saturday in March, the Morrow kids from Callaway County wade into the edge of their grandfather’s farm pond in central Missouri, boots sinking into black mud that smells like rotten eggs. Their grandmother tells them to wash up before lunch, wrinkling her nose at the sulfur stink. What neither the kids nor most visitors to a science center realize is that this smelly mud is a crowded metropolis. In every spoonful, billions of microbes are already at work—breaking down dead leaves, pulling nitrogen from the air, and cycling sulfur through chemical forms that plants and animals eventually need. The pond is alive underground.

*Bacteriopolis* takes that same Missouri pond mud, packs it into a clear tower, and makes the invisible city visible. Over the next two months, the mud turns into stacked ribbons of green, purple, orange, and black. Each band is a neighborhood of bacteria that has found its exact living conditions: its preferred oxygen level, its favorite light wavelength, its source of food. The exhibit does not ask visitors to imagine microbiology from a textbook. It asks them to watch a living ecosystem build itself from the bottom up, using nothing more than mud, newspaper, an egg, and light.

## Real-World Application

Winogradsky columns are not just classroom curiosities. The same metabolic processes on display—sulfate reduction, nitrogen fixation, and anoxygenic photosynthesis—operate in full scale across Missouri’s landscape and economy.

**Soil and agriculture.** The sulfate-reducing and sulfur-oxidizing bacteria that create the black and purple layers in the column are cousins of the microbes that manage sulfur availability in farm soils. Sulfur is an essential plant nutrient, and its cycling in saturated soils (like those in Missouri’s river bottomlands) affects crop yield and drainage tile chemistry. The column makes that cycling visible.

**Wastewater treatment.** Anaerobic digesters at municipal wastewater plants use the same bacterial guilds—fermenters, sulfate reducers, and methanogens—to break down organic sludge in the absence of oxygen. A visitor looking at the black, bubbling bottom of the column is seeing a miniature version of what happens in an industrial digester tank.

**Human health.** Some biology educators use Winogradsky columns as an analog for the human gut microbiome, where different bacterial species sort themselves into micro-environments along the digestive tract based on oxygen, pH, and nutrient gradients. The parallel is pedagogically useful: just as the column layers are interdependent, the gut community processes food molecules that human cells cannot digest alone.

**Museum precedent.** Published assessments of museum microbial ecosystems in public settings are limited. One review cited in preliminary materials (Baxter, *Journal of Oceanology and Limnology*, 2023 — full citation and URL not verified in this draft) suggests such displays can remain stable for years while teaching visitors about artificial versus natural ecosystems, but independent confirmation should be sought before finalizing signage claims. The takeaway for a rural Missouri center is that the concept is field-tested in classrooms; long-term museum-floor protocols are less documented.

## Museum Demo

**What visitors see.** The centerpiece is a single clear acrylic cylinder roughly 4 inches in diameter and 20–24 inches tall, mounted vertically on a low pedestal so the top sits at about the eye level of an average eight-year-old. The cylinder is packed with dark pond mud, with an inch or two of water above the sediment surface. At launch the mud is uniformly brown-gray. By week four it begins to show the first green streaks on the light-facing side. By week eight it displays distinct horizontal bands: an olive-green top layer, a purple-pink middle layer, and a black bottom layer, with occasional orange flecks where iron-oxidizers colonize the boundary zones.

**Controls and interactivity.** Because the column must not be shaken or opened by the public, the physical interaction is limited to observation. Next to the main column, three standard 2-liter soda-bottle columns sit on a shelf, each labeled with a date: “Week 2,” “Week 4,” and “Week 6.” These timeline bottles were started from the same mud batch on staggered dates so that even a visitor who walks in on opening day can see the full progression without waiting two months. A gooseneck LED lamp is clamped to the back of the display, positioned roughly 12 inches from the column surface and timed to 14 hours on / 10 hours off. Visitors can see the timer display but cannot adjust it.

**What visitors hear and read.** A small laminated card explains the gas-venting procedure that staff perform every few days. A larger back-panel graphic shows a cross-section of the column with the color bands mapped to microbe types. There is no audio component; the exhibit is quiet, which suits the slow, observational nature of the biology. Visitors are instructed by signage to look but not touch, because the ecosystem is sensitive to physical disturbance and because the column contains hydrogen sulfide gas.

**Accessibility.** The pedestal is open underneath so wheelchair users can pull up close. Text panels use a sans-serif font at 18 pt minimum. The color-band diagram does not rely on color alone; each layer is also labeled with a texture pattern (dots, stripes, solid) for visitors with color-vision differences.

## Underlying Science

A Winogradsky column is an artificial ecosystem governed by two gradients: oxygen, which is highest at the top and depleted toward the bottom, and light, which penetrates only the upper few centimeters of mud. The third variable is chemistry: shredded newspaper provides cellulose (carbon), and egg yolk provides sulfur and additional carbon. Bacteria sort themselves into metabolic niches along these gradients exactly as they do in a natural pond sediment.

At the very top, oxygen is abundant. Cyanobacteria and algae perform oxygenic photosynthesis—using light, carbon dioxide, and water to make sugars, releasing oxygen as a byproduct. Their growth is often the first visible change, appearing as a green surface film or side streaks.

Beneath them, oxygen has been consumed by respiration. Here live the purple non-sulfur bacteria, which use light and organic carbon but do not produce oxygen. Lower still, where both light and sulfide are present but oxygen is absent, purple sulfur bacteria and green sulfur bacteria thrive. These microbes perform anoxygenic photosynthesis: they use hydrogen sulfide instead of water as an electron donor, producing elemental sulfur or sulfate rather than oxygen. Their pigments—bacteriochlorophylls and carotenoids—give the column its purple, pink, and orange bands.

At the bottom, no light reaches and no oxygen remains. Sulfate-reducing bacteria metabolize the organic carbon from the newspaper and the sulfate from the egg yolk, releasing hydrogen sulfide—the “rotten egg” gas that diffuses upward to feed the sulfur bacteria above them. This bottom layer appears black because of iron sulfide precipitation and dense microbial biomass.

The result is a self-sustaining nutrient loop: decomposers at the bottom generate sulfide; photosynthesizers in the middle consume it; and the whole community is held together by diffusion and light. The column is not sealed because the bacteria produce gases—hydrogen sulfide, carbon dioxide, methane—that must vent to the atmosphere. If the top is sealed, pressure builds until something fails.

**For the deeper reader.** The stratification is fundamentally about redox chemistry. In the top centimeters, oxygen serves as the terminal electron acceptor for respiration, yielding the most energy per mole of glucose. As oxygen diffuses downward and is consumed, the redox potential drops. Once oxygen is exhausted, microbes switch to alternative electron acceptors in order of energy yield: nitrate, then oxidized manganese and iron, then sulfate. The sulfate reducers at the bottom are living at the thermodynamic edge, extracting far less energy per reaction than their aerobic cousins, which is why they grow more slowly and dominate only where competition is absent. The colored bands are therefore a direct visual map of chemical potential energy.

## History

Sergei Winogradsky (also spelled Sergey Nikolayevich Winogradsky; 1856–1953) is widely regarded as the founder of microbial ecology. He demonstrated that certain bacteria could derive energy by oxidizing hydrogen sulfide to sulfur and then to sulfuric acid, establishing the concept of chemosynthesis—work that challenged the assumption that all life depended directly on photosynthesis. According to Britannica, he showed that “the colourless form of these bacteria can obtain energy by oxidizing hydrogen sulfide to sulfur and then to sulfuric acid in the absence of light” [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sergey-Nikolayevich-Winogradsky).

Winogradsky’s insistence on studying microbes in environmental context, rather than in pure culture alone, led him to develop the column method in the late 1880s. By placing mud in a transparent vessel, enriching it with defined nutrients such as cellulose and sulfate, and illuminating it, he could cultivate organisms that depended on chemical gradients and interspecies cooperation—organisms that would not grow on standard petri dishes. He also identified the microbial agents of nitrification, establishing the genera *Nitrosomonas* and *Nitrobacter*.

The transition from laboratory tool to museum exhibit is largely credited to the Exploratorium in San Francisco. Their “Bacterial Terrarium” activity, published in the Exploratorium Science Wire (undated, circa 1990s–2000s), distilled Winogradsky’s classical protocol into a public-friendly recipe using soda bottles, newspaper, eggs, and a light source. That document remains a foundational reference repeated in many later classroom and museum adaptations.

### Step-by-Step Assembly

1. **Collect and prep the inoculum.** Gather mud and water from a local Missouri pond, slow creek, or lake edge. Remove sticks, rocks, and debris. If you must store the mud before assembly, keep it immersed in water in a cool, dark place and do not seal the container. Standard classroom protocols recommend using the sediment within a week of collection.

2. **Mix the base slurry.** In a large bucket or bowl, combine roughly 6 cups of mud with pond or dechlorinated tap water until the consistency resembles thick heavy cream or a dense milkshake. Exploratorium-derived protocols commonly describe this texture as similar to heavy cream.

3. **Enrich with nutrients.** Stir in shredded newspaper (cellulose carbon source) and raw egg yolk (sulfur and protein source). For the large acrylic column, use roughly ½ cup of shredded newspaper and one egg yolk; scale down proportionally for the 2-liter timeline bottles.

4. **Pack the primary column.** Apply aquarium-safe silicone to the bottom acrylic disc and seat the acrylic tube vertically. Allow the sealant to cure fully (usually 24 hours). Once cured, spoon the mud mixture into the tube in small increments, gently tapping the tube against the workbench every few spoonfuls to dislodge air pockets. Fill to about 2 inches below the top rim.

5. **Add the water cap.** Pour dechlorinated water over the mud until the water layer sits about 1 inch below the top. This prevents the surface from drying and provides a diffusion barrier.

6. **Top the column loosely.** Place the loose acrylic cap or sleeve on top. Do not seal it airtight. Classroom protocols from the University of Illinois Project MICROBE and Illinois Agriculture in the Classroom emphatically warn against sealing the container airtight, because bacterial metabolism produces gases that can generate dangerous pressure. If the vessel is sealed, gas buildup can rupture it.

7. **Set the light cycle.** Position the LED lamp roughly 12 inches from the column surface. Plug the lamp into the digital timer and set it to 14 hours on, 10 hours off. The original Exploratorium specification called for a 40-watt incandescent bulb; a 9W full-spectrum LED at roughly the same distance provides similar photosynthetically active radiation without the heat.

8. **Build the timeline bottles.** Repeat steps 2–4 using the 2-liter soda bottles (tops cut off). Cover each with plastic wrap secured by a rubber band. Start these on a staggered schedule: Bottle A 6 weeks before opening day, Bottle B 4 weeks before, Bottle C 2 weeks before.

9. **Place on drip tray.** Set the main column and timeline bottles on the tray. Some water and gas bubbles may escape during the first week as the mud settles.

### Maintenance Protocol

- **Venting:** Every few days, briefly remove the top cap for a few seconds to release accumulated gases. Standard guides note that unchecked gas pressure can blow the top off the container.
- **Hydration:** If the water layer on top drops or the surface mud begins to crack, add a small amount of dechlorinated water.
- **Observation:** Check the column daily for the first two weeks, then every few days. Document with weekly photographs from the same angle.
- **Expected timeline:** Published classroom guides suggest a development window of roughly six to ten weeks before distinct color layers appear. HHMI BioInteractive and Science Friday classroom protocols both describe windows in this range, though local temperature and light conditions may shift the timeline.

### Signage Suggestions

- **“Do Not Touch — Living Ecosystem Under Construction”** (mounted at child height on the pedestal).
- **“Why Does It Smell?”** A brief panel explaining that hydrogen sulfide is a natural byproduct of anaerobic bacteria in the bottom layer, analogous to the sulfur smell of a stagnant farm pond.
- **“Meet the Neighbors”** A vertical diagram matching the visible color bands to microbe types (cyanobacteria = green top; purple sulfur bacteria = pink middle; sulfate reducers = black bottom).
- **“Timeline Prediction”** A card next to each 2-liter bottle stating its age and what changes are visible, inviting visitors to predict what the main column will look like in two, four, or six weeks.

### Safety Considerations

- **Explosion risk from gas pressure.** This is the single most important hazard. The top must never be glued, screwed, or snapped into an airtight seal.
- **Hydrogen sulfide.** The gas is produced in small quantities and at low concentrations in a vented column, but staff should not inhale directly over an open column. Maintain normal room ventilation.
- **Pathogen precaution.** Most environmental soil and pond bacteria are non-pathogenic and cannot grow in the human body. Nevertheless, staff should wear nitrile gloves when handling mud or performing maintenance, and visitors should be prevented from opening the column.
- **Material safety.** Use aquarium-safe silicone only; general household adhesives may leach toxins into the water column.

### Known Failure Modes

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Remedy |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| No color after 8 weeks | Mud inoculum too poor; insufficient light; temperature too cold (< 65 °F) | Collect mud from a more organic, odorous source; verify lamp output and timer; move to warmer location |
| Fuzzy white or green growth on water surface | Fungi or aerobic contaminants on exposed organics | Remove floating debris; ensure all newspaper is buried in mud |
| Strong rotten-egg smell with no color | Sulfate reducers active but photosynthetic bacteria not colonizing | Increase light intensity or duration; check that column is not in total darkness |
| Cracked, dried mud surface | Evaporation exceeding water replacement | Add dechlorinated water to restore surface layer |
| Column pushes off top or bulges | Inadequate venting; gas pressure buildup | Vent more frequently; verify top is not sealed |
| Complete blackening with no banding | All oxygen consumed too fast; excessive egg yolk | Reduce sulfur input in next batch; ensure light reaches upper layers |

### End-of-Life and Refresh

A healthy column can last months to years with periodic water addition, but the color bands will eventually fade as nutrients are exhausted or as one group dominates. When the exhibit is ready for refresh, dispose of the mud in a trash bin or return it to the original pond source. Sanitize the acrylic tube with a dilute bleach solution, rinse thoroughly, and rebuild with fresh mud. Because the budget is low, scheduling a rebuild every 4–6 months is sustainable and also provides an opportunity for a public “mud packing” demonstration day.
