# U.S. Federal Power Expansion and Constitutional Limits, 1900–1940

**Binding question:** How did the constitutional structure of enumerated powers, state sovereignty, and limited federal authority shift during the Progressive and New Deal eras, and what are the competing scholarly interpretations of that shift?

---

## Headlines

**1913 removed two structural constraints on federal power.** The 16th Amendment (federal income tax, ratified February 3, 1913) and the 17th Amendment (direct election of senators, ratified April 8, 1913) were both certified within fourteen months of each other. The 16th Amendment eliminated the constitutional requirement that direct taxes be apportioned among the states according to census; the 17th Amendment eliminated state legislatures as the intermediary body for selecting senators, transferring that power directly to statewide electorates.

**The Supreme Court resisted New Deal expansion, then reversed.** In *A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States* (1935), a unanimous Court struck down the National Industrial Recovery Act as an unconstitutional delegation of legislative power. Less than two years later, in *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.* (1937), the Court upheld the Wagner Act and adopted a broad reading of the Commerce Clause that permitted federal regulation of intrastate labor relations.

**The administrative state was formally blueprinted in 1937.** The President's Committee on Administrative Management (the Brownlow Committee) submitted its report in January 1937, describing the executive branch as an unplanned accretion and urging sweeping reorganization under strengthened presidential control—an institutional inflection point for modern administrative governance.

**Two incompatible narratives frame the same doctrinal shift.** Originalist scholars, notably Randy E. Barnett, describe the New Deal as having "eviscerated altogether" the Founding-era Enumerated Powers Federalism. Mainstream progressive scholars counter that the pre-1937 *Lochner* era protected powerful economic interests under the guise of constitutional principle, and that the post-1937 settlement properly restored deference to legislative majorities.

## Notable Findings

### The 16th and 17th Amendments as structural pivots

Both amendments were products of Progressive-era reform but carried distinct federalism implications.

The 16th Amendment was passed by Congress on **July 2, 1909**, ratified on **February 3, 1913**, and certified by Secretary of State Philander C. Knox on **February 25, 1913**. Its text reads:

> "The Congress shall have power to lay and collect taxes on incomes, from whatever source derived, without apportionment among the several States, and without regard to any census or enumeration."

[16th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution: Federal Income Tax (1913)](https://www.archives.gov/milestone-documents/16th-amendment)

The National Archives milestone page notes that the amendment was proposed during a period when the Supreme Court had struck down a federal income tax in *Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust Co.* (1895), making a constitutional amendment the only available path for Congress to levy such taxes.

The 17th Amendment was passed by Congress on **May 13, 1912**, and ratified on **April 8, 1913**. Its operative text states:

> "The Senate of the United States shall be composed of two Senators from each State, elected by the people thereof, for six years; and each Senator shall have one vote."

[17th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution: Direct Election of U.S. Senators (1913)](https://www.archives.gov/milestone-documents/17th-amendment)

Prior to ratification, state legislative deadlocks could leave Senate vacancies unfilled for extended periods, and the 1912 investigation into the election of Illinois Senator William Lorimer exposed bribery that intensified public demand for direct election.

### The *Lochner* era and judicial resistance to economic regulation

*Lochner v. New York*, decided **April 17, 1905**, struck down a New York maximum-hours law for bakers. The syllabus summarized the holding:

> "The general right to make a contract in relation to his business is part of the liberty protected by the Fourteenth Amendment, and this includes the right to purchase and sell labor, except as controlled by the State in the legitimate…"

[*Lochner v. New York*, 198 U.S. 45 (1905)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/198/45/)

Whether *Lochner* represented principled constitutionalism or judicial favoritism toward capital is the central interpretive dispute. What is undisputed is that the decision epitomized an era in which the Court routinely evaluated the substance of economic legislation against a theory of protected liberty of contract.

### The pre-1937 judicial wall

In *A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States*, decided **May 27, 1935**, the Court unanimously invalidated the National Industrial Recovery Act's code-making provisions. The syllabus quoted the Court's direct holding:

> "Extraordinary conditions, such as an economic crisis, may call for extraordinary remedies, but they cannot create or enlarge constitutional power. P. 295 U. S. 528."

[*A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States*, 295 U.S. 495 (1935)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/295/495/)

This decision represented the high-water mark of judicial resistance to New Deal administrative expansion, rejecting both the delegation of legislative power to the President and an expansive Commerce Clause reading.

### The 1937 pivot

*NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.*, decided **April 12, 1937**, upheld the National Labor Relations Act and affirmed federal authority to regulate labor relations that had substantial effects on interstate commerce. The syllabus stated:

> "The distinction between what is national and what is local in the activities of commerce is vital to the maintenance of our federal form of government. P. 301 U. S. 29."

[*NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.*, 301 U.S. 1 (1937)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/301/1/)

The decision is widely cited as the doctrinal pivot of the "switch in time that saved nine" — a phrase widely attributed to journalists and commentators of the period rather than appearing in any Court opinion — after which the Court ceased rigorous review of federal economic regulation under the Commerce Clause.

### Institutionalizing the administrative state

In **January 1937**, the President's Committee on Administrative Management (Brownlow Committee) submitted its report to President Roosevelt. The Committee described the existing executive branch in striking terms:

> "The executive branch of the government of the United States has thus grown up without plan or design like the barns, shacks, silos, tool sheds, and garages of an old farm."

And it framed its central argument as follows:

> "The forward march of American democracy at this point of our history depends more upon effective management than upon any other single factor."

[Report of the President's Committee on Administrative Management](https://teachingamericanhistory.org/document/report-of-the-presidents-committee-on-administrative-management/)

The report recommended consolidating agencies under twelve major departments, strengthening the Bureau of the Budget, and installing a small cadre of White House executive assistants to manage the flow of information and decision—recommendations that shaped the modern Executive Office of the President.

## Skeptical Takes: Competing Interpretations

### The originalist/federalist "loss of liberty" view

Randy E. Barnett, in "Three Federalisms" (2008), argues that the constitutional structure of the Founding era was progressively dismantled. He contends that "while Fundamental Rights Federalism significantly qualified Enumerated Powers Federalism, it was not until the New Deal's expansion of federal power that Enumerated Powers Federalism was eviscerated altogether." Barnett further characterizes later judicial attempts to revive state sovereignty as an "ahistorical Affirmative State Sovereignty Federalism that was both under- and over-inclusive of the role of federalism that is warranted by the original meaning of the Constitution as amended."

[Randy E. Barnett, "Three Federalisms," 39 Loy. U. Chi. L. J. 285 (2008)](https://lawecommons.luc.edu/luclj/vol39/iss2/2/)

Under this reading, the 16th Amendment enabled limitless federal fiscal extraction; the 17th Amendment severed the Senate's structural dependence on state governments; the 1937 Commerce Clause pivot eliminated judicial enforcement of enumerated powers; and the Brownlow reforms completed the centralization of administrative control in the presidency.

### The mainstream/progressive counter-view

A 2017 critique in *Constitutional Commentary* rebuts Barnett's narrative, defending the post-New Deal constitutional settlement. The author challenges Barnett's portrayal of the statutes struck down during the *Lochner* era:

> "Barnett portrays the statutes at issue in many of the classic Fourteenth Amendment cases—including *Lochner*, *Carolene Products*, and *Lee Optical*—as illustrative of regulations actually designed to protect the economic interests of a powerful faction at the expense of a weaker minority rather than to advance any public interest in health or safety."

The article defends the modern regime of deferential rational-basis review, noting:

> "A statute is not necessarily irrational or contrary to the public interest because it would have been reasonable to do more."

And it identifies Barnett's proposed change as a radical departure:

> "Barnett proposes to alter the current regime of highly deferential judicial review of social and economic legislation in two respects. He would both put more bite into rationality review and reverse the presumption of constitutionality."

["Countering the Majoritarian Difficulty," *Constitutional Commentary* (2017)](https://constitutionalcommentary.lib.umn.edu/article/countering-the-majoritarian-difficulty/)

From this perspective, the pre-1937 Court was not enforcing neutral constitutional limits but rather substituting its own economic preferences for democratic judgment. The 1937 shift is therefore viewed not as a constitutional rupture but as a restoration of appropriate judicial restraint.

## Open Questions

- **Did the 17th Amendment materially alter Senate behavior?** The amendment eliminated state legislative intermediation, but empirical evidence on whether it caused the Senate to become systematically more "national" in orientation—or merely formalized trends already underway—remains contested in the historical literature.

- **Is the administrative state a constitutional departure or an inevitable adaptation?** The Brownlow Committee treated executive reorganization as pragmatic modernization, but critics argue that the proliferation of independent agencies and delegations of legislative power created a "headless fourth branch" not contemplated by the Constitution's text.

- **What remains of enumerated powers after 1937?** *Jones & Laughlin* retained the *language* of a national/local distinction, but subsequent doctrine (especially post-*Wickard* and post-*Raich*) has raised the question of whether any activity can still be classified as purely local and beyond federal commerce power.

- **Was the *Lochner* Court principled or partisan?** The mainstream critique treats *Lochner* as judicial protection of capital; originalists like Barnett treat the *Lochner* era as the last period of meaningful judicial enforcement of constitutional limits on federal power. This interpretive chasm remains unresolved because the two frameworks employ different baseline definitions of legitimate judicial review.
