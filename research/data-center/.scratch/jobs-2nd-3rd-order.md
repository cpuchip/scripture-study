# Scratch — Data Center 2nd/3rd Order Jobs

**Binding question:** A data center goes in with ~15 local permanent jobs. What 2nd and 3rd order jobs does it create — both (a) through the supply chain of building/running the building and (b) through what the servers themselves enable downstream?

**Search horizon:** Exa neural search, three queries — economic-impact studies, hyperscale multiplier studies, and downstream-services/AI-enabled work.

## Categorization Framework Emerging

The user said "based on the servers it hosts" — this is key. Most economic impact studies focus on the **building/operations supply chain** (which is what 2nd-order traditionally means). But the question implicitly includes a **third tier**: jobs that exist *because the compute exists*.

### Tier 1 — Direct on-site (the "15 jobs")
- Operations technicians, facility engineers, security, logistics
- Typical: 30–160 permanent; hyperscale 150–500; large AI campus 300–500 across footprint

### Tier 2 — Indirect (supply chain of the building)
**Construction phase (temporary, but repeats per phase):**
- Construction trades: electricians, plumbers/pipefitters, carpenters, structural steel/iron, concrete, earth drillers, HVAC, controls, commissioning
- Civil engineers, project managers, general contractors
- Materials: steel, concrete, fiber cable installers

**Equipment manufacturing (ongoing):**
- Server, GPU, networking hardware manufacturing
- Power equipment: transformers, generators, UPS systems, switchgear
- Cooling equipment: liquid cooling systems, CRAC/CRAH units, chillers
- Cable and fiber manufacturing
- Semiconductor fabs (TSMC, Samsung, Intel) — upstream
- DRAM/HBM memory production

**Operations supply chain:**
- Utilities/grid operators (electricity supply expansion)
- Water utility expansion
- Fiber/telecom carriers
- Industrial gas suppliers (for cooling/fire suppression)
- Spare parts/logistics suppliers
- Cybersecurity service providers
- Professional services: engineering consultancies, commissioning specialists, regulatory consultants

### Tier 3 — Induced (household spending of T1+T2 workers)
- Restaurants, retail, leisure, hospitality
- Housing/real estate (rental, construction of housing for workforce)
- Local services: schools, healthcare, childcare
- Personal services

### Tier 4 — Enabled-by-compute (the "based on the servers it hosts" angle)
This is what makes the user's question novel and what most impact studies *understate*.

**Direct tenants/customers of the compute:**
- Cloud service customers (every SaaS company renting AWS/Azure/GCP capacity)
- AI labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, etc.) training models
- Enterprise IT migrating from on-prem
- Streaming services (Netflix, YouTube, Twitch)
- Financial services (high-frequency trading, fraud detection)
- Gaming (cloud gaming, multiplayer infrastructure)

**Jobs at companies whose product is delivered via this compute:**
- AI/ML engineers, prompt engineers, AI product managers
- SaaS developers, designers, support
- Content moderators (AI-assisted)
- Data scientists/analysts using cloud compute
- Streaming platform staff

**Jobs at companies that *use* AI/cloud as intermediate input:**
- Per arXiv paper: car manufacturer running AI inference in data centers
- Companies subscribing to Copilot/ChatGPT Enterprise for employee productivity
- Healthcare using AI imaging diagnostics
- Logistics using AI route optimization
- Marketing/creative agencies using generative AI

**Jobs in the "AI value chain" beyond the building:**
- Semiconductor design (Nvidia, AMD, custom silicon teams)
- AI safety/alignment researchers
- Power generation: gas turbines, nuclear (Kewaunee rebuild example), solar/wind for data center PPAs
- Transmission line construction crews (utility-side)

### Honest caveats from sources
1. The Tier 2/3 multipliers (2.58 state, 5.5 ops-only state, 7.5 national, "6.5 jobs per direct" Arizona, "5.9" Google) are mostly **modeled** via IMPLAN/input-output models, not directly observed.
2. Many studies are industry-funded (PwC for Data Center Coalition, Oxford Economics for Google, the Florida study commissioned).
3. The capital-to-job ratio is genuinely extreme: $54M/permanent job in Virginia per Food & Water Watch.
4. Construction jobs are real but **temporary** and often **non-local** (out-of-state crews).
5. The Tier 4 "enabled by compute" jobs are real but **not geographically tied to the data center** — they exist wherever the customers are, which is often nowhere near the host community. This is the crux of why local critics call out the bargain as unbalanced: the host town gets 15 jobs + grid strain + water draw + tax abatement, while the *jobs enabled* land in San Francisco, Seattle, Austin, NYC.
6. Brookings: longer-term local benefit requires intentional ecosystem building (university R&D partnerships, compute vouchers, testbeds). Doesn't happen automatically.
