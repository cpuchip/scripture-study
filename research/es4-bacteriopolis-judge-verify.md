# Bacteriopolis: Winogradsky Column Living Exhibit Brief

## Story / Example

Every August, after the heavy rains sweep through the Ozarks, the farm ponds around Lebanon, Missouri, turn over. The cool rainwater sinks, the warm bottom water rises, and the mud—that jet-black, sulfurous muck layered with last autumn’s leaves—releases a smell strong enough to make a farm dog think twice. To most people, it smells like rot. To a microbiologist, it smells like a city switching on its power grid.

Meet Clara, a seventh-grader from Rolla. Last September, she waded into her grandpa’s pond with a plastic bucket and scooped up three liters of that stinky black sediment, plus a jug of pond water the color of weak tea. Back on the porch, she mixed half the mud with shredded newspaper, a teaspoon of baking soda, and a crushed hard-boiled egg yolk. She packed the mixture into a clear acrylic tube, filled the rest with pond water, covered the top with plastic wrap, and set it on the windowsill above the kitchen sink. For six weeks, nothing happened. The tube sat like a forgotten vase. Then, in week seven, a thin band the color of a bruise appeared just above the black sediment. By week ten, the tube looked like a layered sunset: inky black at the bottom, then a stripe of emerald green, a band of vivid purple, a wide swath of rust-red, and a pale blue-green cap at the waterline. Clara had not added paint, dye, or fertilizer. She had built a city of bacteria—Bacteriopolis—where each neighborhood operated on a different fuel source, in a different atmospheric layer, visible to the naked eye.

That tube is a Winogradsky column. It requires no batteries, no Wi-Fi, and no curator with a pipette. It only asks for light, patience, and the humility to look closely at mud.

## Real-World Application

The same sulfur chemistry that paints Clara’s column governs ecosystems, industries, and environmental challenges across rural Missouri and far beyond.

**Agriculture and pond health.** Farm ponds, wetlands, and manure lagoons are driven by the same anaerobic digestion and sulfur cycling visible in the column. Sulfate-reducing bacteria in oxygen-starved sediment generate hydrogen sulfide, which is toxic to fish at high concentrations and produces the characteristic “rotten egg” smell when bottom mud is disturbed. Farmers managing pond turnover or dredging operations are, in effect, managing a Winogradsky column the size of a watershed.

**Wastewater and infrastructure.** These same sulfur-reducing processes occur in wastewater treatment biofilms and sewer systems, where microbial sulfide production is a major operational and infrastructure concern. The column makes visible an otherwise hidden biogeochemical process that shapes municipal engineering and maintenance protocols.

**Astrobiology and early-Earth research.** NASA Quest educator guides use the Winogradsky column to model how microbial communities structured early Earth’s ecosystems before oxygen became widespread. The metabolic strategies on display—anoxygenic photosynthesis, sulfur oxidation, nitrogen fixation, and cellulose fermentation—are candidate life processes for extraterrestrial environments and a staple of astrobiology outreach curricula [Building a Winogradsky Column: An Educator Guide with Activities in Astrobiology](https://serc.carleton.edu/resources/2224.html).

**Environmental monitoring and education.** Because the visible color bands respond to light, oxygen, and sulfur gradients, the column functions as a teaching bio-indicator. Shifts in band position or the appearance of unexpected algae can open conversations about agricultural runoff, heavy-metal contamination, or the difference between a healthy wetland and a eutrophic ditch. SERC Carleton’s educational index notes that the activity is specifically suited for grades 5–12, making it a natural fit for a science center serving school groups [Winogradsky Column — SERC Carleton](https://serc.carleton.edu/microbelife/topics/special_collections/winogradsky.html).

## Museum Demo

**What visitors see.** A single vertical exhibit station approximately 18 inches wide by 36 inches tall. The centerpiece is a clear acrylic cylinder, 4 inches in outer diameter and 24 inches tall, sealed at the base and mounted in a stained or painted plywood stand. Inside the cylinder, the bottom third is packed pond mud showing distinct horizontal strata: jet-black sediment at the very bottom, then narrow bands of green and purple, a wider red band, and a rust-orange or blue-green cap near the water surface. The water column above the mud is mostly clear but may show a thin surface film, strands of cyanobacterial filaments, or tiny gas bubbles clinging to the glass. The exhibit is silent except for the faint whisper of a ventilation fan.

**Lighting and housing.** A full-spectrum LED grow light is mounted 10–12 inches above the tube on a fixed bracket or gooseneck arm, programmed to a 12-hour on/12-hour off cycle via a digital timer. The light fixture and tube are enclosed on three sides by a simple plywood box painted matte black on the interior to reduce glare and focus attention; the front face is left entirely open for viewing. A small, quiet 40-mm DC cooling fan is mounted in the top panel of the housing, drawing air upward and out to prevent heat buildup and vent any odor that may develop during the first few weeks.

**Controls and interaction.** Visitors do not touch the column. The microbial biofilm forms as a thin, fragile layer against the inner wall, and handling would disrupt the chemical gradients. Instead, interaction is observational and interpretive:
- A rotary dial or flip-panel mounted at child and adult eye level pairs each observed color with its metabolism: “Black = iron sulfide and sulfate reducers,” “Purple = sulfur photosynthesizers,” “Red = purple non-sulfur bacteria,” “Blue-green/Rust = cyanobacteria and iron oxidizers.”
- A laminated timeline strip shows photographs of the column at Week 1, Week 4, and Week 8, so visitors understand they are watching a slow-motion ecosystem unfold. Repeat visitors can look for new bands.
- Optional: a small hand-held magnifying glass on a tether for closer inspection of band boundaries.

**Placement and visitor flow.** Position the unit against a wall or on a low counter where children can kneel and adults can stand comfortably. The exhibit requires a standard 120 V outlet for the light and fan. Because of potential odor during the initial two-to-four-week stabilization period, place it in a well-ventilated gallery away from food-service zones. The Exploratorium’s Bacteriopolis exhibit is the canonical museum implementation, though its public pages were not accessible for direct verification during the preparation of this brief.

## Underlying Science

A Winogradsky column is not a static display; it is a set of steep chemical gradients that select for different microbial metabolisms at different depths.

**The gradients.** Three gradients operate simultaneously from top to bottom:
1. **Light** peaks at the surface and attenuates rapidly through the water and mud.
2. **Oxygen** is present at the surface but depleted within millimeters of the sediment by aerobic respiration. As the University of Edinburgh microbiology teaching notes explain, “Only the very top of the column remains aerated because oxygen diffuses very slowly through water” [The Microbial World: Winogradsky Column](http://archive.bio.ed.ac.uk/jdeacon/microbes/winograd.htm).
3. **Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S)** is generated in the anaerobic bottom by sulfate-reducing bacteria and diffuses upward, decreasing in concentration toward the surface.

These gradients create distinct metabolic neighborhoods.

**The sequence of colonization.**
- **Bottom layer (black):** Cellulose from the shredded newspaper is degraded by *Clostridium* species under anaerobic conditions. The Edinburgh source notes: “Some cellulose-degrading *Clostridium* species start to grow when the oxygen is depleted in the sediment.” They ferment glucose into organic acids. Sulfate-reducing bacteria such as *Desulfovibrio* then use those acids and sulfate as a terminal electron acceptor, producing hydrogen sulfide. The Edinburgh source states: “The H₂S will react with any iron in the sediment, producing black ferrous sulphide. This is why lake sediments (and our household drains) are frequently black” [The Microbial World: Winogradsky Column](http://archive.bio.ed.ac.uk/jdeacon/microbes/winograd.htm).
- **Lower band (green/purple):** Anaerobic photosynthetic bacteria—green sulfur bacteria and purple sulfur bacteria such as *Thiocapsa*—use light and hydrogen sulfide instead of water for photosynthesis. The Edinburgh source gives the chemical parallel:
  > 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O = C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂ (plant photosynthesis)  
  > 6 CO₂ + 12 H₂S = C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 H₂O + 12S (bacterial anaerobic photosynthesis)  
  > — *[The Microbial World: Winogradsky Column](http://archive.bio.ed.ac.uk/jdeacon/microbes/winograd.htm)*
- **Middle band (red):** Purple non-sulfur bacteria grow as photoheterotrophs—using light for energy but organic compounds for carbon. They typically occupy the zone just above the sulfur photosynthesizers, where light is available but hydrogen sulfide has dropped to lower concentrations.
- **Upper water column and surface (blue-green/rust):** Cyanobacteria perform oxygenic photosynthesis, generating oxygen that reshapes the upper gradient and supports aerobic microbes. Surface films may also include iron-oxidizing bacteria that leave rusty deposits.

**For the deeper reader.** The column is a practical demonstration of redox chemistry. Aerobic respiration at the top uses O₂ as the terminal electron acceptor. As oxygen vanishes, microbes switch to nitrate, then sulfate, and finally fermentation. The sulfate reducers “pull” the sulfur cycle by producing H₂S, which then serves as an electron donor for the anoxygenic phototrophs above them. The Edinburgh teaching notes summarize the column's larger significance: “All life on earth can be categorised in terms of the organism's carbon and energy source... Only in the bacteria - and among the bacteria within a single Winogradsky column - do we find all four basic life strategies” [The Microbial World: Winogradsky Column](http://archive.bio.ed.ac.uk/jdeacon/microbes/winograd.htm). The result is a complete biogeochemical cycle—carbon, sulfur, iron, and energy—contained in a single tube.

## History

Sergei Winogradsky (also transliterated as Sergey Nikolayevich Winogradsky) was born September 1, 1856, in Kiev, Russian Empire (now Kyiv, Ukraine), and died February 25, 1953, in Brie-Comte-Robert, France [Britannica — Sergey Nikolayevich Winogradsky](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sergey-Nikolayevich-Winogradsky). After studying natural sciences at the University of St. Petersburg, he moved in 1885 to the University of Straßburg (now Strasbourg) to work under the renowned botanist Anton de Bary.

In 1887, Winogradsky established the specific physiology of sulfur bacteria, demonstrating that colorless forms could obtain energy by oxidizing hydrogen sulfide to sulfur and then to sulfuric acid in the absence of light [Britannica — Sergey Nikolayevich Winogradsky]. That same year, researching the filamentous bacterium *Beggiatoa*, he discovered the first known form of lithotrophy—the use of inorganic compounds as an energy source. Wikipedia’s entry, citing his 1887 paper in *Botanische Zeitung*, notes: “He reported that *Beggiatoa* oxidized hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) as an energy source and formed intracellular sulfur droplets” [Wikipedia — Sergei Winogradsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Winogradsky).

Between 1889 and 1890, while at the University of Zürich, he identified the microbial agents of nitrification, establishing the genera *Nitrosomonas* and *Nitrobacter* [Britannica — Sergey Nikolayevich Winogradsky]. He returned to St. Petersburg and headed the division of general microbiology at the Institute of Experimental Medicine until 1905, discovering in that period the free-living nitrogen-fixer *Clostridium pasteurianum*. After the 1917 Russian Revolution, he resumed work at the Pasteur Institute in Paris in 1922, remaining active until his second retirement in 1940.

The Winogradsky column itself emerged from his 1880s work on selective enrichment and sulfur bacteria. Wikipedia notes that “He is best known in school science as the inventor of the Winogradsky column technique for the study of sediment microbes” [Wikipedia — Sergei Winogradsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Winogradsky). However, neither Britannica nor Wikipedia assigns a specific invention date to the column technique; it appears to have developed as a natural extension of his selective-enrichment methods during the late 19th century. His broader legacy is that of “among the first students of microbial ecology and environmental microbiology,” having pioneered the investigation of microorganisms outside of a strictly medical context [Wikipedia — Sergei Winogradsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Winogradsky).

**Assembly — Step by Step**

1. **Build the housing.** Cut the plywood into a base (12" × 12"), a back panel (12" × 36"), and two side panels (10" × 36"). Assemble into a three-sided tower with the acrylic tube centered on the base. Drill a 1½" hole in the top panel for the grow-light stem and a 1" hole for the fan exhaust. Paint interior surfaces matte black to reduce glare.
2. **Collect mud and water.** Gather approximately 3 liters of water-saturated sediment and 1 liter of overlying water from a local pond, marsh, or stream. The University of Waterloo protocol advises: “Any water-saturated (and stinky!) sediment should be fine” [University of Waterloo — DIY Winogradsky Column](https://uwaterloo.ca/science/outreach/science-home/winogradsky-column).
3. **Enrich the mud.** Divide the mud in half. To one half, add one crushed hard-boiled egg yolk (sulfur source), one teaspoon of baking soda (carbonate/carbon source), and a small handful of shredded newspaper (cellulose/carbon source). Mix thoroughly. The Waterloo instructions specify: “To one half add an egg yolk (crushed from a hard-boiled egg; sulfur source), a teaspoon of baking soda (carbonate), and a small handful of shredded newspaper. Mix well” [University of Waterloo — DIY Winogradsky Column](https://uwaterloo.ca/science/outreach/science-home/winogradsky-column).
4. **Pack the column.** Wearing gloves, fill the bottom one-third of the acrylic tube with the enriched mud, packing firmly with a spatula or dowel to exclude large air bubbles. Add the unenriched mud on top to roughly the two-thirds mark. Slowly add pond water to within 2 inches of the top. Rinse any mud from the upper walls.
5. **Seal for gas exchange.** Cover the opening with plastic wrap secured by a rubber band. Then—critically—cut two small slits in the plastic wrap. The Center for Biofilm Engineering protocol warns: “Be sure to cut a slit or two in the plastic wrap or parafilm to permit gas exchange” [Center for Biofilm Engineering — Construction of a Winogradsky Column](https://biofilmbook.hypertextbookshop.com/public_version/contents/appendices/appendix002/pages/page019.html). Do not seal airtight; hydrogen sulfide and other metabolic gases must vent.
6. **Install and light.** Place the tube in the housing. Set the grow light 10–12 inches above the water surface. Program the timer for 12 hours on, 12 hours off. Mount the cooling fan in the top panel, oriented to exhaust upward.
7. **Position.** Place the completed exhibit in a well-ventilated gallery area. The Center for Biofilm Engineering notes: “Place the column near a window or tungsten lamp in a well ventilated room. They can smell until they stabilize” [Center for Biofilm Engineering — Construction of a Winogradsky Column](https://biofilmbook.hypertextbookshop.com/public_version/contents/appendices/appendix002/pages/page019.html).

**Signage Suggestions**
- Front panel: “Bacteriopolis — A Living City in Mud.” Subtitle explaining that each color is a different metabolic neighborhood.
- Color key: Black = sulfate reducers and iron sulfide; Green/Purple = sulfur photosynthesizers; Red = purple non-sulfur bacteria; Blue-green/Rust = cyanobacteria and iron oxidizers.
- Timeline card: “Week 1: Mud. Week 4–8: Color bands appear. Month 3+: Full ecosystem maturity.”
- “Please do not touch — the biofilm is thinner than a coat of paint.”

**Safety Considerations**
- **Mud contamination:** “There is no easy way to determine if the mud is contaminated with sewage, farm waste, heavy metals or other toxic materials. Wear latex or vinyl gloves when mixing materials and packing the column” [Center for Biofilm Engineering — Construction of a Winogradsky Column](https://biofilmbook.hypertextbookshop.com/public_version/contents/appendices/appendix002/pages/page019.html). Collect from a site you know; avoid drainage ditches receiving concentrated agricultural or septic runoff.
- **Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S):** “In the highly anaerobic base of the column, decomposition and the activity of sulfate reducing bacteria results in the production of hydrogen sulfide gas” [Center for Biofilm Engineering — Construction of a Winogradsky Column](https://biofilmbook.hypertextbookshop.com/public_version/contents/appendices/appendix002/pages/page019.html). At the small scale of a single tube, H₂S generation is low, but the gas is toxic at high concentrations. Ventilation is mandatory; do not place in a sealed display case.
- **Heat:** Check that the LED lamp does not raise the tube temperature more than a few degrees above ambient. Excessive heat will kill the microbial community.
- **Physical stability:** Acrylic tubes can tip. Secure the base to the housing and consider a low retaining ring, especially in a gallery with young visitors.

**Known Failure Modes**
- **Airtight seal:** Sealing without gas-exchange slits can cause pressure buildup, rupture, or anoxic crash. Always vent.
- **Direct sunlight through glass/acrylic in a sealed housing:** Creates a greenhouse effect that overheats the column and encourages algal overgrowth. Use timed LED lighting instead of uncontrolled sun.
- **Drying out:** Evaporation concentrates salts and shrinks the water column. Top up monthly with distilled water if the level drops more than an inch.
- **Agricultural contaminants:** Mud from fields with heavy pesticide or fertilizer loads may produce aberrant coloration or fail to develop the expected banding.
- **End-of-life uncertainty:** Primary sources do not specify a standard disposal or refresh protocol. SERC Carleton notes that classroom columns require “about 3 months to see observable growth patterns” [Winogradsky Column — SERC Carleton](https://serc.carleton.edu/microbelife/topics/special_collections/winogradsky.html), while the University of Waterloo reports that “Colours will change and will be most impressive after 1-3 years” [University of Waterloo — DIY Winogradsky Column](https://uwaterloo.ca/science/outreach/science-home/winogradsky-column). Plan to either maintain the column as a long-term installation or retire and rebuild it seasonally. If dismantling, treat the contents as non-hazardous biological waste suitable for burial in an outdoor flower bed or disposal according to local municipal guidelines; the literature does not prescribe a specific end-of-life protocol.
