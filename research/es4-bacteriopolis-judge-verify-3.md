# Bacteriopolis: Winogradsky Column Living Exhibit Brief

## 1. Story / Example

In late May, the Miller kids—ages ten and thirteen—wade into the shallow end of their grandfather’s stock pond outside Versailles, Missouri, with a five-gallon bucket and a trowel. The mud smells like rotten eggs and looks like chocolate pudding that has gone wrong. They haul it home, mix it with water from the same pond, shred a quarter page of the *Lake Sun Leader*, crack a raw egg into the slurry, and pack it all into a clear acrylic tube their 4-H leader salvaged from a hardware-store cutoff bin. For the first week, the tube sits on a shelf under a cheap grow light and does nothing worth a selfie. By week four, a pencil-thin band of emerald green appears just above the black sludge at the bottom. By week six, the green band is topped by a stripe of grape-jelly purple, then a rusty orange, then a surface layer of blue-green scum that looks like spilled paint. The millimeter-scale city in a jar has partitioned itself by chemistry the way a small town sorts itself by trade—some organisms mining sulfur in the anaerobic depths, others catching sunlight at the top, each living on the waste of the one below it.

The Millers are not running an art project. They are re-creating, in a single tube, the metabolic cooperation that has governed mud, soil, and sediment since before there were plants or animals. The exhibit takes something visitors routinely scrape off their boots and reveals it as a working ecosystem.

## 2. Real-World Application

The bacterial partnerships visible in a Winogradsky column are the same ones that drive agriculture, wastewater treatment, and global biogeochemical cycles. In the 1880s and 1890s, Sergei Winogradsky isolated the nitrifying bacteria *Nitrosomonas*, *Nitrococcus*, and *Nitrobacter*—organisms that convert ammonium to nitrite and then to nitrate, the form of nitrogen most accessible to crops [Victorian Web biography](https://www.victorianweb.org/science/biology/bacteriology/winogradsky.html). Without this microbial assembly line, synthetic fertilizers would be far less effective and natural soil fertility would collapse.

The sulfur cycle on display in the column is equally practical. Sulfate-reducing bacteria such as *Desulfovibrio* generate hydrogen sulfide in waterlogged soils and pond sediments; sulfur-oxidizing bacteria return it to sulfate. The same chemistry governs the anaerobic digesters used by municipal wastewater plants and the bioremediation of acid mine drainage. In rural Missouri, where cattle outnumber people and stock ponds dot every pasture, these microbes quietly prevent hydrogen sulfide from accumulating at toxic levels while recycling organic matter into forms usable by algae and aquatic plants.

The column also makes visible the process that lets grazing animals digest cellulose. As one early Exploratorium recipe notes, “neither cows nor termites would enjoy their cellulose-based diets without the helpful bacteria in their guts that digest the cellulose for them” [Bacterial Terrarium](https://annex.exploratorium.edu/the-world/glow/grow.html). For a farming community, the exhibit is a direct line from pond mud to livestock health.

Current research continues to use Winogradsky-style enrichment cultures to isolate novel microbes. The University of Delaware’s World Wide Winogradsky Project frames the device as a “microbial garden” and “microcosm” that remains a standard tool for bringing sediment organisms into laboratory culture [The World Wide Winogradsky Project](https://sites.udel.edu/winogradsky/the-columns/). In an era of microbiome research and precision agriculture, the classical technique is still the front door to discovery.

## 3. Museum Demo

**What visitors see.** A vertical, clear polycarbonate or acrylic tube roughly 30 cm tall and 5–8 cm in diameter, mounted on a stained wooden base and lit from above by a full-spectrum LED grow light on a timer. Inside, stratified bands of color have developed in the mud-and-water column: jet-black sediment at the bottom, then a thin green band, a purple band, a pink or rusty-red zone, and finally a blue-green or yellow-orange surface layer. A laminated photo strip beside the tube shows the same column at Week 1 (uniform gray), Week 4 (first green specks), and Week 8 (full color), so visitors understand they are looking at a slow-motion process.

**What visitors do.** They read the color-key signage, use a handheld 5× magnifier to inspect the boundaries between bands, and rotate a small dial or lift a flap on an interpretive panel to match each color with its metabolism: “Black = iron sulfide, made by sulfate reducers,” “Purple = sulfur photosynthesis,” “Blue-green = oxygen-making cyanobacteria.” A small acrylic plaque quotes the University of Edinburgh’s observation that “only in the bacteria—and among the bacteria within a single Winogradsky column—do we find all four basic life strategies” [University of Edinburgh](https://archive.bio.ed.ac.uk/jdeacon/microbes/winograd.htm). A nearby dish of dried, shredded newspaper and a blown eggshell—sealed in acrylic—show visitors the raw ingredients without the smell.

**What visitors hear.** Nothing electronic is required; the exhibit is silent except for the faint hum of the LED driver. Optional audio is a 45-second loop of local pond ambience recorded at the same site where the mud was collected, grounding the display in Missouri ecology.

**Floor placement.** The column is best sited near a window with supplemental light, or in an interior bay with a 12-hour LED cycle. Because the tube must be vented periodically to release gas, it sits on a waist-high shelf within easy reach of floor staff, not inside a sealed vitrine. A small “Please Do Not Shake” label is mandatory; vibration collapses the delicate gradients.

**Maintenance rhythm.** Staff vent the cap for five seconds every two to three days and top off evaporated water weekly. The light timer is checked during daily opening rounds. After three to four months, the color bands peak and then begin to degrade; the exhibit plan should budget for a fresh build every semester.

## 4. Underlying Science

A Winogradsky column is a self-contained redox gradient. Oxygen dissolves only slowly into stagnant water, so the top centimeter of the column remains aerobic while everything below it becomes anoxic within days. That single chemical divide sorts the microbes by metabolism.

**Bottom zone (anaerobic, dark).** Cellulose-degrading *Clostridium* species break down the shredded newspaper, consuming the last traces of oxygen and producing organic acids and alcohols. Once oxygen is gone, sulfate-reducing bacteria such as *Desulfovibrio* use sulfate as a terminal electron acceptor, generating hydrogen sulfide (H₂S). The H₂S reacts with iron in the mud to produce black ferrous sulfide, which gives the bottom layer its dark color [University of Edinburgh](https://archive.bio.ed.ac.uk/jdeacon/microbes/winograd.htm).

**Lower water column (anaerobic, lit).** Hydrogen sulfide diffuses upward, enabling anaerobic photosynthetic bacteria to grow. Green sulfur bacteria appear first, then purple sulfur bacteria such as *Thiocapsa*. These organisms use light for energy but do not use water as a reductant; they use H₂S, producing elemental sulfur or sulfate. The Edinburgh resource gives the simplified parallel equations:

> Plant photosynthesis: 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂  
> Bacterial anaerobic photosynthesis: 6 CO₂ + 12 H₂S → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 H₂O + 12 S

**Mid water column (low oxygen, lit).** Purple non-sulfur bacteria—*Rhodospirillum*, *Rhodopseudomonas*—form a red or pink band. These are photoheterotrophs: they use light for energy but require organic carbon (the fermentation products of *Clostridium*) for biomass. They sit above the sulfur bacteria because they are less tolerant of high H₂S concentrations.

**Upper water column (aerobic, lit).** Cyanobacteria perform oxygenic photosynthesis exactly as plants do, using water and light to fix CO₂. Once established, they can oxygenate much of the water column. Sulfur-oxidizing chemoautotrophs also live here, converting any upward-drifting H₂S to sulfate and gaining energy from the oxidation. These are the “chemosynthetic” organisms whose discovery was Winogradsky’s most noted contribution [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Winogradsky).

**For the deeper reader.** The column neatly demonstrates why microbiologists now speak of “microbial ecology” rather than single-species biology. All four fundamental metabolic strategies—photoautotrophs, chemoheterotrophs, photoheterotrophs, and chemoautotrophs—coexist in a volume smaller than a soda can [University of Edinburgh](https://archive.bio.ed.ac.uk/jdeacon/microbes/winograd.htm). The evolutionary implications are equally striking: the chloroplasts inside plant cells almost certainly descended from cyanobacteria like those growing at the top of the column, while mitochondria likely originated from purple bacteria related to the *Rhodospirillum* group in the mid-zone. The jar is not merely a model of ancient ecosystems; it is a partial reenactment of the metabolic partnerships that built the eukaryotic cell.

## 5. History

Sergei Nikolaevich Winogradsky was born 13 September 1856 in Kiev, Russian Empire, and died 24 February 1953 in Brie-Comte-Robert, France [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Winogradsky). After training in law, then piano at the Saint Petersburg Conservatory, he entered Saint Petersburg Imperial University in 1877 to study chemistry and botany, receiving his degree in 1881 and his master’s in 1884 [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Winogradsky). In 1885 he moved to the University of Strasbourg to work under the botanist Anton de Bary.

At Strasbourg in 1887 he discovered “sulphur bacteria,” and by 1890, working in Zurich, he had also isolated the **nitrifying** bacteria *Nitrosomonas*, *Nitrococcus*, and *Nitrobacter* [Victorian Web biography](https://www.victorianweb.org/science/biology/bacteriology/winogradsky.html). His research on *Beggiatoa* in 1887 provided the first known example of lithotrophy—deriving energy from the oxidation of an inorganic compound—and his subsequent work on nitrifying bacteria established the first known form of chemoautotrophy, the fixation of carbon dioxide using energy from chemical rather than light reactions [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Winogradsky).

Winogradsky returned to Saint Petersburg from 1891 to 1905, headed the division of general microbiology at the Institute of Experimental Medicine, and identified the free-living nitrogen-fixing anaerobe *Clostridium pasteurianum*. After retiring to his estate, then emigrating following the 1917 revolution, he accepted a position in 1922 at the Pasteur Institute in France, where he worked until 1940 [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Winogradsky).

He is best known in school science as the inventor of the Winogradsky column technique for the study of sediment microbes [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Winogradsky). The columns were invented as a way to enrich for microbes from sediments and soils, providing “a whole range of environments in one small setting, a microcosm” [The World Wide Winogradsky Project](https://sites.udel.edu/winogradsky/the-columns/). Precisely which year Winogradsky first packed mud into a tall glass vessel is not dated in the gathered sources; the technique emerged from his 1880s investigations of sulfur and nitrogen bacteria, and an early Exploratorium recipe attributes the observation that “water mud poured into a tall bottle and placed in the sun turned many different colors” to the 1880s [Bacterial Terrarium](https://annex.exploratorium.edu/the-world/glow/grow.html). What is certain is that the device has been a mainstay of microbiology instruction for well over a century.

For a higher-end installation within the $300 cap, build **three columns at staggered ages** (fresh, 4-week, and 8-week) inside a single wooden plinth with a shared LED bar, plus a small digital photo frame cycling the week-by-week progression.

### Step-by-step assembly

1. **Collect.** Gather mud and water from a local freshwater pond in late spring or summer when microbial diversity is high. Remove sticks, rocks, and visible invertebrates. The “Bacterial Terrarium” recipe notes that “the smellier the better” [Bacterial Terrarium](https://annex.exploratorium.edu/the-world/glow/grow.html).
2. **Mix.** In a dedicated bowl, stir mud with pond water until it reaches the consistency of heavy cream.
3. **Charge.** Place shredded newspaper and the raw egg (shell removed) into the bottom of the tube. Add the first inch of mud and mix thoroughly with a spoon.
4. **Pack.** Fill the tube with mud to within 2.5 cm (1 inch) of the top. Tap gently to dislodge large air pockets.
5. **Flood.** Add pond water to fill the remaining headspace, leaving a small air gap.
6. **Cap.** Seal with the vented cap or loose plastic wrap. The seal must allow gas exchange; it cannot be airtight.
7. **Light.** Position the grow light 20–30 cm above the tube and set the timer for 12–16 hours of light daily. Modern LEDs have replaced the 40-watt incandescent bulbs specified in older recipes.
8. **Vent.** Every two to three days, staff briefly loosen the cap for a few seconds to release accumulated gases.
9. **Observe.** Expect no visible change for 1–2 weeks. Distinct color bands typically appear by week 4 and mature by week 6–8. Top off with distilled or pond water if the surface dries.

### Signage suggestions

- **Front panel:** “This jar is a city. No worm, no fish, no plant—just billions of bacteria sorting themselves by job and chemistry.”
- **Color key:** Match each band to its metabolism and name (e.g., “Purple = *Thiocapsa*, photosynthesis with sulfur”).
- **Safety note:** “Please do not open. Staff vent this column regularly. It contains living bacteria that produce natural gases.”
- **Timeline strip:** Photos at Weeks 0, 2, 4, and 8 with the caption “Nature is slow. Come back.”

### Safety considerations

- **Gas pressure.** The primary hazard is pressure buildup from bacterial fermentation. The early Exploratorium protocol explicitly warns: “Every few days, briefly remove the container's top to vent off the gases (if too much gas is allowed to build up, it can blow the top off)” [Bacterial Terrarium](https://annex.exploratorium.edu/the-world/glow/grow.html). Use a vented cap and train floor staff on the venting schedule.
- **Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S).** Sulfate-reducing bacteria generate H₂S, recognizable by a rotten-egg odor. In this small volume concentrations remain low, but staff should vent the tube toward a fume hood or open window, never inhale directly over the opening, and discard the column if the smell becomes acutely overpowering.
- **Pathogen handling.** Raw eggs carry *Salmonella* risk. Builders should wear nitrile gloves, wash hands after assembly, and dedicate all utensils to non-food use.
- **Mud source safety.** Avoid collecting near storm-drain outfalls, septic leach fields, or agricultural chemical runoff. If the pond receives cattle waste, treat the mud as potentially pathogenic; builders should wear gloves and eye protection.
- **Stability.** Acrylic tubes can tip. The base must have a center-of-gravity low enough that a leaning child cannot pull it over. Consider a recessed collar or screw-on base.

### Known failure modes

- **No growth after two weeks.** The mud may have been too hot, too cold, or chemically treated (e.g., algal bloom treated with copper sulfate). Collect from a different pond.
- **Uniform black sludge, no bands.** Usually indicates insufficient light or an overly rich sulfur load. Ensure the LED is bright enough and positioned within 30 cm.
- **Surface scum only; no deep colors.** The light may be too intense, favoring cyanobacteria that oxygenate the column and suppress anaerobic layers. Raise the light or reduce the photoperiod.
- **Dry-out cracking.** Evaporation collapses the gradient. Check water level weekly; add pond water, not tap water (chlorine kills anaerobes).
- **Invasion of grazing protists.** If the water turns clear and colors fade, ciliates or rotifers may have colonized and eaten the bacterial lawns. Starting with filtered pond water can reduce this risk, but some grazing is inevitable in an open system.
- **End-of-life decay.** After 3–5 months the nutrient pulse is exhausted and the column turns uniformly dark olive or gray. Plan a rebuild schedule; this is a living exhibit, not a permanent installation. The Exploratorium describes its installation as able to live “indefinitely” with only sunlight and occasional water, but gathered classroom protocols and the original archival recipe suggest a functional color lifespan of several months before enrichment exhaustion [Bacterial Terrarium](https://annex.exploratorium.edu/the-world/glow/grow.html). For a science center, budget for seasonal re-culture.
