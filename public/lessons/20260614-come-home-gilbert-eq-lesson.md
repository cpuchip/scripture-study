# Lesson: Come Home — What the Finder Does

Based on Elder Clark G. Gilbert, ["Come Home"](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2026/04/15gilbert?lang=eng) (April 2026 General Conference), read alongside [Luke 15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng).

---

## Lesson Information

**Prepared:** 2026-06-14
**Class:** Elders Quorum
**Source:** Elder Gilbert's "Come Home" + the three parables of Luke 15
**Time:** ~40 minutes

---

## Preparation

### Personal Spiritual Preparation
- [ ] Pray for the quorum — and for the specific "one" each brother is carrying in his heart.
- [ ] Read Luke 15 slowly, then Elder Gilbert's talk. Notice what the *finder* does in each story.
- [ ] Ponder honestly: am I more the seeking shepherd right now, or the elder brother who won't go in?

### Know Your Learners
A quorum holds all of Luke 15 at once: faithful brethren who are the ninety-and-nine; brethren watching a road for a son, a sibling, a friend who has gone; and — quietly, unspoken — brethren who feel like *the one*. Teach so all three are welcome. The tone of this chapter is a **father running**, never a father scolding. Keep it that way.

---

## The Connecting Thread (the big idea)

Luke 15 shows **God as the relentless Finder** who seeks every kind of lost thing. Elder Gilbert names what the Finder does once He reaches us: He is **Redeemer *and* Repairer** — He buys us back *and* mends the breach we cannot cross ourselves. The quorum is called to do both: to seek and to help repair — and not to murmur like the elder brother when the lost come home.

> **Honesty flag for the teacher:** Elder Gilbert does **not** cite Luke 15 in "Come Home" — he builds it around four kinds of people who hesitate to return and around Christ as Redeemer/Repairer ([Isaiah 58:12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/58?lang=eng&id=p12#p12), his own footnote 4). The parable bridge is *ours* to make, but it is well-founded: his framing harmonizes with Luke 15. He says *"in the end, everyone must make their own choices to come home"* (the prodigal's agency), and he closes with the hymn line *"Patiently Jesus is waiting and watching"* (the father watching the road). Present the bridge as our synthesis; his text earns it.

## Lesson Outline

### Opening (~5 min) — The murmur that started it all

The three parables aren't free-floating. Read [Luke 15:1–2](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng&id=p1-p2#p1):

> "Then drew near unto him all the publicans and sinners for to hear him. And the Pharisees and scribes murmured, saying, **This man receiveth sinners, and eateth with them**."

Jesus answered that murmur with three stories. Write on the board: **sheep · coin · son**. Ask: *"These are three kinds of lost. How is each one lost differently — and who does the work of finding?"*

---

### Part 1 — What the Finder does (Luke 15, ~15 min)

Read the three finders and watch how the seeking *changes* with the kind of lostness:

- **The sheep** wandered and cannot return. The shepherd "go(es) after that which is lost, **until he find it**," then "**layeth it on his shoulders**, rejoicing" ([15:4–5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng)). The sheep contributes nothing — it is *carried*.
- **The coin** is inert, and lost *inside the house*. The woman "**light(s) a candle, and sweep(s) the house, and seek(s) diligently till she find it**" ([15:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng)). Again, all the labor is the finder's.
- **The son** chose to leave — full agency. So the father does **not** chase him into the far country; the son must "**come to himself**" and turn ([15:17–20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng)). But the father was watching the road: "**when he was yet a great way off, his father saw him**, and had compassion, and **ran**, and fell on his neck, and kissed him" ([15:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng)) — and restored him *beyond his ask* (robe, ring, shoes, feast).

**The pattern:** the finder always takes the initiative — He watches, seeks, carries, runs, restores. He *carries* those who cannot return; He *runs* to those who turn. And every story ends the same way: joy, and calling others to "**Rejoice with me**" — "joy in heaven… joy in the presence of the angels of God" ([15:6, 7, 9, 10](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng)).

> **Discuss:** Where the lost one *has* agency, its only job is to **turn** — and even a turn "a great way off" is met at a run. How does that reframe repentance? (It is not crawling back to earn your way; it is turning, and being met.)

---

### Part 2 — Redeemer *and* Repairer (Gilbert + Isaiah 58, ~12 min)

Elder Gilbert witnesses of Christ as **Redeemer and Repairer**, tying *Repairer* to [Isaiah 58:12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/58?lang=eng&id=p12#p12):

> "thou shalt be called, **The repairer of the breach, The restorer of paths to dwell in**."

That is exactly the difference between *finding* and *restoring* in the parables. The shepherd doesn't only locate the sheep — he **carries** it across the gap it can't cross. The father doesn't only receive the son — he **repairs his standing** (a son again, not a hired servant). **Redeemer** buys back; **Repairer** mends the breach. To Brother Vargas — a man who felt he could never *measure up* to the people he saw at church — Elder Gilbert said, on the steps of the San Antonio Temple:

> "You don't have to be perfect to be in this Church. You just have to do your best, and **Christ will make up the difference**."

"Make up the difference" *is* the shepherd's shoulders and the father's run. Elder Gilbert tells four such stories, one for each hesitation: **those who feel they don't belong** (Sister Anglesey, 30 years away, who once dressed for church, parked, watched others walk in, then drove home and wept), **those who feel they don't measure up** (the Vargas family), **those who doubt** (the Valdez family), and **those trapped in traditions** (John Raass, holding to his late parents' faith). Each one came home.

> **Discuss:** Which of those four describe people we actually know? What "breach" have you watched Christ repair — something a person could not fix on their own?

---

### Part 3 — The elder brother in the mirror (~8 min)

There is a *fourth* lost son in Luke 15, and he never left home. The elder brother "was angry, and would not go in: therefore came his father out, and **entreated him**" ([15:28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng)). He is lost in resentment and self-righteousness — he *is* the murmuring Pharisees of verse 2 in the mirror. Notice the father goes **out a second time**: he seeks the resentful son as earnestly as the prodigal.

This is the sharpest word for an elders quorum. Elder Gilbert's category "**trapped in traditions**" lands here: it is possible to keep every rule and still be lost at the edge of the feast, unwilling to rejoice when a sinner comes home. The quorum's call is the opposite of the elder brother — to *run with* the Shepherd and to *rejoice* when the lost return.

> **Discuss + invite:** Who is "a great way off" that this quorum should be watching the road for? Are we keeping the feast, or standing outside it offended? Where do *I* need Christ as **Repairer**, not just Redeemer — to mend a breach I cannot cross myself?

---

### Closing (~3 min)

Elder Gilbert ends with two images worth landing the lesson on. First, what a quorum *does*: at a packed devotional with a few seats left, students turned on their phone flashlights to wave the latecomers in — *"as if they were holding up a light to say, 'Please come sit with me; we saved a seat just for you.'"* That is the exact opposite of the elder brother. That is the ninety-and-nine rejoicing.

Then the hymn he chose, which is simply the father of Luke 15 set to music:

> *"Patiently Jesus is waiting and watching— / Watching for you and for me! / Come home! Come home! Ye who are weary, come home!"*

Close with his own witness, verbatim: *"I witness that Christ is our Redeemer. When we fall short, **He repairs the breaches in our lives**."* The chapter that began with a murmur — "this man receiveth sinners" — ends as the gospel's whole answer: He does. He receives them, carries them, runs to them, repairs them, and saves them a seat at the feast. Extend His invitation: **come home.**

## Teacher notes / honesty flags

1. **Gilbert does not quote Luke 15** — the parable connection is the teacher's synthesis (a well-founded one; his own framing harmonizes). Say so if asked.
2. **Quotes verified** against the local copy of the talk (`gospel-library/.../2026/04/15gilbert.md`). Two attribution traps: "do your best, and Christ will make up the difference" is what Gilbert said *to Brother Vargas*; "more exhausting to seek happiness where you can never find it" is **President Nelson**, quoted by Gilbert.
3. **Tone:** the father *runs*; he does not shame. Hold the whole lesson in that posture, especially Part 3 — name the elder-brother temptation in *ourselves*, never point it at the absent.
