# The Art of Presidency — Consent, Consecration, and the Covenant of Presiding

*Study date: March 27, 2026*
*Born from: the stewardship pattern study's three sabbath seeds (March 22) and the question of what we actually commit when we raise our hands*
*Study mode: study (phased writing with externalized memory)*
*Predecessor: [stewardship-pattern.md](stewardship-pattern.md)*

---

## The Binding Question

When I raise my hand to sustain someone — or accept a calling myself — what am I actually covenanting to do, and what does scripture teach about how those mutual commitments create a functioning presidency rather than a one-cylinder operation?

---

## I. The Lifted Hand — What Common Consent Actually Requires

The phrase appears twice in the Doctrine and Covenants, and both times it bears more weight than we usually give it.

> "And all things shall be done by common consent in the church, by much prayer and faith, for all things you shall receive by faith." ([D&C 26:2](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/26?lang=eng&id=p2#p2))

> "For all things must be done in order, and by common consent in the church, by the prayer of faith." ([D&C 28:13](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/28?lang=eng&id=p13#p13))

"All things." Not some things. Not the important things. All things. And the mechanism is not democratic — it's spiritual: "by much prayer and faith." The Lord is not establishing a ballot system. He's establishing a spiritual process where the body of the Church participates in His governance through the Spirit.

Webster 1828 defines "consent" in two dimensions: to *agree in sentiment* and to *express a willingness*. Both are operating in D&C 26. When you raise your hand, you're saying two things simultaneously: I concur with this decision, and I'm willing to support it. Agreement of mind plus commitment of will.

The context of D&C 28 sharpens this further. The revelation came because Hiram Page was receiving false revelations through a seer stone, and some Church members accepted them. Verse 12 draws the doctrinal line: "neither shall anything be appointed unto any of this church contrary to the church covenants." Common consent isn't courtesy. It's the safeguard. Without it, false authority creeps in unchecked.

D&C 42:11 extends the principle: no one can "go forth to preach my gospel, or to build up my church, except he be ordained by some one who has authority, and it is known to the church that he has authority and has been regularly ordained by the heads of the church." Known to the church. The congregation has a right and a need to know who holds authority. Sustaining accomplishes this: public knowledge, public accountability.

Elder Mark E. Petersen, in a 1974 conference talk, made the claim explicit: "Their vote was a covenant, made with uplifted hands before God and witnesses." He connected sustaining to D&C 21:4-5, where the Lord tells the Church that the prophet's "word ye shall receive, as if from mine own mouth, in all patience and faith." Petersen concluded: "As members of the Church, by voting to sustain our new President, we have placed ourselves under a solemn covenant to give diligent heed to the words of eternal life as he gives them to us." ([The People Say Amen](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1974/04/the-people-say-amen?lang=eng))

The scripture itself does not use the word "covenant" for sustaining. But the language of D&C 107:22 is unmistakably covenantal: the First Presidency is to be "upheld by the confidence, faith, and prayer of the church." Three words. Confidence — active trust. Faith — not just belief but spiritual investment. Prayer — ongoing intercession for the person. This is not a checkbox. It's a relationship.

And consider Webster 1828's definition of "sustain": to keep from falling, to keep from sinking in despondence, to maintain and nourish, to aid and comfort, to vindicate. When we sustain a leader, we're committing to all of this. The sustaining vote is a promise of *active, ongoing support* — not a one-time gesture that expires when the hand comes down.

Mosiah understood this. When he argued for judges over kings, his reasoning was not just political but covenantal: "The burden should come upon all the people, that every man might bear his part" ([Mosiah 29:34](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/29?lang=eng&id=p34#p34)). Governance is not the leader's burden alone. When we sustain someone, we're shouldering our share, not offloading the weight onto them.

But Mosiah also named the vulnerability in the system. "It is not common that the voice of the people desireth anything contrary to that which is right," he said — and the qualifier "not common" acknowledges that it sometimes happens ([Mosiah 29:26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/29?lang=eng&id=p26#p26)). The footnote in D&C 26:2 cross-references 1 Samuel 8:7, where Israel demanded a king and the Lord told Samuel: "they have not rejected thee, but they have rejected me, that I should not reign over them." The people can choose wrongly. Common consent is not infallible. It is protected by the spiritual dimension — "by much prayer and faith" — not by the mechanism of voting itself.

This scales without changing. In a Sunday School presidency, sustaining means the ward pledges to support the president and counselors, to attend their classes, to accept their teaching. In a stake presidency, sustaining means the stake will uphold and pray for those called. In a solemn assembly for the President of the Church, it means the entire membership covenants to follow the Lord's prophet. The scope widens. The principle is identical.

## II. The Consecrated Calling — What We Give When We Accept

Before anyone in the Church ever received a calling, they made a covenant at the waters of baptism. Alma stated the terms:

> "As ye are desirous to come into the fold of God, and to be called his people, and are willing to bear one another's burdens, that they may be light; yea, and are willing to mourn with those that mourn; yea, and comfort those that stand in need of comfort, and to stand as witnesses of God at all times and in all things, and in all places that ye may be in, even until death." ([Mosiah 18:8-9](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18?lang=eng&id=p8-p9#p8))

Bear burdens. Mourn with mourners. Comfort those in need. Stand as witnesses. This is the foundation under every specific calling. When I accept a calling as Sunday School president, I'm not making a new covenant. I'm accepting a *specific stewardship* within the baptismal covenant I already hold. The calling directs what the covenant made available.

Elder Oaks made this chain explicit: "In partaking of the sacrament each week, we witness our commitment to serve the Lord and our fellowmen. In sacred temple ceremonies we covenant to sacrifice and consecrate our time and talents for the welfare of others." ([Unselfish Service](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2009/04/unselfish-service?lang=eng)) The pipeline is clear: baptism establishes the covenant, the sacrament renews it weekly, the temple deepens it with the laws of sacrifice and consecration, and callings give it specific application.

President Uchtdorf drew a distinction that matters:

> "To sacrifice means to give something up in favor of something more valuable. … Consecration is different from sacrifice in at least one important way. When we consecrate something, we don't leave it to be consumed upon the altar. Rather, we put it to use in the Lord's service." ([Our Heartfelt All](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2022/04/57uchtdorf?lang=eng))

A calling involves both. You sacrifice personal time — that time is given up, and it doesn't come back. But you consecrate what you bring to the calling — your energy, your attention, your preparation, your prayers. The sacrifice is what you lose. The consecration is what you redirect. The General Handbook captures both: "Your consecrated service requires a sacrifice of time, but it also brings great blessings to you and those you serve" ([Handbook 30.1](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/manual/general-handbook/30-callings-in-the-church?lang=eng)).

And what is the return? Alma told us:

> "The priests were not to depend upon the people for their support; but for their labor they were to receive the grace of God, that they might wax strong in the Spirit, having the knowledge of God, that they might teach with power and authority from God." ([Mosiah 18:26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18?lang=eng&id=p26#p26))

The compensation for consecrated service is the grace of God. Not salary, not recognition, not position. Grace — the enabling power to wax strong in the Spirit, to gain knowledge, to teach with authority. You give your time and labor; you receive spiritual capacity. The exchange is not economic. It's covenantal.

King Benjamin had already established why this exchange works:

> "Are we not all beggars? Do we not all depend upon the same Being, even God, for all the substance which we have?" ([Mosiah 4:19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/4?lang=eng&id=p19#p19))

Everything we have belongs to God. The substance. The time. The life. Consecration isn't giving God something extra. It's acknowledging that it was always His. When we consecrate time to a calling, we're returning what was given.

Webster 1828 gives "consecration" as "the state of being consecrated; dedication." Not a one-time act. A state. You don't consecrate your time to a calling at the moment of setting apart and then coast. You remain in a state of consecration — or you don't. The ongoing dedication is the difference between a calling fulfilled and a calling occupied.

This also scales. In a Sunday School presidency, consecration looks like preparing lessons, training teachers, attending council meetings, praying for students by name. In a bishopric, it means late-night interviews, heavy administrative hours, spiritual burdens carried in private. In the First Presidency, it means a life entirely given over to the Lord's service. The cost increases with the scope. The principle — putting what you have to use in the Lord's service — does not change.

And Uchtdorf offered the reframe that keeps consecration from becoming overwhelming: "When we look at our lives and see a hundred things to do, we feel overwhelmed. When we see one thing — loving and serving God and His children, in a hundred different ways — then we can work on those things with joy." One calling. One consecration. A hundred different expressions.

## III. Presiding as Watching — The Abraham 4:18 Pattern

Webster 1828 gives two definitions of "preside." The first is what we usually mean — to sit in the place of authority, to direct and regulate as chief officer. The second is the one that changes everything: "To exercise superintendence; to watch over."

Presiding is watching.

We established this in the [stewardship pattern study](stewardship-pattern.md), but that study traced the *mechanics* of delegation — who assigns, who reports, at what ratio. This study asks the prior question: on what terms does presiding authority actually operate?

The answer fills an entire revelation:

> "The rights of the priesthood are inseparably connected with the powers of heaven, and that the powers of heaven cannot be controlled nor handled only upon the principles of righteousness." ([D&C 121:36](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121?lang=eng&id=p36#p36))

> "No power or influence can or ought to be maintained by virtue of the priesthood, only by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned; by kindness, and pure knowledge, which shall greatly enlarge the soul without hypocrisy, and without guile." ([D&C 121:41-42](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121?lang=eng&id=p41-p42#p41))

These aren't suggestions. They're the only terms on which presiding authority functions. Without them, "the heavens withdraw themselves; the Spirit of the Lord is grieved; and when it is withdrawn, Amen to the priesthood or the authority of that man" ([D&C 121:37](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121?lang=eng&id=p37#p37)). The calling remains. The authority disappears. A presidency can exist structurally while being dead spiritually.

D&C 107:21 establishes that presiding is necessary: "Of necessity there are presidents." But the verse that follows it (107:22) immediately subordinates presidential authority to the body: the Presidency is "upheld by the confidence, faith, and prayer of the church." Their necessity as presidents does not grant them independence from the body. The same section requires that "every decision made by either of these quorums must be by the unanimous voice of the same" ([D&C 107:27](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107?lang=eng&id=p27#p27)). And the conditions for valid decisions read like a catalog of the highest Christian virtues: "all righteousness, in holiness, and lowliness of heart, meekness and long-suffering, and in faith, and virtue, and knowledge, temperance, patience, godliness, brotherly kindness and charity" ([D&C 107:30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107?lang=eng&id=p30#p30)). The quorum must be as virtuous in its deliberation as its members are expected to be individually.

President Hinckley mapped the presidency lifecycle from personal experience. He served as a deacons quorum counselor, eventually as counselor in the First Presidency. He found the principles unchanged across fifty years and every level of scaling:

> "The president, if he is wise, will assign to these chosen assistants particular duties and then leave them free to perform, requiring from them accountability for what happens." ([In Counsellors There Is Safety](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1990/10/in-counsellors-there-is-safety?lang=eng))

Assign, release, and require accountability. This is Abraham 4:18 in practice: "The Gods watched those things which they had ordered until they obeyed." The presidency pattern is counsel → assign → watch → evaluate. Not assign → abandon. And Hinckley was explicit about what happens in the presidency meeting:

> "In presidency meetings, each counselor is free to speak his mind on all issues that come before the presidency. However, it is the prerogative of the president to make the decision, and it is the duty of the counselors to back him in that decision. His decision then becomes their decision, regardless of their previous ideas."

> "When three kneel together in prayer, discuss every aspect of the problem which is before them, and under the impressions of the Spirit reach a united conclusion, then we may have the assurance that the decision is in harmony with the will of the Lord."

There is a tension here worth naming. Hinckley says the president decides. D&C 107:27 says decisions must be by unanimous voice. These operate at different levels. In a presidency, free counsel leads to a presidential decision that the counselors then back. In a quorum (like the Twelve or a high council), unanimity is required for the decision to carry full validity. The General Handbook bridges these: "Sometimes council members may have unsettled feelings about an important decision. When this happens, the leader may wait for another meeting to consider the matter further and seek spiritual confirmation and unity" ([Handbook 4.4.3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/manual/general-handbook/4-leadership-in-the-church-of-jesus-christ?lang=eng)). The system makes room for disagreement without breaking.

D&C 102 gives the most detailed constitution of council deliberation in all of scripture. When the first high council was organized in Kirtland, the members "were then asked whether they accepted their appointments, and whether they would act in that office according to the law of heaven" ([D&C 102:4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/102?lang=eng&id=p4#p4)). Consent runs in both directions — the body consents to the calling, and the called consents to serve. And in that establishing session, forty-three people voted: nine high priests, seventeen elders, four priests, and thirteen *members* ([D&C 102:5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/102?lang=eng&id=p5#p5)). Not just leaders. The whole community.

The high council's deliberation process has a feature I find remarkable: "The accused, in all cases, has a right to one-half of the council, to prevent insult or injustice" ([D&C 102:15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/102?lang=eng&id=p15#p15)). Half the councilors are structurally assigned to advocate for the accused. This is not a jury system. It's a system that builds in protection against groupthink and injustice by design. And after the president renders a decision, any non-speaking councilor who "discover[s] an error in the decision of the president" can call for a rehearing ([D&C 102:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/102?lang=eng&id=p20#p20)). The system expects disagreement and creates mechanisms for it.

Elder Ballard described what happens when presiding collapses into one-cylinder operation:

> "The one-cylinder ward is the ward where the bishop handles all of the problems, makes all of the decisions, and follows through on all of the assignments. Then, like an overworked cylinder in a car engine, he is soon burned out." ([Counseling with Our Councils](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1994/04/counseling-with-our-councils?lang=eng))

In his demonstration, a bishop "took charge of the situation immediately and said, 'Here's the problem, and here's what I think we should do to solve it.' Then he made assignments." Ballard called this "a good exercise in delegation, I suppose, but it did not even begin to use the experience and wisdom of council members." When the bishop opened the floor instead, "the effect was like opening the floodgates of heaven."

The failure of one-cylinder presiding isn't just organizational inefficiency. It's blocking the Spirit from flowing through multiple channels. Ballard's insight is that the council system is a *revelation* system. A "reservoir of insight and inspiration" flows between council members when they counsel together. One cylinder means one channel. Every channel shut down is revelation lost.

The General Handbook traces councils back before the world was created: "Councils existed before the world was created" ([Handbook 4.3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/manual/general-handbook/4-leadership-in-the-church-of-jesus-christ?lang=eng)), referencing Abraham 3:22-28 and the noble and great ones in premortal council. The presidency pattern is not a post-fall organizational accommodation. It's the original pattern. Abraham 4:26 — "the Gods took counsel among themselves" — before any act of creation.

And the handbook's counsel on how the presiding officer should function: "he or she should listen more than talk" ([Handbook 4.4.3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/manual/general-handbook/4-leadership-in-the-church-of-jesus-christ?lang=eng)). The president's primary mode is listening. Not directing. Not deciding prematurely. Listening — and then, having heard, presiding: watching over the decision and its implementation.

## IV. The Covenantal Triangle — Why Each Depends on the Others

Three things, then:

**Consent** — the body's covenantal commitment to sustain with confidence, faith, and prayer.
**Consecration** — the individual's dedication of time, talent, and self to a specific stewardship.
**Presiding** — the authority to watch over, exercised only by persuasion, long-suffering, gentleness, love unfeigned.

This framework is mine — a way to organize what the texts teach. No single scripture articulates these as a triangle. But the interdependencies are there.

**Sustaining without consecration is empty ceremony.** If I raise my hand to sustain but don't actually bring my confidence, faith, and prayer — if I don't offer the active support Webster 1828 defines as "sustaining" — my hand is a formality. D&C 107:22 doesn't say the Presidency is *elected* by the Church. It says they are *upheld* by the confidence, faith, and prayer of the Church. The active verbs matter.

**Consecration without presiding is unorganized zeal.** If everyone in the ward is individually willing to serve but nobody is watching, coordinating, or ensuring accountability, the work doesn't come together. This is the Jethro problem: Moses had a consecrated heart but no delegates. The result was a judge who "will surely wear away" ([Exodus 18:18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ex/18?lang=eng&id=p18#p18)). D&C 28:13 puts it plainly: things must be done *in order* and by common consent. Order requires someone who presides.

**Presiding without consent is unrighteous dominion.** D&C 121 makes this as explicitly as scripture gets. The moment a president exercises "control or dominion or compulsion upon the souls of the children of men, in any degree of unrighteousness," the heavens withdraw ([D&C 121:37](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121?lang=eng&id=p37#p37)). And D&C 102:9 states the mechanism: the president "is appointed by revelation, and acknowledged in his administration by the voice of the church." Appointment by revelation is not enough. Acknowledgment by the Church — consent — must accompany it.

D&C 104:21 brings all three together in a single verse: "Let all things be done according to the counsel of the order, and united consent or voice of the order." Counsel — presiding authority leading the discussion. Consent — the body's voice. And the context is stewardship of consecrated property. The Lord doesn't separate these. They go together.

This triangle operates at every level of Church governance. In a Sunday School presidency: the president presides (watches over teachers, coordinates the program), the counselors and teachers consecrate (prepare, teach, show up), and the ward body sustains (supports by attending, participating, praying for those who serve). If any leg fails, the whole thing wobbles. A Sunday School president who presides without trusting his counselors runs a one-cylinder organization. Teachers who don't prepare are occupying a calling, not consecrating to it. A ward that doesn't show up to Sunday School has withdrawn support.

Scale it up. In a bishopric: the bishop presides in the same way — counsel, assign, watch — while the ward sustains through attendance, service, and prayer, and every auxiliary leader consecrates their specific stewardship. In a stake presidency the same. In the Quorum of the Twelve, D&C 107:24 says they are "equal in authority and power" to the First Presidency, meaning the same covenantal infrastructure applies — even at the highest levels, decisions require the unanimous voice ([D&C 107:27](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107?lang=eng&id=p27#p27)).

President Eyring pointed to the spiritual prerequisite underneath the entire structure: unity comes from changed hearts, not organizational efficiency. "What will become more obvious to us is that the Atonement brings the same changes in all of us." ([Our Hearts Knit as One](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2008/10/our-hearts-knit-as-one?lang=eng)) He anchored this back to the Waters of Mormon — Mosiah 18:21: "hearts knit together in unity and in love one towards another." The triangle's foundation is not competence or even good intent. It's the Atonement working in the hearts of the people who consent, who consecrate, and who preside.

## V. Becoming — What This Means for My Presidency

I have two counselors. We are a Sunday School presidency. The question this study raises is not abstract.

When my counselors were sustained, the ward raised their hands. Those hands meant something — confidence, faith, prayer. They promised to support us. But how many of them know what a Sunday School presidency does? How many know what sustaining actually costs them? If Petersen is right that the sustaining vote is a covenant, what are the terms of their commitment, and am I helping them understand those terms?

When I accepted this calling, I accepted a specific expression of the baptismal covenant I made at the waters of my own baptism. I covenanted to bear burdens, to mourn with mourners, to comfort those in need. My calling as Sunday School president is the current shape of that older promise. The question is whether I'm treating it as a state of consecration or a title I carry. Am I putting what I have to use in the Lord's service, or am I occupying a position while the real work of teaching goes uncoordinated?

And when I preside — or fail to preside — over our little three-person presidency, what kind of presiding is it? Am I watching? Do I know how my counselors' stewardships are going? Do we kneel together and counsel? Or do I run a one-cylinder Sunday School where I handle problems, make decisions, and ask my counselors to execute assignments they didn't help shape?

Ballard's floodgates stayed closed when the bishop presented the problem and the solution. They opened when he invited the council to *counsel*. There's my practical test: in our next presidency meeting, do I come with solutions or with questions? Do I listen more than I talk?

Three commitments from this study:

1. **Counsel before assigning.** Bring questions to presidency meetings, not pre-made decisions. Let my counselors speak first. The handbook says the presiding officer should listen more than talk. I'll try that.

2. **Watch what I've ordered.** Abraham 4:18. When I delegate a responsibility to a counselor, I don't abandon it. I check in. I ask how it's going. Not because I don't trust them, but because watching IS presiding. Hinckley said: assign, leave them free to perform, require accountability. All three parts.

3. **Name the covenant.** When we extend callings to Sunday School teachers, I want to be more deliberate about what we're asking. Not just "will you teach the 14-year-olds?" but "this calling is a stewardship within the covenant you've already made — to bear burdens, to stand as a witness. Here is the specific shape that covenant takes right now."

The grace of God was the compensation for Alma's priests. It will be mine too, if I consecrate rather than merely occupy.
