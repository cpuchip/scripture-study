# Zion in a Presidency

*The third in an arc.* The first study, [Art of Presidency](art-of-presidency.md), traced the triangle a presidency stands on: consent, consecration, presiding. The second, [Art of Delegation](art-of-delegation.md), worked the mechanics — willing burden-bearing as a shadow of the Atonement, vertical and horizontal. This study asks what those two together actually *make*.

The binding question:

> What does it look like to build Zion inside a presidency — and how does Zion, once kindled among the few who counsel together, propagate outward through the teachers we lead and the classes they teach?

I've been a Sunday School president for a few weeks. Last Sunday I taught my first teacher council meeting. We followed the handbook pattern (counsel together, learn something new, decide what to take away) and the counsel went so deep we almost ran out of time for the rest. That meeting is the proximate reason for this study. The pattern of Zion is supposed to come *from* meetings like that and propagate outward into classrooms. So I want to know what the scriptures say about how that actually works.

---

## I. The Zion Definition — Four Conditions, One Verse

There is one anchor verse for what Zion *is*:

> "And the Lord called his people Zion, because they were of one heart and one mind, and dwelt in righteousness; and there was no poor among them." — [Moses 7:18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/pgp/moses/7?lang=eng&id=p18#p18)

The word *because* is doing serious work. The Lord didn't call them Zion as a goal-state or a courtesy title. He called them Zion *because* four conditions were already true: one heart, one mind, dwelt in righteousness, no poor among them. These are diagnostic criteria, not aspirations.

Two verses earlier the order is set out:

> "And from that time forth there were wars and bloodshed among them; but the Lord came and dwelt with his people, and they dwelt in righteousness." — [Moses 7:16](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/pgp/moses/7?lang=eng&id=p16#p16)

The Lord came and dwelt with His people, and *then* they dwelt in righteousness, and *then* they were called Zion. Zion is a label that lands on a community after the Lord has already arrived. It is given, not manufactured. A presidency cannot strategize itself into Zion. It can only put itself in a posture where the Lord is willing to come and dwell.

That reframes the whole task. The president's job is not to build Zion. The president's job is to remove the obstacles to the Lord coming and dwelling.

The same diagnostic appears with different vocabulary throughout scripture. Acts 4:32 calls it being "of one heart and of one soul." Mosiah 18:21 calls it "hearts knit together in unity and in love." 4 Nephi 1:17 calls it being "in one, the children of Christ." D&C 38:27 puts it as starkly as scripture ever puts anything:

> "Be one; and if ye are not one ye are not mine." — [D&C 38:27](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/38?lang=eng&id=p27#p27)

Binary. Not "if you are not striving for unity" — *not one, not mine*. The verse just before it gives the parable of the twelve sons, one in robes and one in rags, and asks whether a father who treated his sons that way could call himself just. Unequal treatment fails the Zion test before it starts.

There is a real risk of misreading "one heart and one mind" as a demand for uniformity — that to be Zion, everyone has to think the same things, hold the same opinions, want the same outcomes. President Uchtdorf addressed this directly in April 2025:

> "But that doesn't mean changing my heart to align with yours. Nor does it mean changing your heart to align with mine. It means that we all change our hearts to align with the Savior. … The unity we seek is not to have everyone stand in the same place; it is to have everyone face in the same direction—toward Jesus Christ." — [Uchtdorf, April 2025](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2025/04/27uchtdorf?lang=eng)

Common direction, not common position. A presidency of three people who all face Christ will be one in the way that matters, even if they disagree about meeting times and lesson formats. A presidency of three people who agree on every operational decision but face three different directions is not one in any sense Moses 7:18 cares about.

## II. The Council as Zion's Cell

Where does Zion start? Not where you'd expect.

Enoch's Zion was a city, eventually, but it began with one prophet preaching in a violent land. The early Christian Zion was a multitude in Jerusalem, eventually, but it began with the apostles in an upper room. The Mosiah 18 Zion was a covenant people of about four hundred and fifty souls, eventually, but it began with one fugitive priest at the edge of a fountain in a forest. Every Zion in scripture starts as a *cell*: one teacher, a few hearers, a body small enough to know each other's names.

The Mosiah 18 cell is the one I keep coming back to, because it's the closest scriptural analogue to what we actually do in a ward. Alma, recently converted by Abinadi's words and on the run from King Noah, gathers a goodly number at the place of Mormon. He preaches the covenant of baptism in language that could be read aloud at any modern font:

> "As ye are desirous to come into the fold of God, and to be called his people, and are willing to bear one another's burdens, that they may be light; … and now when the people had heard these words, they clapped their hands for joy, and exclaimed: This is the desire of our hearts." — [Mosiah 18:8, 11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18?lang=eng&id=p8#p8)

Alma then commands them:

> "That there should be no contention one with another, but that they should look forward with one eye, having one faith and one baptism, having their hearts knit together in unity and in love one towards another." — [Mosiah 18:21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18?lang=eng&id=p21#p21)

That single verse is Moses 7:18 spoken in a different dialect. *No contention, one eye, one faith, hearts knit, unity*. The four conditions are all there, slightly rephrased. Then comes the practice:

> "There was one day in every week that was set apart that they should gather themselves together to teach the people, and to worship the Lord their God." — [Mosiah 18:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)

> "Alma commanded that the people of the church should impart of their substance, every one according to that which he had … and to him that had not should be given." — [Mosiah 18:27](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18?lang=eng&id=p27#p27)

> "And they did walk uprightly before God, imparting to one another both temporally and spiritually according to their needs and their wants." — [Mosiah 18:29](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18?lang=eng&id=p29#p29)

A weekly gathering to teach. Mutual imparting according to need. Walking uprightly. That is the structure of Zion in a forest, with no temple, no city, and four hundred and fifty people.

Alma is the de facto president of this body. He teaches, he ordains priests at a one-to-fifty ratio, he sets the gathering rhythm, he commands the imparting. But he does it inside a covenant the people have *clapped for joy* over. He is not building Zion *on top of* an unwilling congregation. He is naming and shaping a desire they already had. The cell forms because the desire was there and the leader gave it structure.

The early Christians show the same pattern at scale. Acts 2:42 lists the four practices that held them: "the apostles' doctrine and fellowship, and in breaking of bread, and in prayers." Verse 46 adds the structural detail: they continued daily "with one accord in the temple, and breaking bread from house to house." Both scales operating at once — the large gathering and the small. Acts 4:32 declares the result: "of one heart and of one soul." Acts 4:34 reports the outcome: "Neither was there any among them that lacked." Moses 7:18's third condition, tested in Jerusalem.

Two scriptures, two cultures, one pattern. Zion forms in cells, propagates outward, manifests itself in shared substance and one-heartedness. The presidency *is* the cell. It is not the manager of Zion; it is one of the small bodies inside which Zion can actually be lived. If the presidency is not one heart and one mind, no organizational chart will rescue the ward.

## III. The Teacher Council as Practice

Last Sunday, in our teacher council meeting, the part of the agenda meant to take twenty minutes took twenty-five. We were supposed to spend that time sharing experiences and counseling together, then move into a fifteen-minute "learn something new" segment, then close with ten minutes of "what will you take away." Instead the counsel ran long, the learning ran shorter, and we barely got to "take away."

That trade was right. What was happening in the overrun was the four practices of Acts 2:42 condensed into a single hour. We were holding the apostles' doctrine: *Teaching in the Savior's Way* and the principles of Christlike teaching. We were in fellowship; these are people I see every week, but in this meeting I was hearing what they actually carry as teachers. There was no breaking of bread, but there was something close: the mutual imparting that Mosiah 18:27 describes, the willingness to say "this is hard for me" and "here's what worked for me." And there were prayers, opening and closing, framing the rest as something held by the Lord.

The handbook gives the format. The handbook doesn't and can't make the meeting Zion. What makes a teacher council Zion-shaped is the same thing that made Mosiah 18 Zion-shaped: people willing to bear one another's burdens, looking with one eye toward the same Christ, imparting both temporally and spiritually according to need.

*Counseling together about how to teach the gospel of Jesus Christ* is itself a form of teaching the gospel of Jesus Christ. It isn't pre-teaching, or planning-to-teach. It is teaching. When the four of us sat in that room and tried to figure out how to apply *Teaching in the Savior's Way* to the actual challenges in our actual classes, the doctrine was being lived between us in real time. The counsel is the curriculum.

This is what Elder Ballard meant in 1994 when he described the bishop who finally opened the floor to his council and the effect "was like opening the floodgates of heaven." The floodgates language is Zion language. When a council functions as designed, *something heavenly flows*. The flow is not the result of the council. It is one of the ways the Lord comes to dwell. Moses 7:16 again: He comes, and then they are called Zion. The council is one of the rooms He is willing to come into.

## IV. Classes as Councils

The General Handbook distinguishes teacher council meetings (held quarterly, attended by teachers) from Sunday classes (attended by everyone). Different forums, different purposes. Nothing in this section erases that distinction.

But the spiritual structure of a teacher council, stripped of its labels, is the same structure every Zion gathering in scripture wears. Mosiah 18:25 calls it gathering "to teach the people." Acts 2:46 calls it being "with one accord in the temple, and breaking bread from house to house." Mosiah 18:29 calls it "imparting to one another both temporally and spiritually according to their needs and their wants." None of those describe a lecture. They describe a body that gathers, hears doctrine, and works out together how to live it.

A Sunday School class meets weekly. It holds the doctrine of the lesson. The people in the room are *living* that doctrine, or trying to. Acts 2:46 puts the large gathering and the small in one sentence — temple AND house to house — because the same people moved between scales without changing their posture. The hour of class is closer to "house to house" than we usually treat it. It is small enough that the people can know each other, address each other, mourn and comfort each other in real time.

What if Sunday School classes (the actual classroom hour, not the teacher council) were held closer to that spiritual structure? Not "let's just have a discussion" instead of a lesson; that's the failure mode in the other direction. Closer to: a class that knows the doctrine being taught is the same doctrine the people in the room are *living*, and that the hour is a chance to counsel together about how the principles in this lesson can help with the daily struggles we have.

The textual ground for this is mostly indirect. TSW's principles. Acts 2:46's house-to-house pattern. Ballard's floodgates. The Abraham 4:26 council pattern that runs from creation through every priesthood quorum and Relief Society meeting we hold. Scripture doesn't legislate classroom format. This isn't doctrine; it is a thoughtful application.

But there is one piece of textual ground that is direct, and it points at what Zion *stops* looking like:

> "And they began to be divided into classes; and they began to build up churches unto themselves to get gain." — [4 Nephi 1:26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/4-ne/1?lang=eng&id=p26#p26)

The English word *classes* there means social and economic strata. But it sits in a scripture about how a once-Zion society organized itself into divided groups. The word is a small accident; the principle isn't. Whenever a body of believers stops gathering as one and starts gathering as *categories* (by status, by ability, by familiarity, by who sits with whom), Zion is breaking down. The opposite of council is *classes-of-people*. A Sunday School class that operates as a council resists that breakdown by structure. A class that operates as a hierarchy of teacher-over-students rehearses the failure 4 Nephi 1:26 names.

## V. The 4 Nephi Trajectory

Zion doesn't just exist in scripture as a finished state. It exists as a story with a beginning, a duration, and an end. 4 Nephi is the most carefully documented Zion story in the standard works, and the way it falls is more instructive than the way it rose.

It rose because of love:

> "And it came to pass that there was no contention in the land, because of the love of God which did dwell in the hearts of the people." — [4 Nephi 1:15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/4-ne/1?lang=eng&id=p15#p15)

Notice the cause. Not because of structures, not because of leadership, not because of correct doctrine. *Because of the love of God which did dwell in the hearts of the people*. The structures (no -ites, all things common, no contention) are downstream symptoms of an interior state.

> "Neither were there Lamanites, nor any manner of -ites; but they were in one, the children of Christ." — [4 Nephi 1:17](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/4-ne/1?lang=eng&id=p17#p17)

That is what one heart actually does to identity. The dividing categories don't get reconciled — they collapse. Nephites and Lamanites don't learn to get along; *neither category remains*. They are in one, the children of Christ. Uchtdorf's "one direction, not one position" is exactly this. They didn't all become identical. They all faced Christ, and the inherited categories that used to define them stopped being where they took their identity from.

Then came the fall, and the sequence is precise:

> "Now, in this two hundred and first year there began to be among them those who were lifted up in pride, such as the wearing of costly apparel, and all manner of fine pearls, and of the fine things of the world. And from that time forth they did have their goods and their substance no more common among them. And they began to be divided into classes; and they began to build up churches unto themselves to get gain, and began to deny the true church of Christ." — [4 Nephi 1:24-26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/4-ne/1?lang=eng&id=p24-p26#p24)

Four steps:
1. Pride (costly apparel, comparison enters)
2. Goods no longer common (the economic Zion condition fails)
3. Divided into classes (the social Zion condition fails)
4. Churches for gain (the theological Zion condition fails)

The first failure is *internal*. Pride. A heart-condition. Everything else cascades from it. The visible markers (economic stratification, social stratification, counterfeit institutions) are all downstream of one private interior shift in some unnamed group of people.

Elder Cook in October 2020 named the parallel directly:

> "At this 200-year hinge point in our Church history, let us commit ourselves as members of the Lord's Church to live righteously and be united as never before." — [Cook, October 2020](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2020/10/15cook?lang=eng)

The 200-year hinge in 4 Nephi was the moment the heart-condition shifted. Cook's invitation is to notice that we are at a 200-year hinge of our own and to choose differently. For a presidency, the 4 Nephi diagnostic is precise: watch for pride. Watch for comparison. Watch for one teacher being elevated above another. Watch for "the good class" and "the hard class." Watch for the moment when the goods and substance of the council (the time, the attention, the credit) stop being common. The economic and social symptoms come later. The first symptom is in someone's heart, possibly your own.

## VI. How a President Helps

So how does a Sunday School president help his teachers? The Section I reframe has to govern the answer: the president does not manufacture Zion; he removes obstacles to the Lord coming and dwelling. With that constraint, here is what scripture and the predecessor studies actually authorize.

**1. Be one with your counselors first.** Before anything propagates outward, the cell has to be Zion. If your presidency of two or three people is not of one heart and one mind facing Christ, no amount of teacher-council scheduling will fix it. The first work is small, hidden, and unglamorous: praying together, counseling honestly, esteeming each other as Christ esteems each of you. D&C 82:19 names the operational test: "every man seeking the interest of his neighbor." If you are seeking your counselor's interest before your own, the cell is forming. If you are not, you are still pre-Zion.

**2. Make the teacher council a place where doctrine is lived between you, not just discussed.** The handbook format is a scaffold. What makes the meeting Zion-shaped is willingness to bear burdens, not adherence to the timing. If the counsel runs long because something true is happening, let it run. If the "learn something new" segment becomes ten minutes shorter, the trade is fine. The four practices of Acts 2:42 (doctrine, fellowship, breaking of bread, prayers) are what you are actually scaffolding. Schedule serves the practice; practice does not serve the schedule.

**3. Watch for pride before you watch for performance.** 4 Nephi 1:24 is the diagnostic. The first failure is internal. As you watch over your teachers (which is what *preside* means in the [art-of-presidency](art-of-presidency.md) study — Webster 1828's "to watch over"), watch for the small pride-tells: comparison between teachers, possessiveness about classes, irritation when someone teaches better than you do, defensiveness when feedback comes. Address those quietly and early. By the time pride becomes visible behavior, the cascade has started.

**4. Bear burdens vertically and horizontally as the [art-of-delegation](art-of-delegation.md) study laid out.** The teachers carry real weight: preparation, confrontation, the spiritual responsibility of standing in front of God's people week after week. Some of that weight you can lift directly (substituting, planning together, removing logistical friction). Some of it you can only share by being present to it, the way Christ in [Alma 7:11-12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/7?lang=eng&id=p11-p12#p11) takes on infirmities by experience. Both forms count. The president who only delegates and never bears is not presiding; the president who only bears and never delegates is exhausted and useless. Both at once, willingly, is the [shape of the Atonement at human scale](art-of-delegation.md).

**5. Cultivate the desire before you implement the structure.** Mosiah 18's people clapped for joy. The covenant landed because the desire was already there. If your teachers are not yet at "this is the desire of our hearts," then no teacher council format, no class-as-council aspiration, no quarterly meeting cadence will produce Zion. The first work in that case is not structural. It is invitation, witness, modeling. You teach what one-heartedness feels like by being one-hearted toward the teachers themselves. You demonstrate the council pattern by treating each meeting with them as a council — not as a downstream of decisions you have already made.

D&C 105:5 stands behind all five:

> "And Zion cannot be built up unless it is by the principles of the law of the celestial kingdom; otherwise I cannot receive her unto myself." — [D&C 105:5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/105?lang=eng&id=p5#p5)

There is no shortcut. A presidency that lives celestial law in miniature, esteeming and bearing and seeking each other's interest and watching for pride and cultivating desire, is a presidency the Lord can receive. A presidency that is administratively efficient but interiorly fractured is not Zion regardless of how well its meetings run.

## VII. Becoming — Next Sunday and Beyond

I am teaching Sunday School this coming Sunday. The class will be larger than the teacher council was. Most of the people in the room will not have been in the council meeting where doctrine moved between us last week. Section IV's inference about classes-as-councils does not get implemented in one Sunday by an act of will. The small thing first.

**One commitment for this Sunday:** When I teach, I will treat the class the way Mosiah 18:21 describes the covenant body — looking with one eye, hearts knit, in love one towards another. Practically, that means I will not perform a lesson at them. I will hold the doctrine in front of us and ask honest questions about how it lands in the lives of the people in the room. The hour is theirs to counsel as much as it is mine to teach.

**One commitment for the presidency:** Before our next presidency meeting, I will pray about whether we are one in the Section I sense — facing the same direction, not standing in the same place. If we are not, the next meeting needs to be about that and not about logistics. D&C 82:19's test will be the standard: am I seeking my counselors' interest, or am I seeking my own?

**One commitment for the teacher council, next time:** Less anxiety about the timing. More attention to whether the four practices of Acts 2:42 are actually present: doctrine, fellowship, breaking of bread (or its functional equivalent, mutual imparting), prayers. If those four are happening, the meeting is doing its work whether or not the segments balance to the minute.

**One thing to watch in myself:** the 4 Nephi 1:24 diagnostic. Pride. Comparison. The temptation to evaluate my teachers against each other, or my classes against other classes, or my presidency against other presidencies. That is the first failure. If I catch it in myself, I have a chance. If I don't, the cascade has a head start.

The waters of Mormon were a fountain in a forest, four hundred and fifty souls, no temple, no city, no organizational chart. They were Zion because their hearts were knit and they were willing to bear one another's burdens and they imparted what they had. We have more than they had. The question is whether we will use it for the same thing.
