# Science and Hypothesis — Henri Poincaré

## The core argument / thesis

Poincaré argues that the basic principles of geometry and mechanics are neither empirical facts nor purely logical necessities, but *conventions* that the mind adopts freely because they are convenient and fruitful. Arithmetic, by contrast, rests on a true *synthetic a priori* intuition—reasoning by recurrence—which is irreducible to logic and explicitly *not* a convention. Experiment guides our choice of conventions without imposing it, while the true object of science is not the intrinsic nature of things, but the enduring *relations* between things; what survives the rise and fall of physical theories are the equations expressing these relations, not the mechanical images or ontological claims attached to them.

---

## Structure

- **Part I: Number and Magnitude.** Poincaré first attacks the idea that mathematics is pure tautology. He shows that mathematical reasoning is fruitful because it relies on reasoning by recurrence (mathematical induction), a synthetic, intuitive leap that compresses an infinite cascade of syllogisms into a single act and cannot be reduced to formal logic. He then traces how the mind constructs the mathematical continuum to resolve the contradictions of the physical continuum, showing that magnitude and number are imposed frameworks rather than raw givens of sensation.

- **Part II: Space.** Having established that arithmetic is built on synthetic intuition, Poincaré extends the argument to geometry. He uses non-Euclidean geometries to demonstrate that geometrical axioms are neither synthetic *à priori* intuitions nor experimental truths, but conventions. Geometry is the study of the group of displacements of invariable solids; if there were no solid bodies, there would be no geometry. Space is a framework we impose, and experiment can never directly decide between Euclidean and non-Euclidean spaces because experiments concern bodies and their relations, not space itself.

- **Part III: Force.** Poincaré turns to classical mechanics and shows that its fundamental principles—such as inertia, force, and mass—follow a similar path. They begin as apparent experimental truths, but careful analysis reveals they have become disguised conventions and definitions, insulated from direct refutation by experiment. Even the principle of relative motion and the conservation of energy, when pushed to absolute generality, border on tautology or convention.

- **Part IV: Nature.** In the final part, Poincaré examines the method of physics proper. He defends the necessity of hypothesis and generalisation while distinguishing natural, indifferent, and real hypotheses. He argues that the ephemeral nature of physical theories does not mean bankruptcy, because what endures are the true *relations* captured by differential equations. He concludes with an examination of the calculus of probabilities, showing that it too rests on conventions guided by the "principle of sufficient reason" and the continuity of nature.

## Key passages

> "The aim of science is not things themselves, as the dogmatists in their simplicity imagine, but the relations between things; outside those relations there is no reality knowable."

*Science is not ontology; its legitimate object is the network of relations between phenomena.*

> "The essential characteristic of reasoning by recurrence is that it contains, condensed, so to speak, in a single formula, an infinite number of syllogisms."

*Mathematical induction is the non-analytical, creative act that allows the mind to pass from the finite to the infinite.*

> "The geometrical axioms are therefore neither synthetic à priori intuitions nor experimental facts. They are conventions."

*Geometry occupies a middle ground: it is neither imposed by pure reason nor dictated by the senses, but chosen for its convenience.*

> "If, then, there were no solid bodies in nature there would be no geometry."

*Geometry arises from the specific experience of compensating for the displacement of invariable solids, not from space as a pre-given intuition.*

> "The principles of dynamics appeared to us first as experimental truths, but we have been compelled to use them as definitions."

*Mechanical principles have gradually become conventions disguised as laws, which is why experiment can never overturn them.*

> "It cannot be said that this is reducing physical theories to simple practical recipes; these equations express relations, and if the equations remain true, it is because the relations preserve their reality."

*What survives theory change is not the physical imagery but the mathematical relations that correctly link phenomena.*

## Themes

- **Convention disguised as necessity.** Across geometry and mechanics, Poincaré uncovers a repeated pattern: principles that appear to be either empirical discoveries or logical necessities are in fact free conventions that experience has merely guided us to adopt. Arithmetic, however, escapes this classification; its foundation in reasoning by recurrence is a genuine synthetic intuition.
- **The primacy of relations over essences.** A recurring insistence that science cannot grasp things-in-themselves, only the stable relations between observables; equations are valuable because they encode these relations.
- **Mathematical recurrence and the infinite.** The creative power of mathematics stems from its ability to treat the infinite through induction, a faculty that formal logic alone cannot supply.
- **The empirical genesis and conventional status of space.** Geometry is born from the observation of solid bodies and the "group" of their displacements, yet it is not an experimental science because its objects are idealised and its axioms are chosen for simplicity.
- **The relativity of scientific frameworks.** Whether discussing Euclidean vs. non-Euclidean geometry, absolute vs. relative motion, or competing mechanical hypotheses, Poincaré shows that multiple frameworks can describe the same facts, and the "best" one is the most convenient, not the most "true."
- **Hypothesis as a necessary but dangerous tool.** Generalisation requires hypothesis, but only those that are verifiable and not overly numerous are safe; the true residue of even a falsified theory is the relation it correctly identified.

## Tensions & objections

### What the digest flattened or missed

1. **Arithmetic is not a convention.** The original digest incorrectly lumped arithmetic together with geometry and mechanics under the heading of "convention." Poincaré is explicit that reasoning by recurrence is "the exact type of the *à priori* synthetic intuition" and that "we cannot see in it a convention as in the case of the postulates of geometry." This three-tier epistemology—logical tautology, synthetic intuition (arithmetic), and convention (geometry/mechanics)—is central to the book's architecture, and blurring it misrepresents Poincaré's debt to and revision of Kant.

2. **The group-theoretic backbone.** Poincaré does not merely say that geometry comes from solids; he defines geometrical space through the structure of the group formed by the displacements of invariable solids. The digest mentions the group only in passing, but for Poincaré the *group* is what makes space homogeneous and isotropic, and what allows him to imagine alternative geometries by imagining alternative groups. The conventionality of geometry is inseparable from the conventionality of which group we select as our standard.

3. **How conventions are manufactured in mechanics.** The digest says mechanical principles "have become" conventions, but flattens Poincaré's precise mechanism: an approximate experimental law relating two real terms (A and B) is split by introducing an imaginary intermediate term C, which is *by definition* related to A in exactly the way the law requires. The law is thereby promoted to an absolute principle (A→C) while the residual empirical claim (C→B) remains approximate. This process is what shields the principle from refutation, and it is more specific than a vague "becoming conventional."

4. **Energy as tautology at the cosmic limit.** When the conservation of energy is extended to the whole universe in a deterministic system, Poincaré argues it collapses into the statement that "there is something which remains constant"—a tautology, since any deterministic system of *n* variables possesses *n–1* integrals. The digest says it "borders on tautology," but Poincaré's point is stronger: without independent parameters, the principle loses all definite meaning.

5. **Representative vs. geometrical space.** The digest skips Poincaré's detailed phenomenology of visual, tactile, and motor space, and the crucial distinction between the non-homogeneous, multi-dimensional "representative space" of our sensations and the homogeneous, three-dimensional "geometrical space" of the geometer. This undermines his claim that geometrical space is a constructed framework rather than a form of sensibility.

### The strongest objection (the null case)

Poincaré's argument for the conventionality of geometry rests on the claim that no possible experiment can ever decide between Euclidean and non-Euclidean geometry, because one can always preserve Euclidean space by adjusting physical laws (e.g., by claiming that light rays curve or that bodies deform). From this underdetermination, he concludes that geometry is a convention, not an empirical fact.

The strongest objection is that **underdetermination does not entail conventionality**, and later physics has shown that the supposed underdetermination is illusory. General Relativity demonstrated that spacetime geometry is a dynamical, empirically testable component of physics: mass-energy directly determines a non-Euclidean geometry, and this geometry predicts observable phenomena (gravitational light bending, perihelion precession, Shapiro delay) that a Euclidean physics with compensating hypotheses cannot replicate without extreme, *ad hoc* complexity. The "most convenient" system turned out to be non-Euclidean not because of a free choice of language, but because a non-Euclidean geometry is part of the simplest and most predictive total theory.

More fundamentally, Poincaré illicitly slides from "not directly verifiable in isolation" to "conventional." That a proposition is deeply entrenched in our theoretical framework, or that it can be preserved in the face of recalcitrant data by modifying auxiliary assumptions, does not make it a definition. It makes it a **framework-level empirical commitment**. The fact that we *can* treat force as mass × acceleration by definition, or mass as a convenient coefficient, does not prove these are mere stipulations; it proves only that in any rich theoretical network, concepts are holistically related. The null case insists that the resilience of a principle to refutation reflects its centrality in our best account of nature, not its emptiness. If a "convention" systematically yields successful predictions while alternative conventions fail, then calling it conventional is a verbal maneuver: what Poincaré labels convenience is better described as **approximate truth**.

## What's worth learning — and what we could do with it

1. **Audit your own "necessary truths."** Poincaré's method is portable: take any principle you treat as axiomatic (in software architecture, team process, or personal habit) and ask whether it is a logical tautology, an empirical regularity, or a disguised convention. If you cannot imagine an alternative that would work nearly as well, you have probably stopped noticing that you chose it. Try explicitly running a "Poincaré audit" on your team's most sacred workflow: state the principle, construct a minimally different alternative, and ask what experiment could decide between them. If none exists, you are holding a convention—treat it as one and revisit it periodically.

2. **Separate relations from imagery.** When a model or theory is overturned, the equations that correctly predicted phenomena often survive even when the mechanical picture attached to them is abandoned. In practice, this means documenting *invariant relationships* separately from *ontological commitments* in any system you build. For a codebase, this is the difference between "this service scales because it is stateless" (ontological claim) and "this service scales because request latency is independent of concurrent user count" (relational claim). The latter survives refactoring; the former may not.

3. **Distinguish the three epistemic tiers.** Poincaré's taxonomy—logical tautology, synthetic intuition, convention—is a sharper scalpel than the common "fact vs. opinion" binary. Apply it to arguments in technical reviews or policy debates. Is a claim being defended as self-evident (tautology), as empirically grounded (synthetic), or as pragmatically chosen (convention)? Misidentifying the tier is the root of most intractable disagreements. A convention defended as a tautology becomes dogma; a tautology treated as empirical becomes pseudo-science.

4. **Watch for the C-term maneuver.** Poincaré's specific mechanism for manufacturing conventions—splitting an approximate law A→B into a definition A→C plus a residual claim C→B—is alive in modern discourse. In software, it appears when a metric is reified into a target ("velocity is productivity"), splitting an approximate correlation into a definitional identity plus a residual empirical claim that is never tested. Name the intermediate term when you see it; ask what would falsify the residual claim, and whether anyone is still checking.

5. **Build with group structure in mind.** Poincaré's insight that geometry is the study of a group of displacements is not mere abstraction. Any system with reversible, composable operations has a group structure, and the choice of that structure is a design decision with consequences. In distributed systems, the "group" might be the set of permitted state transitions; in data modeling, the set of valid transformations. Ask what makes your chosen group "convenient" and whether an alternative group would simplify the system. The exercise often reveals that your "natural" architecture is one convention among several.

6. **Preserve the residue of falsified theories.** When a model fails, extract the relational claims it got right before discarding it. Poincaré's physics survived theory change because its equations encoded real relations even when its mechanical pictures did not. For a substrate or a team, this means maintaining a "relation registry": a running list of invariant patterns observed across experiments, independent of the models that produced them. The next theory can inherit these relations without inheriting the ontology.
