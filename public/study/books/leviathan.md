# Leviathan — Study Digest

**Author:** Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679)
**Published:** 1651
**Source:** [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/3207/3207-h/3207-h.htm)
**Edition consulted:** NLNRAC Hackett edition excerpts

## Structure

The work divides into four parts:

| Part | Chapters | Subject |
|------|----------|---------|
| I: Of Man | 1–16 | Sense, imagination, passions, state of nature, laws of nature |
| II: Of Common-wealth | 17–31 | Generation of commonwealth, sovereign rights, liberty of subjects, dissolution |
| III: Of a Christian Common-wealth | 32–43 | Scripture, church, kingdom of God |
| IV: Of the Kingdome of Darknesse | 44–47 | Attack on papacy and scholastic philosophy |

## Core Argument

Hobbes argues from a materialist psychology of human nature to a political conclusion: only an absolute sovereign can rescue humanity from the state of nature, which is a war of every man against every man.

### The State of Nature (Ch. 13)

Without a common power to keep them in awe, humans exist in a condition where:

> *"The life of man [is] solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."*

Three causes drive men to conflict:
1. **Competition** — for gain
2. **Diffidence** — for safety
3. **Glory** — for reputation

In this state, every person has a *right of nature* (jus naturale): *"the liberty each man hath to use his own power, as he will himself, for the preservation of his own nature; that is to say, of doing whatever he in his own judgment and reason shall think best."*

### The Laws of Nature (Ch. 14–15)

Reason discovers precepts for peace:

> *"The first and Fundamentall Law of Nature; which is, to seek Peace, and follow it."*

And: *"That a man do not do that which he thinks not to be convenient to be done by each and every one of the rest."* (the golden rule)

But Hobbes is clear: *"Laws of Nature oblige in foro interno [in conscience], but not in foro externo [in action]"* without security. Covenants without enforcement are empty:

> *"Covenants, without the Sword, are but Words."*

### The Commonwealth (Ch. 17)

The solution is a covenant of every man with every man to authorize a sovereign:

> *"There is no way, whereby men can be secured but by a Power visible to all men..."*

The sovereign's power is absolute and indivisible. The sovereign is not a party to the covenant (in commonwealths by institution) and therefore cannot break it.

### Two Modes of Generation (Ch. 17–20)

- **By Institution:** Subjects covenant with each other to authorize a sovereign. The sovereign is not a party to the covenant.
- **By Acquisition:** Dominion through conquest, where the vanquished submit to the victor out of fear of death. Here the relationship is contractual between conqueror and conquered.

### The Inalienable Right of Self-Preservation (Ch. 14.8, 21)

> *"a man cannot lay down the right of resisting them that assault him by force, to take away his life."*

The sovereign's power is qualified in the most important case: the subject may always resist being killed. Fear and liberty are consistent — fear of the sovereign is the mechanism of peace, not a bug but the operating principle.

## Key Themes

### 1. Materialist Psychology

Hobbes's materialism is a methodological commitment for political philosophy. He reduces human motivation to motion caused by sense impressions, driven by appetite (desire) and aversion. Passions are the springs of action; reason is the "scout" or "counselor" that finds paths to satisfy passions. Hobbes allows for limited uses of terms like "spirit" (Ch. 34) but attacks the scholastic notion of "incorporeal substance" as meaningless (Ch. 46).

### 2. The Enforcement Problem

Morality without enforcement is inert. The laws of nature are rationally discoverable but practically indeterminate without a sword to back them. This is Hobbes's central diagnostic insight: the problem of politics is not primarily moral education but institutional design.

### 3. Fear as Politically Constitutive

Fear is not merely pathological in Hobbes — it is the operating principle of peace. Chapter 21 explicitly argues "Feare and Liberty [are] Consistent." The central paradox of Hobbesian politics: freedom through subjection, security through fear.

### 4. Part IV's Structural Role

Part IV is not merely polemical. Hobbes's case for absolute sovereignty *depends* on showing that the real threat to commonwealths is not popular liberty but *ecclesiastical power claiming independent authority*. Without Part IV, the argument for why sovereignty must be absolute (and why it must control doctrine) loses its target.

## Tensions and Corrections

### What Earlier Readings Flattened

| Claim | Correction |
|-------|-----------|
| "The sovereign is not a party to the contract and therefore cannot break it" | True only for commonwealths by institution. By acquisition (Ch. 20), the relationship is contractual. |
| "Sovereign's power is absolute and indivisible" | Qualified by the inalienable right of self-preservation (Ch. 14.8, 21). |
| "Morality is not self-enforcing" | True, but Hobbes also says laws of nature oblige "in conscience alwayes" (Ch. 15) — they have moral force even without the sword. |
| Part IV is "polemical" | It's structurally essential to the argument for why sovereignty must control doctrine. |

## The Null Case — Strongest Objections

### A: The Self-Defeat Problem

Hobbes argues that reason dictates we seek peace and covenant with others. But the same reason that leads us to covenant also tells us that **we cannot know whether others will keep their covenant**. If reason cannot resolve this uncertainty, then reason cannot ground the obligation to covenant in the first place. The sovereign is supposed to solve the enforcement problem, but the sovereign himself is subject to the same passions (Ch. 10: *"Power begeth a desire of more power"*). The Leviathan is itself a man — or assembly of men — and thus subject to the same competitive, diffident, glory-driven dynamics Hobbes identifies in Chapter 13. **The sovereign doesn't escape the state of nature; he embodies it at scale.**

### B: The Incommensurability of Security and Obedience

Hobbes claims the right of self-preservation is inalienable (Ch. 14.8) and that subjects may resist being killed. But if the sovereign's primary function is to provide security, and the subject may resist the sovereign when that security is withdrawn, then **the obligation to obey is conditional on the sovereign's effectiveness**. This collapses the distinction between Hobbesian absolutism and Lockean conditional obligation. If obedience depends on protection, then the sovereign is accountable — which contradicts the indivisibility of sovereignty.

### C: The Epistemic Problem of "Right Reason"

Hobbes derives the laws of nature from reason (Ch. 14–15). But he also argues that in the state of nature, *"every man is judge of what is necessary to his preservation"* (Ch. 14). If each person's reason can err about what is necessary for peace, and there is no higher authority to adjudicate (because the sovereign doesn't exist yet), then **the laws of nature are rationally binding but practically indeterminate**. The whole edifice rests on the assumption that humans can reliably use reason to recognize their interest in peace — but Hobbes's own psychology (Ch. 6–11) shows that passion routinely overrides reason.

### D: The Regress of Authorization

The commonwealth is generated by covenant of "every man with every man" (Ch. 17). But for a covenant to be binding, there must already be some framework that makes covenants meaningful — namely, the laws of nature, which are themselves only binding "in conscience" (Ch. 15). **Hobbes needs morality to ground the state, but claims the state grounds morality.** This is not a contradiction Hobbes adequately resolves.

## What's Worth Learning — and What We Could Do With It

### 1. Start from clear definitions, then deduce

Hobbes's method — define terms precisely, then build by logical deduction — is a template for rigorous thinking in any domain. **Actionable:** When analyzing any complex system (an organization, a codebase, a policy), begin by writing down explicit definitions of the key terms. Don't proceed until everyone agrees on what the words mean.

### 2. Distinguish internal obligation from external compliance

Hobbes's insight that laws of nature oblige "in foro interno" but not "in foro externo" without enforcement maps onto a practical distinction: **people can believe something is right while having no incentive to do it.** **Actionable:** When designing systems (technical or social), don't assume shared values produce shared behavior. Always ask: what enforces compliance when incentives diverge?

### 3. Fear is not the opposite of freedom — it can be its precondition

Hobbes shows that predictable consequences (even feared ones) create the space for liberty. This is counterintuitive but practically vital. **Actionable:** In any collaborative environment, clarity about consequences is not oppressive — it's what makes safe experimentation possible. Write down the "sword" (the enforcement mechanism) explicitly, so people know the boundaries within which they're free.

### 4. Watch for the sovereign's self-defeat

The null case reveals a pattern: **any authority built to solve coordination problems inherits the same coordination problems.** The Leviathan is made of the same material as the state of nature. **Actionable:** When designing governance (for a team, a project, or this substrate), don't assume the authority you create escapes the dynamics it's meant to resolve. Build in mechanisms for the sovereign to be checked by the very principles that justified it.

### 5. The fiction of personation is a template for analyzing institutions

Hobbes's deepest insight may not be that we need a sovereign, but that *all* authority rests on a fiction — the personation of the multitude in one. Someone always speaks "for" the group, and that representation is always partial. **Actionable:** When any institution claims to speak for "the people," "the team," or "the users," ask: who authorized this personation? What interests are being represented, and which are being silenced? This is a lens for analyzing any claim to legitimacy.

### 6. Even rigorous deduction can be built on unstable foundations

Hobbes's failure to notice that his own premises undermine his conclusions (the self-defeat problem) is a warning about intellectual hubris. **Actionable:** After building any argument or system, deliberately try to break it using its own premises. The strongest critique of any position comes from within its own framework, not from outside it. Make "null-case testing" a regular practice.

---

## Final Assessment

**Strengths:** Hobbes's diagnostic insight — that coordination requires enforcement, not just moral agreement — is enduring. His method of clear definitions and deductive reasoning remains a model. His treatment of fear as constitutive rather than pathological is genuinely original.

**Weaknesses:** The self-defeat problem is fatal if taken seriously. Hobbes cannot have it both ways: the sovereign is either subject to the same passions (and thus the same problems) or not (and his psychology is wrong). The regress of authorization remains unresolved.

**Worth reading for:** The clarity of argument, the honesty about human nature, and the template it provides for analyzing any system of authority. Not for the conclusion (absolutism), but for the diagnosis (the enforcement problem) and the method (deduction from clear premises).
