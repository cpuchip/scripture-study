# How Is It Done? — Part 2: The Prophetic Witness

*A companion to [How Is It Done? Part 1](how-is-it-done.md), which traced the Atonement's mechanics through scripture. This study asks: what do modern prophets see in those mechanics that the text alone doesn't show us?*

---

## The Binding Question

The [first study](how-is-it-done.md) asked Enos's question — "Lord, how is it done?" — and traced the answer through scripture: who Jesus is, what He took upon Himself, the comprehension principle, the cost, and the result in us. That study drew on one conference talk: Holland's "None Were with Him." One voice in 54 years of apostolic witness.

The question now: **What do modern prophets and apostles see in the mechanics of the Atonement that scripture alone doesn't show us?**

Specifically: How do they theologize the Father's withdrawal at the cross? What do they add to the comprehension principle? How do they understand the Father's role — not as absent observer but as anguished participant? And do they agree with each other, or does the apostolic witness contain tensions the text smooths over?

Matthew 27:46 has been cited in conference 33 times since Brigham Young — from the Journal of Discourses through Holland's 2024 talk. That's not a footnote. That's a theological lineage. This study follows that lineage.

---

## Part I — The Talmage Foundation

Every modern apostle who speaks about the divine withdrawal at the cross begins — whether they know it or not — with James E. Talmage. In *Jesus the Christ* (1916), Talmage wrote the passage that would echo through a century of conference addresses:

> "In that bitterest hour the dying Christ was alone. [So] that the supreme sacrifice of the Son might be consummated in all its fulness, the Father seems to have withdrawn … His immediate Presence, leaving to the Savior of men the glory of complete victory over the forces of sin and death." (*Jesus the Christ*, p. 661; quoted in [David B. Haight, "Jesus of Nazareth," April 1994](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1994/04/jesus-of-nazareth?lang=eng))

Three things about this passage matter for everything that follows:

1. **"Seems to have withdrawn"** — Talmage hedges. He doesn't say the Father *did* withdraw; he says the Father *seems to have* withdrawn. The distinction is enormous and will echo forward.
2. **"His immediate Presence"** — Talmage distinguishes between God's general sustaining power and His *immediate* Presence. The withdrawal is of presence, not of being.
3. **"The glory of complete victory"** — The withdrawal *served* something. It wasn't punitive. It was for victory.

This language set the terms for the theological conversation for a hundred years. When [Neal A. Maxwell says "apparent withdrawal"](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1989/04/irony-the-crust-on-the-bread-of-adversity?lang=eng) in 1989, he's reading Talmage. When [Jeffrey R. Holland says "briefly withdrew the comfort of His Spirit"](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2009/04/none-were-with-him?lang=eng) in 2009, he's building on Talmage. The foundation is one man's careful, hedged, scholarly sentence — and everything built on it has honored the hedge.

---

## Part II — Maxwell's Architecture

If Talmage laid the foundation, Neal A. Maxwell built the theology. Across five conference talks spanning twenty-two years (1981–2003), Maxwell developed a theological architecture for understanding Atonement mechanics that no other apostle has matched in scope.

### 1981: The First Connection

In his very first address as a member of the Quorum of the Twelve, Maxwell already had the comprehension principle in view:

> "In ways we cannot comprehend, our sicknesses and infirmities were borne by Him even before these were borne by us. The very weight of our combined sins caused Him to descend below all. We have never been, nor will we be, in depths such as He has known. Thus His atonement made His empathy and His capacity to succor us perfect." ([Neal A. Maxwell, "O, Divine Redeemer," October 1981](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1981/10/o-divine-redeemer?lang=eng))

And in a line no one else has used since — the Abraham/Isaac echo applied to the cross:

> "There was no ram in the thicket at Calvary to spare Him, this Friend of Abraham and Isaac."

The theological chain is here from the beginning: *descended below → comprehended → enabled to succor*. Maxwell saw it at the start of his ministry and would sharpen it for two decades.

### 1985: The Hinge Was Submissiveness

In "Willing to Submit," Maxwell adds what might be his most original contribution — the idea that Christ's own experience of the Atonement included *surprise*:

> "Later, in Gethsemane, the suffering Jesus began to be 'sore amazed' ([Mark 14:33](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/mark/14?lang=eng&id=p33#p33)), or, in the Greek, 'awestruck' and 'astonished.' Imagine, Jehovah, the Creator of this and other worlds, 'astonished'! Jesus knew cognitively what He must do, but not experientially. He had never personally known the exquisite and exacting process of an atonement before. Thus, when the agony came in its fulness, it was so much, much worse than even He with his unique intellect had ever imagined!" ([Neal A. Maxwell, "Willing to Submit," April 1985](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1985/04/willing-to-submit?lang=eng))

The scale:

> "His suffering — as it were, *enormity* multiplied by *infinity* — evoked His later soul-cry on the cross, and it was a cry of forsakenness."

And then the hinge — what made the Atonement *work*:

> "The wondrous and glorious Atonement was the central act in all of human history. It was the hinge on which all else that finally matters turned. But it turned upon Jesus' spiritual submissiveness!"

Scripture tells us what Christ suffered. Maxwell tells us *how He bore it*: through submissiveness so deep that the word *nevertheless* became its signature. "Nevertheless not as I will, but as thou wilt" ([Matthew 26:39](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/26?lang=eng&id=p39#p39)).

### 1989: The Defining Paragraph

In "Irony," Maxwell writes what may be the single most important non-scriptural paragraph on Atonement mechanics in the entire conference record:

> "We come now to the last and most terrible irony of Jesus: His feeling forsaken at the apogee of His agony on Calvary. The apparent withdrawal of the Father's spirit then evoked the greatest soul cry in human history. This deprivation had never happened to Christ before — never. Yet, thereby, Jesus became a fully comprehending Christ and was enabled to be a fully succoring Savior. Moreover, even in that darkest hour, while feeling forsaken, Jesus submitted Himself to the Father." ([Neal A. Maxwell, "Irony: The Crust on the Bread of Adversity," April 1989](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1989/04/irony-the-crust-on-the-bread-of-adversity?lang=eng))

This passage connects three things no speaker had linked before:
- The withdrawal was the *last irony* — the one without parallel
- The result was **comprehension** — not just endurance
- Comprehension enabled **succoring**

And the closing of that talk opens a window into the Father's grief — a theme Maxwell would develop and Holland would inherit:

> "'Glory be to the Father' — first, for rearing such an Incomparable Son. Second, 'Glory be to the Father' for allowing His special Son to suffer and to be sacrificed for all of us. On Judgment Day, brothers and sisters, will any of us want to rush forward to tell our Father how we, as parents, suffered when we watched our children suffer?"

### 2001–2003: The Theology Matures

By 2001, Maxwell states the comprehension principle as straightforward doctrine:

> "Uniquely, atoning Jesus also 'descended below all things, in that he comprehended all things' ([D&C 88:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/88?lang=eng&id=p6#p6)). How deep that descent into despair and abysmal agony must have been! He did it to rescue us and in order to comprehend human suffering." ([Neal A. Maxwell, "Plow in Hope," April 2001](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2001/04/plow-in-hope?lang=eng))

But Maxwell also does something remarkable here — he names the limit of what prophets know about the Father's experience:

> "On occasion, the God of heaven has wept (see [Moses 7:28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/pgp/moses/7?lang=eng&id=p28#p28)). One ponders, therefore, the agonies of Jesus' infinite Atonement and the feelings of the Father — for His Son and for us. There are no instructive, relevant revelations, but our finite, emotional extrapolations come flooding in anyway!"

"There are no instructive, relevant revelations." This is an apostle saying: *we don't have a revealed answer to this question.* What Holland, Scott, Maxwell himself, and others offer about the Father's experience during the Atonement is inference — powerful, Spirit-guided inference, but inference. This sentence is an act of extraordinary intellectual honesty, and it sets a proper boundary around everything that follows in our study.

By 2003, the formula is so established it shows up as a single sentence in a talk about Joseph Smith:

> "Jesus, who performed the 'infinite atonement,' thereby suffered infinitely and is a fully comprehending Savior, having 'descended below all things' and 'comprehended all things.'" ([Neal A. Maxwell, "How Choice a Seer," October 2003](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2003/10/how-choice-a-seer?lang=eng))

What started as a fresh theological insight in 1981 had become, by 2003, axiomatic — something Maxwell could invoke in a single sentence without needing to argue it.

## Part III — The Father's Anguish

When Matthew records the cry from the cross — "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" ([Matthew 27:46](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/27?lang=eng&id=p46#p46)) — the camera is on the Son. But several modern apostles have turned the camera toward the Father, asking: What did this cost *Him*?

### Holland: "His Every Impulse"

In his 1999 Easter talk, Holland offers the most vivid portrait of the Father's experience at the cross:

> "I am a father, inadequate to be sure, but I cannot comprehend the burden it must have been for God in His heaven to witness the deep suffering and Crucifixion of His Beloved Son in such a manner. His every impulse and instinct *must* have been to stop it, to send angels to intervene — but He did not intervene. He endured what He saw because it was the only way that a saving, vicarious payment could be made for the sins of all His other children, from Adam and Eve to the end of the world." ([Jeffrey R. Holland, "The Hands of the Fathers," April 1999](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1999/04/the-hands-of-the-fathers?lang=eng))

Holland speaks as a father, not only as a theologian. The Abraham/Isaac narrative runs beneath this passage: the Father who *could* send a ram but chose not to. Not because He didn't love His Son, but because He loved *all* His children. The verb "endured" — typically reserved for the Son — is applied here to the Father.

### Scott: "He Made It Possible"

A year after Holland's "None Were with Him," Richard G. Scott offers his own reading of the Father's role. Where Holland emphasizes the Father's nearness-in-withdrawal, Scott emphasizes the Father's *wisdom*:

> "I don't believe Father in Heaven forsook His Son on the cross. I do believe the cry was motivated when that Son felt removed the sustaining support He had always enjoyed from His Father. His Father recognized that the Savior needed to accomplish the Atonement totally and completely on His own, without external support. The Father did not abandon His Son. He made it possible for His perfect Son to win the eternal fruits of the Atonement." ([Richard G. Scott, "He Lives! All Glory to His Name!" April 2010](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2010/04/he-lives-all-glory-to-his-name?lang=eng))

Note Scott's language: "made it possible." The withdrawal was *enabling*, not punitive. The Father didn't withdraw because His Son failed — He withdrew because His Son needed to succeed *alone*. This reframes the entire moment from abandonment to investment.

Scott also names the three challenges of the Atonement as he understands them: (1) the crushing responsibility — if done imperfectly, no one returns; (2) personally feeling the consequences of all sins; and (3) enduring Satan's attack while pressed to the limit. It is in the *third* challenge, at the extremity of capacity, that "His Father allowed Him to shoulder the onerous responsibility with only His own strength and capacity." The withdrawal came at the worst possible moment *by design*.

### Maxwell: "No Instructive Revelations"

And behind both Holland and Scott stands Maxwell's honest confession: "There are no instructive, relevant revelations" about the Father's feelings. What we have is inference — "our finite, emotional extrapolations." This doesn't make the inference wrong. It makes it honest.

## Part IV — Holland's Summit: "None Were with Him"

Holland's April 2009 General Conference address represents the theological summit of this lineage. Everything Talmage framed, Maxwell built, and Holland himself explored in 1999 converges here.

The architecture of the talk is itself significant. Holland traces a shrinking circle: the public reception on Palm Sunday → religious leaders who reject Him → political rulers who condemn Him → the crowd that chooses Barabbas → Judas who betrays → Apostles who flee → Peter who denies → and finally, the Father:

> "I speak very carefully, even reverently, of what may have been the most difficult moment in all of this solitary journey to atonement. I speak of those final moments for which Jesus must have been prepared intellectually and physically but which He may not have fully anticipated emotionally and spiritually — that concluding descent into the paralyzing despair of divine withdrawal when He cries in *ultimate* loneliness, 'My God, my God, why hast *thou* forsaken me?'" ([Jeffrey R. Holland, "None Were with Him," April 2009](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2009/04/none-were-with-him?lang=eng))

"Paralyzing despair of divine withdrawal." That phrase holds the full weight of the tradition.

Then Holland offers the paradox at the heart of this theology:

> "With all the conviction of my soul I testify that He *did* please His Father perfectly and that a perfect Father did *not* forsake His Son in that hour. Indeed, it is my personal belief that in all of Christ's mortal ministry the Father may never have been closer to His Son than in these agonizing final moments of suffering."

The Father *never closer* and the Son *most alone*. Both true at the same time. The proximity was real; the withdrawal of comfort was also real. Holland continues:

> "Nevertheless, that the supreme sacrifice of His Son might be as complete as it was voluntary and solitary, the Father briefly withdrew from Jesus the comfort of His Spirit, the support of His personal presence."

"The comfort of His Spirit, the support of His personal presence" — Holland is precise about what was withdrawn. Not the Father's love. Not His attention. Not His existence. The *comfort* of His Spirit and the *support* of His presence. The Father was there. He simply stopped making His presence felt.

And the purpose:

> "It was required, indeed it was central to the significance of the Atonement, that this perfect Son who had never spoken ill nor done wrong nor touched an unclean thing had to know how the rest of humankind — us, all of us — would feel when we did commit such sins. For His Atonement to be infinite and eternal, He had to feel what it was like to die not only physically but spiritually, to sense what it was like to have the divine Spirit withdraw, leaving one feeling totally, abjectly, hopelessly alone."

This is Maxwell's comprehension principle restated in Holland's language — but now with an explicit connection to spiritual death. Christ had to experience what sinners experience: the withdrawal of divine presence. Not because He sinned, but because without that experience, His comprehension would have been incomplete and His succoring capacity imperfect.

Note "my personal belief" — Holland marks this explicitly as testimony, not doctrinal proclamation. He is offering his apostolic witness, not defining doctrine. This matters.

## Part V — The Broader Witness

The Talmage-Maxwell-Holland lineage is the theological backbone, but they aren't the only voices.

### McConkie: The Gethsemane-Calvary Recurrence

Bruce R. McConkie's final testimony, "The Purifying Power of Gethsemane" (April 1985), doesn't directly theologize the withdrawal. But he makes a startling claim:

> "And truly he was, for while he was hanging on the cross for another three hours, from noon to 3:00 p.m., all the infinite agonies and merciless pains of Gethsemane recurred." ([Bruce R. McConkie, "The Purifying Power of Gethsemane," April 1985](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1985/04/the-purifying-power-of-gethsemane?lang=eng))

If the agonies of Gethsemane *recurred* on the cross, then the cross wasn't only about physical suffering and the withdrawal — it was about the full weight of all human sin being borne *again*, this time in public, nailed down, exposed. McConkie also establishes the epistemic boundary that runs through all these talks: "We do not know, we cannot tell, no mortal mind can conceive the full import of what Christ did in Gethsemane."

### Haight: Talmage Preserved

David B. Haight's "Jesus of Nazareth" (April 1994) quotes Talmage and Farrar extensively, narrating the Passion without adding original theological interpretation. This is important in its own right: Haight is the tradition-bearer, passing along the received understanding for a new generation. Where Maxwell builds on Talmage and Holland reimagines him, Haight simply quotes him. All three approaches are valid.

### Bateman: The Individual Line

Merrill J. Bateman's "A Pattern for All" (October 2005) moves the Atonement from mass to individual:

> "For many years I thought of the Savior's experience in the garden and on the cross as places where a large mass of sin was heaped upon Him. Through the words of Alma, Abinadi, Isaiah, and other prophets, however, my view has changed. Instead of an impersonal mass of sin, there was a long line of people." ([Merrill J. Bateman, "A Pattern for All," October 2005](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2005/10/a-pattern-for-all?lang=eng))

His conclusion grounds the comprehension principle in intimacy:

> "The Atonement was an intimate, personal experience in which Jesus came to know how to help each of us."

This echoes the "one by one" pattern in [3 Nephi 17:21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/17?lang=eng&id=p21#p21) — the Resurrected Christ blessing the children individually. Bateman says the suffering was equally individual: not abstract, not aggregate, but personal.

### Oaks: Maxwell Acknowledged

Dallin H. Oaks explicitly credits Maxwell in "Strengthened by the Atonement of Jesus Christ" (October 2015):

> "Our Savior has revealed that He 'descended below all things' ([D&C 88:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/88?lang=eng&id=p6#p6)). As Elder Neal A. Maxwell taught, 'Having descended below all things, He comprehends, perfectly and personally, the full range of human suffering.'" ([Dallin H. Oaks, "Strengthened by the Atonement of Jesus Christ," October 2015](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2015/10/strengthened-by-the-atonement-of-jesus-christ?lang=eng))

Oaks also asks a question others haven't: who are "His people" in Alma's promise that Christ would "succor his people"? His answer: everyone. The succoring is universal, not covenantal. This broadens the comprehension principle from a theological insight into a lived reality for all of humanity.

## Part VI — What Do They Add?

The [first study](how-is-it-done.md) asked Enos's question and found answers in scripture. What do these prophetic voices add that scripture alone doesn't show us?

**1. The withdrawal was purposeful, not punitive.** Scripture records the cry — "Why hast thou forsaken me?" — and leaves the reader to wonder: *Did* God forsake His Son? Every modern apostle who addresses this says no. The withdrawal was deliberate, temporary, and served the Atonement's purposes. Talmage says the Father withdrew to give the Son "the glory of complete victory." Holland says it was "required" so Christ would know the experience of sinners. Scott says the Father "made it possible" for the Son to win alone. The withdrawal was an act of love, not absence.

**2. The comprehension principle.** [D&C 88:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/88?lang=eng&id=p6#p6) says Christ "descended below all things, in that he comprehended all things." [Alma 7:11–12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/7?lang=eng&id=p11-p12#p11) says He took upon Himself our pains and sicknesses "that his bowels may be filled with mercy, according to the flesh, that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people." Scripture presents these as facts. Maxwell showed they form a *mechanism*: descent → comprehension → empathy → succoring. The suffering wasn't incidental to the Atonement; it was *how* Christ became qualified to help us. Holland added that the withdrawal itself was *part of* the comprehension — without experiencing spiritual abandonment, Christ's understanding of sinners' experience would have been incomplete.

**3. The Father's anguish.** Scripture is silent on what the Father experienced during the Atonement. The prophetic witness fills that silence — cautiously. Holland imagines the Father's "every impulse" to intervene. Maxwell asks whether any of us will rush to tell God how *we* suffered watching our children. Scott attributes wisdom to the Father's choice: He "recognized" what the Son needed. Maxwell warns that "there are no instructive, relevant revelations" on this point. That honesty makes the inference more — not less — trustworthy.

**4. Christ's surprise.** Maxwell's observation that Jesus was "sore amazed" — awestruck, astonished — adds a dimension scripture implies but doesn't amplify. Christ knew intellectually what He would face, but "when the agony came in its fulness, it was so much, much worse than even He with his unique intellect had ever imagined." This means the Atonement wasn't performed on autopilot. It required real-time choosing, real-time faithfulness, what Maxwell calls "deep, divine determination."

**5. The individual nature of His suffering.** Bateman's shift from "impersonal mass of sin" to "long line of people" is a prophetic addition. Combined with 3 Nephi 17's "one by one" pattern, it means the comprehension wasn't a global download — it was an individual encounter with every human life.

**6. The pastoral bridge.** Robert D. Hales is the only voice in this study who turns the Father's withdrawal directly into pastoral counsel for ordinary suffering. His wealthy-father parable reframes apparent abandonment as deliberate preparation — a parent who withdraws visible support so a child can grow. This is where theology lands in the pew.

## Part VII — Do They Agree?

It would be easier — and less honest — to present these voices as a unified choir. They mostly harmonize. But there are tensions worth noticing.

### "Apparent" vs. "Actual" Withdrawal

Maxwell consistently says *"apparent* withdrawal" — language that implies the Father's distance was subjective, experienced by Christ but not objectively real. Holland says the Father "briefly withdrew from Jesus the comfort of His Spirit, the support of His personal presence" — language that describes an actual act, something the Father *did*. Talmage sits between: "seems to have withdrawn." These aren't contradictory — Holland and Maxwell may be describing the same reality from different angles — but the language matters. Was the Father's withdrawal something He actively *performed*, or something Christ *perceived* in the extremity of His suffering?

### What Exactly Was Withdrawn?

Each speaker uses slightly different words. Talmage: "His immediate Presence." Maxwell: "the Father's spirit" (lowercase). Holland: "the comfort of His Spirit, the support of His personal presence" (uppercase Spirit). Scott: "the sustaining support." These could describe the same thing or could imply different theologies. If "Spirit" means the Holy Ghost, that's one claim. If "spirit" means something like God's sustaining power or divine fellowship, that's another.

### "I Don't Believe He Forsook" vs. "Why Hast Thou Forsaken Me?"

Both Holland and Scott explicitly state that God did *not* forsake Christ. But the scriptural text — Christ's own words — is a cry of forsakenness. The resolution, implicit in all these talks, is that the *experience* was real even if the *abandonment* wasn't. Christ genuinely felt forsaken. The Father was genuinely present. Both can be true because what was withdrawn was the experiential evidence of presence, not presence itself.

This is theologically important because it mirrors our own experience. When we feel the Spirit withdraw — through sin, depression, or the dark nights of discipleship — we are experiencing something real even if God has not actually left us. The apostolic witness on the cross *validates* the feeling of forsakenness without *confirming* the abandonment. That distinction is pastoral gold.

### The Voice Nobody Cites: Mosiah 15:5

One notable absence in this prophetic lineage: Abinadi's teaching that "the will of the Son being swallowed up in the will of the Father" ([Mosiah 15:5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15?lang=eng&id=p5#p5)). This verse frames the Atonement not as the Father withdrawing from the Son, but as the Son's will being *consumed* by the Father's will — a total absorption of one into the other. None of the voices in this study engage Mosiah 15:5 in relationship to the forsakenness. It sits as an unexplored counterpoint: maybe the cry from the cross wasn't about distance at all, but about *completion* — the final swallowing-up of the Son's independent will into the Father's purpose. That reading is speculative, but Abinadi's absence from this conversation is notable.

## Part VIII — When We Feel Forsaken

Of all the voices in this study, Robert D. Hales may be the one who speaks most directly to the person sitting alone in a pew wondering: *Has God forgotten me?*

> "In the last moments upon the cross Jesus asked His Father a very simple question: 'Why hast thou forsaken me?' Are there times in our lives when we think that we have been forsaken by God, or by our fellow men, or by our families? That is the moment when we have to turn our thoughts back to Christ and endure to the end." ([Robert D. Hales, "Lessons from the Atonement," October 1985](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1985/10/lessons-from-the-atonement-that-help-us-to-endure-to-the-end?lang=eng))

And then the parable. A wealthy father whose daughter had gone through a divorce. He could have provided everything — but he withdrew his support so she could learn to stand. The family's reaction mirrored the cry from the cross: "Why would Father do this now when we need him the most?"

> "But you see, he was preparing them that they might be strengthened by providing for their own needs, so that when they did gain their inheritance, they would be able to endure to the end and remain self-sufficient."

This is the entire theology compressed into a parable. God isn't absent. He's preparing. The withdrawal is for growth. The temporary aloneness is the pathway to the inheritance. This is what Holland, Maxwell, and Scott all say theologically — but Hales says it to a grieving single mother, to a father out of work, to anyone who has prayed and heard silence.

Holland says the Father was "never closer" during the withdrawal. Hales says the same thing with a story about a dad who kept loving his daughter even when she couldn't see it.

---

## Becoming

This study traces 13 voices across 30 years of conference address — from Talmage's careful hedge to Holland's paradoxical summit. What emerges isn't one apostle's interpretation but a theological *lineage*: a community of witnesses building on each other, refining the same insight, honoring the same epistemic humility.

Maxwell started it. Holland crowned it. Scott validated it. Hales pastored it. Bateman personalized it. Oaks cited it. And behind them all, Talmage's century-old sentence still whispers: "seems to have withdrawn."

But the deepest lesson may be Maxwell's honest confession: "There are no instructive, relevant revelations" about what the Father felt. The apostolic witness on the Atonement's mechanics is inference — powerful, Spirit-guided, extraordinarily consistent inference. These men speak from personal testimony, not doctrinal definition, and they say so. Holland calls it "my personal belief." Scott says "I don't believe" — not "the Church teaches." Maxwell names it "our finite, emotional extrapolations."

This matters because it invites us in. If the apostles' understanding of the cross is developing — growing, building on each other, maturing across decades — then our own understanding is allowed to do the same. We aren't required to have a complete mechanics of the Atonement before we can stand in its light. We're invited to stand in the light and let understanding grow.

And the pastoral heart of it: **we have a Savior who has felt forsaken.** Not because He was abandoned, but because He chose to feel what we feel when we think we are. That is comprehension. That is the mechanism Enos asked about. And it's operational — not just historical — in every dark night of the soul, every prayerless silence, every season when heaven seems closed.

The Atonement was done *this way* — through voluntary aloneness, through the Father's costly restraint, through Christ's "nevertheless" — because it was the only way for a sinless God to comprehend sinful us.

And He did. And He does.
