# How Is It Done?

*Lord, how is it done?* — [Enos 1:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/enos/1?lang=eng&id=p7#p7)

---

## The Binding Question

Enos, face in the dirt, guilt swept away, asks the question that echoes across every soul who has felt the Atonement work: **"Lord, how is it done?"**

Not "what did you do?" — we know the story. Gethsemane. Calvary. The tomb. But *how*? How does one Being absorb the suffering of every soul who ever lived? How does His pain become my healing? How does justice acknowledge the debt paid when the debtor is someone else entirely? How does He know *my* name, *my* sorrow, when billions have come and gone?

This study doesn't aim to explain the Atonement — it aims to stand closer to it. To read what the scriptures actually say about the mechanics, the scope, the cost, and the result. And to love Him more for it.

> "I know in whom I have trusted." — [2 Nephi 4:19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/4?lang=eng&id=p19#p19)

---

## Part 1: Who Is Jesus?

Before asking *how* He did it, we need to sit with *who* He is. The scriptures don't present the Atonement as a transaction by a stranger. They present it as the act of someone whose identity made the whole thing possible — and necessary.

John opens his gospel at the beginning of everything:

> "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. The same was in the beginning with God. All things were made by him; and without him was not any thing made that was made." — [John 1:1-3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/1?lang=eng&id=p1-p3#p1)

Every atom, every star, every cell — His work. He made it all. And then He chose to enter the creation as a creature.

King Benjamin's angel puts the contrast in terms that stop you cold:

> "The Lord Omnipotent who reigneth, who was, and is from all eternity to all eternity, shall come down from heaven among the children of men, and shall dwell in a tabernacle of clay." — [Mosiah 3:5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/3?lang=eng&id=p5#p5)

*Omnipotent.* All-powerful. And *clay.* Fragile, mortal, breakable. He who reigns from eternity chose the breakable container.

Jacob says it plainly: "the great Creator... suffereth himself to become subject unto man in the flesh, and die for all men" ([2 Nephi 9:5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/9?lang=eng&id=p5#p5)). The one with all power chose subjection. This wasn't inevitable tragedy. It was voluntary descent.

And at the far end of that descent: "He that ascended up on high, as also he descended below all things, in that he comprehended all things, that he might be in all and through all things, the light of truth" ([D&C 88:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/88?lang=eng&id=p6#p6)).

The Creator *is* the Redeemer. Not coincidence — necessity. Amulek will explain why. But first, notice how the scriptures keep insisting on this identity: the one who made you is the same one who saves you. The hands that formed the world are the hands that bear the nail prints.

---

## Part 2: What He Took Upon Himself

Most of us would say "He took our sins." The scriptures say that — and then keep going. The scope is staggering.

Alma, teaching in Gideon, lays out the full inventory:

> "He shall go forth, suffering pains and afflictions and temptations of every kind; and this that the word might be fulfilled which saith he will take upon him the pains and the sicknesses of his people. And he will take upon him death, that he may loose the bands of death which bind his people; and he will take upon him their infirmities, that his bowels may be filled with mercy, according to the flesh, that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people according to their infirmities." — [Alma 7:11-12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/7?lang=eng&id=p11-p12#p11)

Count them. Pains. Afflictions. Temptations. Sicknesses. Death. Infirmities. This is not just a moral debt being paid. This is every form of human suffering being absorbed into one life.

And the reason? "That he may know according to the flesh how to *succor* his people according to their infirmities." Webster's 1828 dictionary defines "succor" as *"to run to, or run to support; hence, to help or relieve when in difficulty, want, or distress."* The root meaning is movement — urgent, active movement *toward* the one in distress. He doesn't wait for us to reach Him. He runs to us.

But He didn't need to run. Alma adds a devastating qualifier: "Now the Spirit knoweth all things; *nevertheless* the Son of God suffereth according to the flesh" ([Alma 7:13](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/7?lang=eng&id=p13#p13)). He already knew everything. He could have succored us from omniscience alone. He chose to know by experience. Knowledge wasn't enough. He chose immersion.

Isaiah, seven centuries before Bethlehem, saw the same scope:

> "Surely he hath borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows... he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed." — [Isaiah 53:4-5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/53?lang=eng&id=p4-p5#p4)

"The chastisement of our peace was upon him" — the punishment that *buys* our peace fell on Him. Peace costs something. He paid it.

And Jacob drives the scope to its absolute limit: "He suffereth the pains of all men, yea, the pains of every living creature, both men, women, and children, who belong to the family of Adam" ([2 Nephi 9:21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/9?lang=eng&id=p21#p21)). Not just the faithful. Not just the repentant. Every living creature in Adam's family. The scope is total.

## Part 3: The Mechanics — How Is It Done?

Now Enos's question. What do the scriptures actually reveal about the *how*?

### Why Infinite?

Amulek builds the logic chain with devastating clarity:

> "For it is expedient that an atonement should be made; for according to the great plan of the Eternal God there must be an atonement made, or else all mankind must unavoidably perish." — [Alma 34:9](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/34?lang=eng&id=p9#p9)

Not one of several options. *The* option. Without it, everyone perishes. No exceptions.

Then the argument tightens:

> "For it is expedient that there should be a great and last sacrifice; yea, not a sacrifice of man, neither of beast, neither of any manner of fowl; for it shall not be a human sacrifice; but it must be an infinite and eternal sacrifice." — [Alma 34:10](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/34?lang=eng&id=p10#p10)

Human sacrifice can't cover it. Animal sacrifice pointed toward it. Only infinite covers infinite. "There is not any man that can sacrifice his own blood which will atone for the sins of another... therefore there can be nothing which is short of an infinite atonement which will suffice for the sins of the world" ([Alma 34:11-12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/34?lang=eng&id=p11-p12#p11)).

Webster 1828 defines "infinite" as *"without limit in power, capacity, knowledge, or excellence; boundless; immeasurably or inconceivably great."* Not just very large. Inconceivable. The Atonement exceeds our capacity to measure — and it must, because the fall it addresses has no bounds either.

Jacob had made the same point: "It must needs be an infinite atonement — save it should be an infinite atonement this corruption could not put on incorruption" ([2 Nephi 9:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/9?lang=eng&id=p7#p7)). The infinite nature isn't theological decoration. It's structural necessity. A finite fix can't repair an infinite break.

### The Comprehension Principle

Here I'm naming a pattern that surfaces again and again across sources — not a single verse but a thread the scriptures weave:

D&C 88:6 says Christ "descended below all things, in that he comprehended all things." Webster 1828 defines "comprehend" as both *"to contain; to embrace; to include"* and *"to take into the mind; to grasp with the understanding."* Both meanings apply. Christ *contains* all experience within Himself — He includes every human moment in His own — and He *understands* it all because He went there. The descent *is* the comprehension.

Alma 7:12 says the same thing in pastoral language: He suffered "that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people." Isaiah 53:11 puts it in juridical terms: "by his knowledge shall my righteous servant justify many."

The *how* of the Atonement, as far as the scriptures reveal it, is inseparable from His choosing to *know by experience.* He didn't observe suffering from heaven. He entered it. And that entering gave Him — the Creator of all things — the experiential knowledge to comprehend and contain every sorrow any of His children would ever face.

John 1:5 adds a stunning inversion: "the light shineth in darkness; and the darkness comprehended it not." In Webster's sense, the darkness could not *contain* the light. But D&C 88:6 reverses this: the Light *comprehended* (contained, embraced, grasped) the darkness. What could not hold Him, He held.

### Faith as Conduit

Enos gets the most direct answer the scriptures offer to "how is it done?":

> "And he said unto me: Because of thy faith in Christ, whom thou hast never before heard nor seen." — [Enos 1:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/enos/1?lang=eng&id=p8#p8)

Faith. That's the mechanism on our side. Not earning. Not understanding completely. Trusting someone you haven't seen. And notice: Enos is ~420 BC. Christ doesn't come for another four centuries. The Atonement was already operational, reaching backward through time. The power isn't bound by chronology.

Amulek confirms: "this being the intent of this last sacrifice, to bring about the bowels of mercy, which overpowereth justice, and bringeth about means unto men that they may have faith unto repentance" ([Alma 34:15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/34?lang=eng&id=p15#p15)). The sacrifice doesn't bypass justice — it *overpowers* it. The sacrifice is so complete that justice has nothing left to demand. And what it creates in us is not compliance but *faith unto repentance.* The capacity to turn.

## Part 4: The Cost

The scriptures don't let us look away from what it cost Him. And the most remarkable source is Christ Himself.

### His Own Words

> "For behold, I, God, have suffered these things for all, that they might not suffer if they would repent; but if they would not repent they must suffer even as I; which suffering caused myself, even God, the greatest of all, to tremble because of pain, and to bleed at every pore, and to suffer both body and spirit — and would that I might not drink the bitter cup, and shrink —" — [D&C 19:16-18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/19?lang=eng&id=p16-p18#p16)

Look at the emphasis on identity: "myself, even God, the greatest of all." He wants us to understand *who* is trembling. Not a man. Not an angel. God. The greatest of all. And He trembled. He bled. He shrank. He wanted it to stop.

This is not stoic endurance. This is raw honesty about what it cost. "Suffer both body and spirit" — the Atonement wasn't only physical. The spiritual dimension may have been the worse agony.

> "Nevertheless, glory be to the Father, and I partook and finished my preparations unto the children of men." — [D&C 19:19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/19?lang=eng&id=p19#p19)

"Nevertheless." The word that held eternity together. (See the [Eternity Paused](../plan-of-salvation/eternity-paused.md) study for the full exploration of this moment.) Everything before "nevertheless" is the cost. Everything after it is the choice. The pivot between "I would that I might not" and "I partook and finished."

### Gethsemane

Luke gives us the scene:

> "And he was withdrawn from them about a stone's cast, and kneeled down, and prayed, Saying, Father, if thou be willing, remove this cup from me: nevertheless not my will, but thine, be done." — [Luke 22:41-42](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/22?lang=eng&id=p41-p42#p41)

"Nevertheless" again. The same hinge word. The same moment of submission overcoming resistance.

"And there appeared an angel unto him from heaven, strengthening him. And being in an agony he prayed more earnestly: and his sweat was as it were great drops of blood falling down to the ground" ([Luke 22:43-44](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/22?lang=eng&id=p43-p44#p43)).

The Father sent angelic support — but did *not* remove the cup. Strengthened to endure, not delivered from the task. And notice: "prayed more earnestly." The agony intensified and so did the prayer. He pressed *in*. The direction of His response to pain was toward the Father, not away.

King Benjamin's angel, 150 years before it happened, described it with clinical precision: "He shall suffer temptations, and pain of body, hunger, thirst, and fatigue, even more than man can suffer, except it be unto death; for behold, blood cometh from every pore, so great shall be his anguish" ([Mosiah 3:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/3?lang=eng&id=p7#p7)).

"Even more than man can suffer, except it be unto death." He went to the absolute edge of what a mortal body can survive. The point of death without dying — yet.

### The Cross — "Why Hast Thou Forsaken Me?"

> "And about the ninth hour Jesus cried with a loud voice, saying, Eli, Eli, lama sabachthani? that is to say, My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" — [Matthew 27:46](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/27?lang=eng&id=p46#p46)

Elder Holland, in his April 2009 address [None Were with Him](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2009/04/none-were-with-him?lang=eng), offers what may be the most penetrating reflection on this moment by any modern apostle:

> "The loss of mortal support He had anticipated, but apparently He had not comprehended this. Had He not said to His disciples, 'Behold, the hour … is now come, that ye shall be scattered, every man to his own, and shall leave me alone: and yet I am not alone, because the Father is with me'?"

He had prepared for human abandonment. He had *not* prepared for divine withdrawal.

Holland continues (and I note this is apostolic reflection, not formal doctrinal declaration, though it carries the weight of an apostle speaking with conviction):

> "It was required, indeed it was central to the significance of the Atonement, that this perfect Son who had never spoken ill nor done wrong nor touched an unclean thing had to know how the rest of humankind — us, all of us — would feel when we did commit such sins. For His Atonement to be infinite and eternal, He had to feel what it was like to die not only physically but spiritually, to sense what it was like to have the divine Spirit withdraw, leaving one feeling totally, abjectly, hopelessly alone."

The comprehension principle again — but at its most terrible expression. The Son who "descended below all things" in order to "comprehend all things" ([D&C 88:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/88?lang=eng&id=p6#p6)) descended even below the felt presence of His Father. He had to know what *we* feel when we are separated from God — and He had to know it without ever having caused that separation Himself.

Holland adds something pastorally essential: "It is my personal belief that in all of Christ's mortal ministry the Father may never have been closer to His Son than in these agonizing final moments of suffering." Present but unfelt. Near but withdrawn. If that's true — and I believe it is — then it means something for every dark night when we can't feel God: He may never be closer than in those very moments we feel most alone.

## Part 5: The Result — What Changes in Us

The Atonement is not just a historical event in a garden and on a hill. It is a living, ongoing power that *changes what we are.*

### The Mighty Change

King Benjamin's people, having heard the angel's message about the suffering Messiah, respond collectively:

> "We know of their surety and truth, because of the Spirit of the Lord Omnipotent, which has wrought a mighty change in us, or in our hearts, that we have no more disposition to do evil, but to do good continually." — [Mosiah 5:2](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/5?lang=eng&id=p2#p2)

"No more disposition to do evil." Not just behavior modification. The *desire* changes. The Atonement rewrites inclination, not just action. And then they covenant — willingly — and hear the stunning result: "Ye shall be called the children of Christ, his sons, and his daughters; for behold, this day he hath spiritually begotten you" ([Mosiah 5:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/5?lang=eng&id=p7#p7)).

This is Isaiah 53:10 and Mosiah 15:10-12 fulfilled: "when his soul has been made an offering for sin he shall see his seed." *Who* is His seed? "All those who have hearkened unto their words, and believed that the Lord would redeem his people... these are they whose sins he has borne; these are they for whom he has died" ([Mosiah 15:11-12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15?lang=eng&id=p11-p12#p11)). Through the Atonement, we become not just beneficiaries but *family.* His children. His seed.

### The Song of Redeeming Love

Alma asks Zarahemla the question that measures whether the change has gone deep enough:

> "Were the bands of death broken, and the chains of hell which encircled them about, were they loosed? I say unto you, Yea, they were loosed, and their souls did expand, and they did sing redeeming love." — [Alma 5:9](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/5?lang=eng&id=p9#p9)

"Their souls did *expand.*" The Atonement doesn't just remove chains. It creates new capacity. Freedom isn't merely the absence of bondage — it's the presence of something larger. They don't just stop grieving. They *sing.*

And then the follow-up question that never quite lets us rest: "I say unto you, my brethren, if ye have experienced a change of heart, and if ye have felt to sing the song of redeeming love, I would ask, *can ye feel so now?*" ([Alma 5:26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/5?lang=eng&id=p26#p26)). The Atonement isn't a one-time event. It requires ongoing relationship.

### Every One

The resurrected Christ at Bountiful shows us the Atonement made visible:

> "Have ye any that are sick among you? Bring them hither. Have ye any that are lame, or blind, or halt, or maimed, or leprous, or that are withered, or that are deaf, or that are afflicted in any manner? Bring them hither and I will heal them, for I have compassion upon you; my bowels are filled with mercy." — [3 Nephi 17:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/17?lang=eng&id=p7#p7)

"Afflicted in any manner." The invitation is total. And "he did heal them every one as they were brought forth unto him" ([3 Nephi 17:9](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/17?lang=eng&id=p9#p9)). Not some. Not the deserving. *Every one.*

Then He does something I can't read without my throat tightening:

> "He took their little children, one by one, and blessed them, and prayed unto the Father for them. And when he had done this he wept again." — [3 Nephi 17:21-22](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/17?lang=eng&id=p21-p22#p21)

He wept *twice* in this encounter. The first time because "my joy is full" ([3 Nephi 17:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/17?lang=eng&id=p20#p20)). The second after blessing each child individually. This is the resurrected Lord — the Atonement and Resurrection are complete — and He is still moved to tears by individual children. One by one.

The answer to "how is it done?" includes this: He knows your name because He cares about individual faces. The scope is universal — every living creature — but the execution is one by one.

### The Final Invitation

Moroni, the last voice in the Book of Mormon, writing alone, gives the end state:

> "Come unto Christ, and be perfected in him, and deny yourselves of all ungodliness; and if ye shall deny yourselves of all ungodliness, and love God with all your might, mind and strength, then is his grace sufficient for you, that by his grace ye may be perfect in Christ." — [Moroni 10:32](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/10?lang=eng&id=p32#p32)

"Perfected *in* him." Not by yourself. Not through effort alone. *In* Him. Perfection is a location, not a personal achievement. You get there by being *in* Christ — and His grace, not your striving, is what makes you sufficient.

> "Then are ye sanctified in Christ by the grace of God, through the shedding of the blood of Christ, which is in the covenant of the Father unto the remission of your sins, that ye become holy, without spot." — [Moroni 10:33](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/10?lang=eng&id=p33#p33)

The shedding of blood isn't random or isolated. It's "in the covenant of the Father" — planned, intentional, covenantal. And the end: "that ye become holy." Not just forgiven. Not just freed. *Holy.* Without spot. The Atonement's final word in the Book of Mormon isn't about suffering or payment. It's about what you become.

## Part 6: Nephi's Psalm — The Personal Witness

2 Nephi 4 is the model for how a soul relates to the Atonement once they've seen both their own weakness and Christ's power. It's the most personal text we have from Nephi, and it moves through the exact emotional architecture the Atonement creates.

It begins in love:

> "My soul delighteth in the things of the Lord; and my heart pondereth continually upon the things which I have seen and heard." — [2 Nephi 4:16](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/4?lang=eng&id=p16#p16)

Then crashes into self-awareness:

> "Nevertheless, notwithstanding the great goodness of the Lord, in showing me his great and marvelous works, my heart exclaimeth: O wretched man that I am! Yea, my heart sorroweth because of my flesh; my soul grieveth because of mine iniquities." — [2 Nephi 4:17](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/4?lang=eng&id=p17#p17)

"Nevertheless" — the hinge-word again. But here it moves in the *opposite* direction from D&C 19:19. There, it went from despair to choice. Here it goes from gratitude to grief. Same word, opposite movement. The Atonement holds both directions.

And then the turn:

> "And when I desire to rejoice, my heart groaneth because of my sins; nevertheless, I know in whom I have trusted." — [2 Nephi 4:19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/4?lang=eng&id=p19#p19)

Two "neverthelessses" in three verses. Verse 17: *nevertheless* I am wretched. Verse 19: *nevertheless* I know in whom I have trusted. The second "nevertheless" overcomes the first. This *is* the Atonement working in real time in a real soul.

From there, the psalm ascends. Remembrance: "My God hath been my support... He hath filled me with his love, even unto the consuming of my flesh" ([2 Nephi 4:20-21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/4?lang=eng&id=p20-p21#p20)). Then the self-command that rises from that remembrance: "Awake, my soul! No longer droop in sin. Rejoice, O my heart, and give place no more for the enemy of my soul" ([2 Nephi 4:28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/4?lang=eng&id=p28#p28)). And finally bedrock:

> "O Lord, I have trusted in thee, and I will trust in thee forever." — [2 Nephi 4:34](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/4?lang=eng&id=p34#p34)

Past tense *plus* future tense. "I *have* trusted... I *will* trust." The Atonement works backward and forward — same as Enos's retroactive forgiveness, same as Moroni's future-perfect holiness.

The psalm begins in the valley and ends on the rock. That's the shape of every soul who encounters the Atonement.

## The "Nevertheless" Pattern

This emerged organically from the reading — I wasn't looking for it, but it kept appearing:

- **D&C 19:19** — "Nevertheless, glory be to the Father, and I partook and finished" (cost → choice)
- **Luke 22:42** — "Nevertheless not my will, but thine, be done" (resistance → submission)
- **2 Nephi 4:17** — "Nevertheless... O wretched man" (gratitude → grief)
- **2 Nephi 4:19** — "Nevertheless, I know in whom I have trusted" (grief → trust)
- **Alma 7:13** — "The Spirit knoweth all things; nevertheless the Son of God suffereth" (omniscience → chosen experience)
- **Alma 5:7** — "In the midst of darkness; nevertheless, their souls were illuminated" (darkness → light)

"Nevertheless" is the Atonement's grammar. It's the word that marks the pivot — the moment where what *should* destroy instead transforms. Every "nevertheless" in these passages is a place where ruin meets rescue. The Atonement lives in the hinge.

## Becoming

Enos asked "how is it done?" and received an answer: "Because of thy faith in Christ."

So. How does this change how I live?

When I kneel at the sacrament table and hear "this do in remembrance of me" — I'm remembering not just a story but a *comprehension.* He knows my specific sorrows because He chose to. He runs toward me when I'm in distress (Webster's *succor*). He stands between me and what I deserve ([Mosiah 15:9](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15?lang=eng&id=p9#p9)).

When I face something I don't know how to carry — the grief, the inadequacy, the things I can't fix — I can size those problems against the one who descended below all things and comprehended all things. There is no depth He hasn't been. There is no sorrow He doesn't hold.

When my heart groaneth because of my sins — *nevertheless, I know in whom I have trusted.*

When I desire to rejoice but feel unworthy — "thy faith hath made thee whole" ([Enos 1:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/enos/1?lang=eng&id=p8#p8)). Not my performance. My faith in *Him.*

When I look at my children — He blessed them one by one. He wept twice. He said "behold your little ones." The Atonement means my family is *His* family, and that the hands that formed the worlds are the same hands placed on their heads.

This Easter, the question is not "do I believe the Atonement happened?" The question is Alma's follow-up: "Can ye feel so now?" Can I still sing the song of redeeming love? Can I still expand toward Him?

The answer, by His grace, is nevertheless.
