### Claims that are confabulated or misquoted

**The D&C 121:26 quote is wrong.** The post's Claude renders the verse as:

> "by his Holy Spirit, yea, by the unspeakable gift of the Holy Ghost, that has not been revealed since the world was, nor since men were in the earth, unto this time..."

The actual text reads:

> *"God shall give unto you knowledge by his Holy Spirit, yea, by the unspeakable gift of the Holy Ghost, that has not been revealed since the world was until now"*
> — [D&C 121:26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121?lang=eng&id=p26#p26)

The phrase **"nor since men were in the earth, unto this time"** is **not in the verse**. This is a confabulation — the post's Claude inserted a phrase that does not exist in the canonical text. The error is not theologically large (the sense is preserved), but the read-before-quoting rule names this exact failure mode: training-data memory rendered as direct quote.

**The post also condenses vv. 28-31 with an ellipsis that omits v. 29** ("All thrones and dominions, principalities and powers, shall be revealed and set forth upon all who have endured valiantly for the gospel of Jesus Christ"). The condensation isn't a misquote per se but the elided clause names *who* the revealing is "set forth upon" — the valiant. That's load-bearing material the post's argument would have benefited from rather than removed.

## IV. The CFM lesson itself

The June 7 Come Follow Me lesson is **1 Samuel 1-3**, *"The Lord Looketh on the Heart"* (the standard manual title for this block in the OT 2026 schedule). The chapters move from Hannah's prayer to Samuel's calling.

What's actually in the text, without Philpot's framework:

- **Hannah's situation** (1 Sam 1:1-8): she is provoked by Peninnah year after year because she has no children. Her husband loves her but does not understand her grief.
- **Hannah's prayer** (1 Sam 1:9-18): she goes to the house of the Lord at Shiloh, prays in bitterness of soul, vows that if the Lord gives her a son she will give him back. Eli, the priest, watches and misreads her — *"only her lips moved, but her voice was not heard"* (v. 13). He thinks she is drunk. She corrects him: *"I have poured out my soul before the Lord."* Eli sends her in peace.
- **Samuel born and given** (1 Sam 1:19-28): the Lord remembers Hannah; Samuel is born; Hannah waits until he is weaned, then brings him to Shiloh and gives him to the Lord.
- **Hannah's song** (1 Sam 2:1-10): a hymn that prefigures Mary's Magnificat — *"My heart rejoiceth in the Lord… he raiseth up the poor out of the dust… the bows of the mighty men are broken."*
- **Eli's sons + Samuel growing** (1 Sam 2:11-36): Hophni and Phinehas are corrupt; Samuel ministers before the Lord.
- **The calling** (1 Sam 3): *"the word of the Lord was precious in those days; there was no open vision"* (v. 1). The Lord calls Samuel three times; he runs to Eli; Eli finally perceives it is the Lord and tells him to say *"Speak, Lord; for thy servant heareth"* (v. 9).

The lesson's load-bearing theological move is not the dispensational framework. It is:

1. **God hears the prayer of the bitter.** Hannah's prayer is not theologically tidy. She is desperate. She poured out her soul. The text does not soften this. The Lord remembered her.
2. **The voice of the Lord can sound like an ordinary voice.** Samuel runs to Eli three times because he does not yet know how to recognize the divine voice. Eli teaches him posture: *"Speak, Lord."*
3. **A young heart can become a prophet's heart.** Samuel grows up before the Lord. The chapter ends with Israel knowing he is established as a prophet.

These are the moves to teach. Philpot's framework can sit underneath if useful, but the chapter does not require it. The chapter requires Hannah's voice, the Lord's remembering, and Samuel's *Here am I*.
