**Observation:** "All things" — not some things. And it's "by much prayer and faith," not by majority vote. The consent mechanism is spiritual, not democratic. Footnote 2a cross-references 1 Sam 8:7, Mosiah 29:26, Alma 29:4.

## Webster 1828

### Preside
- **Definition 1:** "To be set, or to sit, in the place of authority; to occupy the place of president, chairman, moderator, director, etc.; to direct, control, and regulate, as chief officer"
- **Definition 2:** "To exercise superintendence; to watch over."
- **Study relevance:** Definition 2 is the one that matters. Presiding = watching over. Abraham 4:18 IS presiding. A president who isn't watching — who isn't aware of what's happening in the stewardships they've assigned — isn't presiding in the Webster sense at all.

### Consent
- **Definition 1:** "To agree in opinion or sentiment; to be of the same mind; to accord; to concur."
- **Definition 2:** "To indicate or express a willingness; to yield to guidance, persuasion, or necessity; to give assent or approval; to comply."
- **Study relevance:** "Common consent" in D&C 26:2 carries both meanings — agreement of opinion AND expression of willingness. You're not just voting yes. You're saying: I concur with this decision, and I'm willing to support it. Both dimensions.

### Consecrate
- **Definition:** "Consecrated; devoted; dedicated; sacred."
- **Study relevance:** Webster treats it as an adjective — the state of BEING dedicated. Consecration isn't an event but a condition. A calling isn't consecrated at the moment of setting apart — it remains consecrated through ongoing dedication.

### Consecration
- **Definition:** "The act or ceremony of consecrating; the state of being consecrated; dedication."
- **Study relevance:** "The state of being consecrated." Not a one-time act. A state. You don't consecrate your time to a calling once and then coast. You remain in a state of consecration — or you don't.

### Sustain
- **Definition 1:** "To keep from falling; to bear; to uphold; to support"
- **Definition 2:** "Hence, to keep from sinking, as in despondence, or the like; to support."
- **Definition 3:** "To maintain; to keep alive; to support; to subsist; to nourish"
- **Definition 4:** "To aid, comfort, or relieve; to vindicate."
- **Definition 8:** "To prove; to establish by evidence; to corroborate or confirm"
- **Study relevance:** When we "sustain" a leader, Webster says we're doing ALL of these: keeping them from falling, keeping them from sinking in despondence, maintaining and nourishing them, aiding and comforting them, vindicating them. The sustaining vote is a commitment to physically and spiritually support the person. It's not a yes/no checkbox — it's a promise of active, ongoing support.

## Conference Talks

### Ballard, "Counseling with Our Councils," Apr 1994 — [gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1994/04/counseling-with-our-councils.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1994/04/counseling-with-our-councils?lang=eng)
> "The one-cylinder ward is the ward where the bishop handles all of the problems, makes all of the decisions, and follows through on all of the assignments. Then, like an overworked cylinder in a car engine, he is soon burned out."

> "Without exception, the bishop took charge of the situation immediately and said, 'Here's the problem, and here's what I think we should do to solve it.' Then he made assignments... This was a good exercise in delegation, I suppose, but it did not even begin to use the experience and wisdom of council members to address the problem."

> "When the bishop opened the meeting to council members and invited them to counsel together, the effect was like opening the floodgates of heaven."

**Observation:** The one-cylinder pattern isn't just about overwork — it's about closed off revelation. Ballard's insight is that the council system is a REVELATION system: "A reservoir of insight and inspiration suddenly began to flow between council members." The failure of the one-cylinder approach isn't just organizational inefficiency — it's blocking the Spirit from flowing through multiple channels.
**Connects to:** Section III, Section IV

### Hinckley, "In Counsellors There Is Safety," Oct 1990 — [gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1990/10/in-counsellors-there-is-safety.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1990/10/in-counsellors-there-is-safety?lang=eng)
> "The president, if he is wise, will assign to these chosen assistants particular duties and then leave them free to perform, requiring from them accountability for what happens."

> "When three kneel together in prayer, discuss every aspect of the problem which is before them, and under the impressions of the Spirit reach a united conclusion, then we may have the assurance that the decision is in harmony with the will of the Lord."

> "No president in any organization in the Church is likely to go ahead without the assurance that his counselors feel good about the proposed program."

> "Two counselors, working with a president, preserve a wonderful system of checks and balances. They become a safeguard that is seldom, if ever, in error and affords great strength of leadership."

> "In presidency meetings, each counselor is free to speak his mind on all issues that come before the presidency. However, it is the prerogative of the president to make the decision, and it is the duty of the counselors to back him in that decision. His decision then becomes their decision, regardless of their previous ideas."

**Observation:** Hinckley maps the ENTIRE presidency lifecycle from personal experience — from deacons quorum counselor to counselor in the First Presidency. The principles don't change with scale. A deacons quorum presidency functions on the same principles as the First Presidency. Also crucial: "it is difficult to have responsibility without authority" (quoting J. Reuben Clark). The counselor's position requires trust from the president.
**Connects to:** Section III, Section IV

### Uchtdorf, "Our Heartfelt All," Apr 2022 — [gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2022/04/57uchtdorf.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2022/04/57uchtdorf?lang=eng)

> "Consecration is different from sacrifice in at least one important way. When we consecrate something, we don't leave it to be consumed upon the altar. Rather, we put it to use in the Lord's service. We dedicate it to Him and His holy purposes."

> "Very few of us will ever be asked to sacrifice our lives for the Savior. But we are all invited to consecrate our lives to Him."

> "When we look at our lives and see a hundred things to do, we feel overwhelmed. When we see one thing—loving and serving God and His children, in a hundred different ways—then we can work on those things with joy."

**Observation:** The sacrifice/consecration distinction is critical for callings. You sacrifice personal time (give it up), but you CONSECRATE what remains — you put your time, talent, and energy to use in the Lord's service. A calling isn't loss. It's redirection. The widow's mites principle: the Lord measures by what it costs the giver, not what it adds to the treasury.

### D&C 102:3-5, 8-9, 15-23 — The High Council Constitution
> "Joseph Smith, Jun., Sidney Rigdon and Frederick G. Williams were acknowledged presidents by the voice of the council" (v. 3)
> "The above-named councilors were then asked whether they accepted their appointments, and whether they would act in that office according to the law of heaven" (v. 4)
> "The number composing the council, who voted in the name and for the church in appointing the above-named councilors were forty-three, as follows: nine high priests, seventeen elders, four priests, and thirteen members." (v. 5)
> "it shall be filled by the nomination of the president or presidents, and sanctioned by the voice of a general council of high priests" (v. 8)
> "The president of the church, who is also the president of the council, is appointed by revelation, and acknowledged in his administration by the voice of the church." (v. 9)
> "The accused, in all cases, has a right to one-half of the council, to prevent insult or injustice." (v. 15)
> "Those councilors who draw even numbers, that is, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, and 12, are the individuals who are to stand up in behalf of the accused, and prevent insult and injustice." (v. 17)
> "the president shall give a decision according to the understanding which he shall have of the case, and call upon the twelve councilors to sanction the same by their vote." (v. 19)
> "But should the remaining councilors, who have not spoken, or any one of them, after hearing the evidences and pleadings impartially, discover an error in the decision of the president, they can manifest it, and the case shall have a re-hearing." (v. 20)

**Observation:** This is the constitution of council deliberation in the Lord's Church, and it is remarkably detailed. Key patterns: (1) Acknowledged by voice — the consent principle (vv. 3, 5, 8, 9). (2) Asked whether they accepted — voluntary consent from the CALLED, not just the callers (v. 4). (3) Half the council assigned as advocates for the accused — built-in structural protection against groupthink and injustice (v. 15, 17). (4) The president decides, but calls for sanction by vote — decision + consent (v. 19). (5) The error-correction mechanism: any councilor can flag an error and force a rehearing (v. 20). This is not rubber-stamp governance. The system EXPECTS disagreement and builds in mechanisms for it. Also: forty-three people voted in the establishing session — nine high priests, seventeen elders, four priests, thirteen members. Not just leaders. Members.
**Connects to:** Section I, Section III, Section IV

## Critical Analysis Notes

### Confirmed Strong
- **Common consent = covenant, not formality.** Petersen says it directly ("their vote was a covenant"); D&C 107:22 specifies what sustaining means (upheld by confidence, faith, prayer); handbook says "pledge." Multiple independent sources converge. Confident.
- **Presiding = watching.** Webster 1828 definition 2, Abraham 4:18, handbook 4.2.4. All say the same thing from different angles. The stewardship pattern study already established this; this study deepens it with the covenantal dimension.
- **Consecration ≠ sacrifice.** Uchtdorf's distinction is clear and explicit. Mosiah 18:26 confirms — the priests receive grace of God, not just lose time. The exchange is covenantal, not economic. Confident.
- **D&C 121:34-46 as the failure mode for presiding without consent.** The text IS this argument. No inference needed.
- **D&C 102 as the deliberation constitution.** The text speaks for itself. The advocacy system, error-correction mechanism, and mixed voting body (40+ including members, not just leaders) are all right there.

### Needs Qualification
- **"Sustaining is a covenant."** The word "covenant" appears in Petersen's talk but not in the canon itself. D&C 26:2 says "common consent." D&C 107:22 says "upheld by confidence, faith, and prayer." The handbook says "pledge." All covenantal language — but the explicit word "covenant" is Petersen's apostolic teaching, not scripture. Present as: scripturally grounded inference strongly supported by apostolic teaching. Not: "the scriptures say sustaining is a covenant."
- **"The covenantal triangle."** This is MY synthesis. No single source articulates this as a three-part interdependent system. Each leg is well-sourced individually. The mutual dependency argument (sustaining without consecration is empty, etc.) is inference. Present the framework transparently as organizing the textual evidence, not as something the texts explicitly teach as a triangle.
- **Mosiah 18:8-10 as foundation under ALL callings.** Oaks makes the chain (baptism → sacrament → temple → callings). The connection is strong. But the baptismal covenant = personal discipleship. A calling = specific stewardship. Related and nested, not identical. The baptismal covenant makes you available; the calling directs you. Don't collapse the distinction.

### Acknowledged Gaps
- **The Sunday School presidency specifically.** Hinckley traces presidency principles from deacons quorum to First Presidency. The handbook covers ward councils and bishopric. But a Sunday School presidency isn't explicitly sourced at the same level. I'm applying general principles to a specific case. Honest about this — the principles are stated generally and Michael's presidency is the specific application.
- **Numbers 11 (Spirit shared among seventy elders)** — Already covered in stewardship study. Relevant to consent but not essential here. Declining to chase.

### Tensions to Name
- **1 Samuel 8:7 / Mosiah 29:26 — the people CAN choose wrongly.** D&C 26's footnote cross-references Israel demanding a king. Mosiah acknowledges "it is not common that the voice of the people desireth anything contrary to that which is right" — the qualifier "not common" means it sometimes happens. Common consent isn't infallible. It's protected by "much prayer and faith" (D&C 26:2). The spiritual dimension is the safeguard, not the mechanism itself.
- **Presidential authority vs. unanimous voice.** Hinckley: "it is the prerogative of the president to make the decision." D&C 107:27: "every decision made by either of these quorums must be by the unanimous voice." These operate at different levels. In a presidency, the president decides after counsel. In a quorum, unanimity is required. The handbook's "unsettled feelings" provision (4.4.3) bridges this — when unity isn't reached, wait. Name both principles without flattening the distinction.
- **Sustaining as formality vs. sustaining as covenant.** In current practice, sustaining votes in wards are nearly always unanimous. Does this mean the covenant of consent has become pro forma? Or does it mean the system is working well and the Spirit generally confirms the calls? Both readings are possible. Don't resolve — name the tension and let Michael's own experience inform it.
