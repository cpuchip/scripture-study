## I. Binding question

*From an LDS standpoint, is Jesus Christ God?*

Michael's specific framing: "I think he is when you look at Moses and Ether." So the study has to answer the question directly (yes), and it has to make its core case from Moses 1 and Ether 3 — those are the load-bearing exhibits the question turned on.

The shape the answer must hold: Christ is God — fully, in his own right, before the incarnation, named so by himself and by inspired narrators — AND he is not the Father. The Restoration holds both. Affirming his divinity is not Trinitarianism; denying it is not Restoration.

The complement: this study runs in the inverse direction of [father-and-son.md](../plan-of-salvation/father-and-son.md). That one preserves the *distinction* between Elohim and Jehovah so the two are not collapsed. This one preserves the *divinity* of Jehovah so the Son is not minimized. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone.

## III. The argument shape

The study answers in four moves:

1. **Name the question honestly.** LDS distinguish Father from Son. That correction (against Trinitarian fusion) can over-correct into minimizing the Son's divinity. The scriptures don't permit that minimization.

2. **The sighting (Ether 3).** Premortal Christ shows himself, names himself God in the first person, and Moroni confirms it: "this man might know that he was God." This is not Christ-as-Father-by-investiture only; it is Christ-as-God in his own right, before the flesh.

3. **The Word who creates (Moses 1 + John 1).** The Father says "by the Son I created them." The Son is God's creative word made personal. To create as God is to be God. This is also where the Old Testament Jehovah is identified as Christ.

4. **The named-God language in the Restoration.** D&C 19's "I, God, have suffered these things for all" — Christ speaking, named God. D&C 130:22 — Father and Son both have glorified bodies, both are God. The First Vision sees two glorified persons; the Father introduces the Son. The Restoration's structural claim is: two persons, one Godhead, both fully God.

5. **What this means.** Sacrament, prayer "to the Father in the name of the Son," reading the Old Testament — none of these are about Christ as a really impressive servant. They are about Christ as God.

## IV. Tensions surfaced

1. **The Mosiah 15 / Alma 11 "Father and Son" language.** This is the canonical proof-text BOTH for "Christ is God" and for "Christ is sometimes called Father." Reading it as proof of one-person Godhead is the error father-and-son.md guards against. This study explicitly preserves both: yes, Christ is God; no, that does not mean Christ is Elohim. The 1916 exposition's three senses do the work. Pointer is to father-and-son.md, not a rehash.

2. **John 14:28 "my Father is greater than I."** Cited briefly. The presidential order (Father presides; Son executes) does not contradict the Son's full divinity any more than a King's presidency over a co-regent makes the co-regent not royal. Greatness here means order, not worth. (Handled compactly so the section doesn't become a tangent.)

3. **"Christ-as-Father-by-investiture" reading of Ether 3:14.** That reading is correct as far as it goes — Christ speaks for the Father in many places — but Ether 3 is more than investiture, because Moroni's third-party narrator voice in v. 18 calls Christ "God" outright, with no investiture frame. The being shown is God. The body shown is "the body of my spirit" (v. 16) — pre-incarnation, pre-Mary, fully divine. Investiture explains the Father language; it doesn't exhaust the God language.
