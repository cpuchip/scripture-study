# Scratch — Divine Love: What Is Conditional?

> **Binding Question:** If God "esteemeth all flesh in one" and "loveth those who will have him to be their God" — spoken in the same breath by the same prophet (1 Nephi 17:35, 40) — what exactly is conditional about divine love?

## Outline

1. The Tension in Nephi's Own Words — 1 Ne 17:35 + 17:40 in one sermon
2. Nelson's Claim: "Unconditional" Is Not a Scriptural Word — Divine Love article (Liahona Feb 2003)
3. What John Says: God IS Love — 1 John 4:8, 16
4. Paul's Answer: Nothing Can Separate Us — Romans 8:35-39
5. The Father Who Was Already Watching (Luke 15) — the prodigal son parable
6. Love and Law: Oaks' Synthesis — Oct 2009 conference talk
7. The Distinction That Resolves the Tension — love vs. blessings, disposition vs. access
8. When He Came to Himself — repentance as returning to what was always there
9. Moroni's Final Word: Charity Never Faileth — Moroni 7:45-48

## Verified Quotes

### 1 Nephi 17:35
**Source:** `[1 Nephi 17](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/17?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)
> "Behold, the Lord esteemeth all flesh in one; he that is righteous is favored of God."

**Observation:** This is the verse that holds both sides of the tension. God sees everyone equally ("esteemeth all flesh in one") AND has a preferential posture toward the righteous ("he that is righteous is favored"). But notice the structure — the esteeming is universal, the favoring is responsive. It's not that God loves some and not others; it's that the righteous have positioned themselves to receive.

**Footnote 35c:** "favored" — links to TG "God, the Standard of Righteousness" — suggesting favor IS righteousness-linked, not arbitrary.

### 1 Nephi 17:40
**Source:** `[1 Nephi 17](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/17?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)
> "And he loveth those who will have him to be their God. Behold, he loved our fathers, and he covenanted with them, yea, even Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob; and he remembered the covenants which he had made; wherefore, he did bring them out of the land of Egypt."

**Observation:** "loveth those who WILL HAVE him to be their God" — willingness, not perfection. The covenant patriarchs were flawed men. Abraham lied about Sarah. Jacob deceived for his birthright. Yet God "loved" them because they chose Him. This isn't performance-based love; it's relationship-based. Nelson cites this as proof of conditionality, but the condition is choosing relationship, not earning merit.

### Nelson — "Divine Love" (Liahona, Feb 2003)
**Source:** `[divine-love](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/liahona/2003/02/divine-love?lang=eng)` (read_file verified — full article, 250 lines)

**Core thesis:**
> "While divine love can be called perfect, infinite, enduring, and universal, it cannot correctly be characterized as *unconditional.* The word does not appear in the scriptures."

**Key conditional scriptures he cites:**
> "If ye keep my commandments, ye shall abide in my love" — John 15:10
> "If you keep not my commandments, the love of the Father shall not continue with you" — D&C 95:12
> "If a man love me, he will keep my words: and my Father will love him" — John 14:23
> "I love them that love me; and those that seek me shall find me" — Proverbs 8:17

**His own distinction (critical):**
> "Does this mean the Lord does not love the sinner? Of course not. Divine love is infinite and universal. The Savior loves both saints and sinners."

**On immortality vs. eternal life:**
> "Thanks to the Atonement, the gift of immortality is *unconditional.* The greater gift of eternal life, however, is *conditional.*"

**Observation:** Nelson actually makes the key distinction himself but then buries it. He says love is "infinite and universal" and that God "loves both saints and sinners" — but then frames the overall argument as love being conditional. The article conflates love-as-disposition with blessings-as-conditional. His strongest case is about BLESSINGS, not about love itself.

**Nelson on Nehor (dangerous):**
> "These arguments are used by anti-Christs to woo people with deception. Nehor, for example, promoted himself by teaching falsehoods: He 'testified unto the people that all mankind should be saved at the last day.'"

**Observation:** This is the strongest rhetorical move — associating "unconditional love" with Nehor's false doctrine. But Nehor wasn't teaching about love; he was teaching universal salvation without conditions. That's a different claim. Saying "God loves everyone unconditionally" is NOT the same as saying "everyone is saved regardless of behavior." Nelson may be right that the word "unconditional" doesn't appear, but the conflation with Nehor is a stretch.

### D&C 95:1-2
**Source:** `[D&C 95](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/95?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)
> "Verily, thus saith the Lord unto you whom I love, and whom I love I also chasten that their sins may be forgiven, for with the chastisement I prepare a way for their deliverance in all things out of temptation, and I have loved you— Wherefore, ye must needs be chastened and stand rebuked before my face"

**Observation:** "Whom I LOVE I also CHASTEN." The chastening IS the love. This is not conditional-love language — it's "because I love you, I correct you." The love precedes and motivates the correction. Same section Nelson cites v. 12 as proof of conditionality, but v. 1 frames the entire section in prior, active love.

### Oaks — "Love and Law" (Oct 2009)
**Source:** `[Love and Law (October 2009)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2009/10/love-and-law?lang=eng)` (read_file verified — full talk)

**Core thesis:**
> "The love of God does not supersede His laws and His commandments, and the effect of God's laws and commandments does not diminish the purpose and effect of His love."

**Key passage (anger as evidence of love):**
> "God's love is so perfect that He lovingly requires us to obey His commandments because He knows that only through obedience to His laws can we become perfect, as He is. For this reason, God's anger and His wrath are not a *contradiction* of His love but an *evidence* of His love."

**On always-available love:**
> "If only we will listen, we can know of God's love and feel it, even when we are disobedient."

**Woman's testimony he quotes:**
> "He has always been there for me, even when I rejected Him. He has always guided me and comforted me with His tender mercies all around me, but I [was] too angry to see and accept incidents and feelings as such."

**On choicest blessings:**
> "God's universal and perfect love is shown in all the blessings of His gospel plan, including the fact that His choicest blessings are reserved for those who obey His laws."

**Observation:** Oaks' framing is more nuanced than Nelson's. He doesn't argue that love itself is conditional — he argues that love and law coexist, and that the highest blessings require obedience. The woman's testimony he quotes is striking: God was ALWAYS there. She was too angry to SEE it. This is the access-vs-disposition distinction. The love doesn't stop; our capacity to feel it does.

### Luke 15:11-32 — The Prodigal Son
**Source:** `[Luke 15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

**Key structural observations:**

v. 2: "The Pharisees and scribes murmured, saying, This man receiveth sinners, and eateth with them." — The AUDIENCE triggers the parable. Jesus is responding to people who think God shouldn't love sinners.

v. 17: "And when he came to himself" — not "when he became worthy." He didn't earn anything. He "came to himself." The language is awakening, not achievement.

v. 18: "I will arise and go to my father" — the decision to return. No prerequisites mentioned. No penance required first. Just: go.

v. 20: "But when he was yet a great way off, his father saw him, and had compassion, and ran, and fell on his neck, and kissed him." — THE father ran. Before the son could speak. Before any confession. Before any conditions were met. The father was WATCHING. He saw the son "a great way off." He had been watching the road. The love never left.

v. 21: The son begins his rehearsed confession: "Father, I have sinned..."

v. 22: The father doesn't even let him finish. Doesn't address the sin. Calls for the robe, the ring, the shoes. Full restoration.

v. 31 (to the elder son): "Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine." — The faithful son already had EVERYTHING. The love was already complete for him too. He just didn't realize it.

v. 32: "It was meet that we should make merry, and be glad: for this thy brother was dead, and is alive again; and was lost, and is found."

**Observation:** This parable demolishes the idea that the father's love was conditional. He did nothing differently toward the wayward son based on the son's behavior. The son's departure didn't diminish the father's love — it diminished the son's ACCESS to the father's household. The return brought restoration, not a new granting of love. This is the clearest illustration in scripture of the distinction between love-as-disposition and blessings-as-conditional-on-return.

### 1 John 4:8, 16, 19
**Source:** `[1-jn 4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-jn/4?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

v. 8: "He that loveth not knoweth not God; for God is love."
v. 16: "God is love; and he that dwelleth in love dwelleth in God, and God in him."
v. 19: "We love him, because he first loved us."

**Observation:** John doesn't say "God loves" (verb) — he says "God IS love" (identity). Love isn't something God does conditionally; it's what He IS. This is ontological, not behavioral. A God who could stop loving would cease to be God — the same logic Alma 42 uses about justice.

v. 10: "Herein is love, not that we loved God, but that he loved us, and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins."

**Observation:** John explicitly makes the sequencing clear — God loved FIRST. Before we loved Him. Before any condition was met. The Atonement itself is evidence that divine love precedes human obedience. You don't die for people who've met your conditions; you die for those who haven't.

### Romans 8:35, 38-39
**Source:** `[Romans 8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/rom/8?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

v. 35: "Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? shall tribulation, or distress, or persecution, or famine, or nakedness, or peril, or sword?"
v. 38-39: "For I am persuaded, that neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor principalities, nor powers, nor things present, nor things to come, Nor height, nor depth, nor any other creature, shall be able to separate us from the love of God, which is in Christ Jesus our Lord."

**Observation:** Paul's list is exhaustive and deliberately so. Nothing in creation, nothing temporal, nothing spiritual, nothing present or future can separate us from God's love. This is about as absolute and unconditional as language can get. If Paul were aware of conditions that voided God's love, surely he would have mentioned sin in this list. He doesn't. He mentions tribulation, persecution, peril — but NOT sin. Why? Because sin doesn't separate us from God's love. It separates us from God's blessings and presence, but not from God's love.

### Alma 42:13-15, 22-25
**Source:** `[Alma 42](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/42?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

v. 13: "according to justice, the plan of redemption could not be brought about, only on conditions of repentance of men in this probationary state; for except it were for these conditions, mercy could not take effect except it should destroy the work of justice."
v. 15: "God himself atoneth for the sins of the world, to bring about the plan of mercy, to appease the demands of justice, that God might be a perfect, just God, and a merciful God also."
v. 24: "justice exerciseth all his demands, and also mercy claimeth all which is her own; and thus, none but the truly penitent are saved."
v. 25: "What, do ye suppose that mercy can rob justice? I say unto you, Nay; not one whit. If so, God would cease to be God."

**Observation:** Alma 42 is the structural foundation for understanding this topic. The PLAN is conditional (repentance, justice, mercy). The LOVE that created the plan is not. God "himself atoneth" — He enters the equation personally, bearing the cost, because He loves His children. The Atonement is the act of love; repentance is the condition for accessing its full redemptive power. Love motivates the system; conditions govern the system.

### Mosiah 4:6-7, 11
**Source:** `[Mosiah 4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/4?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

v. 6: "if ye have come to a knowledge of the goodness of God, and his matchless power, and his wisdom, and his patience, and his long-suffering towards the children of men"
v. 11: "if ye have known of his goodness and have tasted of his love, and have received a remission of your sins, which causeth such exceedingly great joy in your souls"

**Observation:** "tasted of his love" — the verb is experiential. You TASTE it. The love is already on the table; tasting requires turning toward it, opening your mouth, receiving. King Benjamin's people "fell to the earth" (v. 1), cried for mercy (v. 2), and THEN "were filled with joy" (v. 3). The love was already there. Their posture — falling, crying, asking — opened them to experience it. Condition: turning. But the love was already there waiting.

### Moroni 7:45-48
**Source:** `[Moroni 7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/7?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

v. 45: "And charity suffereth long, and is kind, and envieth not, and is not puffed up, seeketh not her own, is not easily provoked, thinketh no evil, and rejoiceth not in iniquity but rejoiceth in the truth, beareth all things, believeth all things, hopeth all things, endureth all things."
v. 47: "But charity is the pure love of Christ, and it endureth forever"
v. 48: "pray unto the Father with all the energy of heart, that ye may be filled with this love, which he hath bestowed upon all who are true followers of his Son, Jesus Christ"

**Observation:** If charity is "the pure love of Christ" and charity "endureth forever" and "never faileth" — then the pure love of Christ is, by Moroni's definition, unfailing and eternal. It endureth all things. It doesn't stop. The condition in v. 48 is about being FILLED with this love (experiencing it), not about God deciding to withdraw it.

### John 15:9-14
**Source:** `[John 15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/15?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

v. 9: "As the Father hath loved me, so have I loved you: continue ye in my love."
v. 10: "If ye keep my commandments, ye shall abide in my love; even as I have kept my Father's commandments, and abide in his love."
v. 12: "This is my commandment, That ye love one another, as I have loved you."
v. 13: "Greater love hath no man than this, that a man lay down his life for his friends."
v. 14: "Ye are my friends, if ye do whatsoever I command you."

**Observation:** "Continue ye in my love" / "abide in my love" — the language isn't "earn my love" but "stay in it." The vine metaphor from vv. 1-8 helps: the vine doesn't withdraw sap from branches that bear less fruit; branches that disconnect from the vine wither because they've left the source. Abiding is relational presence, not transactional performance.

Nelson cites v. 10 as a conditional statement. But the grammar points the other direction: keeping commandments is how we REMAIN in (abide in) His love — not how we EARN it. The love is the starting condition. Obedience is the mechanism of remaining connected to it.

## Webster 1828 Analysis

### LOVE (n.)
"A feeling of strong attachment induced by that which delights or commands admiration; preëminent kindness or devotion to another; affection; tenderness."
**Def. 5:** "Due gratitude and reverence to God."

**Observation:** Definition 5 is interesting — it frames love toward God as "due gratitude and reverence," suggesting love is a response to what God has already done, not a currency to earn His regard.

### ESTEEM (v.)
"To set a value on; to appreciate the worth of; to estimate; to value; to reckon."
"To set a high value on; to prize; to regard with reverence, respect, or friendship."

**Observation:** When Nephi says God "esteemeth all flesh in one," the Webster meaning is: God sets the same value on all flesh. He prizes all equally. He reckons all at the same worth. This is a declaration of universal equal regard — God doesn't value some people more than others.

### FAVOR (n.)
"Kind regard; propitious aspect; countenance; friendly disposition; kindness; good will."
Def. 2: "The act of countenancing, or the condition of being countenanced, or regarded propitiously; support; promotion; befriending." Includes the citation: "And Jesus increased in wisdom and stature, and in favor with God and man" (Luke 2:52).

**Observation:** Favor in 1828 English is not identical to love. Love is "strong attachment, affection, tenderness." Favor is "kind regard, support, befriending." Favor has an active, directional quality — it's turned toward someone. Love is dispositional; favor is engaged support. This distinction may be key: God can love all equally while FAVORING (actively supporting, befriending) those who choose relationship with Him.

## Key Tensions / Threads to Pull

1. **Nelson conflates love with blessings.** His strongest arguments are about blessings being conditional (D&C 130:20-21, D&C 82:10). But he titles the article "Divine Love" not "Divine Blessings." The conflation is the source of confusion.

2. **The Nehor move is rhetorically powerful but logically weak.** Nehor taught universal salvation without conditions. Saying "God's love is unconditional" is NOT the same claim. You can believe God loves everyone while still believing salvation requires repentance.

3. **D&C 95:12 vs. D&C 95:1.** Nelson cites v. 12 as proof love is conditional. But v. 1 says "whom I love I also chasten." The chastening IS the love. The section frames God's correction as flowing FROM love, not as a withdrawal of it.

4. **Both Nephi verses in one sermon.** The fact that 1 Ne 17:35 and 17:40 are part of the same speech is the structural key. Nephi doesn't see a contradiction. God esteems all in one AND loves those who will have Him — these are not opposing truths but nested ones.

5. **The prodigal father was watching the road.** The love never stopped. The condition for restoration was coming home, not earning the father's love back.

6. **"God IS love" (1 John) vs. "love is conditional" (Nelson).** If love is God's nature, can it be conditional? Can God cease to be Himself? Alma 42's own "God would cease to be God" logic applies here.

7. **Paul's silence about sin.** Romans 8:35-39 lists everything that CAN'T separate us from God's love. Sin isn't on the list. This seems deliberate.

### Moses 7:28-33, 36-37, 40 — God Weeps
**Source:** `[Moses 7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/pgp/moses/7?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

v. 28: "And it came to pass that the God of heaven looked upon the residue of the people, and he wept; and Enoch bore record of it, saying: How is it that the heavens weep, and shed forth their tears as the rain upon the mountains?"
v. 29: "And Enoch said unto the Lord: How is it that thou canst weep, seeing thou art holy, and from all eternity to all eternity?"
v. 32-33: "The Lord said unto Enoch: Behold these thy brethren; they are the workmanship of mine own hands, and I gave unto them their knowledge, in the day I created them; and in the Garden of Eden, gave I unto man his agency; And unto thy brethren have I said, and also given commandment, that they should love one another, and that they should choose me, their Father; but behold, they are without affection, and they hate their own blood"
v. 36: "among all the workmanship of mine hands there has not been so great wickedness as among thy brethren."
v. 37: "the whole heavens shall weep over them, even all the workmanship of mine hands; wherefore should not the heavens weep, seeing these shall suffer?"
v. 40: "Wherefore, for this shall the heavens weep, yea, and all the workmanship of mine hands."

**Observation:** This may be the single most powerful passage against conditionally-withdrawn love. God weeps. Over the WICKED. Not the righteous — the wicked. He sees their wickedness ("so great wickedness as among thy brethren") and His response is not cold withdrawal but tears. The God of the universe, infinite and eternal, weeps because those He created "hate their own blood." His anger is kindled (v. 34), yes — but so are His tears. Anger and grief coexist, and both come from love. A God who didn't love the wicked wouldn't weep over them. He'd be indifferent. The weeping proves the love persists through the rejection.

Enoch's question is the question of this study: "How is it that thou canst weep?" A God of conditional love would simply stop loving the disobedient and move on. But this God weeps. The heavens themselves weep. The whole of creation mourns.

### 3 Nephi 17:5-7, 14, 20-21 — Jesus Weeps Over the Nephites
**Source:** `[3 Nephi 17](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/17?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

v. 5-6: "he cast his eyes round about again on the multitude, and beheld they were in tears, and did look steadfastly upon him as if they would ask him to tarry a little longer with them. And he said unto them: Behold, my bowels are filled with compassion towards you."
v. 7: "Have ye any that are sick among you? Bring them hither... for I have compassion upon you; my bowels are filled with mercy."
v. 14: "Jesus groaned within himself, and said: Father, I am troubled because of the wickedness of the people of the house of Israel."
v. 20-21: "Blessed are ye because of your faith. And now behold, my joy is full. And when he had said these words, he wept, and the multitude bare record of it, and he took their little children, one by one, and blessed them"

**Observation:** Jesus weeps here. Not from sorrow for sin, but from the fullness of joy. His bowels are filled with compassion. He prays in language that "cannot be written." And in v. 14, even in this moment of tenderness, He's "troubled because of the wickedness of the people of the house of Israel" — the ABSENT ones. The wicked who aren't present. He holds compassion for the righteous before Him AND grief for the wicked who aren't. Love doesn't choose one or the other.

### D&C 121:41-46 — The Pattern of Divine Influence
**Source:** `[D&C 121](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

v. 41: "No power or influence can or ought to be maintained by virtue of the priesthood, only by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned"
v. 43: "Reproving betimes with sharpness, when moved upon by the Holy Ghost; and then showing forth afterwards an increase of love toward him whom thou hast reproved, lest he esteem thee to be his enemy"
v. 44: "That he may know that thy faithfulness is stronger than the cords of death."
v. 45: "Let thy bowels also be full of charity towards all men"

**Observation:** This is the revealed pattern de Dieu — how God Himself operates. "Love unfeigned." Feigned means fake; love unfeigned is love without pretense, without conditions. And after correction? An INCREASE of love. Not withdrawal. Not conditional suspension. More love. The divine pattern is correction-followed-by-more-love, not correction-followed-by-withdrawal. V. 45 makes it universal: "charity towards ALL men." Not towards the obedient men.

### D&C 95:1 — "Whom I Love I Also Chasten"
**Source:** `[D&C 95](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/95?lang=eng)` (read_file verified)

v. 1: "Verily, thus saith the Lord unto you whom I love, and whom I love I also chasten that their sins may be forgiven, for with the chastisement I prepare a way for their deliverance in all things out of temptation, and I have loved you"

**Observation:** This verse is from the SAME section Nelson cites for v. 12 ("if you keep not my commandments, the love of the Father shall not continue with you"). But v. 1 frames everything: "whom I LOVE I also CHASTEN." The chastening IS the love. The sentence ends with "I have loved you" — past tense, ongoing reality. This love precedes and motivates everything that follows, including the conditional language in v. 12.

## Critical Analysis Notes

### Claims to qualify
1. **Don't say "Nelson was wrong."** He's a prophet. His own article makes the love-vs-blessings distinction ("divine love is infinite and universal"; "God loves both saints and sinners"). Honor that. The issue is that his title ("Divine Love") and framing conflate what his own text distinguishes.
2. **D&C 95:12 is a genuine tension.** "If you keep not my commandments, the love of the Father shall not continue with you" — this IS about love, not just blessings. Don't smooth it over. But note that v. 1 frames the ENTIRE section with "whom I love I also chasten" — the love precedes and motivates the correction. The "love not continuing" may mean experienced intimacy of relationship, not God's underlying disposition. Like the prodigal — the father's love continued, but the son couldn't feel it in the far country.
3. **John 15:10 has conditional syntax.** "If ye keep my commandments, ye shall abide in my love." This is conditional. But "abide in" is relational presence language, not transactional. The vine imagery (vv. 1-8) helps: branches don't earn sap; they stay connected to the vine. Abiding is remaining in a relationship, not earning one.

### Tensions to name
1. **Nelson's prophetic authority vs. the weight of scriptural evidence.** The study should respectfully note that Nelson's article carries the weight of an apostolic teaching, AND that the scriptural voices (1 John, Paul, Luke 15, Moses 7, Moroni 7) consistently portray love as dispositional and permanent.
2. **God's anger coexists with love.** Moses 7:34 shows God's anger kindled. D&C 95 shows chastisement. Oaks says anger is "evidence of love." The study shouldn't portray God as only tender — He's also fiercely corrective. But the correction flows FROM love.

### Ring check
The binding question asks: "What exactly is conditional about divine love?" The answer: **God's love is His nature — it doesn't stop. What is conditional is our access to specific blessings and our capacity to feel that love.** The prodigal son demonstrates this: the father's love was watching the road the entire time. The son's return didn't restore the father's love; it restored the son's access to the father's household. This answers the question directly and holds Nephi's double truth: God esteems all flesh in one (universal love) AND loveth those who will have Him (relational engagement deepens with willingness).

### Posture check
Partially confirming — Michael's intuition about the prodigal son and "returning in sincerity of heart" is on target. But the study surfaces insights neither of us expected: the Moses 7 weeping passage, D&C 95:1 reframing 95:12, the structural coincidence of 1 Ne 17:35 and 17:40, and the Webster distinction between "love" (attachment, affection) and "favor" (active support, befriending). We're not merely confirming; we're discovering a more nuanced picture than "conditional vs. unconditional."
