# Scratch — Brother of Jared, the Three Problems, and Working with Stewards

> **Binding question:** What does the Lord's pattern with the brother of Jared's three barge problems teach us about how the Master engages a steward — and by analogy, how we should engage with AI agents we steward?

## Status

Phase 1 just opened. Phase 2 reading begins next.

## What we know going in (to be verified)

The story is in Ether 2–3. Sequence as recalled:
1. Barges built per Lord's specifications — but they are "tight like unto a dish" with no openings.
2. Brother of Jared raises **three problems** to the Lord:
   - **Air** — they will suffocate inside sealed barges
   - **Steering** — there is no way to steer them
   - **Light** — there is no light inside
3. The Lord's response to each is different. That asymmetry is the point.

The "interesting" pattern (to be verified in Phase 2):
- **Air:** Lord gives a direct prescription — make a hole in top and bottom, stop them when water comes.
- **Steering:** Lord takes it Himself — declares He will be the steering: winds, currents, no human helm needed.
- **Light:** Lord throws it back — "what will ye that I should do?" Brother of Jared molds 16 stones and asks the Lord to touch them.

## Outline (working)

I. The three problems (the asymmetry the Lord builds in)
II. Air — the prescription pattern (Master gives the solution)
III. Steering — the "I will be" pattern (Master takes it Himself)
IV. Light — the "what will ye that I should do" pattern (Master throws it back)
V. Why three patterns? — what the Lord is forming in the brother of Jared
VI. The veil tearing (Ether 3) — what the third pattern made possible
VII. Application: stewardship and working with agents
VIII. Becoming

## Verified scripture quotes

### Ether 2:18-25 — the three problems and three responses (read in full)

The brother of Jared, having built the barges per the Lord's specification ("tight like unto a dish"), comes to the Lord with three concerns *in a single prayer*:

> "18. … O Lord, I have performed the work which thou hast commanded me, and I have made the barges according as thou hast directed me.
> 19. And behold, O Lord, in them there is **no light**; **whither shall we steer**? And also we shall perish, for in them we **cannot breathe**, save it is the air which is in them; therefore we shall perish."
> — [Ether 2:18-19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/ether/2?lang=eng&id=p18-p19#p18)

Three problems raised in one breath: light, steering, air. The Lord then handles them on *three different patterns*, in three different moments.

**Pattern 1 — Prescription (air):**

> "20. And the Lord said unto the brother of Jared: Behold, **thou shalt make a hole** in the top, and also in the bottom; and when thou shalt suffer for air thou shalt unstop the hole and receive air. And if it be so that the water come in upon thee, behold, ye shall stop the hole, that ye may not perish in the flood."

Exact mechanism. Specific physical action. No room for the brother of Jared to "figure it out." The Lord prescribes, the steward obeys: "21. And it came to pass that the brother of Jared did so, according as the Lord had commanded."

**Then the brother of Jared returns** — but notice: he does *not* re-raise the steering question. He only brings back the light question:

> "22. And he cried again unto the Lord saying: O Lord, behold I have done even as thou hast commanded me; and I have prepared the vessels for my people, and behold there is no light in them. Behold, O Lord, wilt thou suffer that we shall cross this great water in darkness?"

**Pattern 2 — "I have already done it" (steering):**

The Lord, before responding to the light question, *volunteers* the answer to steering — even though the brother of Jared did not bring it back up:

> "24. For behold, ye shall be as a whale in the midst of the sea; for the mountain waves shall dash upon you. Nevertheless, **I will bring you up again out of the depths of the sea; for the winds have gone forth out of my mouth**, and also the rains and the floods have I sent forth.
> 25. And behold, **I prepare you against these things**; for ye cannot cross this great deep save I prepare you against the waves of the sea, and the winds which have gone forth…"

Past tense: "the winds **have gone forth** out of my mouth." The steering was already taken care of before the question was raised. The brother of Jared had nothing to figure out and nothing to do; the Lord had already done it. No human helm. No course-plotting. The wind itself is the answer.

**Pattern 3 — "What will ye that I should do?" (light):**

> "23. And the Lord said unto the brother of Jared: **What will ye that I should do that ye may have light in your vessels?** For behold, **ye cannot have windows**, for they will be dashed in pieces; **neither shall ye take fire with you**, for ye shall not go by the light of fire.
> … 25. Therefore **what will ye that I should do** that ye may have light when ye are swallowed up in the depths of the sea?"

The Lord refuses to prescribe. He also rules out two obvious answers (windows, fire) — narrowing the solution space so the brother of Jared cannot simply default. The question "what will ye that I should do?" is asked twice in three verses. This is the formation pattern: the Lord wants the steward to *bring something forward*.

### Ether 3:1-6 — the brother of Jared's proposal

> "1. And it came to pass that the brother of Jared, (now the number of the vessels which had been prepared was eight) went forth unto the mount, which they called the mount Shelem, because of its exceeding height, and did **molten out of a rock sixteen small stones**; and they were white and clear, even as transparent glass; and he did carry them in his hands upon the top of the mount, and cried again unto the Lord, saying:
> … 4. And I know, O Lord, that thou hast all power, and can do whatsoever thou wilt for the benefit of man; therefore **touch these stones, O Lord, with thy finger, and prepare them that they may shine forth in darkness**…
> 5. Behold, O Lord, thou canst do this. We know that thou art able to show forth great power, which looks small unto the understanding of men."
> — [Ether 3:1, 4-5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/ether/3?lang=eng&id=p1#p1)

The brother of Jared (a) *labored* (molten stones out of a rock, on a high mountain), (b) *proposed* (sixteen stones, specific number, specific material, specific transparency), and (c) *specified what only the Lord can do* (touch them; prepare them to shine). He did not ask for an abstract miracle. He brought his own work to the threshold and asked the Lord to add what only the Lord can add.

**v.6** — "the Lord stretched forth his hand and touched the stones one by one with his finger. And the veil was taken from off the eyes of the brother of Jared, and he saw the finger of the Lord."

The third pattern is what tore the veil. The first two patterns formed faith. The third pattern formed *seership*.

### Ether 6:5-9 — the voyage, the steering proved

> "5. And it came to pass that the Lord God caused that there should be a furious wind blow upon the face of the waters, towards the promised land; and thus they were tossed upon the waves of the sea before the wind…
> 8. And the **wind did never cease to blow** towards the promised land while they were upon the waters; and thus they were driven forth before the wind.
> 9. And they did sing praises unto the Lord; yea, the brother of Jared did sing praises unto the Lord, and he did thank and praise the Lord all the day long; and when the night came, they did not cease to praise the Lord."
> — [Ether 6:5, 8-9](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/ether/6?lang=eng&id=p5#p5)

The voyage took 344 days (v.11). The brother of Jared never had to steer; the wind never ceased; he sang praises night and day. The "I have already done it" pattern was proved over nearly a year at sea.

### Ether 12:19-21 — Moroni's commentary (the interpretive lens)

> "19. And there were many whose faith was so exceedingly strong, even before Christ came, who could not be kept from within the veil, but truly saw with their eyes the things which they had beheld with an eye of faith, and they were glad.
> 20. And behold, we have seen in this record that one of these was the brother of Jared; for so great was his faith in God, that when God put forth his finger he could not hide it from the sight of the brother of Jared, because of his word which he had spoken unto him, which word he had obtained by faith.
> 21. And after the brother of Jared had beheld the finger of the Lord, because of the promise which the brother of Jared had obtained by faith, the Lord could not withhold anything from his sight; wherefore he showed him all things, for he could no longer be kept without the veil."
> — [Ether 12:19-21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/ether/12?lang=eng&id=p19-p21#p19)

Moroni's framing: the brother of Jared "obtained [a word/promise] by faith" before God's finger came. The faith preceded the seeing. Pattern 3 — the steward proposing — is what stretched faith past the veil.

## Gap & Critical Analysis

### What the text actually does (and does not) say

The text is clear on:
- Three problems are raised together in one prayer (Ether 2:19).
- The Lord answers them differently — air with a specific prescription (v.20), steering with a declaration that the wind is already going forth (v.24-25), light with the question "what will ye that I should do?" (v.23, 25).
- The third pattern — the proposal of the stones — is the one that breaks open the veil (Ether 3:6).
- Moroni names this as a faith story (Ether 12:19-21), not a leadership-design story.

The text does *not* say:
- That the Lord deliberately designed three patterns to teach the brother of Jared a leadership framework. That is interpretation. The Lord's stated purpose in Ether 3:14-15 is to redeem and to be seen — not to model stewardship.
- That the three patterns are pure categories. They overlap: pattern 1 still required the steward to drill the holes; pattern 2 still required him to board the barge; pattern 3 still required the Lord's specific touch (v.6).
- That the brother of Jared understood what the Lord was doing across the three responses. He simply obeyed, persisted, returned, and proposed.

### Strongest claims, stress-tested

**Claim:** "The Lord uses three different patterns with the same steward in the same prayer cycle."
- Verifiable in the text. Air (v.20) ≠ steering (v.24-25) ≠ light (v.23). The structural asymmetry is real.

**Claim:** "The third pattern is what tears the veil."
- The veil tears at the moment of the Lord's touch on the brother of Jared's stones (Ether 3:6). The first two patterns did not produce a theophany; this one did. But: Moroni's reading (Ether 12:19-21) attributes this to *faith* and *word obtained by faith*, not to the structural property of the pattern itself. Honest framing: the third pattern *was the occasion* of the seeing, but the faith was the cause.

**Claim:** "This maps to how we should engage AI agents."
- This is analogical, not exegetical. The Lord is omniscient; the human steward of an AI is not. The Lord can declare "the winds have gone forth out of my mouth"; the human cannot literally cause winds. The patterns are useful as a *thinking architecture* — when do I prescribe, when do I take it on myself, when do I throw it back — not as a Christ-imitation algorithm. Frame it that way in the draft.

### The thing I almost missed

The brother of Jared **dropped** the steering question on his second prayer (v.22). He came back asking only about light. The Lord *volunteered* the steering answer anyway (v.24-25). This is significant: pattern 2 — "I've already done it" — was given to a steward who had *stopped asking*. The Lord chose to comfort about the thing the steward had quietly let go of, before handing back the question the steward was still actively holding.

For the AI-stewardship application: there are things I quietly stopped worrying about because I trust the Master will handle them. The Master sometimes names those out loud — "by the way, I've already done that one" — even when I didn't ask.

### The other thing I almost missed

"What will ye that I should do?" is **constrained**, not open. Before the Lord asks the question, He rules out two obvious answers: "ye cannot have windows… neither shall ye take fire" (Ether 2:23). The brother of Jared is not handed a blank slate. He is handed a question with the dumb answers already deleted. That changes the application meaningfully: when the Master throws a problem back to a steward, He often does so by first eliminating the obvious wrong moves and only then asking what the steward will bring.

For working with AI agents: the constraint *is* part of the question. "Build me anything" is not the third pattern. "Don't use the obvious wrong answers; now propose" is.

### Ring check

Does the study answer its binding question? Yes — the three patterns *do* teach a real thing about how a Master engages a steward, and the text supports the reading. The AI-agent application is honest as analogy, not as exegesis.

### Posture check

Am I reading to discover or to confirm? Some checks:
- The "brother of Jared dropped the steering question" observation is a discovery, not confirmation of a pre-formed thesis.
- The "what will ye is constrained, not open" observation pushes against the management-framework reading by saying it is *not* "delegate freely."
- The "Moroni attributes the veil-tearing to faith, not pattern" observation forces me to hedge claim #2 above.
- I am NOT smoothing over Ether 2:14-15, where the Lord *chastens* the brother of Jared "because he remembered not to call upon the name of the Lord." The story begins with a relational rebuke, not with a clean management framework.

### Voice in the AI-agent section

The section that applies this to AI stewardship has a specific risk: making the Lord sound like a project manager. He is not. The patterns are useful for thinking about how to delegate without abdicating, but they belong to a story about a man melting stones and seeing the Lord — not to a productivity framework. Keep the section grounded in *Michael's* practice (covenant work, voice work, skill-building with the agent) and avoid the abstract "leaders should…" register.

## Notes on the application angle

Michael's framing: "How should we apply it to working with agents and stewards?"

Initial brainstorm seeds (to be tested against the text, not asserted):
- The agent/steward needs to know which kind of problem they're holding. Some problems the Master will prescribe. Some He will take entirely. Some He will throw back so the steward grows.
- The Master is not equally hands-on across all problems. That's a feature.
- The brother of Jared had to bring something forward (16 stones, molten out of a rock) before the Lord would touch them. The steward's labor is real labor, not stalling for the Master to do it all.
- The "what will ye that I should do?" question is itself the formation. It treats the steward as a partner who has agency to propose.
- Application to AI: as the human steward of agents, which problems do I prescribe? Which do I take entirely on myself? Which do I throw back to the agent to propose? This is the architecture, not a list of cases.
- Application to me as a steward (of work the Lord has assigned): same three categories. Some prescriptions are written (commandments, covenants). Some things the Lord just does. Some the Lord wants me to bring molten stones for.
- WARNING during critical-analysis phase: this is exactly the kind of study most likely to bend the text toward a tidy management framework. Need to read Ether 2-3 carefully for what the text actually says, not what makes a clean three-column table for working with AI.
