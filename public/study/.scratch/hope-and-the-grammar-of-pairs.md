## Verified Quotes & Observations

### Webster 1828 — HOPE (n.)

> "A desire of some good, accompanied with an expectation of obtaining it, or a belief that it is obtainable; an expectation of something which is thought to be desirable; confidence; pleasing expectancy."

> "One who, or that which, gives hope, furnishes ground of expectation, or promises desired good. The Lord will be the hope of his people. Joel iii. 16."

**Observation:** Webster gives hope two components — *desire* + *expectation*. Neither alone is hope. Wishing without expectation is wistfulness. Expectation without desire is prediction. Hope is the fusion: I want it AND I expect it. The second sense is striking: a *person* can be hope ("the Lord will be the hope of his people"). Hope can be embodied. This already pulls toward the Christ-centered definitions in Moroni and Ether.

### Moroni 7:3 (Mormon to "the peaceable followers of Christ")

> "I judge these things of you because of your peaceable walk with the children of men. For I remember the word of God which saith by their works ye shall know them; for if their works be good, then they are good also. ... ye have obtained a sufficient hope by which ye can enter into the rest of the Lord, from this time henceforth until ye shall rest with him in heaven."

**Observation:** Mormon names hope as the *qualifier for entering rest*. Not knowledge, not perfection — *sufficient hope*. Hope is the threshold currency.

### Moroni 7:40-44

> "And again, my beloved brethren, I would speak unto you concerning hope. How is it that ye can attain unto faith, save ye shall have hope?
> And what is it that ye shall hope for? Behold I say unto you that ye shall have hope through the atonement of Christ and the power of his resurrection, to be raised unto life eternal, and this because of your faith in him according to the promise.
> Wherefore, if a man have faith he must needs have hope; for without faith there cannot be any hope.
> And again, behold I say unto you that he cannot have faith and hope, save he shall be meek, and lowly of heart.
> If so, his faith and hope is vain, for none is acceptable before God, save the meek and lowly in heart; and if a man be meek and lowly in heart, and confesses by the power of the Holy Ghost that Jesus is the Christ, he must needs have charity; for if he have not charity he is nothing; wherefore he must needs have charity."

**Observation:** Mormon's logic chain is reciprocal, not linear. v.40 says faith depends on hope ("how is it that ye can attain unto faith, save ye shall have hope?"). v.42 says hope depends on faith ("without faith there cannot be any hope"). They are mutually constitutive — neither comes first, both arrive together. And the *content* of hope is specified: "raised unto life eternal." Hope is not generic optimism; it has Christological cargo. v.43-44 then makes meekness and lowliness the precondition, and charity the necessary consequence. The triplet is held together by humility.

### Moroni 7:48 (the chapter's climax)

> "Wherefore, my beloved brethren, pray unto the Father with all the energy of heart, that ye may be filled with this love, which he hath bestowed upon all who are true followers of his Son, Jesus Christ; that ye may become the sons of God; that when he shall appear we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is; that we may have this hope; that we may be purified even as he is pure. Amen."

**Observation:** The grammar is dense but pivotal. "We shall see him as he is" — that vision — is what Mormon names as "this hope." Hope here equals seeing Christ as he is, AND it equals being purified into his likeness. The seeing and the becoming are bound together. Michael's intuition that hope is "the vision" finds direct support here. But note the qualification: Mormon doesn't say hope IS the vision; he says we shall see him *that we may have this hope*. The vision GIVES hope. Hope is the orientation produced by the seeing — and that orientation is what purifies us into his likeness. Hope is the eye that beholds Christ AND is changed by what it beholds (cf. 2 Cor 3:18).

### Ether 12:4 (Moroni on the brother of Jared)

> "Wherefore, whoso believeth in God might with surety hope for a better world, yea, even a place at the right hand of God, which hope cometh of faith, maketh an anchor to the souls of men, which would make them sure and steadfast, always abounding in good works, being led to glorify God."

**Observation:** Hope as ANCHOR — the same metaphor Paul uses in Heb 6:19. Hope holds the soul fast against drift. And it has *direction*: "a better world... a place at the right hand of God." Hope is not vague; it is locational, eschatological. And it has fruit: "always abounding in good works." This is hope as ground for action, not as escape from action.

### Ether 12:6

> "And now, I, Moroni, would speak somewhat concerning these things; I would show unto the world that faith is things which are hoped for and not seen; wherefore, dispute not because ye see not, for ye receive no witness until after the trial of your faith."

**Observation:** Moroni paraphrases Heb 11:1 but inverts the syntax — "faith IS things which are hoped for" rather than "faith is the substance of things hoped for." In Moroni's grammar, faith and the hoped-for-thing are nearly identified. Then: no witness until after the trial. Witness is the third term that arrives after faith holds.

### Ether 12:28

> "Behold, I will show unto the Gentiles their weakness, and I will show unto them that faith, hope and charity bringeth unto me—the fountain of all righteousness."

**Observation:** Christ's voice. The triplet *bringeth unto me*. The triplet has a destination — Christ himself. This is decisive for the binding question: how does the triplet drive toward salvation? It drives toward the Person.

### Ether 12:32

> "And I also remember that thou hast said that thou hast prepared a house for man, yea, even among the mansions of thy Father, in which man might have a more excellent hope; wherefore man must hope, or he cannot receive an inheritance in the place which thou hast prepared."

**Observation:** "Man must hope, or he cannot receive an inheritance." Hope is the necessary condition for receiving exaltation. Not optional. Not encouraged. Required. Despair forecloses the inheritance.

### Alma 32:21

> "And now as I said concerning faith—faith is not to have a perfect knowledge of things; therefore if ye have faith ye hope for things which are not seen, which are true."

**Observation:** Alma's epistemology places hope between belief and knowledge. Faith *produces* hope as its mode of relation to the unseen-but-true. The qualifier "which are true" is critical — hope is not directed at any wished-for thing but at *real* things that happen to be unseen. This is what distinguishes gospel hope from wishful thinking. The truth-content of the object is presupposed.

### Alma 32:27 (faith as desire/experiment)

> "But behold, if ye will awake and arouse your faculties, even to an experiment upon my words, and exercise a particle of faith, yea, even if ye can no more than desire to believe, let this desire work in you, even until ye believe in a manner that ye can give place for a portion of my words."

**Observation:** "Desire to believe" as the smallest viable seed. Notice: desire is also the first component of Webster's hope. The seed that becomes a tree starts as a *desire*, which is the embryonic form of both faith and hope.

### Romans 5:3-5

> "And not only so, but we glory in tribulations also: knowing that tribulation worketh patience; And patience, experience; and experience, hope: And hope maketh not ashamed; because the love of God is shed abroad in our hearts by the Holy Ghost given unto us."

**Observation:** Paul gives a chain — tribulation → patience → experience → hope → love. Hope is the penultimate step that opens into love (charity). And it "maketh not ashamed" — hope vindicates the one who holds it. Note the ordering: charity arrives AFTER hope, mediated by the Spirit, just as in Moroni 7:43-44.

### Romans 8:24-25

> "For we are saved by hope: but hope that is seen is not hope: for what a man seeth, why doth he yet hope for? But if we hope for that we see not, then do we with patience wait for it."

**Observation:** This is the strongest counterweight to "hope = vision." Paul defines hope precisely by what it does NOT see. Hope ends where sight begins. Reconciliation with Moroni 7:48: hope is not the present possession of the vision but the *anticipation* of it — the patient orientation toward what is yet unseen but will be seen. Hope is the eye fixed on what is not yet visible. The "vision" in Moroni is eschatological ("when he shall appear we shall see him as he is") — hope is the present-tense version of that future seeing.

### 1 Corinthians 13:7, 13

> "[Charity] beareth all things, believeth all things, hopeth all things, endureth all things."
>
> "And now abideth faith, hope, charity, these three; but the greatest of these is charity." (Footnote 13a: GR love.)

**Observation:** v.7 nests hope INSIDE charity — charity "hopeth all things." So while hope and charity are distinct in v.13, in v.7 charity contains hope as one of its modes of operation. The triplet is not three separate gifts but three concentric layers; faith expands into hope, hope expands into charity, and charity then contains both. The Greek footnote (charity = love) reminds us this is *agape*, not affection.

### Hebrews 6:11-12, 18-19

> "And we desire that every one of you do shew the same diligence to the full assurance of hope unto the end: That ye be not slothful, but followers of them who through faith and patience inherit the promises."
>
> "That by two immutable things, in which it was impossible for God to lie, we might have a strong consolation, who have fled for refuge to lay hold upon the hope set before us: Which hope we have as an anchor of the soul, both sure and steadfast, and which entereth into that within the veil."

**Observation:** "Two immutable things" — God's promise and his oath, given to Abraham (vv.13-17). Paul builds his hope on a *doublet of divine speech-acts.* Why two? Because (per Deut 19:15 / 2 Cor 13:1) "in the mouth of two or three witnesses every word shall be established." God himself satisfies the witness law — he gives both promise and oath. Then v.19 gives hope's full geometry: anchor of soul, sure and steadfast, *entering into that within the veil.* Hope is not just a feeling — it pierces into the unseen. It anchors here BY entering there. This is the structural answer to the binding question. Hope is the link between mortal experience and the Holy of Holies.

### Hebrews 11:1

> "Now faith is the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen."

**Observation:** Paul's most compressed definition. Faith is the *substance* (Gr. hypostasis — underlying reality, ground) of what is hoped for. So hope furnishes the object; faith supplies the underlying reality of that object. Hope sees the thing; faith makes the thing real before it is seen. Together they are the past-and-future of the same trust.

### D&C 4:5-6 (qualifying for the ministry)

> "And faith, hope, charity and love, with an eye single to the glory of God, qualify him for the work.
> Remember faith, virtue, knowledge, temperance, patience, brotherly kindness, godliness, charity, humility, diligence."

**Observation:** v.5 expands the triplet to a *quadruplet* — faith, hope, charity, AND love. If charity already means love (per 1 Cor 13:13a footnote), why both? Possibly: charity = the pure love of Christ TOWARD others (cf. Moro 7:47), love = the disposition of heart Godward and brotherward broadly. v.6 then explodes into a list of TEN attributes, with charity appearing in both lists (the only repeat). Charity is the hinge between the smaller and larger lists. Note also "an eye single to the glory of God" — the eye/seeing motif appears AGAIN at the moment hope is named (cf. Moro 7:48 "we shall see him as he is").

### Moroni 10:20-22

> "Wherefore, there must be faith; and if there must be faith there must also be hope; and if there must be hope there must also be charity.
> And except ye have charity ye can in nowise be saved in the kingdom of God; neither can ye be saved in the kingdom of God if ye have not faith; neither can ye if ye have no hope.
> And if ye have no hope ye must needs be in despair; and despair cometh because of iniquity."

**Observation:** Moroni's farewell makes the triplet salvifically necessary — *all three* are required for entry into the kingdom. Then he gives hope's negation: despair. And the etiology of despair: iniquity. So the fight to keep hope is the fight to keep oneself from sin. Hope is not merely a gift; it is a moral discipline maintained by repentance.

### Topical Guide — HOPE (cross-reference catalog)

The TG entry stitches hope across all five standard works. Notable nodes not yet quoted in detail:
- **OT:** Job 27:8 (the hypocrite's hope perishes); Ps 16:9 / Acts 2:26 ("my flesh shall rest in hope" — resurrectional); Ps 33:22 ("mercy according as we hope in thee"); Ps 42:11 ("hope thou in God"); Prov 13:12 ("hope deferred maketh the heart sick"); Jer 17:7 (blessed is he whose hope the Lord is); Ezek 37:11 ("our bones are dried, and our hope is lost" — the dry-bones complaint); Joel 3:16 (the Lord himself as hope of his people).
- **NT:** Rom 4:18 ("against hope believed in hope" — Abraham); Rom 12:12 ("rejoicing in hope"); Rom 15:13 ("abound in hope, through the power of the Holy Ghost"); Eph 4:4 ("called in one hope of your calling"); 1 Thes 5:8 ("for an helmet, the hope of salvation" — armor metaphor); 1 Tim 1:1 ("Christ, which is our hope" — Christ as hope itself); Titus 1:2 / 3:7 ("hope of eternal life"); 1 Pet 1:3 ("begotten us again unto a lively hope, by the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead"); 1 Pet 3:15 ("a reason of the hope that is in you"); 1 Jn 3:3 ("every man that hath this hope in him purifieth himself" — exact parallel to Moro 7:48).
- **BoM:** 2 Ne 31:20 ("perfect brightness of hope"); Jacob 4:4; Alma 7:24; Alma 30:13 ("vain hope" — Korihor's diagnosis of believers); Moro 8:14 (no faith, hope, nor charity = perilous state); Moro 8:26 ("the Comforter filleth with hope and perfect love").
- **D&C:** 4:5; 6:19; 12:8; 18:19; 42:45 ("those that have not hope of a glorious resurrection"); 128:21.
- **PoGP:** A of F 1:13 ("we hope all things").

**Observation:** Two patterns from the catalog. (1) Hope is repeatedly anchored to *resurrection* — Ps 16:9, 1 Pet 1:3, D&C 42:45. The resurrection is what makes hope rational. (2) 1 Jn 3:3 mirrors Moro 7:48 almost word for word: "this hope... purifieth himself, even as he is pure." Mormon was reading John (or both were reading the Spirit's same teaching). The purifying function of hope is a doctrine, not an inference.

### Bible Dictionary — FAITH (relevant excerpt)

> "Faith is to hope for things which are not seen, but which are true (Heb. 11:1; Alma 32:21), and must be centered in Jesus Christ in order to produce salvation. ... Faith is a principle of action and of power... A lack of faith leads one to despair..."

**Observation:** BD defines faith *through* hope — they are reciprocally defined. Note also: lack of faith → despair, which matches Moroni 10:22 (no hope → despair). Despair is the shared opposite of both faith and hope, which suggests the two share enough overlap to share a single antonym.

### Note on "doublet/triplet" sources for Section IV

Search for "obedience first law of heaven" returned multiple manuals and the *General Handbook* §3 lists the temple covenants as a five-fold sequence: **obedience, sacrifice, gospel of Jesus Christ, chastity, consecration.** The "sacrifice and obedience" pair Michael named is one node in a five-link chain — itself a structured group. Also surfaced: the *Romans 7-16* manual quote: "the laws of obedience and sacrifice are indelibly intertwined." The pairing of these two laws is canonical, not just intuited.

For "two or three witnesses" — confirmed Deut 17:6, Deut 19:15, Matt 18:16, 2 Cor 13:1, D&C 5:15, Ether 5:4. The witness law is the structural rationale for *why* Latter-day Saint doctrine consistently arrives in twos and threes: it satisfies God's own evidentiary standard. ### 2 Nephi 31:20 (in the context of Nephi's "doctrine of Christ")

> "Wherefore, ye must press forward with a steadfastness in Christ, having a perfect brightness of hope, and a love of God and of all men. Wherefore, if ye shall press forward, feasting upon the word of Christ, and endure to the end, behold, thus saith the Father: Ye shall have eternal life."

**Observation:** The triplet appears as walking instructions: *steadfastness in Christ* (faith in motion), *perfect brightness of hope* (vision in motion), *love of God and of all men* (charity in motion). And it sits inside Nephi's larger five-step doctrine of Christ (faith, repentance, baptism, Holy Ghost, endure to the end — vv.13-21). The triplet is what carries you across the strait and narrow path *between* baptism and the end. It is the road, not the gate.

### Lectures on Faith — Lecture 6 (The Law of Sacrifice)

> "A religion that does not require the sacrifice of all things, never has power sufficient to produce the faith necessary unto life and salvation; for from the first existence of man, the faith necessary unto the enjoyment of life and salvation never could be obtained without the sacrifice of all earthly things... When a man has offered in sacrifice all that he has, for the truth's sake, not even withholding his life, and believing before God that he has been called to make this sacrifice, because he seeks to do his will, he does know most assuredly, that God does and will accept his sacrifice and offering... Under these circumstances, then, he can obtain the faith necessary for him to lay hold on eternal life." (LoF 6:7)

**Observation:** Lecture 6 treats sacrifice as the *epistemic* mechanism by which faith becomes saving. Without sacrifice, you can have belief, but not the certain confidence ("knowledge, not merely belief") that produces saving faith. This reframes the "sacrifice and obedience" doublet: obedience is the daily form, sacrifice is the maximal form, and the maximal form is what supplies the certainty the daily form needs. The pair is not redundant — one tests and confirms the other.

### Lectures on Faith — Lecture 7:9 (the saved being is Christ)

> "Where shall we find a saved being? for if we can find a saved being, we may ascertain, without much difficulty, what all others must be, in order to be saved—they must be like that individual or they cannot be saved... that it is Christ: all will agree in this that he is the prototype or standard of salvation... Thus says John, in his first epistle, 3:2 and 3: Behold, now we are the sons of God, and it doth not appear what we shall be; but we know, that when he shall appear we shall be like him; for we shall see him as he is. And any man that has this hope in him purifies himself, even as he is pure.—Why purify himself as he is pure? because, if they do not they cannot be like him."

**Observation:** This is the bridge text. Joseph (or whoever authored Lecture 7 — Sidney Rigdon by most accounts, but published with Joseph's authority) explicitly cross-wires the John 3:2-3 / Moroni 7:48 hope-vision-purification grammar with the doctrine that *the standard of salvation is Christ himself, whom we are to become like.* Hope's content is Christ's likeness. Hope's mechanism is the seeing. Hope's effect is the purifying. This is not three meanings of hope — it is one meaning expressed in three tenses (future content, present orientation, ongoing transformation).

### Lectures on Faith — Lecture 7:16 ("on this hinge turns the door of salvation")

> "These teachings of the Savior must clearly show unto us the nature of salvation; and what he proposed unto the human family when he proposed to save them—That he proposed to make them like unto himself; and he was like the Father, the great prototype of all saved beings: And for any portion of the human family to be assimilated into their likeness is to be saved; and to be unlike them is to be destroyed: and on this hinge turns the door of salvation."

**Observation:** "Assimilation into their likeness" is the salvific mechanism. The faith-hope-charity triplet, viewed through Lecture 7's frame, is the engine of assimilation: faith trusts the prototype, hope sees the prototype, charity becomes the prototype's love. The triplet is *one process* viewed at three stages of transformation.
