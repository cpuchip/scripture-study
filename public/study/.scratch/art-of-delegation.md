## Binding Question

When I ask someone to carry a burden for me, and they willingly agree, something happens — relief, trust, gratitude. Christ did this infinitely: He took upon Himself our sins and sorrows. Is delegation in the Church a shadow of the Atonement's burden-bearing pattern, and if so, what does that teach about how to delegate, how to receive delegation, and why the whole system depends on willingness?

> "as ye are desirous to come into the fold of God, and to be called his people, and are willing to bear one another's burdens, that they may be light" (v. 8)

> "Yea, and are willing to mourn with those that mourn; yea, and comfort those that stand in need of comfort, and to stand as witnesses of God at all times and in all things, and in all places that ye may be in, even until death" (v. 9)

Also: the response is *joy* — they clapped their hands for joy at the prospect of burden-bearing. That's counterintuitive unless burden-bearing is itself a gift.

### [Alma 7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/7?lang=eng) — Alma 7:11-13
> "And he shall go forth, suffering pains and afflictions and temptations of every kind; and this that the word might be fulfilled which saith he will take upon him the pains and the sicknesses of his people." (v. 11)

> "And he will take upon him death, that he may loose the bands of death which bind his people; and he will take upon him their infirmities, that his bowels may be filled with mercy, according to the flesh, that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people according to their infirmities." (v. 12)

> "Nevertheless the Son of God suffereth according to the flesh that he might take upon him the sins of his people, that he might blot out their transgressions according to the power of his deliverance" (v. 13)

He takes burdens so He can HELP. The same purpose should animate human delegation: we share burdens not merely for efficiency but so we can help each other.

> "What is this thing that thou doest to the people? why sittest thou thyself alone" (v. 14)

**Footnote chase:** v. 18 cross-references Num. 11:14-17 (Moses: "I am not able to bear all this people alone, because it is too heavy for me"), Deut. 1:9, and D&C 10:4 (the pacing principle).

### [Matthew 11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/11?lang=eng) — Matthew 11:28-30
> "Come unto me, all ye that labour and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest." (v. 28)

> "Take my yoke upon you, and learn of me; for I am meek and lowly in heart: and ye shall find rest unto your souls." (v. 29)

> "For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light." (v. 30)

**Observation:** The yoke image is shared labor. A yoke joins two animals to pull together. Christ doesn't say "give me your burden and rest" — He says "take my yoke upon you." He's inviting us INTO work alongside Him. The delegation is bidirectional: He bears our sins, but then He yokes us to His work. The burden becomes light not because it disappears but because it's shared. Also: "learn of me" — the purpose of the shared yoke is education, becoming, not just load relief.

### [Galatians 6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/gal/6?lang=eng) — Galatians 6:2, 5

> "For every man shall bear his own burden." (v. 5)

Delegation operates in the first category — the baros, the things too heavy for one person.

> "Surely he hath borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows: yet we did esteem him stricken, smitten of God, and afflicted." (v. 4)

> "But he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed." (v. 5)

The Son accepts willingly. "He bare the sin" uses the same root as Exodus 18:22 "they shall bear the burden with thee."

### [D&C 58](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/58?lang=eng) — D&C 58:26-29
> "For behold, it is not meet that I should command in all things; for he that is compelled in all things, the same is a slothful and not a wise servant; wherefore he receiveth no reward." (v. 26)

> "Verily I say, men should be anxiously engaged in a good cause, and do many things of their own free will, and bring to pass much righteousness" (v. 27)

> "For the power is in them, wherein they are agents unto themselves." (v. 28)

> "But he that doeth not anything until he is commanded, and receiveth a commandment with doubtful heart, and keepeth it with slothfulness, the same is damned." (v. 29)

**Observation:** God delegates. He explicitly refuses to micromanage. "It is not meet that I should command in all things" — this is the most direct statement of divine delegation philosophy in all scripture. The delegate is expected to be "anxiously engaged" — to take initiative, to use their own agency, to "bring to pass much righteousness" without waiting for instruction. "The power is in them" — God affirms that the delegate has the capacity. This is the anti-micromanagement scripture. And the consequence of waiting to be commanded is strong: "the same is damned." Not punished — damned. Stopped. Unable to progress. The refusal to act without direction is itself a spiritual failure.
**Connects to:** Outline §4 (willing receiver — agency is the engine), §5 (trust — God trusts us), §6 (failure — waiting to be commanded)

### [Numbers 11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/num/11?lang=eng) — Numbers 11:11-17
> "Wherefore hast thou afflicted thy servant? and wherefore have I not found favour in thy sight, that thou layest the burden of all this people upon me?" (v. 11)

Fourth: "they shall bear the burden of the people WITH thee" — same "with" as Exodus 18:22.

## Webster 1828

### Bear
- **Definition 1:** "To support or sustain; to hold up."
- **Definition 2:** "To support and remove or carry; to convey."
- **Definition 8:** "To endure; to tolerate; to undergo; to suffer."
- **Definition 10:** "To sustain, or be answerable for, as blame, expense, responsibility, etc. 'He shall bear their iniquities.' Is. liii. 11."
- **Study relevance:** Webster's range covers the full spectrum: physical support (hold up), transport (carry), endurance (suffer), and responsibility (be answerable for). The fact that "bear" means both "carry a load" and "be answerable for" maps to delegation — the delegate both carries the work AND becomes accountable for it. Webster cites Isaiah 53:11 directly.

### Burden
- **Definition 1:** "That which is borne or carried; a load."
- **Definition 2:** "That which is borne with labor or difficulty; that which is grievous, wearisome, or oppressive."
- **Critical synonym distinction:** "A burden is a weight to be borne; a load is something laid upon us to be carried. Our burdens may be of such a nature that we feel bound to bear them cheerfully or without complaint. They may arise from the nature of our situation; they may be allotments of Providence; they may be the consequences of our errors. What is upon us, as a load, we commonly carry with greater reluctance or sense of oppression."
- **Study relevance:** Burden (borne willingly, Providence-allotted) vs. load (carried reluctantly). A calling is a burden in the best sense — bound to bear it willingly, arising from our covenant situation. This is delegation language. The baptismal covenant uses "burden" precisely.

### Delegate
- **Definition 1:** "Any one sent and empowered to act for another; one deputed to represent; a chosen deputy; a representative; a commissioner; a vicar."
- **Study relevance:** "Sent AND empowered." Not just assigned — empowered. Matches Numbers 11:17 exactly: God transfers His Spirit to the seventy. Delegation without empowerment is merely assignment. Also "vicar" — one who stands in place of another.

### Yoke
- **Definition 7:** "That which connects or binds; a chain; a link; a bond connection."
- **Definition 9:** "Two animals yoked together; a couple; a pair that work together."
- **Study relevance:** The yoke is fundamentally about JOINING two together. Christ's "Take my yoke upon you" is an invitation to be coupled in work. The burden becomes light because two pull together.

## Additional Scripture: D&C 84:33-42 (Oath and Covenant of the Priesthood)

**Source:** [D&C 84:35-38](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/84?lang=eng&id=p35-p38#p35)
**Verified:** Yes (read_file)

> **35.** And also all they who receive this priesthood receive me, saith the Lord;
> **36.** For he that receiveth my servants receiveth me;
> **37.** And he that receiveth me receiveth my Father;
> **38.** And he that receiveth my Father receiveth my Father's kingdom; therefore all that my Father hath shall be given unto him.
> **39.** And this is according to the oath and covenant which belongeth to the priesthood.

**Observation:** This is the cosmic delegation chain. Each "receiveth" is an act of accepting delegated authority. The chain runs: Father → Christ → servants → those who receive servants. Verse 36 is the pivot: receiving a delegated servant IS receiving the Lord. This means that when Michael asks a counselor to take on a responsibility and that counselor agrees, the person being served by that counselor is, in a real sense, receiving Michael's authority — and through Michael, the bishop's, and through the bishop's, the Lord's. Delegation doesn't dilute authority; it extends it. The oath and covenant (v. 39) frames this entire chain as covenantal, not administrative.

**Observation:** v. 33 — "magnifying their calling" is the delegate's obligation. Not just doing the task, but expanding it, making it greater. This is what D&C 58:26-29 describes from the other direction — the delegate should be "anxiously engaged" without being commanded in all things. Magnifying = the delegate's response to trust.

## Additional Scripture: D&C 121:34-46 (Powers of Heaven / Unrighteous Dominion)

**Source:** [D&C 121:34-46](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121?lang=eng&id=p34-p46#p34)
**Verified:** Yes (read_file)

> **37.** That they may be conferred upon us, it is true; but when we undertake to cover our sins, or to gratify our pride, our vain ambition, or to exercise control or dominion or compulsion upon the souls of the children of men, in any degree of unrighteousness, behold, the heavens withdraw themselves; the Spirit of the Lord is grieved; and when it is withdrawn, Amen to the priesthood or the authority of that man.

> **39.** We have learned by sad experience that it is the nature and disposition of almost all men, as soon as they get a little authority, as they suppose, they will immediately begin to exercise unrighteous dominion.

> **41.** No power or influence can or ought to be maintained by virtue of the priesthood, only by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned;
> **42.** By kindness, and pure knowledge, which shall greatly enlarge the soul without hypocrisy, and without guile—

> **44.** That he may know that thy faithfulness is stronger than the cords of death.

> **46.** The Holy Ghost shall be thy constant companion, and thy scepter an unchanging scepter of righteousness and truth; and thy dominion shall be an everlasting dominion, and without compulsory means it shall flow unto thee forever and ever.

**Observation (Trust):** v. 41-42 is the METHOD of delegation. Every tool listed is relational, not positional: persuasion, long-suffering, gentleness, meekness, love unfeigned, kindness, pure knowledge. The leader delegates by persuading and trusting, not by commanding and controlling. These are the conditions under which delegation becomes covenant-like rather than merely transactional.

**Observation (Failure Mode - Micromanagement/Control):** v. 37, 39 — "exercise control or dominion or compulsion" is the ANTI-delegation scripture. This describes the leader who can't trust, who hoards authority, who delegates the task but not the autonomy. The consequence is devastating: "the heavens withdraw." Unrighteous dominion doesn't just harm the delegate — it severs the dominator from God.

**Observation (Failure Mode - Result of Trust):** v. 46 — The reward for delegating through persuasion rather than compulsion: "without compulsory means it shall flow unto thee forever and ever." Dominion that flows without compulsion IS delegation working perfectly — people follow because they choose to, not because they must. This is the ultimate trust outcome.

**Observation (Faithfulness):** v. 44 — "That he may know that thy faithfulness is stronger than the cords of death." After reproof (v. 43), you show increased love so the delegate knows you're still faithful to them. This maps to delegation: the leader who corrects but stays present, who gives feedback but doesn't rescind the delegation. The opposite of abandonment.

**Connects to:** Outline §5 (Trust as Prerequisite), §6 (Failure Modes — micromanagement and control), and §4 (the relational tools that make delegation work)

### M. Russell Ballard, "O Be Wise" (October 2006)

**Source:** [general-conference/2006/10/o-be-wise.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2006/10/o-be-wise?lang=eng)
**Verified:** Yes (gospel_get, read_file)

> They refuse to delegate or to allow others to grow in their respective responsibilities.

**Observation:** Ballard frames refusal to delegate as a spiritual failure, not an administrative preference. The consequence is unbalanced lives and deteriorating family relationships.

> There is a difference between being responsible for getting the work done and doing the work yourself.

**Observation:** This is the critical distinction. Delegation doesn't transfer accountability — it shares labor while retaining stewardship. This maps to the Atonement: Christ bears our sins but we still must repent. The burden-bearer enables, not replaces.

> Assignments should be made, responsibilities should be delegated, and members should be allowed to fulfill their stewardship as best they can. Counsel, advise, persuade, motivate—but don't do the work for them. Allow others to progress and grow, even if it means sometimes getting less-than-perfect results on the reports.

**Observation:** "Allow others to progress and grow" — delegation serves the DELEGATE's becoming, not just the delegator's relief. Less-than-perfect results are part of the learning. This connects to trust: the delegator trusts the delegate enough to accept imperfection.

> As the power of the Atonement begins to work in our lives, we come to understand that the Savior has already borne the burden of our guilt. O that we may be wise enough to understand, to repent as necessary, and to let go of our guilt.

**Observation:** Ballard explicitly connects his delegation teaching to the Atonement. ## Critical Analysis Notes (Phase 3a)

**Central claim:** Delegation in the Church is a shadow of the Atonement's burden-bearing pattern.

**Confirmed:**
- Shared vocabulary (bear, burden, carry, take upon, yoke) is verifiable across 10+ sources — not a stretch.
- Ballard explicitly connects delegation and Atonement in the same talk ("O Be Wise"). An apostle made this connection, not just the study.
- Mosiah 18 uses burden-bearing as the defining act of the baptismal covenant. Covenant = delegation framework.

**Strongest counterargument:**
The Atonement is centripetal — gathering ALL burdens TO one person (Christ). Delegation is centrifugal — spreading burden FROM one person to many. They're structurally opposite. **Resolution:** The Church operates on BOTH patterns simultaneously: vertical (Christ bears what we can't) and horizontal (we bear what we can share). Delegation is the horizontal expression of the same principle the Atonement expresses vertically. The study should name this distinction rather than collapsing the two.

**Tensions to sit with (don't resolve, just name):**
1. Delegation can be self-serving; the Atonement is purely selfless. Mixed motive is human reality.
2. The Atonement's offer is universal ("Come unto me, ALL ye"); delegation is selective (Jethro: "able men, fearing God"). Universal invitation vs. selective stewardship.
3. Not every task assignment is covenantal. Or is it? If Mosiah 18 defines the covenant as burden-bearing, then every willing act of shared work IS covenant fulfillment. The study should note this without forcing an answer.

**Framing adjustment:**
Don't claim delegation IS the Atonement. Claim that the Atonement establishes the VOCABULARY and PATTERN that delegation enacts at human scale. The vertical pattern (Christ → us) creates the language; the horizontal pattern (us → each other) extends it. Both depend on willingness.

**Weaknesses acknowledged:**
- Jethro's narrative is practical, not theological. The Atonement reading is an overlay from Mosiah/Alma, not inherent in Exodus 18. Name this.
- The study sources almost all support the thesis. The counterarguments above were generated by critical analysis, not by the sources themselves. This is typical for a thematic study but should be transparent.

**Ring check:** Binding question asks three things (how to delegate, how to receive, why willingness). Sources answer all three. Study circles back. ✓

**Posture check:** Adjusted framing from "delegation IS the Atonement" to "delegation enacts at human scale the pattern the Atonement establishes." More calibrated. ✓
