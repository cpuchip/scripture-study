# Scratch — How Is It Done? Part 2: The Prophetic Witness

## Binding Question
What do modern prophets and apostles see in the mechanics of the Atonement that scripture alone doesn't show us? How do they theologize the Father's withdrawal, the comprehension principle, the Father's role, and the cost — and do they agree?

## Outline
1. The Talmage Foundation — where the modern theology starts (Jesus the Christ, p. 613/661)
2. Maxwell's Contributions — comprehension, irony, "enormity × infinity," the "apparent" withdrawal
3. Holland's Arc — 1989, 1995, 1999, 2007, 2009, 2021, 2024: a career-spanning theology
4. Scott's Father Perspective — "I don't believe Father in Heaven forsook His Son"
5. The Father's Anguish — Holland 1999, Scott 2010: what it cost the Father
6. Robert D. Hales 1985 — pastoral application: when WE feel forsaken
7. The Broader Chorus — Haight, Faust, Hinckley, Monson, Nelson, others
8. What Do They Add? — synthesis of apostolic additions to scripture
9. Do They Agree? — tensions in the witness (apparent vs. actual withdrawal, forsaken vs. not forsaken)
10. Becoming

### Talmage, *Jesus the Christ* (1916), pp. 613/661 — via Haight (1994, line 85) and Maxwell (1989, line 99)

The fountainhead for all modern apostolic commentary on the withdrawal:

> "In that bitterest hour the dying Christ was alone. [So] that the supreme sacrifice of the Son might be consummated in all its fulness, the Father seems to have withdrawn … His immediate Presence, leaving to the Savior of men the glory of complete victory over the forces of sin and death."

And then the recovery:

> "realizing that He was no longer forsaken, but that His atoning sacrifice had been accepted by the Father, and that His mission in the flesh had been carried to glorious consummation, He exclaimed in a loud voice of holy triumph: *'It is finished.'*"

**Key language:** "seems to have withdrawn" — hedged, not absolute. "Complete victory" — the withdrawal *served* the victory. Talmage frames it as purposeful, not abandonment.

**File:** Quoted in `../../[Jesus of Nazareth (April 1994)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1994/04/jesus-of-nazareth?lang=eng)` line 85

### Maxwell, "O, Divine Redeemer" (Oct 1981) — `../../[O Divine Redeemer (October 1981)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1981/10/o-divine-redeemer?lang=eng)`

Maxwell's *first* apostolic address. Already contains the descent → comprehension → succoring connection:

> "In ways we cannot comprehend, our sicknesses and infirmities were borne by Him even before these were borne by us. The very weight of our combined sins caused Him to descend below all. We have never been, nor will we be, in depths such as He has known. Thus His atonement made His empathy and His capacity to succor us perfect." (line 29)

The forsakenness, framed as beyond human comprehension:

> "And when we feel so alone, can we presume to teach Him who trod 'the wine-press alone' anything at all about feeling forsaken?" (line 37)

The Abraham/Isaac echo — critical detail no one else uses:

> "There was no ram in the thicket at Calvary to spare Him, this Friend of Abraham and Isaac." (line 29)

**Observation:** Maxwell names the comprehension-to-succoring chain in his VERY FIRST TALK as an apostle. This is 1981 — 8 years before "Irony" and 28 years before Holland's "None Were with Him." The theological DNA is already present.

### McConkie, "The Purifying Power of Gethsemane" (April 1985) — `../../[The Purifying Power of Gethsemane (April 1985)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1985/04/the-purifying-power-of-gethsemane?lang=eng)`

McConkie's final testimony. Makes a startling claim about the cross:

> "And truly he was, for while he was hanging on the cross for another three hours, from noon to 3:00 p.m., all the infinite agonies and merciless pains of Gethsemane recurred."

Also establishes epistemic humility as a boundary:

> "We do not know, we cannot tell, no mortal mind can conceive the full import of what Christ did in Gethsemane."

**Observation:** McConkie speaks in apostolic authority — "I shall use my own words, though you may think they are the words of scripture... they are now mine, for the Holy Spirit of God has borne witness to me." This is personal testimony language, not scholarly hedging. He doesn't directly theologize the Father's withdrawal, but his claim that Gethsemane's agonies RECURRED on the cross implies the double suffering (body + spirit) was active at Calvary too.

### Maxwell, "Willing to Submit" (April 1985) — `../../[Willing to Submit (April 1985)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1985/04/willing-to-submit?lang=eng)`

The Gethsemane passage — Jesus "sore amazed" (Greek: "awestruck"):

> "Imagine, Jehovah, the Creator of this and other worlds, 'astonished'! Jesus knew cognitively what He must do, but not experientially. He had never personally known the exquisite and exacting process of an atonement before. Thus, when the agony came in its fulness, it was so much, much worse than even He with his unique intellect had ever imagined!" (line 107)

The scale:

> "His suffering—as it were, *enormity* multiplied by *infinity*—evoked His later soul-cry on the cross, and it was a cry of forsakenness." (line 117)

The hinge:

> "Even so, Jesus maintained this sublime submissiveness, as He had in Gethsemane: 'Nevertheless not as I will, but as thou wilt.'" (line 119)

And the stunning conclusion — What turned on this?

> "The wondrous and glorious Atonement was the central act in all of human history. It was the hinge on which all else that finally matters turned. But it turned upon Jesus' spiritual submissiveness!" (line 127)

**Observation:** Maxwell's distinctive contribution here is the SURPRISE. Christ knew intellectually but not experientially. The forsakenness wasn't just pain — it was astonishment. Combined with "Irony" four years later, Maxwell builds a picture: withdrawal → surprise → comprehension → submissiveness → victory. The ORDER matters.

### Robert D. Hales, "Lessons from the Atonement That Help Us to Endure to the End" (Oct 1985) — `../../[Lessons From the Atonement That Help Us to Endure to the End (October 1985)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1985/10/lessons-from-the-atonement-that-help-us-to-endure-to-the-end?lang=eng)`

The pastoral application — unique among all these voices:

> "In the last moments upon the cross Jesus asked His Father a very simple question: 'Why hast thou forsaken me?' Are there times in our lives when we think that we have been forsaken by God, or by our fellow men, or by our families? That is the moment when we have to turn our thoughts back to Christ and endure to the end." (line 63)

The parable — a wealthy father deliberately withdrawing from his daughter:

> "His daughter had recently been through a divorce. ... Her father could have provided for all of their needs and cares, but he said it was time for them to live more modestly and to help support themselves, to be more self-sufficient, to be able to stand on their own. This experience is similar to the Lord's asking His Father, 'Why hast thou forsaken me?' for the family's reaction was, 'Why would Father do this now when we need him the most?' But you see, he was preparing them that they might be strengthened by providing for their own needs." (line 65)

**Observation:** Hales does something NO ONE else does: he takes the forsakenness and makes it ours. Not "what did the Father do to Christ" but "what do WE feel when we think we've been forsaken?" And then he answers with a parable that reframes apparent abandonment as deliberate preparation. The wealthy father didn't stop loving his daughter — he withdrew visible support so she could grow. THIS IS THE PASTORAL BRIDGE from theology to lived experience.

### Maxwell, "Irony: The Crust on the Bread of Adversity" (April 1989) — `../../[Irony the Crust on the Bread of Adversity (April 1989)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1989/04/irony-the-crust-on-the-bread-of-adversity?lang=eng)`

THE definitive passage connecting withdrawal → comprehension → succoring:

> "We come now to the last and most terrible irony of Jesus: His feeling forsaken at the apogee of His agony on Calvary. The apparent withdrawal of the Father's spirit then evoked the greatest soul cry in human history. This deprivation had never happened to Christ before—never. Yet, thereby, Jesus became a fully comprehending Christ and was enabled to be a fully succoring Savior. Moreover, even in that darkest hour, while feeling forsaken, Jesus submitted Himself to the Father." (line 99)

The word "nevertheless":

> "No wonder the Savior tells us that the combined anguish in Gethsemane and on Calvary was so awful that He would have shrunk. 'Nevertheless,' He finished His 'preparations.' The word *nevertheless* reflects deep, divine determination." (line 101)

And the Father's grief — Maxwell saw this BEFORE Holland:

> "'Glory be to the Father'—first, for rearing such an Incomparable Son. Second, 'Glory be to the Father' for allowing His special Son to suffer and to be sacrificed for all of us. On Judgment Day, brothers and sisters, will any of us want to rush forward to tell our Father how we, as parents, suffered when we watched our children suffer?" (line 105)

**Key language:** "apparent withdrawal" — Maxwell is careful. Also: "the greatest soul cry in human history." Also: "This deprivation had never happened to Christ before—never." The repetition.

**Observation:** This single paragraph may be the most important non-scriptural passage on Atonement mechanics in all of conference history. It connects three things no prior speaker had connected: (1) the withdrawal was *the last irony* — the one without parallel, (2) the result was COMPREHENSION — not just endurance, (3) comprehension enabled SUCCORING. Maxwell built this theology. Holland would give it its fullest expression 20 years later, but the architecture is Maxwell's.

### Haight, "Jesus of Nazareth" (April 1994) — `../../[Jesus of Nazareth (April 1994)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1994/04/jesus-of-nazareth?lang=eng)`

Primarily narrates the Passion through Talmage and Farrar. The forsakenness section (line 85) quotes Talmage directly:

> "At the ninth hour Christ uttered that anguished cry, 'My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?' 'In that bitterest hour the dying Christ was alone. [So] that the supreme sacrifice of the Son might be consummated in all its fulness, the Father seems to have withdrawn … His immediate Presence, leaving to the Savior of men the glory of complete victory over the forces of sin and death.'"

And the recovery (line 87):

> "Later, 'realizing that He was no longer forsaken, but that His atoning sacrifice had been accepted by the Father...'"

**Observation:** Haight doesn't theologize the withdrawal — he channels Talmage. This is the tradition-bearer approach: pass on the received understanding without adding to it. Contrasts with Maxwell (who builds on Talmage) and Holland (who reimagines it).

### Holland, "The Hands of the Fathers" (April 1999) — `../../[The Hands of the Fathers (April 1999)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1999/04/the-hands-of-the-fathers?lang=eng)`

The Father's anguish — from the Father's perspective:

> "I am a father, inadequate to be sure, but I cannot comprehend the burden it must have been for God in His heaven to witness the deep suffering and Crucifixion of His Beloved Son in such a manner. His every impulse and instinct *must* have been to stop it, to send angels to intervene—but He did not intervene. He endured what He saw because it was the only way that a saving, vicarious payment could be made for the sins of all His other children, from Adam and Eve to the end of the world." (line 17)

And: "I am eternally grateful for a perfect Father and His perfect Son, neither of whom shrank from the bitter cup nor forsook the rest of us."

**Observation:** Holland shifts the camera from the Son to the Father. The Father's "impulse" to intervene = the Abraham/Isaac parallel, but from God's side. The verb "endured" applied to the Father — not just the Son. Holland is building toward "None Were with Him" but hasn't arrived yet. In 1999, the Father is an anguished observer. By 2009, Holland will say the Father was "never closer" — a presence that deliberately withdrew its comfort while remaining present. That's evolution.

### Maxwell, "Plow in Hope" (April 2001) — `../../[Plow in Hope (April 2001)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2001/04/plow-in-hope?lang=eng)`

The comprehension principle, stated plainly:

> "Uniquely, atoning Jesus also 'descended below all things, in that he comprehended all things' (D&C 88:6). How deep that descent into despair and abysmal agony must have been! He did it to rescue us and in order to comprehend human suffering." (line 61)

The Father's feelings — Maxwell acknowledges the gap in revelation:

> "On occasion, the God of heaven has wept (see Moses 7:28). One ponders, therefore, the agonies of Jesus' infinite Atonement and the feelings of the Father—for His Son and for us. There are no instructive, relevant revelations, but our finite, emotional extrapolations come flooding in anyway!" (line 65)

The personal dimension:

> "The infinite Atonement is so vast and universal, but finally, it is so very personal!" (line 73)

**Observation:** Maxwell in 2001 is STILL developing this theology. Here he explicitly says there are "no instructive, relevant revelations" about the Father's feelings — acknowledging that Holland (1999), he himself, and others are offering "finite, emotional extrapolations." This is remarkable intellectual honesty from an apostle. It means the theological tradition around the Father's anguish is INFERENCE, not revelation. Important for the "tensions" section.

### Maxwell, "How Choice a Seer" (Oct 2003) — `../../[How Choice a Seer (October 2003)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2003/10/how-choice-a-seer?lang=eng)`

The condensed formula:

> "Jesus, who performed the 'infinite atonement,' thereby suffered infinitely and is a fully comprehending Savior, having 'descended below all things' and 'comprehended all things' (2 Nephi 9:7; D&C 88:6)." (line 67)

**Observation:** By 2003, Maxwell's comprehension formula is so well-established it shows up as a single sentence in a talk primarily about Joseph Smith. The theology has become axiomatic — it doesn't need to be argued anymore.

### Bateman, "A Pattern for All" (Oct 2005) — `../../[A Pattern for All (October 2005)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2005/10/a-pattern-for-all?lang=eng)`

The transformation in understanding:

> "For many years I thought of the Savior's experience in the garden and on the cross as places where a large mass of sin was heaped upon Him. Through the words of Alma, Abinadi, Isaiah, and other prophets, however, my view has changed. Instead of an impersonal mass of sin, there was a long line of people, as Jesus felt 'our infirmities,' '[bore] our griefs, … carried our sorrows … [and] was bruised for our iniquities.'" (line 47)

The conclusion:

> "The Atonement was an intimate, personal experience in which Jesus came to know how to help each of us." (line 49)

And the Moses parallel:

> "If Moses beheld every soul, then it seems reasonable that the Creator of the universe has the power to become intimately acquainted with each of us. He learned about your weaknesses and mine. He experienced your pains and sufferings. He experienced mine." (line 51)

**Observation:** Bateman moves from mass → individual. This is the comprehension principle made personal: not "He comprehended all suffering" but "He comprehended YOUR suffering, and mine, one by one." This connects to 3 Nephi 17:21 — "one by one."

### Holland, "None Were with Him" (April 2009) — `../../[None Were with Him (April 2009)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2009/04/none-were-with-him?lang=eng)`

The theological summit. Three key passages:

**The divine withdrawal:**

> "I speak of those final moments for which Jesus must have been prepared intellectually and physically but which He may not have fully anticipated emotionally and spiritually—that concluding descent into the paralyzing despair of divine withdrawal when He cries in *ultimate* loneliness, 'My God, my God, why hast *thou* forsaken me?'" (line 49)

**The Father's nearness:**

> "With all the conviction of my soul I testify that He *did* please His Father perfectly and that a perfect Father did *not* forsake His Son in that hour. Indeed, it is my personal belief that in all of Christ's mortal ministry the Father may never have been closer to His Son than in these agonizing final moments of suffering. Nevertheless, that the supreme sacrifice of His Son might be as complete as it was voluntary and solitary, the Father briefly withdrew from Jesus the comfort of His Spirit, the support of His personal presence." (line 53)

**The why:**

> "It was required, indeed it was central to the significance of the Atonement, that this perfect Son who had never spoken ill nor done wrong nor touched an unclean thing had to know how the rest of humankind—us, all of us—would feel when we did commit such sins. For His Atonement to be infinite and eternal, He had to feel what it was like to die not only physically but spiritually, to sense what it was like to have the divine Spirit withdraw, leaving one feeling totally, abjectly, hopelessly alone." (line 53)

**Key language:** "my personal belief" — Holland explicitly marks this as testimony, not doctrinal definition. "May never have been closer" — paradox at the heart of the talk. "Briefly withdrew" — the Father withdrew COMFORT and PRESENCE, not love.

**Observation:** Holland completes what Maxwell started. Maxwell: withdrawal → comprehension → succoring. Holland adds: the Father was CLOSER during the withdrawal, not farther. And the PURPOSE was experiential empathy — Christ had to feel what WE feel when the Spirit withdraws due to sin. This is the fullest articulation in conference history.

### Scott, "He Lives! All Glory to His Name!" (April 2010) — `../../[He Lives All Glory to His Name (April 2010)](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2010/04/he-lives-all-glory-to-his-name?lang=eng)`

Three challenges of the Atonement (line 51-57):

1. Enormous sense of responsibility — if imperfect, no one returns
2. Had to personally feel the consequences of ALL mankind's sins
3. Had to endure Satan's attack while pressed to the limit

The Father's role:

> "Then, for reasons we do not fully know, while at the extremity of His capacity, at the time the Savior most needed succor, His Father allowed Him to shoulder the onerous responsibility with only His own strength and capacity." (line 57)

The direct declaration:

> "I don't believe Father in Heaven forsook His Son on the cross. I do believe the cry was motivated when that Son felt removed the sustaining support He had always enjoyed from His Father. His Father recognized that the Savior needed to accomplish the Atonement totally and completely on His own, without external support. The Father did not abandon His Son. He made it possible for His perfect Son to win the eternal fruits of the Atonement." (line 59)

**Key language:** "I don't believe" — personal testimony language like Holland. "Allowed Him" — agency language for the Father. "Made it possible" — the withdrawal was ENABLING, not punitive. "For reasons we do not fully know" — epistemic humility.

**Observation:** Scott and Holland are saying essentially the same thing 12 months apart, independently. Both say: not forsaken, but sustaining presence withdrawn. Both say: it was FOR the Son, not AGAINST Him. Scott adds something Holland doesn't: the Father "recognized" what the Son needed. This attributes understanding to the Father — not just endurance (Holland 1999) but wisdom.

## Key Threads & Patterns

### Thread 1: The Lineage — Talmage → Maxwell → Holland
- Talmage (1916): "seems to have withdrawn" — establishes the hedged language
- Maxwell (1981): descend → comprehend → succor — the functional chain
- Maxwell (1985): "enormity × infinity," Christ's surprise, submissiveness as hinge
- Maxwell (1989): "apparent withdrawal," "fully comprehending Christ," "fully succoring Savior" — names the connection
- Holland (1999): camera shifts to Father's anguish
- Maxwell (2001): "There are no instructive, relevant revelations" about the Father's feelings — epistemic honesty
- Holland (2009): Father "never closer," "briefly withdrew comfort" — the paradox fully stated
- Scott (2010): Father "made it possible" — enabling, not punishing

### Thread 2: The Language of Withdrawal
- Talmage: "seems to have withdrawn"
- Maxwell: "apparent withdrawal"
- Holland: "briefly withdrew the comfort of His Spirit, the support of His personal presence"
- Scott: "felt removed the sustaining support"
- Haight: channels Talmage verbatim
- Hales: doesn't theologize it; uses it pastorally out loud

ALL of them hedge. None says "the Father literally abandoned the Son." The tradition is careful and consistent on this point.

### Thread 3: What the Withdrawal ACCOMPLISHED
- For the Son: comprehension (Maxwell), spiritual death experience (Holland), victory alone (Haight/Talmage), submissiveness tested (Maxwell 1985)
- For us: empathy/succoring (Maxwell), knowledge of our individual suffering (Bateman), model for our own moments of feeling forsaken (Hales)
- For the Father: glory (Maxwell 1989), victory of His plan (Talmage), costly endurance (Holland 1999)

### Thread 4: The Father's Experience
- Maxwell (1989): "will any of us want to rush forward to tell our Father how we, as parents, suffered when we watched our children suffer?"
- Holland (1999): "His every impulse and instinct must have been to stop it, to send angels to intervene—but He did not intervene"
- Maxwell (2001): "One ponders the agonies of Jesus' infinite Atonement and the feelings of the Father... There are no instructive, relevant revelations"
- Scott (2010): "His Father recognized that the Savior needed to accomplish the Atonement totally and completely on His own"

### Thread 5: Tension — Do They Actually Agree?
- **"Apparent" vs. "actual"**: Maxwell consistently says "apparent withdrawal" — implying the Father was never actually gone, the experience was subjective. Holland says the Father "briefly withdrew the comfort of His Spirit" — this sounds more like an actual (if temporary) act of withdrawal. Talmage sits between: "seems to have withdrawn."
- **What was withdrawn**: Spirit? Presence? Comfort? Support? Each speaker uses slightly different language. Holland distinguishes: "the comfort of His Spirit, the support of His personal presence." Scott: "sustaining support." Maxwell: "the Father's spirit" (lowercase).
- **"I don't believe Father forsook" vs. "Why hast thou forsaken me?"**: Both Holland and Scott insist God didn't forsake Christ. But the text of scripture itself is a cry of forsakenness. The resolution: the *experience* was real even if the *abandonment* wasn't. The Father withdrew something, and Christ FELT forsaken, even though He wasn't.
- **The broader tradition vs. Mosiah 15:5**: Abinadi says "the will of the Son being swallowed up in the will of the Father." Neither Holland nor Scott nor Maxwell explore this — that the Son's will was CONSUMED by the Father's will. This is a different framing than "the Father withdrew."
