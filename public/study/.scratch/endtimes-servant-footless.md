## Verified Quotes

### Isaiah 42 — Two Servants in One Chapter
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/42.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/42?lang=eng)

**v.1** "Behold my servant, whom I uphold; mine elect, in whom my soul delighteth; I have put my spirit upon him: he shall bring forth judgment to the Gentiles."
**v.2-3** Won't cry or lift up voice in street; bruised reed not broken, smoking flax not quenched — *gentle*, not forceful.
**v.4** "He shall not fail nor be discouraged, till he have set judgment in the earth: and the isles shall wait for his law."
**v.6** "I the Lord have called thee in righteousness...and give thee for a covenant of the people, for a light of the Gentiles"
**v.7** "To open the blind eyes, to bring out the prisoners from the prison"
**v.19** "Who is blind, but my servant? or deaf, as my messenger that I sent? who is blind as he that is perfect, and blind as the Lord's servant?"

**Observation (text only):** Isaiah presents TWO servant figures in this single chapter. The ideal servant (vv.1-7) is spirit-endowed, gentle, brings judgment/light to Gentiles. The blind servant (v.19) is Israel herself — deaf, blind, yet still called "my servant." Without headings saying "the Messiah," both readings are open on textual grounds alone.

### Isaiah 49 — The Servant Named Israel Who Gathers Israel
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/49.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/49?lang=eng)

**v.1** "The Lord hath called me from the womb; from the bowels of my mother hath he made mention of my name."
**v.3** "And said unto me, Thou art my servant, O Israel, in whom I will be glorified."
**v.5** "And now, saith the Lord that formed me from the womb to be his servant, to bring Jacob again to him"
**v.6** "I will also give thee for a light to the Gentiles, that thou mayest be my salvation unto the end of the earth."
**v.7** "Thus saith the Lord, the Redeemer of Israel, and his Holy One, to him whom man despiseth, to him whom the nation abhorreth, to a servant of rulers, Kings shall see and arise, princes also shall worship"

**Observation (text only):** This is one of the most paradoxical passages. The servant is NAMED "O Israel" (v.3) yet tasked to "bring Jacob again to him" (v.5). He simultaneously IS Israel and gathers Israel. He is "a light to the Gentiles" (same phrase as 42:6). He is despised, abhorred by the nation, a "servant of rulers" — yet kings will arise and worship. Without footnotes, this could be Christ, could be a prophetic figure, could be a collective personification of covenant Israel. The text holds all three open.

### Isaiah 52:13-15 — The Servant Exalted and Marred
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/52.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/52?lang=eng)

**v.13** "Behold, my servant shall deal prudently, he shall be exalted and extolled, and be very high."
**v.14** "As many were astonied at thee; his visage was so marred more than any man, and his form more than the sons of men:"
**v.15** "So shall he sprinkle many nations; the kings shall shut their mouths at him: for that which had not been told them shall they see; and that which they had not heard shall they consider."

**Observation:** "Marred" and "exalted" in the same figure. "Sprinkle many nations" — priestly/purifying language. "Kings shall shut their mouths" — echoed verbatim in 3 Nephi 21:8. Without headings: this could be (a) Messianic suffering, (b) a prophetic figure whose work astonishes, (c) both.

### Isaiah 53 — The Suffering Servant
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/53.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/53?lang=eng)

**v.3** "He is despised and rejected of men; a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief"
**v.5** "But he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities; the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed."
**v.7** "He was oppressed, and he was afflicted, yet he opened not his mouth; he is brought as a lamb to the slaughter"
**v.10** "Yet it pleased the Lord to bruise him...when thou shalt make his soul an offering for sin he shall see his seed"
**v.11** "by his knowledge shall my righteous servant justify many; for he shall bear their iniquities"
**v.12** "he hath poured out his soul unto death; and he was numbered with the transgressors; and he bore the sins of many, and made intercession for the transgressors"

**Observation (text only):** This is the most clearly Messianic/atoning servant passage. "Offering for sin," "bear their iniquities," "intercession for the transgressors" — the atonement language is unmistakable. On the text alone, this points to Christ with high confidence. The question: does v.10's "he shall see his seed" ONLY mean Christ, or could it have a secondary referent?

### Isaiah 11 — Stem, Rod, Branch, Root
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/11.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/11?lang=eng)

**v.1** "And there shall come forth a rod out of the stem of Jesse, and a Branch shall grow out of his roots"
**v.2** "And the spirit of the Lord shall rest upon him, the spirit of wisdom and understanding, the spirit of counsel and might, the spirit of knowledge and of the fear of the Lord"
**v.10** "And in that day there shall be a root of Jesse, which shall stand for an ensign of the people; to it shall the Gentiles seek: and his rest shall be glorious."
**v.11** "the Lord shall set his hand again the second time to recover the remnant of his people"
**v.12** "he shall set up an ensign for the nations, and shall assemble the outcasts of Israel"

**Observation (text only):** Without the heading ("The stem of Jesse (Christ)"), the rod, stem, branch, and root are formally separate terms. The "root of Jesse" in v.10 stands as "an ensign" and draws Gentiles — NOT the same function as the "rod" in v.1. This is where D&C 113 becomes crucial.

### D&C 113 — The Rosetta Stone (Revelation)
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/113.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/113?lang=eng)

**v.1-2** Q: "Who is the Stem of Jesse?" A: "Verily thus saith the Lord: It is Christ."
**v.3-4** Q: "What is the rod?" A: "It is a servant in the hands of Christ, who is partly a descendant of Jesse as well as of Ephraim, or of the house of Joseph, on whom there is laid much power."
**v.5-6** Q: "What is the root of Jesse?" A: "it is a descendant of Jesse, as well as of Joseph, unto whom rightly belongs the priesthood, and the keys of the kingdom, for an ensign, and for the gathering of my people in the last days."
**v.7-8** Q: "What is meant by 'Put on thy strength, O Zion' (Isa 52:1)?" A: "He had reference to those whom God should call in the last days, who should hold the power of priesthood to bring again Zion...to put on her strength is to put on the authority of the priesthood, which she, Zion, has a right to by lineage"
**v.10** "The scattered remnants are exhorted to return to the Lord...the bands of her neck are the curses of God upon her, or the remnants of Israel in their scattered condition among the Gentiles."

**Observation:** This is revelation through Joseph Smith, not editorial commentary. It explicitly distinguishes THREE figures from Isaiah 11: Stem = Christ, Rod = separate servant (of Jesse AND Ephraim/Joseph, much power), Root = separate descendant (Jesse + Joseph, holds priesthood/keys, gathers Israel in last days). On DnC 113 alone — without any footnote — we already have a multi-referent framework. The "rod" and "root" are NOT Christ. They are last-days figures of dual lineage (Davidic royal + Josephite covenant). The text itself insists on this distinction.

Also: vv.7-10 interpret Isaiah 52:1-2 as about the priesthood "by lineage" — connecting directly to D&C 86:8-11's "lawful heirs."

### 3 Nephi 20:43-46 — Jesus Quotes Isaiah 52:13-15
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/20.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/20?lang=eng)

**v.43** "Behold, my servant shall deal prudently; he shall be exalted and extolled and be very high."
**v.44** "As many were astonished at thee—his visage was so marred, more than any man, and his form more than the sons of men—"
**v.45** "So shall he sprinkle many nations; the kings shall shut their mouths at him, for that which had not been told them shall they see; and that which they had not heard shall they consider."
**v.46** "Verily, verily, I say unto you, all these things shall surely come, even as the Father hath commanded me. Then shall this covenant which the Father hath covenanted with his people be fulfilled"

**Observation:** Jesus himself quotes these Isaiah verses and frames them eschatologically ("shall surely come"). He doesn't say "this is about me" — he says it IS COMING. This is a future-oriented use. Combined with the next chapter (21), the "servant" here takes on a last-days dimension that would be separate from Christ's mortal ministry.

### 3 Nephi 21:8-11 — The Critical Passage
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21?lang=eng)

**v.8** "And when that day shall come, it shall come to pass that kings shall shut their mouths; for that which had not been told them shall they see; and that which they had not heard shall they consider."
**v.9** "For in that day, for my sake shall the Father work a work, which shall be a great and a marvelous work among them"
**v.10** "But behold, the life of my servant shall be in my hands; therefore they shall not hurt him, although he shall be marred because of them. Yet I will heal him, for I will show unto them that my wisdom is greater than the cunning of the devil."
**v.11** "Therefore it shall come to pass that whosoever will not believe in my words, who am Jesus Christ, which the Father shall cause him to bring forth unto the Gentiles, and shall give unto him power that he shall bring them forth unto the Gentiles, (it shall be done even as Moses said) they shall be cut off from among my people who are of the covenant."

**FOOTNOTES WE ARE SETTING ASIDE:**
- 10a links "marred" → D&C 135:1-3 (Joseph Smith martyrdom)
- 11a links "him" → 2 Ne. 3:11 (choice seer)

**Observation (text only, no footnotes):** Without the footnotes, what does the text tell us?
1. "My servant" is distinct from Jesus (Jesus speaks of "my servant" in third person)
2. His life is protected ("in my hands"), he can't be destroyed early
3. He WILL be "marred" — harmed, damaged — but healed
4. He brings forth Christ's words to the Gentiles
5. "It shall be done even as Moses said" — links to a Moses-like figure
6. The "marvelous work" and this servant are in the same context

The text alone DOES suggest a specific individual (not collective, not Christ). The "even as Moses said" phrase ties to 2 Nephi 3's choice seer prophecy. But without the footnote, an alert reader would need to know 2 Nephi 3 independently to make that connection. The text is pointing toward Joseph Smith, but it requires cross-referencing — which is exactly what footnotes do. The question: how naturally would a reader make this connection without the apparatus?

### 2 Nephi 3 — The Choice Seer
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3?lang=eng)

**v.6** "A seer shall the Lord my God raise up, who shall be a choice seer unto the fruit of my loins."
**v.7** "A choice seer will I raise up out of the fruit of thy loins; and he shall be esteemed highly"
**v.9** "And he shall be great like unto Moses"
**v.11** "But a seer will I raise up out of the fruit of thy loins; and unto him will I give power to bring forth my word unto the seed of thy loins—and not to the bringing forth my word only, saith the Lord, but to the convincing them of my word, which shall have already gone forth among them."
**v.12** "the fruit of thy loins shall write; and the fruit of the loins of Judah shall write; and that which shall be written by the fruit of thy loins, and also that which shall be written by the fruit of the loins of Judah, shall grow together"
**v.14** "they that seek to destroy him shall be confounded"
**v.15** "And his name shall be called after me; and it shall be after the name of his father. And he shall be like unto me"
**v.24** "And there shall rise up one mighty among them, who shall do much good, both in word and in deed, being an instrument in the hands of God"

**Observation (text only):** This is the STRONGEST text-only case for Joseph Smith as a named servant figure. Without any footnotes:
- Named after Joseph (v.15) — father also named Joseph
- Of the lineage of Joseph of Egypt (fruit of his loins)
- Compared to Moses (v.9) — same comparison as 3 Nephi 21:11 ("even as Moses said")
- Brings forth scripture (v.11) — Book of Mormon fits exactly
- Two records grow together (v.12) — Bible + Book of Mormon
- "They that seek to destroy him shall be confounded" (v.14) — protective language, like 3 Ne 21:10
- "One mighty among them" (v.24) — but note: this might be a different figure than vv.6-15

The text alone DOES identify Joseph Smith without footnotes. The name is given. The lineage is given. The Moses comparison is given. The footnotes didn't CREATE this reading — they simply pointed to it.

### Ezekiel 34:23-24 — "My Servant David" as Shepherd
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ezek/34.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ezek/34?lang=eng)

**v.23** "And I will set up one shepherd over them, and he shall feed them, even my servant David; he shall feed them, and he shall be their shepherd."
**v.24** "And I the Lord will be their God, and my servant David a prince among them; I the Lord have spoken it."
**v.25** "And I will make with them a covenant of peace"

**Observation (text only, no heading):** The chapter heading says "The Messiah will be their Shepherd." Remove it. The text says "my servant David." David had been dead for ~400 years when Ezekiel wrote. So this is either (a) a future Davidic king — a "new David," (b) the resurrected/returned David, or (c) the Messiah as David's heir. The text alone leaves this genuinely ambiguous. "My servant David a prince" — not king, prince. The Lord is their God; David is the prince under God.

### Ezekiel 37:24-25 — David My Servant, King Forever
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ezek/37.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ezek/37?lang=eng)

**v.24** "And David my servant shall be king over them; and they all shall have one shepherd: they shall also walk in my judgments, and observe my statutes, and do them."
**v.25** "And they shall dwell in the land...and my servant David shall be their prince for ever."
**v.26** "Moreover I will make a covenant of peace with them; it shall be an everlasting covenant"

**Observation:** Same pattern as Ezekiel 34 but stronger. "King over them" + "prince for ever" + "everlasting covenant." Without the heading ("David (the Messiah)") this figure could be the literal David (resurrection?), a Davidic descendant, or the Messiah. The "everlasting covenant" and "forever" language pushes toward messianic, but the insistence on "David" as a name rather than a title leaves room.

### Jeremiah 23:5-6 — The Righteous Branch
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/jer/23.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/jer/23?lang=eng)

**v.5** "Behold, the days come, saith the Lord, that I will raise unto David a righteous Branch, and a King shall reign and prosper, and shall execute judgment and justice in the earth."
**v.6** "In his days Judah shall be saved, and Israel shall dwell safely: and this is his name whereby he shall be called, The Lord Our Righteousness."

**Observation (text only, no heading):** The heading says "The Branch, who is the King (the Messiah)." Remove it. The text says "a righteous Branch" raised unto David. Named "The Lord Our Righteousness" (Heb: *Yahweh Tsidkenu*). This divine name strongly suggests Messiah—but Isaiah 11:1 also has a "Branch" that D&C 113 identifies as something other than the stem (Christ). On the text alone, the "Branch" and "righteous Branch" overlap with D&C 113's rod/root vocabulary but are not identical.

### D&C 85:7 — One Mighty and Strong
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/85.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/85?lang=eng)

**v.7** "And it shall come to pass that I, the Lord God, will send one mighty and strong, holding the scepter of power in his hand, clothed with light for a covering, whose mouth shall utter words, eternal words; while his bowels shall be a fountain of truth, to set in order the house of God, and to arrange by lot the inheritances of the saints"

**Observation:** A distinct figure — "one mighty and strong." His role: set in order the house of God, arrange inheritances. "Clothed with light," "scepter of power," "eternal words," "fountain of truth." This is NOT identified as Christ in the text. The function (arranging inheritances, ordering the house) is administrative/governmental, not atoning. The context is about consecration and Zion's organization. Some have read this as a future individual; the First Presidency in 1905 clarified it likely referred to the presiding bishop's role. Without footnotes, the text leaves the identity genuinely open.

### D&C 103:16,21 — The Moses Figure Explicitly Named
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/103.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/103?lang=eng)

**v.16** "Therefore, I will raise up unto my people a man, who shall lead them like as Moses led the children of Israel."
**v.21** "Verily, verily I say unto you, that my servant Joseph Smith, Jun., is the man to whom I likened the servant to whom the Lord of the vineyard spake in the parable which I have given unto you."

**Observation:** This is the most explicit text in the entire canon. No footnotes needed. D&C 103 directly says: (1) the Lord will raise up a Moses-like leader, (2) that leader is "my servant Joseph Smith, Jun." The Moses comparison connects back to 2 Nephi 3:9 and 3 Nephi 21:11. This is revelation, not editorial. This single verse demolishes any argument that the Joseph Smith identification only comes from footnotes.

### D&C 86:8-11 — The Collective Servant
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/86.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/86?lang=eng)

**v.8** "Therefore, thus saith the Lord unto you, with whom the priesthood hath continued through the lineage of your fathers—"
**v.9** "For ye are lawful heirs, according to the flesh, and have been hid from the world with Christ in God—"
**v.10** "Therefore your life and the priesthood have remained, and must needs remain through you and your lineage until the restoration of all things"
**v.11** "Therefore, blessed are ye if ye continue in my goodness, a light unto the Gentiles, and through this priesthood, a savior unto my people Israel."

**Observation:** This expands "the servant" to a COLLECTIVE: the priesthood holders who are "lawful heirs" by lineage, "hid from the world," and now called to be "a light unto the Gentiles" (Isaiah 49:6 language!) and "a savior unto my people Israel." D&C 113:8 connects to this — Zion's strength = priesthood authority by lineage. The collective-servant reading is in the text of D&C itself, not added by footnotes.

### Mosiah 14-15 — Abinadi's Multi-Referent Reading
**Source:** [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/14.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/14?lang=eng) + [15.md](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15?lang=eng)

Mosiah 14 = Abinadi quoting Isaiah 53 verbatim.
Mosiah 15 = Abinadi's INTERPRETATION.

**15:1** "God himself shall come down among the children of men, and shall redeem his people."
**15:10-11** "when his soul has been made an offering for sin he shall see his seed. And now what say ye? And who shall be his seed? Behold I say unto you, that whosoever has heard the words of the prophets...and believed that the Lord would redeem his people...these are his seed"
**15:13** "are not the prophets, every one that has opened his mouth to prophesy, that has not fallen into transgression...they are his seed."
**15:14-18** Beautiful feet passage — those who publish peace = past prophets (v.14), current publishers of peace (v.16), future publishers of peace (v.17), and Christ himself as "the founder of peace" (v.18).

**Observation:** Abinadi demonstrates the multi-referent hermeneutic that was at the heart of the original study. Isaiah 53's servant IS Christ (Mosiah 15:1, the suffering/atoning figure). But the "seed" of that servant = all who believe + the prophets themselves. And the "beautiful feet" = prophets past, present, and future + Christ. This is the Book of Mormon's own interpretive model: the servant is Christ primarily, but his servants (plural!) participate in his mission. No footnotes needed — Abinadi teaches this method in the text itself.

### Avraham Gileadi / Isaiah Institute — Servant Typology
**Sources:**
- "Question about How Many End-Time Servants" (isaiahinstitute.com, Apr 2024): https://isaiahinstitute.com/question-about-how-many-end-time-servants-in-the-book-of-isaiah/
- "Isaiah's Prophecies of God's End-Time Servant" (isaiahinstitute.com, Feb 2025): https://isaiahinstitute.com/isaiahs-prophecies-of-gods-end-time-servant/
- "How Can We Identify the Endtime Servant?" (isaiahinstitute.com, Aug 2021): https://isaiahinstitute.com/how-can-we-identify-the-endtime-servant/
- "How to Search Isaiah" (isaiahinstitute.com, Oct 2021): https://isaiahinstitute.com/how-to-search-isaiah/
- "Metaphors as Aliases of End-Time Persons" (isaiahinstitute.com, May 2023): https://isaiahinstitute.com/metaphors-as-aliases-of-end-time-persons/

**Gileadi's TWO-servant framework:**
1. **Collective servant** = Jacob/Israel — blind, deaf, still called "my servant" (Isa 42:18-20). God asks them to leave Babylon and return.
2. **Individual end-time servant** = distinct from Christ AND from any past prophet. A Davidic figure who restores the twelve tribes, prepares for Jehovah's coming. His mission is "temporal as well as spiritual" and "distinct from that of Jesus, which was spiritual and distinct from that of any latter-day prophet."

**Key Gileadi claims:**
- The rod/root/branch in Isaiah 11 are ALL separate persons (matches D&C 113!)
- The "end-time servant" is NOT Joseph Smith — Gileadi explicitly pushes back on that identification: "Someone who has read these things but hasn't searched them might say, 'That sounds like the prophet Joseph Smith!' and take the high moral ground when someone believes differently—though they themselves haven't searched the topic but simply jumped to conclusions about it."
- The servant is "hidden from the world for the purpose of testing God's people" — still future
- The servant is "named David" (Isa 37:35; 55:3) — Ezekiel's "my servant David" language
- The servant comes from the east (Isa 41:2, 25; 46:11)
- The servant functions as ensign, light to Gentiles, hand of deliverance
- The servant is "marred beyond human likeness" (Isa 52:14) — but Gileadi reads this as the end-time servant, NOT Christ
- The servant "bears the sins of many" and "makes intercession for transgressors" (Isa 53:11-12) — Gileadi applies parts of Isaiah 53 to this end-time figure, not only to Christ

**Gileadi's method:**
- Typological: past events serve as types/patterns for end-time fulfillment
- Isaiah uses "metaphors as aliases" — rod, staff, fire, sword, ensign, hand, voice, light all refer to either the servant or the Assyrian destroyer depending on context
- Word links across Isaiah identify a single figure by terms like "appoint" (41:27, 42:6, 55:4, 9:6)
- Joshua and Gideon as historical types of the end-time servant

**Critical note:** Gileadi's reading is MUCH more aggressive in applying Isaiah 53's suffering language to a non-Christ end-time figure than standard LDS or Christian exegesis. Most Latter-day Saints read Isaiah 53 as Messianic (Abinadi/Mosiah 15 confirms this). Gileadi allows dual fulfillment but treats the end-time servant as the primary referent for some of these passages. This is a significant departure.

## Critical Analysis Notes (Phase 3a)

### What the text alone STRONGLY supports:
1. **Multiple servants exist** — Isaiah 42 alone has two (ideal + blind Israel). D&C 113 confirms three distinct figures in Isaiah 11. D&C 86 adds a collective priesthood servant. The text is explicitly multi-referent.
2. **Isaiah 53 is Messianic** — Abinadi says so in Mosiah 15:1 without needing footnotes. "God himself shall come down...and shall redeem his people." This reading is IN the text.
3. **Joseph Smith is identified as a named servant** — 2 Nephi 3:15 gives the name ("called after me...after the name of his father"). D&C 103:21 explicitly says "my servant Joseph Smith, Jun." No footnotes needed.
4. **The collective servant is in the text** — D&C 86:11 ("a light unto the Gentiles, a savior unto my people Israel") uses Isaiah's exact servant language. D&C 113:8 connects priesthood by lineage. These are IN the D&C.
5. **3 Nephi 21:10-11 describes a specific last-days individual** — distinct from Jesus, protected, marred, brings forth Christ's words to Gentiles, "even as Moses said."

### What footnotes/headings AMPLIFIED but didn't create:
1. **3 Nephi 21:10a → D&C 135** (martyrdom) — Makes the Joseph Smith connection EXPLICIT for 3 Ne 21. Without it, a reader still gets there through 2 Ne 3 + "even as Moses said" in v.11, but the path is less direct.
2. **3 Nephi 21:11a → 2 Nephi 3:11** — Speed-links between the seer and the servant. Without it, a reader must independently recognize that "even as Moses said" echoes 2 Nephi 3:9.
3. **Isaiah 11 heading** ("The stem of Jesse (Christ)") — Pre-answers the question that D&C 113 answers. Without the heading, a reader confronts the open question fresh.
4. **Ezekiel 34 heading** ("The Messiah will be their Shepherd") — Closes a genuinely open question. The text says "my servant David," not "the Messiah." The heading forecloses the ambiguity.
5. **Jeremiah 23 heading** ("The Branch, who is the King (the Messiah)") — Same pattern: the heading resolves what the text leaves open.

### What footnotes/headings may have OBSCURED:
1. **The end-time servant as distinct from Joseph Smith** — If you follow 3 Ne 21:10a → D&C 135, the servant IS Joseph Smith, period. But Gileadi argues (and D&C 113 partially supports) that the "rod" and "root" are FUTURE figures, not past. The footnote may have closed a door the text left open.
2. **"My servant David" as a literal figure** — Headings saying "the Messiah" in Ezekiel prevent readers from asking: is this a literal Davidic descendant who will rule? The text is open to that reading.
3. **Isaiah 53 as ONLY Christological** — Mosiah 14-15 strongly drives the Christ reading, but Gileadi points out that the servant who is "marred" (52:14) and "bears iniquities" (53:11) has features that could apply to a non-divine servant-leader who takes upon himself the people's burdens in a different sense.

### Weakest links in the footless reading:
1. **Gileadi's future-servant-who-hasn't-come-yet** — This is the most speculative element. No LDS prophet has taught this. The Church's reading is consistently Christ + Joseph Smith + collective. Gileadi is an Isaiah scholar, not a prophet.
2. **Isaiah 53 applied to non-Christ figures** — Abinadi's explicit "God himself shall come down" (Mosiah 15:1) + "offering for sin" (14:10) is about as definitive as scripture gets. Applying 53 to a non-Christ figure requires significant hermeneutical gymnastics.
3. **The "marred" servant = Joseph Smith** — D&C 103:21 establishes Joseph as a Moses figure. 3 Ne 21:10 says the servant will be "marred...yet I will heal him." Joseph WAS martyred but not "healed" in any visible sense. This is a tension whether you use footnotes or not.

### The big answer to the study's core question:
**Do we reach the same conclusions without footnotes?** 

MOSTLY YES. The multi-referent reading (Christ + Joseph Smith + collective priesthood) stands firmly on:
- D&C 113 (revelation, not footnotes) distinguishing rod, root, and stem
- D&C 103:21 (revelation) naming Joseph Smith as the Moses figure
- 2 Nephi 3:15 (text) giving Joseph's name
- D&C 86:11 (text) using servant language for collective priesthood
- Mosiah 15 (text) establishing Christ as Isaiah 53's servant

The footnotes make the CONNECTIONS between these texts easier to see — they're hyperlinks in a pre-internet world. But the conclusions pre-exist the apparatus.

**Where it opens up:** The main NEW reading that emerges without footnotes is GILEADI'S — the possibility of a FUTURE end-time Davidic servant who hasn't yet appeared, distinct from both Christ and Joseph Smith. The footnotes close this door by mapping everything onto known figures. Without them, the door stays ajar. Whether you walk through it depends on whether you trust the prophetic pattern of dual/multiple fulfillment, and whether you think Ezekiel's "my servant David" must be metaphorical (Christ) or could be literal (a future Davidic leader).
