REVIEW: revised

Thomas asked Jesus how to get where he was going. He wanted a path, a plan, directions. Jesus answered by naming himself three times over. He did not hand Thomas a map. He said he is the way, and the truth, and the life. (see John 14:1-6)

That moment sits near the end of the Gospel of John. Earlier in the same room, Jesus had spoken of another three. He told the disciples that in him they would have peace, but in the world they would have trouble. The shape of that peace is trust in a person who has overcome the world. (see John 16:33)

Elsewhere, Paul gathers three words that outlast every other gift. He names faith, hope, and charity, and says the greatest is charity. (see 1 Corinthians 13:13) Mormon later pleads with his readers to pray for this charity with all the energy of their hearts, because without it they are nothing. He teaches that this love comes from Christ and must be received by prayer. (see Moroni 7:44-48)

Two triplets. One names a person. The other names what humans carry. The question is whether they are the same reality seen from two sides, or two different things, or a clean division between Christ-centered and us-centered.

At first, the division looks obvious. The way, the truth, and the life are all Jesus. Faith, hope, and charity are all capacities in us. One triplet is Christ describing himself. The other is apostles describing the church.

But charity breaks the divide immediately. Mormon does not say charity is human love that happens to be directed at Christ. He says it is the love that comes from Christ, and he says we must ask the Father to fill us with it. (see Moroni 7:47-48) That means this third virtue is not a native human capacity at all. It is a gift that arrives from outside. If charity is Christ's own love planted in us, then the human triplet already contains the person of Christ inside it.

Faith and hope do not stay on the human side either. The Book of Mormon often ties faith directly to Christ. It speaks of faith in Christ, hope in Christ, and looking forward to Christ with an eye of faith. (see Alma 32; Moroni 7) These are not generic mental states. They are specific orientations toward a person. Without that person, faith is mere optimism and hope is wishful thinking.

Therefore the human triplet is not us-centric. It is Christ-facing. It describes a soul turned toward its source.

Still, the two triplets are not the same concepts in different costumes. They do different work in the text. When Jesus says he is the way, he is answering a question about access to the Father. When Paul says charity is the greatest, he is ranking gifts within the body of believers. The contexts differ. The grammar differs. One triplet functions as identity. The other functions as character.

But they meet at the point where identity becomes character. Christ is the truth, and Alma describes faith as trusting in things we do not yet see because they are true. (see Alma 32:21) Christ is the life, and the love Mormon describes is what makes eternal life possible. (see Moroni 7:47) The way is a path to walk, and faith is the power that moves men to action. (see 2 Nephi 2:26)

These are not one-to-one equations. Faith is not exactly the way, hope is not exactly the truth, and charity is not exactly the life. Any such mapping crumbles under pressure. They are better understood as two linked descriptions of a single relationship. One describes the one who stands at the center. The other describes what happens to people when they stand near that center.

They are not one reality viewed from two angles. They are two realities that only exist in relation to each other.

The relationship is not symmetrical. Christ is the source. We are the recipients. But the gift we receive is so thoroughly his that our side of the equation does not look like self-help. It looks like possession. Charity possesses us. Faith and hope pull us out of ourselves.

This is why neither triplet works as a self-contained system. The way, the truth, and the life are not abstract principles we can study apart from Christ. Faith, hope, and charity are not psychological strengths we can cultivate apart from him. Both triplets collapse into him.

Becoming

This week, notice when you treat faith, hope, or charity as items on a personal improvement list. Replace that with prayer to be filled with charity. Read John 14 and Moroni 7 not as two separate lists to memorize but as two angles on one person. Before making a difficult decision, ask whether it places you on the way, in the truth, and toward the life. When you feel charity toward someone difficult, recognize that it may not be your own emotion but his love reaching through you.
