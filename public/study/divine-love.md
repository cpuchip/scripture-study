# Divine Love: What Is Conditional?

> **Binding Question:** If God "esteemeth all flesh in one" and "loveth those who will have him to be their God" — spoken in the same breath by the same prophet ([1 Nephi 17:35, 40](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/17?lang=eng&id=p35#p35)) — what exactly is conditional about divine love?

---

## The Tension in Nephi's Own Words

The question lands differently when you realize both sides of it live in the same sermon.

Nephi is standing at the shore, rebuking Laman and Lemuel, when he delivers two declarations five verses apart:

> "Behold, the Lord esteemeth all flesh in one; he that is righteous is favored of God." — [1 Nephi 17:35](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/17?lang=eng&id=p35#p35)

> "And he loveth those who will have him to be their God." — [1 Nephi 17:40](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/17?lang=eng&id=p40#p40)

God sees everyone equally — He "esteemeth all flesh in one." And God has a preferential posture toward the righteous — "he that is righteous is favored." Both statements are Nephi's. Both are true. And Nephi doesn't see a contradiction.

The word *esteem* in Webster 1828 means "to set a value on; to appreciate the worth of; to prize." God prizes all flesh equally. But *favor* means "kind regard; propitious aspect; countenance; friendly disposition — support, befriending." Favor has a directional quality that love doesn't. Love is dispositional — it's what you are. Favor is engaged support — it's what you turn toward someone.

So even in Nephi's language, there's a distinction: God values all equally but turns His active support toward those who choose Him. The esteeming is universal. The favoring is responsive. And the condition in verse 40 isn't perfection — it's willingness. "Those who will have him to be their God." The patriarchs God loved were deeply flawed men. Abraham lied about Sarah twice. Jacob schemed for his birthright. God loved them not because they performed flawlessly but because they *chose relationship*.

This is already a different picture than "love is conditional." It's closer to: love is constant, and those who turn toward it find it working actively in their lives.

---

## Nelson's Claim: "Unconditional" Is Not a Scriptural Word

In February 2003, then-Elder Russell M. Nelson published ["Divine Love"](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/liahona/2003/02/divine-love?lang=eng) in the Liahona. His opening thesis:

> "While divine love can be called perfect, infinite, enduring, and universal, it cannot correctly be characterized as *unconditional.* The word does not appear in the scriptures."

He's right about the word. "Unconditional" doesn't appear in our canon. And his article carries weight — it's an apostolic teaching that has shaped how many Latter-day Saints talk about God's love. The article marshals an impressive array of conditional scriptures:

> "If ye keep my commandments, ye shall abide in my love." — [John 15:10](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/15?lang=eng&id=p10#p10)

> "If you keep not my commandments, the love of the Father shall not continue with you." — [D&C 95:12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/95?lang=eng&id=p12#p12)

> "I love them that love me; and those that seek me … shall find me." — [Proverbs 8:17](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/prov/8?lang=eng&id=p17#p17)

These are genuine conditional statements. The "if … then" structure is undeniable. But here's what's worth noticing: Nelson *himself* makes the key distinction that the rest of his article obscures. Midway through, he asks a direct question:

> "Does this mean the Lord does not love the sinner? Of course not. Divine love is infinite and universal. The Savior loves both saints and sinners."

And later:

> "Thanks to the Atonement, the gift of immortality is *unconditional.* The greater gift of eternal life, however, is *conditional.*"

Nelson says divine love is "infinite and universal" and that God "loves both saints and sinners." Then he says immortality (the gift flowing from love) is unconditional, while eternal life (the ultimate blessing) is conditional. He has actually made the distinction between love and blessings. But his title, framing, and most-quoted line ("cannot correctly be characterized as unconditional") conflate what his own text distinguishes.

The article also makes a rhetorically powerful but logically questionable move — it associates the idea of unconditional love with Nehor, the anti-Christ who taught "that all mankind should be saved at the last day" without conditions ([Alma 1:4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/1?lang=eng&id=p4#p4)). But Nehor wasn't teaching about *love*; he was teaching universal salvation without repentance. Saying "God loves everyone" is not the same as saying "everyone is saved regardless of behavior." You can believe the first while firmly rejecting the second.

## What John Says: God IS Love

The Apostle John doesn't say God *has* love or God *shows* love. He says something ontological:

> "He that loveth not knoweth not God; for God is love." — [1 John 4:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-jn/4?lang=eng&id=p8#p8)

> "God is love; and he that dwelleth in love dwelleth in God, and God in him." — [1 John 4:16](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-jn/4?lang=eng&id=p16#p16)

Love isn't something God does. It's what God *is*. This is identity language. And it raises a question that runs through the rest of this study: can God conditionally stop being what He is?

Alma uses the same logic about justice: "If so, God would cease to be God" ([Alma 42:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/42?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)). If God's love is His nature — not a behavior He can switch on and off — then a God who withdrew His love would cease to be God in the same way that a God who abandoned justice would.

John also makes the sequencing explicit:

> "Herein is love, not that we loved God, but that he loved us, and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins." — [1 John 4:10](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-jn/4?lang=eng&id=p10#p10)

> "We love him, because he first loved us." — [1 John 4:19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-jn/4?lang=eng&id=p19#p19)

God loved *first*. Before we loved Him. Before obedience. Before conditions were met. The Atonement itself — the central act of history — was performed for people who hadn't yet repented. You don't die for people who have already met your conditions. You die for those who haven't.

## Paul's Answer: Nothing Can Separate Us

Paul takes the question head-on:

> "Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? shall tribulation, or distress, or persecution, or famine, or nakedness, or peril, or sword?" — [Romans 8:35](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/rom/8?lang=eng&id=p35#p35)

> "For I am persuaded, that neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor principalities, nor powers, nor things present, nor things to come, Nor height, nor depth, nor any other creature, shall be able to separate us from the love of God, which is in Christ Jesus our Lord." — [Romans 8:38-39](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/rom/8?lang=eng&id=p38-p39#p38)

The list is deliberately exhaustive. Death. Life. Angels. Powers. Things present. Things future. Height. Depth. Any other creature. Paul is saying: *nothing in all of creation* can sever God's love from us.

Notice what's absent from the list: *sin*. Paul mentions tribulation, persecution, peril, sword — but he doesn't mention sin as something that could separate us from God's love. If sin voided God's love, surely Paul would have named it here. He didn't. But elsewhere he makes clear that sin separates us from God's *presence* and *blessings* — just not from God's *love*.

## The Father Who Was Already Watching

Jesus tells the parable of the prodigal son ([Luke 15:11-32](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng&id=p11-p32#p11)) to a specific audience: "the Pharisees and scribes murmured, saying, This man receiveth sinners, and eateth with them" (v. 2). The whole parable is a response to people who think God shouldn't love the undeserving.

The younger son takes his inheritance and wastes it. He ends up feeding swine — about as far from covenant life as a Jewish audience could imagine. Then comes the turn:

> "And when he came to himself, he said, How many hired servants of my father's have bread enough and to spare, and I perish with hunger!" — [Luke 15:17](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng&id=p17#p17)

Not "when he became worthy." Not "when he earned enough to repay." He "came to himself." The language is awakening, not achievement. He decides simply to go:

> "I will arise and go to my father." — [Luke 15:18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng&id=p18#p18)

Now watch the father:

> "But when he was yet a great way off, his father saw him, and had compassion, and ran, and fell on his neck, and kissed him." — [Luke 15:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng&id=p20#p20)

The father *saw him a great way off*. He was watching the road. He had been watching. The son hadn't arrived yet. Hadn't confessed. Hadn't met a single condition. And the father *ran* — in the ancient Near East, a patriarch running was undignified, scandalous — and fell on his neck and kissed him.

The son tries to deliver his rehearsed confession: "Father, I have sinned against heaven, and in thy sight, and am no more worthy to be called thy son" (v. 21). But the father doesn't even let him finish. He calls for the robe, the ring, the shoes — symbols of full restoration, not probation.

And to the elder son, who nursed his faithfulness like a grievance:

> "Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine." — [Luke 15:31](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng&id=p31#p31)

The faithful son already had everything. The love was already complete for him, too. He just didn't understand it.

This parable does not describe conditional love. The father never stopped loving. The son's departure didn't change the father's heart — it changed the son's *access* to the father's household. The return restored access, not love. And the celebration wasn't favoritism — it was the joy of restoration: "This thy brother was dead, and is alive again; and was lost, and is found" (v. 32).

## Love and Law: Oaks' Synthesis

Elder Dallin H. Oaks offered the most nuanced prophetic treatment of this tension in his October 2009 address ["Love and Law"](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2009/10/love-and-law?lang=eng). His framing is careful:

> "The love of God does not supersede His laws and His commandments, and the effect of God's laws and commandments does not diminish the purpose and effect of His love."

Love doesn't override law. Law doesn't cancel love. They coexist. And Oaks makes a key observation about God's anger:

> "God's love is so perfect that He lovingly requires us to obey His commandments because He knows that only through obedience to His laws can we become perfect, as He is. For this reason, God's anger and His wrath are not a *contradiction* of His love but an *evidence* of His love."

This is the parental frame. Every parent knows you can love a child completely while being deeply frustrated by their choices. The frustration doesn't negate the love — it flows from it. You wouldn't be angry if you didn't care.

But the most striking moment in Oaks' talk comes from a woman he quotes — someone who had recently returned to Church activity:

> "He has always been there for me, even when I rejected Him. He has always guided me and comforted me with His tender mercies all around me, but I [was] too angry to see and accept incidents and feelings as such."

God was always there. His love never stopped reaching. She was too angry to *feel* it. This is the access-versus-disposition distinction in lived experience: the love doesn't stop; our capacity to feel it does.

Oaks also introduces the language of "choicest blessings":

> "God's universal and perfect love is shown in all the blessings of His gospel plan, including the fact that His choicest blessings are reserved for those who obey His laws."

Universal love. Conditional blessings. Oaks holds both without conflating them.

## The Distinction That Resolves the Tension

The question "Is God's love conditional?" may be the wrong question. It conflates two different things:

1. **God's disposition toward us** — His nature, His character, who He is
2. **What we can receive and experience** — blessings, presence, spiritual gifts, the fullness of joy

The first is constant. "God is love" ([1 John 4:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-jn/4?lang=eng&id=p8#p8)). He weeps over the wicked ([Moses 7:28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/pgp/moses/7?lang=eng&id=p28#p28)). He was watching the road while the prodigal was still a great way off ([Luke 15:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng&id=p20#p20)). "Nothing can separate us from the love of God" ([Romans 8:39](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/rom/8?lang=eng&id=p39#p39)).

The second is conditional. "When we obtain any blessing from God, it is by obedience to that law upon which it is predicated" ([D&C 130:21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/130?lang=eng&id=p21#p21)). "None but the truly penitent are saved" ([Alma 42:24](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/42?lang=eng&id=p24#p24)). Eternal life — the greatest gift — requires covenant faithfulness ([D&C 14:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/14?lang=eng&id=p7#p7)).

The Atonement itself demonstrates this pattern. God "himself atoneth for the sins of the world, to bring about the plan of mercy, to appease the demands of justice" ([Alma 42:15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/42?lang=eng&id=p15#p15)). Love *motivated* the Atonement. It's the reason the plan exists. But *accessing* the full power of the Atonement — receiving a remission of sins, being sanctified, entering God's presence — requires repentance.

Love created the system. Conditions govern the system.

Even D&C 95, the very section Nelson cites for conditional love, opens this way:

> "Verily, thus saith the Lord unto you whom I love, and whom I love I also chasten that their sins may be forgiven, for with the chastisement I prepare a way for their deliverance in all things out of temptation, and I have loved you." — [D&C 95:1](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/95?lang=eng&id=p1#p1)

"Whom I love I also chasten." The chastening IS the love. The correction flows from love, not from withdrawal of love. And the section ends with "I have loved you" — present tense reality veiled in past tense grammar. God's love precedes and motivates everything that follows, including the conditional language in verse 12.

The revealed pattern in [D&C 121](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121?lang=eng) makes this explicit. The divine model of influence operates by "persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned" (v. 41). And after correction? "Showing forth afterwards an increase of love toward him whom thou hast reproved" (v. 43). Not withdrawal. *Increase*. The pattern is correction-followed-by-more-love. And the scope? "Charity towards all men" (v. 45). Not towards the obedient men. All men.

## When He Came to Himself

So what is the condition?

Not perfection. Not performance. The condition is *turning*. The prodigal "came to himself" and arose ([Luke 15:17-18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/15?lang=eng&id=p17-p18#p17)). King Benjamin's people "fell to the earth" and cried for mercy and were "filled with joy" ([Mosiah 4:1-3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/4?lang=eng&id=p1-p3#p1)). King Benjamin then asks them to remember what happened:

> "If ye have known of his goodness and have *tasted* of his love, and have received a remission of your sins, which causeth such exceedingly great joy in your souls." — [Mosiah 4:11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/4?lang=eng&id=p11#p11)

*Tasted*. The love was already on the table. The tasting required turning toward it — falling, crying, asking. The condition was not earning the feast but sitting down at it.

This reframes what the conditional scriptures are saying. When Jesus says "If ye keep my commandments, ye shall abide in my love" ([John 15:10](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/15?lang=eng&id=p10#p10)), the vine metaphor from earlier in the chapter illuminates: the vine doesn't withdraw sap from branches. Branches that disconnect from the vine wither because they've left the source. "Abide in" is relational presence, not transactional performance. The love is the starting condition. Obedience is how we stay connected to it.

Enoch saw this from the cosmic scale. God looked at the wicked — "so great wickedness" that He would send floods — and wept:

> "How is it that the heavens weep, and shed forth their tears as the rain upon the mountains?" — [Moses 7:28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/pgp/moses/7?lang=eng&id=p28#p28)

> "The whole heavens shall weep over them, even all the workmanship of mine hands; wherefore should not the heavens weep, seeing these shall suffer?" — [Moses 7:37](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/pgp/moses/7?lang=eng&id=p37#p37)

A God of conditional love would simply stop loving the disobedient and move on. But this God weeps. The heavens themselves weep. The weeping proves the love persists through the rejection.

And Jesus, among the Nephites, holds both postures at once — compassion for the righteous before Him ("my bowels are filled with compassion towards you," [3 Nephi 17:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/17?lang=eng&id=p6#p6)) and grief for the wicked who are absent ("Father, I am troubled because of the wickedness of the people of the house of Israel," [3 Nephi 17:14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/17?lang=eng&id=p14#p14)). He weeps for both. Love doesn't choose one or the other.

## Moroni's Final Word: Charity Never Faileth

Mormon, near the end of all things, gives us the definition:

> "Charity is the pure love of Christ, and it endureth forever; and whoso is found possessed of it at the last day, it shall be well with him." — [Moroni 7:47](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/7?lang=eng&id=p47#p47)

If charity is the pure love of Christ, and charity "never faileth" ([Moroni 7:46](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/7?lang=eng&id=p46#p46)), and charity "endureth forever" — then by definition, the pure love of Christ is unfailing and eternal. It beareth all things. Believeth all things. Hopeth all things. Endureth all things (v. 45).

That's not conditional language. That's permanence language. If the pure love of Christ endureth forever, it doesn't have an off switch.

The condition in Moroni's framing is about *us* receiving it:

> "Pray unto the Father with all the energy of heart, that ye may be filled with this love, which he hath bestowed upon all who are true followers of his Son, Jesus Christ." — [Moroni 7:48](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/7?lang=eng&id=p48#p48)

The condition is praying, turning, opening ourselves to be *filled*. But the love that fills us has already been bestowed. It endureth forever. It never faileth.

## Becoming

Here's where this lands for me.

If God's love is His nature — and the scriptures from Genesis to Moroni testify that it is — then I have never, not for a single moment of my life, been unloved by God. Not in my worst moments. Not in the far country. Not when I was feeding swine and couldn't feel anything.

The father was watching the road.

What changes is not whether I'm loved but whether I'm *positioned* to feel it. Repentance isn't earning God's love back. It's coming home to what was always there. "I will arise and go to my father." That's the whole movement. The father does the rest — He runs, He embraces, He restores.

And here's the other side: if I'm the elder son — faithful, diligent, but secretly resentful that the returning prodigal gets a party — I need to hear the Father's words to me too: "Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine." The love was already complete. I already had everything. I just didn't understand it.

Nelson is right that blessings are conditional and that we shouldn't treat God's love as a blanket excuse for disobedience. Nehor's error was real. But the question "is God's love conditional?" deserves a more careful answer than the word "conditional" alone can carry. God is love. His love endureth forever. It never faileth. And when we sin, He weeps. Not from indifference. Not from withdrawal. From love that refuses to stop.

The condition is not earning His love. The condition is turning toward it.
