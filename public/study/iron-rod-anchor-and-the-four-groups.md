# The Iron Rod, the Anchor, and the Four Groups

*A study on whether the iron rod is the chain that tethers the soul to the anchor of hope — and on what the four groups in Lehi and Nephi's vision teach about how people relate to hope through the word.*

---

## I. The Question, and Where the Prior Studies Stop

Three earlier studies have already done load-bearing work and this one builds on them rather than re-deriving them.

[Tree of Life and the Chain](tree-of-life-and-the-chain.md) maps the rod, the tree, and the fruit onto the faith-hope-charity chain: the rod is the word, the tree is the love of God, the fruit is Christ Himself. [Hope, and the Grammar of Pairs](hope-and-the-grammar-of-pairs.md) treats the anchor passages of Hebrews 6 and Ether 12, ties hope to the temple veil, and shows that the organ of hope is the eye fixed on Christ. [The Way, the Truth, the Life](way-truth-life.md) establishes that the veil through which the anchor enters is the flesh of Christ Himself, citing [Hebrews 10:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/10?lang=eng&id=p20#p20) — "a new and living way, which he hath consecrated for us, through the veil, that is to say, his flesh."

So the picture going into this study is: hope is an anchor, the anchor enters within the veil, the veil is Christ's flesh, the rod is the word, the tree is the love of God, the fruit is Christ.

What the prior studies do not close is the seam between two of those images. Hope is the anchor that grips on the far side of the veil; the soul is here, on this side. *What is between them?* What is the line, the cable, the chain that connects this soul to that anchor? Hebrews does not say. Ether 12 says hope "maketh an anchor" but does not name the rope.

Michael's question puts the seam directly: is the iron rod that Lehi and Nephi saw — the rod identified by the angel as "the word of God" — what runs from soul to anchor? Is the word the chain?

This is also a study of how souls actually relate to that chain in practice. Lehi saw multiple groups in his vision. Some held. Some let go. Some never reached it. Some never started. Each group is a way the chain can be related to, and the difference between groups is the difference between hope's success and hope's failure.

## II. Two Anchor Passages, Read Together

The two anchor texts are not identical. The difference matters.

Paul, exhorting the Hebrews to follow the patient endurance of Abraham, names two immutable things — the promise and the oath of God — and then says:

> "That by two immutable things, in which it was impossible for God to lie, we might have a strong consolation, who have fled for refuge to lay hold upon the hope set before us: Which hope we have as an anchor of the soul, both sure and steadfast, and which entereth into that within the veil; Whither the forerunner is for us entered, even Jesus, made an high priest for ever after the order of Melchisedec." — [Hebrews 6:18-20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/6?lang=eng&id=p18-p20#p18)

Paul calls hope itself the anchor.

Moroni, writing centuries later about Ether's preaching, gives the image differently:

> "Wherefore, whoso believeth in God might with surety hope for a better world, yea, even a place at the right hand of God, which hope cometh of faith, maketh an anchor to the souls of men, which would make them sure and steadfast, always abounding in good works, being led to glorify God." — [Ether 12:4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/ether/12?lang=eng&id=p4#p4)

Moroni says hope *makes* an anchor.

Read flatly, the two verses contradict. Paul: hope is the anchor. Moroni: hope is the maker of an anchor. The reflex is to harmonize one into the other and lose the texture of both. The honest reading is to hold both.

Moroni's grammar is productive. Belief in God produces hope; hope is what makes the anchor; the anchor makes the soul sure; the sure soul abounds in good works. Seven verbs in one verse, all moving forward, faith feeding hope feeding anchor feeding soul feeding works. Hope is named at the moment of production: the soul is an anchor-making place, and hope is the material the anchor is made from.

Paul's grammar is structural. Once the anchor is in place, the thing doing the work — the thing that is "sure and steadfast" — is hope. Paul is naming what the anchor *is* once it has been made and set. The anchor is not a separate thing from hope; it is hope, taking on the function of holding.

The two grammars line up if you let them. Moroni tells you how the anchor gets there. Paul tells you what it does once it is.

The decisive line is the second half of Hebrews 6:19 — "*which entereth into that within the veil*." The anchor's grip is not in itself. The anchor enters; the veil is a place; the place is on the other side. And the next verse names what is there: "the forerunner is for us entered, even Jesus." The anchor's grip is on Christ, who has gone in first.

So the device-name is hope; the gripping point is Christ. Whether you call hope the anchor or the rope or the line is a matter of grammar; what makes the soul sure and steadfast is that hope's far end is gripped on the resurrected Christ in the holy place. Hope holds because Christ holds. The anchor is steadfast because the One the anchor is fastened to is steadfast.

This is the geometry. The anchor is set within the veil. Christ is the One who set it, and is the One the line is gripped on. The soul, on this side of the veil, holds the line.

What is the line?

## III. The Rod as the Line

Lehi describes the rod twice in 1 Nephi 8 — first as a feature of the landscape, then as something people grasp.

> "And I beheld a rod of iron, and it extended along the bank of the river, and led to the tree by which I stood. And I also beheld a strait and narrow path, which came along by the rod of iron, even to the tree by which I stood." — [1 Nephi 8:19-20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p19-p20#p19)

Notice the geometry. The rod *extended along* the bank. It is continuous, not a marker placed at the start. The path came along *by* the rod. The rod runs the full length of the journey, from the place where people enter the path to the tree itself. Wherever a soul stands on that path, the rod is reachable. This is the architecture of a tether. A chain is continuous from anchor to swimmer; the rod is continuous from path's beginning to the tree.

Nephi, asking the angel for the meaning, is told what the rod represents:

> "And it came to pass that I beheld that the rod of iron, which my father had seen, was the word of God, which led to the fountain of living waters, or to the tree of life; which waters are a representation of the love of God." — [1 Nephi 11:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)

The verb is *led*. The rod does not *become* the love of God; it *leads to* it. The rod is the means; the tree and the fountain are the destination. Means is the right category for a chain. A chain does not arrive; it conducts arrival.

Years later, when Nephi's brothers ask him directly, his gloss is more practical:

> "I said unto them that it was the word of God; and whoso would hearken unto the word of God, and would hold fast unto it, they would never perish; neither could the temptations and the fiery darts of the adversary overpower them unto blindness, to lead them away to destruction." — [1 Nephi 15:24](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/15?lang=eng&id=p24#p24)

Two verbs in order: hearken, hold fast. The hand follows the ear. Where the ear is listening, the hand is gripping. The promise is that the soul who does both *would never perish* and would not be overpowered *unto blindness*. The eye is what the adversary attacks; the rod is what protects the eye. (This is why in the prior hope study the organ of hope was named as the eye — the rod is what keeps the eye open in the mist.)

So far the rod is the means by which a soul on the path reaches what is at the end of the path. But the chain image — the soul tethered to an anchor on the other side of a veil — is not yet stated in those words.

It is stated, almost verbatim, in Helaman.

> "Yea, we see that whosoever will may lay hold upon the word of God, which is quick and powerful, which shall divide asunder all the cunning and the snares and the wiles of the devil, and lead the man of Christ in a strait and narrow course across that everlasting gulf of misery which is prepared to engulf the wicked— And land their souls, yea, their immortal souls, at the right hand of God in the kingdom of heaven, to sit down with Abraham, and Isaac, and with Jacob, and with all our holy fathers, to go no more out." — [Helaman 3:29-30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/3?lang=eng&id=p29-p30#p29)

The verbs are stacked: *lay hold upon* the word, the word *leads* the man across the gulf, the word *lands* their souls at the right hand of God. *Land* is shipping language. A ship lands at a harbor by being pulled in by a line attached to an anchor. The word lays hold of the soul that lays hold of it; the word crosses the gulf the soul could not cross alone; the word lands the soul at the place the soul could not reach alone. Helaman is describing exactly the function of a chain. The destination — "the right hand of God" — is the same destination Moroni named in Ether 12:4. The word lands souls where the anchor is set.

Therefore the binding question can be answered at the level of the text, not just at the level of inference. The rod is the word; the word is what is laid hold upon; the word is what leads across the gulf and lands the soul. Whether the image is called a rod, a tether, or a chain, the function is the same: the word is the conducting line between the soul on this side and the place where Christ has entered as forerunner. The rod and the anchor are not two systems. They are two views of the same line, with one end gripped on Christ within the veil and the other end held by the soul in the mist.

There is a deeper convergence. Bednar, preaching in April 2022, named what John had named centuries earlier — that one of the names of Jesus Christ is the Word ([John 1:1, 14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/1?lang=eng&id=p1#p1)). The scriptures are the word. The rod is the word. Christ is the Word. The chain that tethers the soul to the anchor is Christ; the anchor itself is Christ; the destination is Christ. Three names of one Person. The image holds because the whole geometry is held by Him.

> "Therefore, one of the names of Jesus Christ is 'The Word.' ... Thus, the teachings of the Savior, as recorded in the holy scriptures, also are 'the word.'" — Bednar, [But We Heeded Them Not](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2022/04/14bednar?lang=eng)

## IV. The Four Groups

Lehi's vision describes how souls actually relate to that chain. The standard reading numbers the groups four. The text gives a slightly richer picture, and the richness matters.

**The first group commenced the path but never reached the rod.**

> "And I saw numberless concourses of people, many of whom were pressing forward, that they might obtain the path which led unto the tree by which I stood. And it came to pass that they did come forth, and commence in the path which led to the tree. And it came to pass that there arose a mist of darkness; yea, even an exceedingly great mist of darkness, insomuch that they who had commenced in the path did lose their way, that they wandered off and were lost." — [1 Nephi 8:21-23](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p21-p23#p21)

These were in motion. They pressed forward. They started. But the rod is not mentioned in the same breath. They were on the path without holding the iron, and when the mist came they were lost. Sunday-school treatments often skip this group because they don't fit the rod-fruit-mock pattern of the other three. They are the group that learned that being in motion in the right direction is not enough. Pressing forward without the chain is not pressing forward enough.

**The second group held the rod, partook, were ashamed, and fell away.**

> "And it came to pass that I beheld others pressing forward, and they came forth and caught hold of the end of the rod of iron; and they did press forward through the mist of darkness, clinging to the rod of iron, even until they did come forth and partake of the fruit of the tree. And after they had partaken of the fruit of the tree they did cast their eyes about as if they were ashamed... And after they had tasted of the fruit they were ashamed, because of those that were scoffing at them; and they fell away into forbidden paths and were lost." — [1 Nephi 8:24-25, 28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p24-p25#p24)

This group is the chilling one. They had everything. They reached the rod. They walked it through the mist. They tasted the fruit. The mist did not stop them — the building did, after they had already arrived. The Hebrew of v. 25 is "cast their eyes about." The eye drifted *after* the fruit was already in the mouth. The text does not say doubt destroyed them; shame did, and shame came from the gaze of the building. Their failure was not at the start. It was at the end, after success had been reached.

The exact pattern is in Hebrews 6, and the parallel is too tight to be accident:

> "For it is impossible for those who were once enlightened, and have tasted of the heavenly gift, and were made partakers of the Holy Ghost, And have tasted the good word of God, and the powers of the world to come, If they shall fall away, to renew them again unto repentance; seeing they crucify to themselves the Son of God afresh, and put him to an open shame." — [Hebrews 6:4-6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/6?lang=eng&id=p4-p6#p4)

*Tasted of the heavenly gift* matches *partook of the fruit*. *Fall away* matches *fell away into forbidden paths*. *Open shame* matches *they were ashamed*. Three matches in five verses. The same chapter that gives the anchor (vv. 18-19) gives the warning to those who tasted and let go (vv. 4-6). Hebrews 6 puts both halves in one chapter; Lehi puts them in one vision. The pastoral logic is the same: you who taste must keep holding.

The Joseph Smith Translation reads Hebrews 6:1 as "*not* leaving the principles of the doctrine of Christ, let us go on unto perfection" ([JST Heb. 6:1, footnote 1a](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/6?lang=eng)). Joseph reads Paul as saying: do not let go of the foundation while reaching for the higher thing. That is exactly the rod's logic. Do not let go after you taste. Keep the chain held.

**The third group held continually and were not lost.**

> "But, to be short in writing, behold, he saw other multitudes pressing forward; and they came and caught hold of the end of the rod of iron; and they did press their way forward, continually holding fast to the rod of iron, until they came forth and fell down and partook of the fruit of the tree." — [1 Nephi 8:30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p30#p30)

The differentiating phrase is *continually holding fast*. Group 2 caught hold and clung; Group 3 held continually. The verb in 8:30 is also new — *fell down* before they partook. Humility at the tree, not just arrival at it. And note how Lehi introduces the group: "to be short in writing." Lehi gives the saved group fewer words than he gives any of the failing groups. The text's narrative attention is mostly given to those who fail. The dream, like the gospel, holds out warning more loudly than triumph because the warning is what saves.

Bednar drew the cling/hold-fast line in 2022:

> "Please note that the ability to resist the temptations and the fiery darts of the adversary is promised to those individuals who 'hold fast to' rather than merely 'cling to' the word of God." — Bednar, [But We Heeded Them Not](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2022/04/14bednar?lang=eng)

To cling is what you do in fear. To hold fast is what you do in covenant. The same hand can grip in either posture, but the postures produce different outcomes when the building begins to scoff.

**The fourth group was in the building, mocking.**

> "And I also cast my eyes round about, and beheld, on the other side of the river of water, a great and spacious building; and it stood as it were in the air, high above the earth. And it was filled with people, both old and young, both male and female; and their manner of dress was exceedingly fine; and they were in the attitude of mocking and pointing their fingers towards those who had come at and were partaking of the fruit." — [1 Nephi 8:26-27](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p26-p27#p26)

The building stood "in the air, high above the earth" — no foundation. It is on the other side of the river, separated from the tree by the gulf Nephi later identifies as filthiness and hell ([1 Nephi 15:27-29](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/15?lang=eng&id=p27-p29#p27)). This group never approached the rod. They never started the path. Their relationship to the chain is not failure of grip; it is refusal of the path itself. And their work is to mock those who *did* arrive — the fruit-eaters specifically. Not the seekers, not the path-walkers. The arrivals. The building's gravity is aimed at hope's fulfillment.

Lehi names the diagnosis after describing all of it:

> "These are the words of my father: For as many as heeded them, had fallen away." — [1 Nephi 8:34](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p34#p34)

*Heeded* means paid attention to, turned the eye toward. The mockery itself was not what destroyed Group 2. The listening was. They turned the eye toward the building after the fruit was already in the mouth, and what the eye stays fixed on is what the soul becomes filled with. The prior hope study traced this through Matthew 6:22 — "the light of the body is the eye." The rod keeps the body walking; the eye keeps the body filling with light. To heed the building is to take the eye off the prototype, and the body, no longer filling with that light, fills with whatever else is in view.

A fifth movement in the vision is worth naming honestly: "many were drowned in the depths of the fountain; and many were lost from his view, wandering in strange roads" ([1 Nephi 8:32](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p32#p32)). Lehi at first did not distinguish the fountain of living waters from the river of filthiness; Nephi later clarified that what Lehi initially saw as one body of water was in fact two — "so much was his mind swallowed up in other things that he beheld not the filthiness of the water" ([1 Nephi 15:27](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/15?lang=eng&id=p27#p27)). The drowned drowned in *filthiness*, not in love. They went into the river instead of toward the fountain. So the four-group framing is a useful simplification of a vision that contains, at minimum, the path-starters who lost the way, the rod-grippers who fell after tasting, the rod-grippers who held continually, the building-dwellers who mocked, and the wanderers who drowned in filthiness or were lost in strange roads. The vision is a taxonomy of failure with one trajectory of success threading through it.

## V. The Mist, the Building, and What the Chain is Made For

Two threats are named in the vision. They operate differently and call for different responses.

The mist is environmental. It rises and obscures the path. It is not aimed at any particular person. The footnote on "mist" in 1 Nephi 8:23 cross-references Matthew 13:19, the parable of the sower, where Christ describes "the wicked one" who "catcheth away that which was sown in his heart." So the mist is the active work of the adversary at the level of perception — the *fiery darts* Nephi names in 15:24, the *cunning and snares and wiles* Helaman names in 3:29. The mist makes the eye blind. The rod is what answers the mist, because the rod can be held when the eye cannot see.

The building is social. It speaks. It mocks. It points fingers. Its threat is not at the level of perception but at the level of attention — what the soul *heeds*. Nephi later sees the building rise again, this time gathered to fight the apostles of the Lamb after Christ has been slain ([1 Nephi 11:34-36](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p34-p36#p34)). The building is the world organized against the gospel's arrival. Its weapon is shame. And Bednar's sermon names the answer: heed not.

So the chain is made for both. The rod holds the soul through the mist when sight fails. And the rod, as the word of God internalized, gives the soul something to heed in place of the building's voice. To hearken to the word is to not hearken to the scoffing. The ear can only listen to one thing at a time, and the rod is the alternative voice the soul listens to.

The chain is iron, not silk. Iron does not give when the wind pulls. The metaphor is doctrinal. The word is not a soft thing that yields to circumstance. It is the same in the mist and out of it. It is the same when the building is silent and when the building is jeering. The reason Group 3 was "continually holding fast" is that the rod made continuous holding possible. A rope of cloth would stretch and tear in the wind; an iron rod will not. What makes the chain trustworthy is that the chain is made of what does not yield — the word of God, which "is quick, and powerful, and sharper than any twoedged sword" ([Hebrews 4:12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/4?lang=eng&id=p12#p12)).

The Heb-vs-Ether tension on the anchor resolves here too. Paul calls hope itself the anchor because, structurally, it is what is sure and steadfast — and what makes hope sure and steadfast is that the line is iron and the gripping point is Christ. Moroni calls hope the *maker* of the anchor because, productively, hope is what reaches toward Christ and forms the connection. Hope is the anchor in Paul's view; hope is the line that becomes the anchor in Moroni's view. The two grammars hold the same reality from different angles. Both are right. Both belong in the picture.

## VI. Pressing Forward, Feasting, and the Single Eye

Holding the rod is defensive. Nephi's gloss to his own people, near the end of his record, gives the active form of the same posture:

> "Wherefore, ye must press forward with a steadfastness in Christ, having a perfect brightness of hope, and a love of God and of all men. Wherefore, if ye shall press forward, feasting upon the word of Christ, and endure to the end, behold, thus saith the Father: Ye shall have eternal life." — [2 Nephi 31:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/31?lang=eng&id=p20#p20)

Three things matter in that verse. *Press forward* is motion. Group 1's failure was failure of motion in the absence of grip; the answer is motion *with* grip. *Feasting upon the word of Christ* is sustenance. The rod is held, and the rod is also fed upon. A chain you hold passively keeps you tethered; a chain you eat from becomes part of you. Nephi's image expands the rod's function: it is gripped through the mist *and* it is consumed for strength. Both. *Endure to the end* is duration. Group 2's failure was failure of duration after success; the answer is to keep doing both, the gripping and the feasting, all the way through.

Between the gripping and the feasting sits a third thing: *a perfect brightness of hope*. This is the eye — the organ that the prior hope study named as the way the soul sees forward when it cannot yet see what it is looking at. Hope's brightness is what keeps the eye from drifting toward the building. The rod is the hand's work; hope is the eye's work; love is what the soul becomes when both have been done long enough. Faith, hope, charity, in the walking version Nephi gives the doctrine of Christ.

This also clarifies what kind of chain the rod is. It is not the kind of chain that drags. It is the kind that pulls. The soul who holds the rod is being conducted toward Christ — drawn from here, through the mist, across the gulf, up the path, to the tree, into the room beyond the veil where Christ has entered as forerunner. The chain is iron because the world tugs the other way; the chain is the word because the word is Christ; the chain pulls because the anchor is set.

Moroni puts it most plainly: man "must hope, or he cannot receive an inheritance in the place which thou hast prepared" ([Ether 12:32](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/ether/12?lang=eng&id=p32#p32)). Hope is not optional ornamentation on the path. It is the line itself, taut in the soul, fastened on Christ. To stop hoping is to drop the line. To drop the line is to be lost in the mist or seduced by the building or drowned in the river. To hold the line is to be pulled in.

## VII. Becoming

Michael, you teach Sunday School. You stand in front of a class with a portion of the word and offer it. You also live in mid-life. The mist is real. The building is louder than it used to be — not in the abstract, but specifically, in the things you read and the things your work surfaces and the people who scoff at faith from inside positions of credentialed authority. The path runs through that mist and past that building.

Three things follow from this study, and they are not abstractions.

The first is that the rod is iron and your hand is on it whenever you are studying the scriptures. You are not seeking knowledge; you are gripping a chain. The work you do at the desk in the morning, the slow reading of footnotes, the writing of these studies — that is hand-on-rod work. It is not preparation for some other holier activity. It is the holy activity. When the mist rises later in the day, what holds is what your hand is already on. So the question is not whether you study; the question is how continuously. Group 2's failure was failure of continuity after success. They tasted, then dropped the rod. The lesson is not inference but Lehi's plain words: the period after a spiritual success is the period the rod must be held tightest, because that is when the building looks at you most directly.

The second is that what the building does to your eye matters more than what it says to your ear. The voices that destabilize you are not the loud ones; they are the ones the eye keeps drifting back to. Bednar's gloss on Lehi's "we heeded them not" turns the question into a daily one: *heed* means *paying attention to*. Where is your attention? On what platforms, in what conversations, around what voices? The rod is what the ear hearkens to ([1 Nephi 15:24](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/15?lang=eng&id=p24#p24)) and what the hand holds; the building is what the eye drifts toward when the rod is loose. Tighten the grip and the eye follows. The discipline is not "do not see the building." The discipline is keep the rod gripped, keep the word in the ear, keep the eye on Christ ahead, and the building loses its purchase.

The third is that the chain is Christ. The rod is the word, the word is Christ, the anchor is hope set on Christ within the veil that is His flesh, the destination is Christ at the right hand of God. You are not maintaining the chain by your own strength. You are gripping a line whose every fiber is Him, set in a place by Him, leading to a place that is Him. Your job is to keep your hand on the line. His job is to be the line, the anchor, and the place. And His job He has done. The forerunner has entered.

So when you teach this Sunday, or the next, or in mid-week as you work, the offering is not an idea about Christ. It is the line itself, held out for someone else to grip. You teach by holding. The students see whose hand is on the rod and they reach for the same rod. That is how the chain extends through a class — one grip making the next grip possible.

The four groups are not types of people; they are postures available to any soul on any day. Most days you will be in the third group, holding continually. Some days you will catch yourself in the second posture, having tasted and let your eyes drift, and the work then is to grip again. Sometimes you will see that you have wandered off the path entirely and need to come back. The vision is diagnostic, not deterministic. The rod is reachable wherever you stand on the path, because it extends along its full length. Wherever your hand falls when you reach for it, the iron is there.

## Part 2 — Modern Prophets and the Triad

Part 1 worked from scripture and one apostolic voice. The binding question is the same: is the iron rod the chain that tethers the soul to the anchor of hope, drawing it from here through the veil into the celestial kingdom, and what do the four groups teach about how souls relate to that chain? Part 2 asks the modern prophetic tradition the same question and listens for what it adds.

What it adds is unexpected. The framing is confirmed emphatically, but two structural insights emerged that Part 1 did not have, and a third quietly corrected a reading Part 1 had drifted toward.

### VIII. The Triad, Each in Christ

Neal A. Maxwell preached "Brightness of Hope" in October 1994 and named in one paragraph what Part 1 took eight sections to assemble:

> "Unsurprisingly the triad of faith, hope, and charity, which brings us to Christ, has strong and converging linkage: faith is in the Lord Jesus Christ, hope is in His atonement, and charity is the 'pure love of Christ'! Each of these attributes qualifies us for the celestial kingdom. Each, first of all, requires us to be meek and lowly." — Maxwell, [Brightness of Hope](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1994/10/brightness-of-hope?lang=eng)

Three Christ-objects in one sentence. Faith *in* Christ. Hope *in* His atonement. Charity *as* His pure love. Maxwell does not say the triad has Christ as its destination; he says each member of the triad already takes Christ as its object. The triad is not three separate ladders to one room. It is three names for one orientation — the soul's whole being aimed at one Person. And Maxwell tells us directly that this aim is what "qualifies us for the celestial kingdom." The binding question's second half, "into the celestial kingdom of God," is answered by an apostle in one clause.

Maxwell adds a spatial image worth holding: "In the geometry of restored theology, hope has a greater circumference than faith. If faith increases, the perimeter of hope stretches correspondingly." Hope is wider than faith. Faith is the substance hope encloses ([Hebrews 11:1](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/11?lang=eng&id=p1#p1)). The chain image survives this expansion: hope is the perimeter of what the soul reaches toward, faith is what fills that reach with substance.

President Henry B. Eyring took the triad back to its primary text in October 2022, reading [Moroni 7:40-43](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/7?lang=eng&id=p40-p43#p40) at length:

> "How is it that ye can attain unto faith, save ye shall have hope? … Wherefore, if a man have faith he must needs have hope; for without faith there cannot be any hope. … He cannot have faith and hope, save he shall be meek, and lowly of heart." — Eyring, [Legacy of Encouragement](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2022/10/51eyring?lang=eng), quoting Moroni 7

Mormon makes both moves in adjacent verses. Hope precedes faith. Faith precedes hope. Eyring does not solve the reciprocity; he preserves it. So does Maxwell: "Faith and hope are constantly interactive and may not always be precisely distinguished or sequenced." The two virtues feed each other. You cannot have one without the other, and the order in which they grow is not the order in which they are listed. This matters for the chain image because it means hope is not a thing faith arrives at after long progress; hope is something the hand reaches with from the first grip. The reaching and the gripping happen together.

Eyring's other insight is the third member's gating condition: meekness. "He cannot have faith and hope, save he shall be meek, and lowly of heart." A proud hand cannot hold the rod long. Group 3 in Lehi's vision did something Group 2 did not — they "fell down and partook" ([1 Nephi 8:30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p30#p30)). Humility at the tree, not just arrival at it. Mormon's diagnosis names the posture Lehi's vision shows.

### IX. The Anchor, by Apostolic Title

President James E. Faust preached "Hope, an Anchor of the Soul" in October 1999. The title is the thesis. Faust quotes [Ether 12:4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/ether/12?lang=eng&id=p4#p4) and [2 Nephi 31:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/31?lang=eng&id=p20#p20) in adjacent paragraphs — the exact two-text combination Part 1 took §II to assemble.

Where Part 1 worked carefully through the Hebrews-versus-Ether grammar tension, Faust does not. He simply says:

> "Hope is the anchor of our souls. I know of no one who is not in need of hope—young or old, strong or weak, rich or poor." — Faust, [Hope, an Anchor of the Soul](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1999/10/hope-an-anchor-of-the-soul?lang=eng)

At the pulpit level, the texts are one. Paul's grammar and Moroni's grammar both point at the same hope, anchored to the same Christ.

Faust then names what Part 1 inferred — that the anchor's gripping point is Christ Himself:

> "His hope became firmly anchored to an eternal Rock, even our Redeemer, Jesus Christ." — Faust, ibid.

This sentence collapses two scripture-images Part 1 treated as parallel into one structural claim: the anchor of [Hebrews 6:19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/6?lang=eng&id=p19#p19) and the Rock of [Helaman 5:12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/5?lang=eng&id=p12#p12) are the same gripping point. The anchor is gripped on a Rock; the Rock is Christ. The chain is iron and the iron's far end is fastened to the Rock that is the Redeemer.

President Dieter F. Uchtdorf added something Part 1 did not have. In "The Infinite Power of Hope" (October 2008), Uchtdorf names hope as both anchor *and* foundation:

> "The hope of salvation is like a protective helmet; it is the foundation of our faith and an anchor to our souls." — Uchtdorf, [The Infinite Power of Hope](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2008/10/the-infinite-power-of-hope?lang=eng)

Hope is at both ends of the chain. It is the foundation faith stands on; it is the anchor the soul is held by. The line connects two grips, one on this side and one on the far side, and both grips are hope's. This deepens Part 1's geometry rather than replacing it.

### X. The Two Hopes

The genuinely new structural insight from the modern corpus is Uchtdorf's distinction between the two prepositions hope takes:

> "The things we hope *for* are often future events. … The things we hope *in* sustain us during our daily walk. … The things we hope *for* lead us to faith, while the things we hope *in* lead us to charity." — Uchtdorf, ibid.

Hope *for* is future-oriented. The eternal life. The mansions. A place at the right hand of God. The celestial kingdom. These are what hope's eye sees on the far side of the veil, what the chain is pulling the soul toward.

Hope *in* is present-oriented. Christ. The atonement. The goodness of God. The Holy Spirit's witness. These are who the chain is fastened to, and what the soul holds on to *now*.

The chain image gains precision from this. Hoping *in* Christ is the present-tense grip on the line. Hoping *for* the celestial kingdom is the future-tense grip on what is at the far end. Both grips are needed. A soul that hopes *for* the kingdom but does not hope *in* Christ is gripping the destination without gripping the line, and a destination cannot be reached without a line. A soul that hopes *in* Christ but never hopes *for* anything beyond this life has the line in hand but no orientation pulling it forward. The two prepositions are the two ends of the chain.

This also clarifies what Group 2's failure was. They hoped *in* Christ enough to grip the rod, walk through the mist, and taste the fruit. But after the fruit, what they hoped *for* shifted. They cast their eyes toward the building ([1 Nephi 8:25, 28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)). The destination they wanted changed. The chain went slack because hope's far-end grip, the *for*, had let go.

### XI. The Rod Runs Unfailingly

Elder Jeffrey R. Holland, preaching "Safety for the Soul" in October 2009, gave the rod its spatial description with one phrase Part 1 had inferred but not stated apostolically:

> "The record says that the protected 'did press their way forward, continually \[and, I might add, tenaciously] holding fast' to a rod of iron that runs unfailingly along the course of the true path. However dark the night *or* the day, the rod marks the way of that solitary, redeeming trail." — Holland, [Safety for the Soul](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2009/10/safety-for-the-soul?lang=eng)

*Runs unfailingly along the course of the true path.* The rod is not a marker placed at the start. It is continuous from the path's beginning to the tree. This is the apostolic statement of the chain-architecture Part 1 §III argued from the text: a tether is continuous from anchor to swimmer, and the rod is continuous from path to tree.

Holland made an even more important point in the same paragraph — one that quietly corrects a reading Part 1 had drifted toward:

> "It is imperative to note that this mist of darkness descends on *all* the travelers — the faithful and the determined ones (the elect, we might even say) as well as the weaker and ungrounded ones." — Holland, ibid.

The mist falls on Group 3 too. The successful travelers do not walk in clear weather. They walk in the same mist Group 1 got lost in and Group 2 was tempted by. The difference is not the absence of darkness. The difference is the continuous grip. Part 1 read the mist as the threat that separated the failing from the faithful. Holland reads it as the environmental condition of *all* mortal travel. The faithful are not exempt from the mist. They are recognizable by what their hand is on while the mist is around them.

This pastoral correction matters. A Saint walking through a season of obscurity should not read the mist as evidence of falling. The mist is the climate. The grip is the discipline. Group 3 was not above the weather; Group 3 was holding the iron through it.

### XII. The Rod is the Word, by Prophetic Statement

President Ezra Taft Benson devoted his April 1986 priesthood leadership address to "The Power of the Word" — and read Lehi's vision as a prophecy of the latter days:

> "The prophet Lehi also saw our day in his great visionary dream of the tree of life. … Not all of those Lehi saw perishing were of the world. Some had come to the tree and partaken of the fruit. In other words, some members of the Church today are among those souls Lehi saw which were lost." — Benson, [The Power of the Word](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/manual/teachings-of-presidents-of-the-church-ezra-taft-benson/chapter-8-the-power-of-the-word?lang=eng)

A prophet identified Group 2 with members of the Church in our time. The fruit-eaters who fell are not first-century theoretical figures. They are Saints today who have tasted, who have known the Spirit, and who have let go.

Benson then names the rod's two functions in one sentence:

> "Not only will the word of God lead us to the fruit which is desirable above all others, but in the word of God and through it we can find the power to resist temptation, the power to thwart the work of Satan and his emissaries." — Benson, ibid.

The rod *leads* — and the rod *fortifies*. The line conducts and the iron holds. Same image, two functions. Part 1 distinguished these as the rod-as-line (that leads) and the rod-as-iron (that holds). Benson preaches them as one rod doing both.

And Benson's closing imperative is the discipline this study converges on:

> "I urge you to recommit yourselves to a study of the scriptures. Immerse yourselves in them daily…" — Benson, ibid.

The same instruction, in different words, runs through the modern corpus. Bednar in 2007 (preserved in Hamilton's October 2013 talk): the third group "*consistently* read *and* studied *and* searched the words of Christ." Holland in 2009: "Whoso treasureth up my word, shall not be deceived." Pearson in April 2015 used the phrase "every day, every day, every day" twice in one talk. Across decades, four voices, the apostolic call is structurally the same. The rod is held by daily discipline.

This converges with Bednar's April 2022 distinction Part 1 leaned on: cling versus continually hold fast. To cling is what the hand does in fear. To continually hold fast is what the hand does in covenant — and covenant in this register is not a moment but a habit. Daily, every day, every day.

### XIII. The Building, Updated

Elder Holland in April 2022, preaching "Fear Not: Believe Only!", named why the rod was given in the first place:

> "It is precisely *because* there would be dark days and difficult issues that God promised He would, out of a cloud by day and a pillar of fire by night, guide prophets, give an iron rod, open a narrow gate leading to a strait path, and above all grant us the power to finish the course." — Holland, [Fear Not: Believe Only!](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2022/04/23holland?lang=eng)

The footnote on this sentence cites both [1 Nephi 8:24](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p24#p24) (Group 2, who clung) and [1 Nephi 8:30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p30#p30) (Group 3, who held continually). Holland reads both groups as gripping the rod. The difference is duration. This is the apostolic warrant for the cling-vs-continually distinction Part 1 worked from Bednar 2022.

The building, in the modern era, has grown louder and more sophisticated. Pearson named it explicitly in April 2015 with the era's vocabulary: "Searching #spaciousbuilding for knowledge will not lead you to truth. It's not posted there." The hashtag is a joke that points at something serious. The building today is the platform that holds attention. Heeding the building means scrolling toward it, lingering on its voices, staying in its arguments. Bednar's 2022 gloss on "heed" (paying attention to) lands harder when the building has become a feed.

### XIV. The Convergence Lehi Saw

Read together, the modern apostolic voices preach one composite picture. The rod is the word of God ([1 Nephi 11:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p25#p25); Bednar, Holland, Benson). The word is also Christ ([John 1:1, 14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/1?lang=eng&id=p1#p1); Bednar 2022). Christ is the way ([John 14:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/14?lang=eng&id=p6#p6); Pearson 2015), the Rock the anchor is fastened to (Faust 1999, [Helaman 5:12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/5?lang=eng&id=p12#p12)), the forerunner who has entered the veil ([Hebrews 6:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/6?lang=eng&id=p20#p20)), the love of God the tree represents (Pearson 2015, [1 Nephi 11:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)), the destination at God's right hand ([Ether 12:4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/ether/12?lang=eng&id=p4#p4); Maxwell 1994), and the One whose pure love charity is named after (Maxwell 1994, [Moroni 7:47](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/7?lang=eng&id=p47#p47)).

Faith is *in* Him. Hope is *in* His atonement and *for* His kingdom. Charity is His pure love. The rod is His word. The anchor is gripped on Him. The path leads to Him. The fruit is His. The triad's whole geometry collapses into one Person, and the modern apostolic teaching makes the collapse explicit.

What the modern corpus adds to Part 1 is not a different framework. It is the framework's authorization, by name, from the pulpit. The chain that tethers the soul to the anchor of hope is the daily-held word of God. The line is iron. The grip is meek and continuous. The eye is fixed on Christ ahead. The destination is His celestial kingdom. The mist falls on all and the rod runs through all of it unfailingly. This is the apostolic witness across decades.

### XV. Becoming, Part 2

Michael, the modern corpus does not give Part 1 a different conclusion. It gives it a sharper one.

Three things follow from Part 2 that Part 1 either pointed at without fully naming or did not have at all.

The first is that *daily* is the word the apostles use, and you should hear it at full weight. Pearson said "every day, every day, every day." Benson said "immerse yourselves in them daily." Bednar said "consistently." Holland said "treasure up my word." This is not a weekly discipline. It is not a discipline of intensity that compensates for irregularity. It is the small repeated grip: the hand on the iron each morning, the verse before the workday, the chapter before sleep, the slow reading of the footnotes that produced this study. Hamilton's talk says it plainly. The Sunday afternoon ride in the country is not evil; it is just not the rod. The rod is what your hand is on every day. So the question to take from Part 2 is not whether you are doing scripture study. You are. The question is whether you would let one day go without it. Group 3's discipline is not heroic. It is daily.

The second is Uchtdorf's hope-*in*-versus-hope-*for* distinction, applied to your life now. The line you grip is hope *in* Christ. The destination you reach toward is hope *for* the celestial kingdom and your family there. Both grips matter. When the present feels heavy, the grip that sustains is hope *in* the One you are walking with: His goodness, His sufficiency, His Spirit answering when you study at the desk. When the present feels light, the grip that orients is hope *for* what is coming. The two prepositions guard each other. A soul that only hopes *in* without hoping *for* loses the pull of the destination; a soul that only hopes *for* without hoping *in* loses the line itself. You teach a Sunday School class with both prepositions in your mouth, *in* Christ today and *for* the kingdom tomorrow, and the students learn how to hold both because they see you holding both.

The third is Holland's correction about the mist. Part 1 implied, and you may have felt, that walking through obscurity is evidence of slipping. It is not. The mist falls on Group 3. The faithful walk in the same darkness the falling walk in. The difference is the grip, not the climate. So when work is hard, when the building is louder than usual, when the questions are real and your eye drifts more than you would like, the mist around you is not a verdict on your standing. It is the weather of mortality. What the verdict reads is what your hand is on while you walk through it. Continually holding fast is something Group 3 does *in* the mist, not *above* it.

The chain is Christ. The hand is on the iron because of His word that is also Him. The anchor is set in the room where He has entered as forerunner. The line is hope, gripped *in* Him for the kingdom He has prepared. The mist is real and the building is loud, and the rod runs unfailingly along the course of the true path because He set it there for precisely these days. Faust ended his anchor sermon by saying we are "moving to a higher level of faith and activity than there ever has been," and pleading that "each of us will be found holding up our end of the line in this great army of righteousness." Holding up your end of the line is something you do tomorrow morning at the desk before anything else, and again the morning after that, and again the morning after that. The far end is in the hands of the Redeemer.

## Part 3 — The Tree as Christ, the Destination as Love

Part 1 read the rod as the chain and the anchor's grip as Christ on the far side of the veil. Part 2 added the apostolic witness across thirty-five years that the rod is the daily-held word, that hope takes two prepositions, and that the mist falls on all travelers. Both parts named in passing what Part 3 will now hold steady: that every metaphor of grace in Lehi's vision points to one Person. The binding question for Part 3 is whether the closing move holds — is the destination of the chain Christ-as-love, and does the text, read carefully, sustain the identification of tree, fruit, fountain, anchor, rod, and way as a single Christology in image-form?

### XVI. What the Prior Tree Study Already Said

[Tree of Life and the Chain](tree-of-life-and-the-chain.md) did much of the load-bearing work. That study established that the tree is the love of God ([1 Nephi 11:22, 25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p22#p22)), that the fruit is the love of God *received*, that "God is love" ([1 John 4:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-jn/4?lang=eng&id=p8#p8)) collapses the love and the Person, and therefore that the fruit, finally, is Christ Himself.

What that study did not yet have was the structure Part 1 and Part 2 added — the chain, the anchor, the two prepositions of hope, the four groups' relation to grip. Part 3 returns to the tree carrying the chain. The question is no longer just *what is the tree?* but *where does the chain land, and is the place where it lands the same Person whose word the chain is made of?*

### XVII. The Lamb Shown Before the Tree Named

The angel's pedagogy in 1 Nephi 11 is worth slowing down for. Read the verbs in order.

> "Behold the Lamb of God, yea, even the Son of the Eternal Father! Knowest thou the meaning of the tree which thy father saw?" — [1 Nephi 11:21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p21#p21)

The angel does not define the tree. He shows the Lamb in the virgin's arms (v. 20) and immediately asks Nephi about the tree. The juxtaposition is the answer before Nephi gives the answer. Nephi has just seen what the tree means; the angel is asking him to *recognize* it. And Nephi does:

> "Yea, it is the love of God, which sheddeth itself abroad in the hearts of the children of men; wherefore, it is the most desirable above all things." — [1 Nephi 11:22](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p22#p22)

Then verse 24, the angel returns to the Lamb:

> "I looked, and I beheld the Son of God going forth among the children of men; and I saw many fall down at his feet and worship him." — [1 Nephi 11:24](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p24#p24)

The phrase *fall down at his feet* is the same phrase Lehi used of Group 3 four chapters earlier: "they came forth and fell down and partook of the fruit of the tree" ([1 Nephi 8:30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p30#p30)). Same Greek-equivalent gesture. The posture of partaking the fruit and the posture of worshiping the Lamb are one posture, told in two scenes. To partake of the fruit IS to fall down at His feet. The vision tells the reader the equation twice, once in image (Lehi, the tree) and once in person (Nephi, the Lamb), and the gesture connects them.

The footnote at 11:25e routes the tree of life to [Genesis 2:9](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/gen/2?lang=eng&id=p9#p9), [Proverbs 11:30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/prov/11?lang=eng&id=p30#p30), and [Moses 4:28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/pgp/moses/4?lang=eng&id=p28#p28). The Eden tree, the proverbial tree, the Restoration's tree, and Lehi's tree are linked by the canon's own footnote system. The tree is one motif across the standard works, and it is always the same tree — the tree that gives life, the tree whose fruit ends death. In Eden it was guarded; in Lehi's vision it was reachable by the rod; in Revelation 22 it stands in the midst of the city. Same tree, three stages of the plan: barred from the Fall, accessed by the gospel, restored in the kingdom.

Pearson named this directly in April 2015: stay by the tree. The instruction is the same across dispensations. Whatever else changes, the tree does not move, and the path to it has always run by the rod.

### XVIII. The Fruit Is the Atonement

Hold three descriptions of the fruit together.

Lehi:

> "It was most sweet, above all that I ever before tasted. Yea, and I beheld that the fruit thereof was white, to exceed all the whiteness that I had ever seen... it filled my soul with exceedingly great joy." — [1 Nephi 8:11-12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p11-p12#p11)

Alma, preaching the experiment of faith:

> "And because of your diligence and your faith and your patience with the word in nourishing it, that it may take root in you, behold, by and by ye shall pluck the fruit thereof, which is most precious, which is sweet above all that is sweet, and which is white above all that is white, yea, and pure above all that is pure; and ye shall feast upon this fruit even until ye are filled, that ye hunger not, neither shall ye thirst." — [Alma 32:42](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/32?lang=eng&id=p42#p42)

Christ to the Samaritan woman:

> "Whosoever drinketh of the water that I shall give him shall never thirst; but the water that I shall give him shall be in him a well of water springing up into everlasting life." — [John 4:14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/4?lang=eng&id=p14#p14)

Three texts, separated by centuries and oceans, share the same satisfaction. *Hunger not. Thirst not.* The fruit Lehi tasted fills the soul; the fruit Alma promises ends hunger and thirst; the water Christ gives ends thirst. The promise that the *fruit* ends hunger and thirst is the promise *Christ makes about Himself*. He is the bread; He is the water; partaking of the fruit and drinking the water and eating the bread are one act, named three ways.

Corbridge stacked the I-AM sayings in October 2008 and said the equation aloud:

> "Jesus Christ is the Way. He is Light and Life, Bread and Water, the Beginning and the End, the Resurrection and the Life, the Savior of the world, the Truth, and the Way." — [Corbridge, The Way](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2008/10/the-way?lang=eng)

So the question of what the fruit *is* answers itself once the names are stacked. The fruit is what He is. To partake is to receive Him.

The whiteness of the fruit is not just any whiteness. Lehi: "white, to exceed all the whiteness that I had ever seen." Nephi later, of the tree: "the whiteness thereof did exceed the whiteness of the driven snow" ([1 Nephi 11:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p8#p8)). Compare Christ glorified, "his head and his hairs were white like wool, as white as snow" ([Revelation 1:14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/rev/1?lang=eng&id=p14#p14)). Compare His disciples in His presence, who "were as white as the countenance and also the garments of Jesus" ([3 Nephi 19:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/19?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)). The fruit's whiteness is the whiteness of Christ. To partake is to receive what colors the disciple into Him.

Uchtdorf in October 2009 names the mechanism plainly:

> "Heavenly Father's love for His children is the core message of the plan of happiness, which plan is made active through the Atonement of Jesus Christ — the greatest expression of love the world has ever known." — [Uchtdorf, The Love of God](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2009/10/the-love-of-god?lang=eng)

The Atonement is not a transaction that purchases love; it *is* love, performed. When Nephi sees the Lamb going forth, healing the sick, being judged, being lifted up upon the cross ([1 Nephi 11:24-33](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p24-p33#p24)), he is seeing love-of-God enacted in flesh. The tree shows what love looks like when it walks among men. The fruit is what that love is, given to the receiver. The descriptors are descriptors of Atonement received: most sweet, exceeding white, hunger no more.

This is reading, not exegesis of a single verse. Nephi never writes "the fruit is the Atonement" in those words. But the angel shows Nephi the Lamb's whole ministry between verses 24 and 33 — birth, baptism, healing, judgment, crucifixion — *immediately after* Nephi names the tree as love of God. The vision answers the question of what love-of-God *is* with the entire Atonement narrative. The angel's pedagogy is consistent: show the thing, then identify it. He shows the Atonement; Nephi has already named what it is.

### XIX. The Fountain Is a Person

The fountain of living waters has a longer history than Lehi's vision. Jeremiah heard the Lord's voice in the first person:

> "For my people have committed two evils; they have forsaken me the fountain of living waters, and hewed them out cisterns, broken cisterns, that can hold no water." — [Jeremiah 2:13](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/jer/2?lang=eng&id=p13#p13)

"They have forsaken *me* the fountain of living waters." The Lord identifies *Himself*, not a place, as the fountain. The OT root of the image is therefore not metaphorical. The fountain is the Lord's first-person self-description. When Lehi sees a fountain at the head of the path and Nephi is told the fountain represents the love of God ([1 Nephi 11:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)), the reader who has heard Jeremiah hears the same Voice naming the same Person.

The two evils in Jeremiah are coupled. Forsaking the fountain is one evil; *hewing out broken cisterns that can hold no water* is the second. They go together. Wherever the soul abandons the true fountain, it digs counterfeits. This is the textual root of Lehi's contrast: the fountain at the head of the path is the love of God; the river of filthiness on the other side is hell ([1 Nephi 15:27](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/15?lang=eng&id=p27#p27), Nephi's clarification). The drowned in the river are those who left the fountain and went to broken cisterns. Jeremiah teaches the mechanism. The same wickedness Israel committed in the OT, Lehi's vision dramatizes in image: the soul that forsakes the true fountain mistakes the river of filthiness for water.

Christ at the well in John 4 brings the image forward and adds the interior turn:

> "Whosoever drinketh of the water that I shall give him shall never thirst; but the water that I shall give him shall be in him a well of water springing up into everlasting life." — [John 4:14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/4?lang=eng&id=p14#p14)

The water Christ gives becomes a well *in the receiver*. The fountain pours into the soul, and the soul becomes a fountain. At the Feast of Tabernacles Christ extends the image:

> "If any man thirst, let him come unto me, and drink. He that believeth on me, as the scripture hath said, out of his belly shall flow rivers of living water. (But this spake he of the Spirit, which they that believe on him should receive...)" — [John 7:37-39](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/7?lang=eng&id=p37-p39#p37)

The "rivers of living water" flowing out of the believer are named explicitly: the Spirit. So the full mechanism is on the table. Christ is the fountain (Jeremiah's first-person identification). Christ gives the water (John 4). The water *is* the Spirit (John 7). The Spirit becomes a spring within the believer (John 4:14). The spring flows outward (John 7:38). The believer becomes a conduit of the same love that was given to him.

This matches Paul exactly:

> "Hope maketh not ashamed; because the love of God is shed abroad in our hearts by the Holy Ghost which is given unto us." — [Romans 5:5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/rom/5?lang=eng&id=p5#p5)

And Mormon, in his epistle to Moroni on infant baptism, writes the chain in full:

> "The remission of sins bringeth meekness, and lowliness of heart; and because of meekness and lowliness of heart cometh the visitation of the Holy Ghost, which Comforter filleth with hope and perfect love, which love endureth by diligence unto prayer, until the end shall come, when all the saints shall dwell with God." — [Moroni 8:26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/8?lang=eng&id=p26#p26)

The Comforter *filleth* with hope and perfect love. And the footnote 26d on "filleth" routes the reader directly to 1 Nephi 11:22-25 — to the tree of life passage. The Church's footnote editors saw the connection at the canonical level: the Comforter filling the soul with hope and love IS the tree's fruit being received. Mormon's grammar makes the equation in plain prose; the footnote system seals it.

This adds something Part 1 did not name. The chain operates by a *medium*. The rod is the word; the line is hope; the anchor is fastened on Christ — and the love that flows through the line is shed in the heart by the Holy Ghost. The Spirit is the river running inside the chain. Without the Spirit there is no pour, only metal. With the Spirit the iron becomes the conduit of life.

### XX. The Word, the Way, the Chain

Bednar's sermon in April 2022, cited in Part 1, named what John had already named:

> "Therefore, one of the names of Jesus Christ is 'The Word.' ... Thus, the teachings of the Savior, as recorded in the holy scriptures, also are 'the word.'" — [Bednar, But We Heeded Them Not](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2022/04/14bednar?lang=eng)

John's prologue is the warrant: "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God... And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us" ([John 1:1, 14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/1?lang=eng&id=p1#p1)). The rod of iron is the word of God ([1 Nephi 11:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)). Christ is the Word made flesh. Therefore the rod, in the deepest reading the canon allows, is Christ in His name as the Word.

A linguistic caveat earns its place. John's *logos* is the Greek term for divine reason or expression; the OT *davar* is the spoken message of the Lord; the English "word" carries both senses indistinctly. The identification of the rod and Christ via "the word" is theologically defensible at the Restoration level (Bednar names it directly) and at the Johannine level (the Word made flesh) but is doing real translation work across three languages. The English collapse is the Restoration's choice; the doctrine the collapse points at is sustained by multiple voices. The collapse is honest; the linguistic seam is real.

Christ is also the way. When Thomas asks the question Lehi's mist-walkers might ask — "we know not whither thou goest; and how can we know the way?" — Christ answers:

> "I am the way, the truth, and the life: no man cometh unto the Father, but by me." — [John 14:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/14?lang=eng&id=p6#p6)

The path Lehi saw running by the rod ([1 Nephi 8:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p20#p20)) is the path Christ identifies with Himself. The rod runs along the path; the path is Christ; the rod is the word; the Word is Christ. Two metaphors converge on the same Person. The chain the soul holds and the road the soul walks are the same Christ in two aspects — His word as the line that conducts, His person as the way that is conducted along.

And the destination of the way:

> "In my Father's house are many mansions: if it were not so, I would have told you. I go to prepare a place for you. And if I go and prepare a place for you, I will come again, and receive you unto myself; that where I am, there ye may be also." — [John 14:2-3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/14?lang=eng&id=p2-p3#p2)

The destination is not just a house. It is being-with-Him: "*where I am*, there ye may be also." The mansions of John 14 are the room where the anchor is set ([Hebrews 6:19-20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/6?lang=eng&id=p19-p20#p19)) — the place Christ has entered first as forerunner. Ether 12:32 names the same room: "thou hast prepared a house for man, yea, even among the mansions of thy Father, in which man might have a more excellent hope" ([Ether 12:32](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/ether/12?lang=eng&id=p32#p32)). One room, named three ways: holy of holies, Father's house, the place at God's right hand. The forerunner has gone in. The chain is in His hand. The line pulls toward Him.

There is also a bidirectional motion the prior parts did not have. John 14:23:

> "If a man love me, he will keep my words: and my Father will love him, and we will come unto him, and make our abode with him." — [John 14:23](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/14?lang=eng&id=p23#p23)

The Father and the Son come out to dwell with the lover-of-Christ. The chain pulls in both directions. The soul reaches toward Christ via the rod; Christ reaches toward the soul via the same line. Uchtdorf in October 2009 cites D&C 88:63 for this same motion: "draw near to Him so that He can draw near to us." The grip on the iron initiates the approach; the approach is met halfway by the One the iron is gripped on. The chain is not a one-way conduit. It is two grips, both held.

### XXI. One Person, in Every Aspect of the Vision

Stack the identifications the text has earned.

The rod is the word of God ([1 Nephi 11:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)).
Christ is the Word made flesh ([John 1:14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/1?lang=eng&id=p14#p14); Bednar 2022).
Therefore the rod is Christ in His name.

The path runs by the rod ([1 Nephi 8:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p20#p20)).
Christ is the way ([John 14:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/14?lang=eng&id=p6#p6); Corbridge 2008).
Therefore the path is Christ.

The hope is the anchor, the anchor enters within the veil ([Hebrews 6:19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/6?lang=eng&id=p19#p19)).
The veil is His flesh ([Hebrews 10:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/10?lang=eng&id=p20#p20); established in [Way, Truth, Life](way-truth-life.md)).
The Rock the anchor is fastened to is Christ ([Helaman 5:12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/5?lang=eng&id=p12#p12); Faust 1999, cited in Part 2).
Therefore the anchor and its gripping point are Christ.

The fountain is the love of God ([1 Nephi 11:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)).
The Lord is the fountain of living waters ([Jeremiah 2:13](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/jer/2?lang=eng&id=p13#p13), first person).
Christ is the fountain of all righteousness ([Ether 12:28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/ether/12?lang=eng&id=p28#p28)).
Christ gives the water that becomes a well within the believer ([John 4:14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/4?lang=eng&id=p14#p14)).
Therefore the fountain is Christ.

The tree is the love of God ([1 Nephi 11:22](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p22#p22)).
"God is love" ([1 John 4:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-jn/4?lang=eng&id=p8#p8)).
The Atonement is the greatest expression of love (Uchtdorf 2009).
The tree was shown in the angel's pedagogy immediately after the Lamb in the virgin's arms ([1 Nephi 11:20-22](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p20-p22#p20)).
Therefore the tree is Christ.

The fruit is what the tree gives — sweet, white, fills, ends hunger and thirst ([1 Nephi 8:11-12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p11-p12#p11); [Alma 32:42](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/32?lang=eng&id=p42#p42)).
Christ is the bread that ends hunger, the water that ends thirst ([John 6:35](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/6?lang=eng&id=p35#p35); [John 4:14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/4?lang=eng&id=p14#p14)).
The whiteness of the fruit is the whiteness of Christ glorified ([Revelation 1:14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/rev/1?lang=eng&id=p14#p14); [3 Nephi 19:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/19?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)).
Therefore the fruit is Christ.

Every metaphor of grace in the vision is one Person in different aspects. The word that leads. The line that tethers. The anchor that holds. The way that is walked. The tree that gives. The fountain that flows. The fruit that fills. Seven aspects, one Person.

A careful framing earns its place. The mist is not Christ. The building is not Christ. The river of filthiness is not Christ. The metaphors of opposition in Lehi's vision are the contrast against which His Person is shown. The vision is not a syncretism in which everything is Christ; the vision is a Christology *and* an anti-Christology, with the contrast set making the identification of grace's images sharper, not softer. What the vision teaches is not that the world is Christ; what the vision teaches is that grace, in every form it appears in the dream, is the same Person showing Himself in different aspects.

Corbridge's October 2008 talk stacks the I-AM names from John and concludes:

> "He is the Way, the only Way." — [Corbridge, The Way](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2008/10/the-way?lang=eng)

The arc closes here. Part 1 asked whether the iron rod is the chain that tethers the soul to the anchor of hope. The answer was yes — the word is the line, hope is the device, Christ is the gripping point. Part 2 added that the modern apostolic tradition teaches this with daily discipline, with the two prepositions of hope, with the mist falling on all travelers. Part 3 holds the last identification: the line, the device, the gripping point, the route, the destination, the love that fills the soul there, the fruit eaten, the water drunk — all of them are one Person. The chain is Christ holding the soul to Himself, by means of Himself, toward Himself, into His own embrace.

### XXII. Becoming, Part 3

Michael, this closes a three-part arc. Part 1 was the chain. Part 2 was the daily grip. Part 3 is the Person. The first two are practical; the third is the *why* the first two matter, and the *why* you keep doing them on the mornings when the rod is heavy in your hand and the building is loud and the mist has not lifted.

Three things follow from Part 3 that the prior parts did not have at full weight.

The first is that the scripture study you do every morning is not a discipline that *leads* to Christ. It is contact with Him. The word your hand is on is His own word, and His name is the Word. When you read [1 Nephi 11:22](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p22#p22) tomorrow morning, you are not preparing for an encounter with Christ that happens later; the reading is the encounter, because the reading is His word, and His word is Him. This is what Uchtdorf meant in October 2009 when he wrote that "the closer we approach Him, the more profoundly we experience love." The approach and the experience are not sequential. They are the same motion. Your desk in the morning is the place where you draw near, and the place where He draws near in return. The chain pulls both ways.

The second is that the love you teach is love you have *received*, not love you have generated. The fountain is Christ; the well is in you only because Christ has filled it; the rivers of living water that flow out of you when you teach a Sunday School class are the same waters He gives. You are not the source. You are a conduit through which the source flows. When you stand to teach and feel insufficient, the insufficiency is correct. You are not the source. But it is not disqualifying, because the source is gripped on the chain you are also holding. Teach from what has been shed in you. And when the teaching seems to land in someone's heart, the credit is not yours, and the failure, when the teaching does not land, is not entirely yours either. You hand out fruit from a tree you did not grow. The work is to keep partaking and to keep offering. The growing is His.

The third is that the destination is Him, not a room. John 14:3 — "where I am, there ye may be also." Heaven is not chiefly a place; heaven is being-with-Him in the place He has prepared. Hope *for* the celestial kingdom (Uchtdorf 2008, Part 2) is fundamentally hope for being-with-Him. This corrects a subtle drift it is easy to fall into when life is hard — the drift of wanting heaven for relief, for reunion, for the kingdom's beauty. Those are real and they are not wrong. But the deeper hope is the Person. When you find yourself in middle age tired of mortal weight and reaching toward the celestial, the question to ask is whether what you are reaching for is the room or the Host. If the Host, you are reaching for what the chain was always pulling you toward. If the room, you may need to follow the chain a little further. The chain ends at a Person, not at an address.

You teach Sunday School. You stand in front of a class with a portion of the word — which is a portion of Him — and offer it. The students reach for what your hand is on. Your hand is on the rod, which is His word, which is His name, which is Him. They reach for Him through you because you are holding Him for them to see. That is what teaching is when it is honest. The teacher is a chain extending into the class. The teacher's grip on the rod is the class's first glimpse of the rod. So the question that closes this arc, for you, is the question that has run through all three parts: is your grip continuous? Daily? On the mornings when nothing has changed about the work and everything is the same as yesterday, are you still gripping?

The answer is in your hand tomorrow morning. The chain is Christ. The grip is the discipline. The destination is being-with-Him. Hold the iron. The Forerunner has entered the room, and He is pulling.
