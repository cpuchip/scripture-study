# D&C 31 — govern your house in meekness

**Setting:** **Fayette, New York, September 1830**, right after the conference — to **Thomas B. Marsh**, baptized earlier that month and just ordained an elder. Marsh was an earnest early convert who would become the first President of the Quorum of the Twelve. This is his call to the ministry — warm, family-conscious, and (in hindsight) quietly prophetic about exactly where his danger lay.

**Binding idea:** A tender mission call wrapped around a single piece of household counsel: "Govern your house in meekness, and be steadfast" (31:9). Marsh is promised his family's eventual faith and his own fruitful labor — *and* told the one discipline he must keep: rule his home not by force but by meekness. (The presiding principle of D&C 121 — persuasion, gentleness, meekness — named in the *domestic* sphere, eight years before Liberty Jail spelled it out.)

**Read:** "Thomas, my son, blessed are you because of your faith in my work" (31:1). "You have had many afflictions because of your family; nevertheless, I will bless you and your family, yea, your little ones; and the day cometh that they will believe and know the truth and be one with you in my church" (31:2). "The hour of your mission is come; and your tongue shall be loosed" (31:3); "thrust in your sickle with all your soul, and your sins are forgiven you… your family shall live" (31:5). "Go from them only for a little time… and I will prepare a place for them" (31:6). "Be patient in afflictions, revile not against those that revile. **Govern your house in meekness, and be steadfast**" (31:9). "You shall be a physician unto the church, but not unto the world" (31:10). "Pray always, lest you enter into temptation and lose your reward" (31:12). "Be faithful unto the end, and lo, I am with you" (31:13).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "Govern your house in meekness" (31:9) — the presiding covenant in the home.* Tucked into a mission call is the whole doctrine of D&C 121:41 ("no power… maintained… only by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness") applied to a man's *family*. To "govern" a house in *meekness* is to preside without compulsion at the most intimate scale — the same principle the workspace's presiding covenant rests on, here in its first and most personal form. (Presiding isn't only the prophet over the Church or the agent over subagents; its root case is a man over his own household, and the rule is identical: *meekness, not force.* Govern by gentleness or you forfeit the governing — at home as everywhere.) And it pairs with "revile not against those that revile" (31:9) and "physician unto the church" (31:10): Marsh's whole posture is to be *gentle and healing*, not combative — meek at home, non-retaliatory abroad, a physician to the body.
- *The counsel was exactly Marsh's fault line (31:9) — noted with care.* Thomas B. Marsh rose to lead the Twelve and then, in 1838, **apostatized** — and the rupture began, famously, in a *household dispute* (a quarrel over milk strippings between his wife and a neighbor that he escalated rather than let go). He returned, humbled and broken, in 1857. The single counsel singled out for him in his very first revelation — *govern your house in meekness* — names the precise weakness that would later undo him. The Lord saw the fault line at the call (the §23:1 pattern: Oliver warned of pride, Marsh told to govern in meekness; the warning given early, the heeding left to agency). I record this as documented history and a sobering datum — **bin-4-adjacent, Michael's to weigh** — not as a verdict on Marsh, who repented and was restored. The point isn't his failure; it's that the Lord's earliest, gentlest word to a man can be the very thing his soul most needs to guard. (And the grace in it: the same Marsh who fell over a home quarrel was promised, at the start, that his family would yet "believe and know the truth and be one with you," 31:2 — a promise that outlasts his fall.)
- *"Nevertheless I will bless you and your family" (31:2) — the family promise.* Marsh had "many afflictions because of [his] family" (likely an unbelieving or strained household). The Lord doesn't minimize it — *nevertheless* (the walk's hinge again), the day will come when "they will believe… and be one with you." A promise to the convert whose family doesn't yet share the faith: the present strain is not the end of the story. (Calibrated comfort — the affliction named honestly, the future held open. Not "your family is fine" but "your family's belief is coming.")
- *"Lo, I am with you" (31:13) — the antidote thread continues.* The section closes on the same words that closed §30 and §24: *I am with you.* Marsh, sent from his family into a hostile world, gets the same assurance as fearful David and persecuted Joseph — the cure for affliction and fear is *Presence*. (The Lord keeps signing these early mission calls with the one promise that answers every fear: not safety, not success, but *with-ness.*)
