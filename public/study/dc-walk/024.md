# D&C 24 — strength within your calling

**Setting:** **Harmony, Pennsylvania, July 1830** — fewer than four months after organization, and persecution had already turned intense. Joseph and Oliver had to seek "safety in partial seclusion." This is the first of three revelations (24-26) given to strengthen and instruct them under that pressure. It is the Lord steadying His prophet right after the founding euphoria collapsed into opposition.

**Binding idea:** "In temporal labors thou shalt not have strength, for this is not thy calling. Attend to thy calling and thou shalt have wherewith to magnify thine office" (24:9). Strength is *calling-bounded* — the Lord supplies power *within* your stewardship, not for the things outside it. And the afflictions are promised, not removed: "be patient in afflictions, for thou shalt have many; but endure them, for, lo, I am with thee" (24:8).

**Read:** "I have lifted thee up out of thine afflictions… delivered from all thine enemies, and… from the powers of Satan and from darkness! Nevertheless, thou art not excusable in thy transgressions; nevertheless, go thy way and sin no more" (24:1-2). "Magnify thine office" (24:3); the Colesville/Fayette/Manchester churches "shall support thee" (24:3). "It shall be given thee in the very moment what thou shalt speak and write" (24:6). To Oliver: "he shall not suppose that he can say enough in my cause… In me he shall have glory, and not of himself, whether in weakness or in strength" (24:10-11). **Thoughts:**
- *★ Strength is calling-bounded (24:9).* This is the section's most usable doctrine and a real corrective. Joseph is a farmer *and* a prophet, and the temptation under poverty and persecution is to grind harder at the farm to survive. The Lord says: you won't have strength for that — "this is not thy calling." The strength is reserved for the *office*: "attend to thy calling and thou shalt have wherewith to magnify thine office." This is the §10:4 pace-to-your-strength principle from the other side — there, *don't run faster than your strength*; here, *your strength is allotted to your stewardship*, so don't spend it elsewhere expecting it to hold. (For the project's `honor_scope` and the presiding covenant: the grace to do the work comes *with the work assigned*, not for self-chosen labors outside it. Stretching past your calling isn't just overreach — it's working without the supply of strength that only the calling carries.)
- *Afflictions promised, not removed — and "I am with thee" is the whole comfort (24:8).* The Lord does not tell the persecuted prophet the trouble will stop. "Thou shalt have many." The promise is not *fewer afflictions* but *presence in them*: "lo, I am with thee, even unto the end of thy days" (Matt 28:20). This is the honest shape of the comfort the D&C offers under pressure — companionship, not exemption. (It will deepen all the way to Liberty Jail, §121-122: "all these things shall give thee experience… the Son of Man hath descended below them all." The seed is here: endure, *I am with thee*.)
- *The double "nevertheless" (24:1-2).* "I have… delivered thee from all thine enemies… *Nevertheless*, thou art not excusable in thy transgressions; *nevertheless*, go thy way and sin no more." Two nevertheless-hinges back to back: deliverance does not excuse sin, *and* sin does not cancel the call ("go thy way and sin no more"). The Lord holds Joseph fully accountable *and* fully restored in the same breath — the §3 / §121:43 reprove-then-love pattern compressed into two clauses. (The walk's hinge-word doing double duty: justice *and* mercy each get a "nevertheless.")
- *The hard missionary law — named honestly (24:13-19).* This section carries the severe early-Restoration register: leave a *cursing* by casting off the dust of your feet (24:15); those who lay hands on you by violence "ye shall command to be smitten" (24:16). These are jarring beside the gentleness elsewhere, and they reflect a Church under real physical threat in 1830, drawing on the New Testament missionary commission (Matt 10, the dust-of-feet, no purse-no-scrip). I name them rather than smoothing them: the early elders were given hard, defensive instructions for a hostile world, and the "cursing" is framed as a *testimony* (a witness left against rejection), not personal vengeance — the smiting is "in mine own due time," reserved to God (24:16). (The presiding-covenant lens: even the cursing is God's act, in His time — the elders *witness*, God *judges*; the force is not theirs to wield freely.)
