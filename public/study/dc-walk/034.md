# D&C 34 — believed, and called

**Setting:** **Fayette, New York, November 4, 1830** — to **Orson Pratt**, *nineteen years old*, baptized just six weeks earlier when he first heard his older brother Parley (§32) preach the restored gospel. Given in the Peter Whitmer Sr. home. A young convert's call to preach, and one of the warmest short ones.

**Binding idea:** A two-step blessing: "blessed are you because you have believed; and more blessed are you because you are called of me to preach my gospel" (34:4-5). Belief is blessed; being *called to give it away* is *more* blessed. And the ground of it is John 3:16 spoken in the first person: Christ "so loved the world that he gave his own life, that as many as would believe might become the sons of God. Wherefore you are my son" (34:3).

**Read:** "My son Orson… I, the Lord God… even Jesus Christ your Redeemer; the light and the life of the world, a light which shineth in darkness and the darkness comprehendeth it not" (34:1-2). "Who so loved the world that he gave his own life, that as many as would believe might become the sons of God. Wherefore you are my son" (34:3). "Blessed are you because you have believed; and more blessed are you because you are called of me to preach my gospel" (34:4-5) — "to lift up your voice as with the sound of a trump… and cry repentance unto a crooked and perverse generation, preparing the way of the Lord for his second coming" (34:6). The signs precede the great day (34:7-9). "Prophesy, and it shall be given by the power of the Holy Ghost" (34:10). "If you are faithful, behold, I am with you until I come—and verily, verily, I say unto you, I come quickly" (34:11-12).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "Blessed… because you have believed; more blessed… because you are called to preach" (34:4-5).* The blessing ladder is the doctrine: believing is good; *giving the gospel away* is better. Orson, six weeks a member at nineteen, is told his greater blessing is not his own salvation but his *commission to others* — the §15-16 / §18 worth-of-souls logic personalized: the most-blessed thing is bringing souls. (And it answers a quiet question — is the convert's first joy enough? No: the joy is meant to *overflow* into declaring it. To be blessed by belief and stop there is to keep the lesser blessing.) Note the gentle parallel to John 20:29 (the footnote): "blessed are they that have not seen, and yet have believed" — Orson, who believed on his brother's word, is in that blessed company, and then raised higher by the call.
- *John 3:16 in the first person (34:3) — "Wherefore you are my son."* The most-quoted verse in Christendom, spoken by Christ *Himself* and applied *directly* to one young man: "who so loved the world that he gave his own life, that as many as would believe might become the sons of God. **Wherefore you are my son.**" The universal becomes the particular — "the world" God so loved narrows to "you, Orson, are my son." (The §25:16 move again: the universal word made personal. And the becoming-sons doctrine, §11:30 / §25:1 — believing makes you a son/daughter of God, which §29's matter-spectrum frames as real transformation, not metaphor.)
- *"A light which shineth in darkness" (34:2) — the Oliver-cluster signature, still running.* The same self-identification from §6:21, §10:58, §11:11 — Christ as the light the darkness "comprehendeth not" (John 1:5). Thirty-plus sections in, it's a refrain: the Lord keeps introducing Himself to each new disciple as *the light in the darkness*. (The matter-spectrum's Christ — the source-light, §14:9 "cannot be hid" — greeting each convert by the same name.)
- *"If you are faithful… I am with you until I come" (34:11) — the antidote, again, with a clock.* The with-ness thread (§24/§30/§31/§32) closes §34 too — but here paired with "I come quickly" (34:12). The presence is *until I come*, and the coming is *soon*. To a nineteen-year-old sent to a hostile generation, the promise is companionship for the whole interval, however short it proves. (Orson Pratt would serve fifty-one years, an apostle and the Church's great mathematician-theologian — the "quickly" was longer than he knew, and the "I am with you" held the whole way.)
