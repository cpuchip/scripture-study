# D&C 111 — not displeased, notwithstanding your follies

**Setting:** **Salem, Massachusetts, August 6, 1836.** The Church was "heavily in debt" from building the Kirtland Temple and the Missouri persecutions. Hearing a rumor that "a large amount of money might be available to them in Salem" — reportedly hidden treasure in a house there — Joseph, Sidney, Hyrum, and Oliver traveled from Kirtland to investigate (along with preaching). The money never materialized; they returned. The revelation is the Lord's word *into a venture that didn't pan out* — and its tone is *mercy*, not rebuke.

**Binding idea:** "I, the Lord your God, am not displeased with your coming this journey, notwithstanding your follies… I have much treasure in this city for you, for the benefit of Zion, and many people in this city, whom I will gather out" (111:1-2). The hoped-for *gold* isn't there — but the Lord is *not displeased*, and reframes the "treasure" as *people* to be gathered.

**Read:** "I, the Lord your God, am not displeased with your coming this journey, notwithstanding your follies" (111:1). "I have much treasure in this city for you, for the benefit of Zion, and many people in this city, whom I will gather out in due time for the benefit of Zion, through your instrumentality" (111:2). "Concern not yourselves about your debts, for I will give you power to pay them" (111:5). "Concern not yourselves about Zion, for I will deal mercifully with her" (111:6). "There are more treasures than one for you in this city" (111:10). "Be ye as wise as serpents and yet without sin; and I will order all things for your good, as fast as ye are able to receive them" (111:11).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "Not displeased… notwithstanding your follies" (111:1).* The first word into a misjudged venture is *grace*. Joseph and the brethren acted on a *rumor* — a treasure-hunt of questionable judgment — and the Lord's response is "I am not displeased with your coming this journey, **notwithstanding your follies**." (A remarkable tenderness: the Lord *names* it a "folly" — He does not pretend the judgment was wise — *and* He is "not displeased." The steward who acted in good faith on a bad call is *not condemned* for the call. This is the §1828 "code is cheap, git walks back" / the Dave-rule [act when intent's clear; the Lord redeems the misstep] at the scale of a *whole journey*: God does not require His servants to be *infallible in judgment*, only *faithful in intent* — and He *redeems the folly*, finding "treasure" and "more treasures than one" even in the trip that failed its purpose. For the project's stewardship: a steward will sometimes act on a misjudgment in good faith; the Lord's posture toward that is "not displeased… notwithstanding your follies" — He works the folly toward good, and the steward is freed from the paralysis of needing every call to be right.)
- *★ The treasure reframed — people, not gold (111:2,10).* The brethren came for *money*; the Lord redirects to *souls*: "I have much treasure in this city for you… and many people in this city, whom I will gather out… for the benefit of Zion" (111:2), "there are more treasures than one for you in this city" (111:10). (The §6:11 / §18:10 worth-of-souls: the real "treasure" of any place is its *people*, gathered to God — not its gold and silver. The brethren's *literal* treasure-hunt is gently *converted* into a *missionary* one: "form acquaintance with men in this city… inquire diligently concerning the more ancient inhabitants" [111:3,9]. The Lord takes the misjudged *money*-errand and reveals the *true* errand underneath it — the gathering of souls. For the project: when a venture aimed at one good [money, a feature, a result] doesn't pan out, the Lord may reveal a *deeper* treasure in it — the "more treasures than one" are usually people, not the thing first sought.)
- *"I will order all things for your good, as fast as ye are able to receive them" (111:11).* The closing providence: "be ye as wise as serpents and yet without sin [Matt 10:16]; and I will order all things for your good, **as fast as ye are able to receive them**." (Rom 8:28 with a *pacing* clause: God orders all for good — *at the pace the servant can bear* [the §78:18 "I will lead you along"; the §10:4 pace-to-strength]. The good is sure, but *timed* to capacity — "as fast as ye are able to receive them." For the discouraged-by-a-failed-venture: the good is coming, ordered by God, *paced* to what you can receive. And "wise as serpents, without sin" — even in the disappointment, be *shrewd and clean*: learn the lesson, don't sour.)
