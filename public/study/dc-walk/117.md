# D&C 117 — the drop, and the more weighty matters

**Setting:** **Far West, Missouri, July 8, 1838** — concerning the "immediate duties" of William Marks, Newel K. Whitney, and Oliver Granger, who were lingering in Kirtland over property and debts while the body of the Saints had moved to Missouri. The Lord calls them to *leave* — and rebukes the *covetousness* that was keeping them tangled in "the drop."

**Binding idea:** "Let them repent… of all their covetous desires… for **what is property unto me?** saith the Lord… Is there not room enough on the mountains of Adam-ondi-Ahman… that you should covet that which is but the drop, and neglect the **more weighty matters**?" (117:4,8). To the God who owns the earth, a parcel of Kirtland property is "but a drop" — and clinging to it while neglecting "the more weighty matters" is the error.

**Read:** "Let them repent of all their sins, and of all their covetous desires, before me… for what is property unto me? saith the Lord. For have I not the fowls of heaven, and also the fish of the sea, and the beasts of the mountains? Have I not made the earth? Do I not hold the destinies of all the armies of the nations of the earth?" (117:4-6). "Will I not make solitary places to bud and to blossom… in abundance?" (117:7). "Is there not room enough on the mountains of Adam-ondi-Ahman… that you should covet that which is but the drop, and neglect the more weighty matters?" (117:8). "Let my servant William Marks be faithful over a few things, and he shall be a ruler over many" (117:10). "Let my servant Newel K. Whitney be ashamed… of all his littleness of soul… and be a bishop unto my people… not in name but in deed" (117:11). "I remember my servant Oliver Granger… his name shall be had in sacred remembrance from generation to generation, forever and ever… when he falls he shall rise again, for his sacrifice shall be more sacred unto me than his increase" (117:12-13).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "What is property unto me?" — the Owner of all (117:4-8).* The rebuke of covetousness is grounded in *scale*: "what is property unto me? For have I not the fowls of heaven… the fish of the sea, the beasts of the mountains? Have I not made the earth?" (117:4-6). (Covetousness is *absurd before the Owner of all*: to grasp a parcel of Kirtland property, clinging to "the drop," before the God who "made the earth" and "holds the destinies of all the armies of the nations" is to misjudge the *proportion* entirely. The §104:17 "enough and to spare" given its *theological* root: God owns *everything* [§104:14 "all things are mine"], so a steward's anxious grasping at a *fragment* reveals he has forgotten *whose* it all is. And the freeing flip side: "I will make solitary places to bud and to blossom" [117:7, Isa 35:1] — the God who owns all can *provide anywhere*; the Saints leaving Kirtland's property behind are not losing security but *trusting the Owner who can bloom a desert*. For the project's stewardship: covetousness is a *scale error* — clinging to a fragment before the Owner of all; the cure is remembering *whose it is* and that He provides everywhere.)
- *★★ "The drop, and the more weighty matters" (117:8).* The proportion lesson, in one image. "That you should covet that which is but **the drop**, and neglect **the more weighty matters**?" (Matt 23:23 — Jesus to the Pharisees, who tithed mint and cumin but "omitted the weightier matters of the law, judgment, mercy, and faith"). (The danger named: getting *absorbed in the small* [the drop — a property dispute, a debt, a fragment] while *neglecting the large* [the weighty matters — the gathering, the kingdom, the soul]. The §90:5 "beware how you hold the oracles" / §84:54 treating-lightly's *inverse*: not treating the *great* lightly, but treating the *small* as great — letting "the drop" consume the attention that belongs to "the more weighty matters." For the project's foresight discipline: the perpetual temptation is to fixate on the *drop* — the immediate, the small, the tangible dispute — and lose the *weighty matters*; the steward must keep the *proportion* right, not letting a fragment crowd out the kingdom. "Is there not room enough…?" — there is *vast* room [the mountains of Adam-ondi-Ahman]; the smallness is in the *grasping*, not the supply.)
- *★ Oliver Granger — "his sacrifice more sacred than his increase" (117:12-15).* A tender honoring of an *obscure* servant. Oliver Granger — the agent Joseph left to settle his Kirtland affairs, a man history would otherwise forget — is given an eternal word: "his name shall be had in **sacred remembrance from generation to generation, forever and ever**… **when he falls he shall rise again, for his sacrifice shall be more sacred unto me than his increase**" (117:12-13). (Two of the most quietly beautiful lines in the canon. First, the *small faithful servant honored*: an unknown man doing a thankless, failing job [settling impossible debts] is promised "sacred remembrance forever" — the Lord *notices and treasures* the obscure faithful labor [the §62:3 testimony-recorded-in-heaven; the §18:10 worth of one soul]. Second, "**his sacrifice shall be more sacred than his increase**": the *trying* — even the *failing* — is worth more to God than the *succeeding*. Granger would *not* clear all the debts [the increase], but his *sacrifice* in the attempt was "more sacred." And "when he falls he shall rise again" — the falling is not the end; the faithful who fall *rise*. For Michael, and for anyone laboring at a thankless or failing task: God treasures the *sacrifice* above the *result* — "more sacred than his increase" — and the obscure faithful servant is held "in sacred remembrance forever." The outcome is not the measure; the offered labor is.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. **Walk's contribution:** **§117 names the proportion error — "that you should covet that which is but the drop, and neglect the more weighty matters?" (117:8).** To leaders lingering in Kirtland over property and debts, the Lord grounds the rebuke of covetousness in *scale*: "what is property unto me?… have I not made the earth? Do I not hold the destinies of all the armies of the nations?" (117:4-6) — before the Owner of all, grasping a parcel of property is *absurd*, "but a drop" (the §104:17 enough-and-to-spare's theological root; and the God who owns all "will make solitary places to bud and to blossom," so leaving security behind is trusting the Owner who blooms deserts). The proportion lesson (Matt 23:23): don't let the *small* — a dispute, a fragment — crowd out "the more weighty matters" (the kingdom, the soul); the perpetual temptation is to fixate on the drop and lose the weighty. And one of the canon's quiet jewels: **Oliver Granger**, the obscure agent settling Joseph's impossible Kirtland debts, is given an eternal word — "his name shall be had in **sacred remembrance from generation to generation, forever and ever**… **when he falls he shall rise again, for his sacrifice shall be more sacred unto me than his increase**" (117:12-13): the Lord treasures the obscure, thankless, even *failing* faithful labor — the *trying* is worth more than the *succeeding*, and the faithful who fall *rise*. For Michael, and anyone at a thankless or failing task: don't covet the drop and neglect the weighty — and know that God holds your *sacrifice* more sacred than your *increase*; the offered labor is the measure, not the outcome.
