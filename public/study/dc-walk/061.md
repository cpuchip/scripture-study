# D&C 61 — mine anger is turned away

**Setting:** **On the bank of the Missouri River, McIlwaine's Bend, August 12, 1831.** Returning to Kirtland, Joseph and ten elders had traveled down the Missouri in canoes; on the third day "many dangers were experienced," and **William W. Phelps, in a daylight vision, saw the destroyer riding in power upon the face of the waters.** They camped, and this revelation came — about the danger of the waters, the protection of the faithful, and the God whose anger toward His own people lasts only a day.

**Binding idea:** The waters carry real, decreed danger ("the destroyer rideth upon the face thereof," 61:19) — *and* "all flesh is in mine hand, and he that is faithful among you shall not perish by the waters" (61:6). The danger is real; the faithful are held. And to the elders He had been displeased with: "I, the Lord, was angry with you yesterday, but today mine anger is turned away" (61:20). God's anger toward His people is *momentary*; His mercy returns.

**Read:** "I, the Lord, forgive sins, and am merciful unto those who confess their sins with humble hearts" (61:2). "It is not needful for this whole company… to be moving swiftly upon the waters, whilst the inhabitants on either side are perishing in unbelief" (61:3); "I suffered it that ye might bear record… there are many dangers upon the waters" (61:4); "all flesh is in mine hand, and he that is faithful among you shall not perish by the waters" (61:6). "I would not suffer that ye should part until you were chastened for all your sins, that you might be one" (61:8). "I, the Lord, will reason with you as with men in days of old" (61:13). "In the beginning blessed the waters; but in the last days, by the mouth of my servant John, I cursed the waters" (61:14); "none is able to go up to the land of Zion upon the waters, but he that is upright in heart" (61:16). "The destroyer rideth upon the face thereof, and I revoke not the decree" (61:19). "I, the Lord, was angry with you yesterday, but today mine anger is turned away" (61:20). "It mattereth not unto me… whether they go by water or by land; let this be as it is made known unto them according to their judgments hereafter" (61:22). "Unto whom is given power to command the waters… let him do as the Spirit of the living God commandeth him" (61:27-28). "Be of good cheer, little children; for I am in your midst, and I have not forsaken you" (61:36).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "Mine anger is turned away" — God's displeasure is momentary (61:20).* The tenderest line in the section: "I, the Lord, was angry with you yesterday, but **today mine anger is turned away**." The elders had sinned (61:8 — they were "chastened for all your sins"); the Lord was *angry*. And the next day, His anger is *gone*. (This is the §3 / §121:43 reprove-then-love at the most intimate, daily scale: God's anger toward His covenant people is *not* a lasting state but a *passing* one — "for a small moment" [Isa 54:7-8; §121:7]. The chastening of yesterday gives way to the mercy of today. And the footnote pairs "angry" with *both* God's-indignation *and* God's-love — because the anger *is* a form of love, the displeasure of a Father, turned away the moment the chastening has done its work. For anyone who has felt under God's displeasure: it doesn't last; "today mine anger is turned away." The §54:10 "patient in tribulation… rest" deepened — even the *anger* is patient, and brief.)
- *★ Real danger, held faithful (61:4-6,19).* The destroyer's threat on the waters is *not* dismissed — "I revoke not the decree" (61:19); the danger is decreed and real. *And* "he that is faithful among you shall not perish by the waters" (61:6); "none is able to go up to the land of Zion upon the waters, but he that is upright in heart" (61:16). (The mature theology of providence: God does *not* promise to remove the danger [the destroyer rides on], but He *holds the faithful within it* — "all flesh is in mine hand." This is the §24:8/§54:10 "I am with thee in tribulation" applied to a *physical* peril: the faithful aren't exempted from the dangerous waters but *preserved* in them, *if* faithful. Not a magic shield over the reckless [hence "forewarn your brethren… lest their faith fail and they are caught in snares," 61:18] but a *covenant preservation* of the upright-in-heart. Faith doesn't make the water safe; it makes the faithful kept.)
- *★ "It mattereth not unto me… according to their judgments" (61:22) — the agency doctrine continues.* On whether to travel "by water or by land," the Lord again declines to specify: "it mattereth not unto me… let this be… according to their judgments hereafter" (the footnote tags it "Agency/Initiative," like §60:5). And He frames the whole revelation as *reasoning*: "I, the Lord, will reason with you as with men in days of old" (61:13, the §1:24/§50:11 period-language). (The §58:26 agency doctrine is becoming a *refrain* across these travel sections — §60:5, §61:22, §62:5,8: on means and methods, God hands the choice to the steward's judgment, commanding only the principles. He *reasons* [period-language, at the human level] rather than dictating. The steward is trusted with the *how*.)
- *Some can command the waters — by the Spirit's direction (61:27-28).* A striking exception: "unto whom is given power to command the waters… let him do as the Spirit of the living God commandeth him." The *gift* to command the elements (the §46 gifts, the working of miracles) exists — but it's exercised *only as the Spirit directs*, "as it remaineth with me to do hereafter." (The gift isn't a power to wield at will; it's *the Spirit's* to command through the gifted one. Even the miracle-worker acts under direction, not autonomy — the §50/§52 discernment governs the gift. Power over the elements is real but *bridled* to God's will, never freelance.)
- *"I have not forsaken you… be of good cheer" (61:36).* The close, after the danger and the anger: *reassurance*. "Be of good cheer, little children; for I am in your midst, and I have not forsaken you." (The §50:40-42 tender close again — after chastening and peril, the word is *presence and good cheer*. The elders who saw the destroyer on the waters and felt God's anger are told: I'm *with* you, I haven't left, be of good cheer. The §59:15 cheerful countenance as the response to even a frightening day.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. **Walk's contribution:** **§61's tenderest word is "mine anger is turned away" (61:20)** — God was angry yesterday, today His anger is *gone*: His displeasure toward His covenant people is *momentary*, the §3/§121:43 reprove-then-love at a *daily* scale, "a small moment" (Isa 54 / §121:7), and the anger itself a form of a Father's love, turned away the moment the chastening has done its work. Plus **real danger, the faithful held** — the destroyer rides the cursed waters and the decree is *not* revoked (61:19), yet "he that is faithful… shall not perish" (61:6): God doesn't remove the peril but *preserves the upright within it* (§24:8 in physical danger; not a shield over the reckless). And the **agency refrain continues** (61:22 — "it mattereth not unto me… according to their judgments"; God *reasons* rather than dictates, §1:24 period-language), with even the gift over the elements *bridled* to the Spirit (61:28). For Michael: the section where elders see a destroyer on the river and feel God's anger — and are told the anger is already *turned away*, that the faithful are held even in real danger, and to *be of good cheer*, for "I am in your midst, and I have not forsaken you."
