# D&C 105 — my people must learn obedience by the things which they suffer

**Setting:** **About four miles north of Fishing River, Clay County, Missouri, June 22, 1834.** Joseph had led **Zion's Camp** — roughly 200 men marched ~900 miles from Ohio — to help restore the expelled Saints to their Jackson County lands once the state militia acted. But the Missouri governor *refused* to call out the militia; the Saints could not retake Zion by any lawful means. Cholera was about to strike the camp. Into that dead end the Lord gives the revelation that *ends* the campaign — and reframes the whole "failure."

**Binding idea:** "My people must needs be chastened until they learn obedience, if it must needs be, by the things which they suffer" (105:6). Zion was *not* redeemed — not because God failed, but because the people were not yet ready; "it is expedient that mine elders should wait for a little season… that my people may be taught more perfectly, and have experience" (105:9-10). The march was a *proving*, not a conquest.

**Thoughts:**
- *★★ "Learn obedience by the things which they suffer" — the camp was a proving (105:6,19).* The reframe that makes sense of "Zion's Camp." The Lord does *not* send the men into battle — "I do not require at their hands to fight the battles of Zion; for… I will fight your battles" (105:14); the redemption of Zion is *deferred* (105:9). And the camp's true purpose: "it is expedient in me that they should be brought thus far for **a trial of their faith**" (105:19). "My people must needs be chastened until they learn obedience, if it must needs be, **by the things which they suffer**" (105:6, Heb 5:8 — "though he were a Son, yet learned he obedience by the things which he suffered"). (The deepest teaching on *apparent failure*: by every visible measure, Zion's Camp *failed* — it marched 900 miles, did not retake an inch of Zion, lost members to cholera, and disbanded. But the Lord's *actual* purpose was never the conquest — it was the *proving*: "a trial of their faith." And the proving *worked* — the men who endured the camp became the future leadership of the Church [the first Quorum of the Twelve and the Seventy were drawn largely from Zion's Camp veterans]. The march that "failed" its *stated* goal *succeeded* at God's *deeper* one: it revealed and forged the faithful. "Learn obedience by the things which they suffer" — even Christ learned this way [Heb 5:8]; the suffering is not the contradiction of the calling but the *curriculum* of it. For the project and for anyone: an enterprise that "fails" its visible objective may have *succeeded* at the proving God actually intended; the suffering teaches what no success could. Don't measure the camp by whether it took the city.)
- *★ Zion requires celestial law — and the people weren't living it (105:3-5).* The doctrinal core of *why* Zion failed: "they… do not impart of their substance… to the poor… and are not united according to the union required by the law of the celestial kingdom; and **Zion cannot be built up unless it is by the principles of the law of the celestial kingdom**" (105:3-5). (The §78:5-7 ["if ye are not equal in earthly things ye cannot be equal in obtaining heavenly things"] and §88:22 ["he who is not able to abide the law of a celestial kingdom cannot abide a celestial glory"] reaching their *practical verdict*: Zion *is* a celestial-law community [the pure in heart, §97; equal, §78], and it *cannot be built by anything less*. The Saints failed not because the mobs were strong but because *they would not live the celestial law* — would not impart to the poor, would not be united. The §97:25 conditional [Zion escapes *if she observe all things*] and the §104:17 ["enough and to spare"] enforced: Zion requires *the higher law lived*, and a people unwilling to share and unite *cannot build it*, no matter how many march to defend it. For the project: a Zion-aspiring community is built by *living the celestial law* [equality, imparting, union], not by force or numbers; the law lived is the only foundation.)
- *★ The elders must be endowed first (105:11,33).* The redemption waits on the *endowment*: "this cannot be brought to pass until mine elders are endowed with power from on high… they should receive their endowment from on high in my house, which I have commanded to be built unto my name in the land of Kirtland" (105:11,33). (The §38:32/§95:8 endowment thread reaching its *sequencing*: before Zion can be redeemed, the elders must be *endowed* in the Kirtland temple [§109/§110, two years off]. The power to build Zion is not raw zeal or military strength but *power from on high*, received through the ordinances [§84:20 the power of godliness]. The march to Missouri was *premature* — the temple, the endowment, the preparation had to come *first*. For the project: the work has a *sequence* — preparation and endowment of power *precede* the great undertaking; zeal that outruns preparation [Zion's Camp before the endowment] is sent home to wait. The §10:4 pace-to-strength: don't run faster than the endowment.)
- *★ Sue for peace, lift the ensign of peace (105:38-40).* The closing command, after the aborted campaign: "**sue for peace**, not only to the people that have smitten you, but also to all people; and lift up an **ensign of peace**, and make a proclamation of peace unto the ends of the earth" (105:38-39). (The §98:16 "renounce war and proclaim peace" enacted at the very moment war seemed justified: the camp came armed, the cause was just, the wrong was real — and the Lord's word is *sue for peace*, even "to the people that have smitten you." The presiding covenant's persuasion-not-compulsion [§121:41] and the §98 standard-of-peace at the end of a *military* expedition: not vengeance, not even the just war the camp came for, but an *ensign of peace*. The "day of choosing" [105:35] follows — the camp revealed the chosen — but the chosen are to be *peacemakers*. For the project: even when force seems warranted and the cause is just, the higher command is *sue for peace*; the proving produces not warriors but peacemakers.)
