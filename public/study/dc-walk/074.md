# D&C 74 — little children are holy

**Setting:** **Wayne County, New York, 1830** — out of chronological place in the book (it sits among the late-1831 Hiram sections but predates them). "Even before the organization of the Church, questions had arisen about the proper mode of baptism," and Joseph sought answers. The revelation is "an explanation of **1 Corinthians 7:14**" — Paul's line ("else were your children unclean, but now are they holy") that had been used for centuries to justify *infant baptism*. The Lord unfolds what Paul actually meant.

**Binding idea:** "Little children are holy, being sanctified through the atonement of Jesus Christ; and this is what the scriptures mean" (74:7). Children don't need baptism to be clean — they are *already* holy, sanctified by the Atonement; the tradition "that little children are unholy" is *done away*.

**Read:** "For the unbelieving husband is sanctified by the wife, and the unbelieving wife is sanctified by the husband; else were your children unclean, but now are they holy" (74:1, quoting 1 Cor 7:14). "In the days of the apostles the law of circumcision was had among all the Jews who believed not the gospel of Jesus Christ" (74:2). "There arose a great contention among the people concerning the law of circumcision, for the unbelieving husband was desirous that his children should be circumcised and become subject to the law of Moses, which law was fulfilled" (74:3). "The children, being brought up in subjection to the law of Moses, gave heed to the traditions of their fathers and believed not the gospel of Christ, wherein they became unholy" (74:4). "For this cause the apostle wrote unto the church, giving unto them **a commandment, not of the Lord, but of himself**, that a believer should not be united to an unbeliever; except the law of Moses should be done away among them" (74:5). "That their children might remain without circumcision; and that the tradition might be done away, which saith that little children are unholy; for it was had among the Jews" (74:6). "But little children are holy, being sanctified through the atonement of Jesus Christ; and this is what the scriptures mean" (74:7).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "Little children are holy… sanctified through the atonement of Jesus Christ" (74:7).* The walk's children-are-redeemed doctrine, reaching its sanctification statement. Where §29:46-47 said children "cannot sin" before accountability and §68:27 set baptism at *eight*, §74 grounds it in the Atonement: children are not *made* holy by an ordinance — they are *already* holy, "sanctified through the atonement of Jesus Christ." (Moroni 8 [Mormon's letter against infant baptism — "little children are alive in Christ… it is solemn mockery before God that ye should baptize little children"] in compressed form. The doctrine dissolves the whole anxiety infant baptism was built to answer: if children are born guilty [original sin] they must be baptized at once or be damned — but they are *not* born guilty; the Atonement covers them from the start. The §19 mercy [Endless punishment dissolved] and the §29/§68 children-redeemed converge: a child who dies before eight needs no ordinance, being already holy in Christ. "This is what the scriptures mean" — the Lord reads Paul's "now are they holy" *plainly*: the children of *believers* are holy not by a ritual but because they're under the Atonement, and the contention about circumcising them under a fulfilled law [74:3] missed it entirely.)
- *★ "A commandment, not of the Lord, but of himself" (74:5) — the nature of apostolic counsel.* A remarkable and project-relevant line. Paul's counsel that "a believer should not be united to an unbeliever" was "a commandment, not of the Lord, but **of himself**" — Paul's own inspired *judgment* for a specific pastoral situation, not a direct dictation from the Lord. (This matches Paul's *own* framing in 1 Cor 7:12 ["But to the rest speak I, not the Lord"] and 7:25 ["I have no commandment of the Lord: yet I give my judgment"]. The doctrine: *not every word of an apostle is direct revelation* — some is inspired counsel adapted to circumstance, the servant's own wise judgment within his stewardship. This sits in fascinating tension-and-complement with §68:3-4 ["whatsoever they shall speak when moved upon by the Holy Ghost shall be scripture"] and §67 [the substance from the Father of lights]: §68 is the *promise* available when the Spirit moves; §74 is the recognition that a servant *also* speaks "of himself" — gives reasoned counsel for a situation [here, protecting children's faith from being raised under a dead law] — and that this *too* can be wise, can even *be in the scriptures*, without being a "thus saith the Lord." The period-language principle's neighbor: scripture contains both direct revelation *and* inspired-judgment-in-context, and reading it well means knowing which is which. For the project working through a servant's [or an instrument's] judgment: not every output need claim "thus saith the Lord" to be faithful — counsel offered honestly "of himself," within stewardship, for the situation at hand, is its own legitimate category. The honesty is in *labeling* it so — Paul did, and the Lord confirms the label here.)
- *The fulfilled law, and protecting the children's faith (74:2-6).* The historical mechanism: in mixed believer-unbeliever marriages, the unbelieving (Jewish) spouse wanted the children circumcised "and become subject to the law of Moses, which law was fulfilled" (74:3) — and children "brought up in subjection to the law of Moses… believed not the gospel… wherein they became unholy" (74:4). (The danger wasn't the child's *infancy* but the child's *upbringing* in a fulfilled, faith-foreclosing tradition. "Unholy" in 74:4 means *grown to reject the gospel* — not the infant's state but the adult's choice, formed by being raised under "the traditions of their fathers." Paul's counsel protected the *next generation's faith*: don't yoke a believer to an unbeliever in a setting where the children will be raised away from Christ. This is the §68:25-28 parental-teaching duty from the *other* side — there, parents must teach the gospel by eight; here, the warning against a marriage that will raise children *against* it. The concern in both is the same: the faith handed to the children.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. **Walk's contribution:** **§74 grounds the children-are-redeemed doctrine in the Atonement — "little children are holy, being sanctified through the atonement of Jesus Christ" (74:7).** Explaining 1 Corinthians 7:14 (long misused to justify infant baptism), the Lord reads Paul plainly: children don't need an ordinance to be clean — they are *already* holy, sanctified by Christ from the start (Moroni 8; §29:46-47/§68:27 converging; the §19 mercy — children aren't born guilty, so the whole infant-baptism anxiety dissolves). And the project-relevant line: **Paul's counsel against believer-unbeliever marriage was "a commandment, not of the Lord, but of himself" (74:5)** — the apostle's own inspired *judgment* for a pastoral situation, not a direct "thus saith the Lord" (matching Paul's own 1 Cor 7:12, "I, not the Lord"). This complements §68:3-4 (Spirit-moved speech *is* scripture) and §67 (substance from the Father of lights): scripture holds *both* direct revelation *and* inspired-judgment-in-context — and the honesty is in *labeling* which it is (Paul did; the Lord confirms the label). For a project working through a servant's or an instrument's judgment, that's its own legitimate category: counsel offered honestly "of himself," within stewardship, for the situation at hand — as long as it doesn't dress itself as "thus saith the Lord." The historical concern underneath (74:4) is the same as §68:25's: the faith handed to the children — don't raise them under a fulfilled, faith-foreclosing tradition. For Michael: the section that says a dying child needs no baptism because the Atonement already holds them — and quietly models the difference between "the Lord says" and "I judge," teaching that an honest servant labels which he's giving.
