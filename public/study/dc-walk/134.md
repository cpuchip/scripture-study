# D&C 134 — a declaration of belief on governments and laws

**Setting:** **Adopted by unanimous vote at a general assembly, Kirtland, Ohio, August 17, 1835** — not a revelation but a *declaration of belief*, written (likely by Oliver Cowdery) and approved by the Church to state "our belief with regard to earthly governments and laws in general… that [it] may not be misinterpreted nor misunderstood." It was placed near the close of the first edition. Twelve articles on the proper relation of citizen, church, and state.

**Binding idea:** **"We believe that governments were instituted of God for the benefit of man" (134:1)** — and therefore must "secure to each individual the free exercise of conscience, the right and control of property, and the protection of life" (134:2). Government exists to protect agency; "the civil magistrate should restrain crime, but never control conscience… should punish guilt, but never suppress the freedom of the soul" (134:4).

**Read:**
- The purpose of government: "We believe that governments were instituted of God for the benefit of man; and that he holds men accountable for their acts in relation to them" (134:1). "...no government can exist in peace, except such laws are framed and held inviolate as will secure to each individual the free exercise of conscience, the right and control of property, and the protection of life" (134:2).
- Freedom of conscience: "...we do not believe that human law has a right to interfere in prescribing rules of worship to bind the consciences of men… that the civil magistrate should restrain crime, but never control conscience; should punish guilt, but never suppress the freedom of the soul" (134:4).
- Church and state distinct: "We do not believe it just to mingle religious influence with civil government, whereby one religious society is fostered and another proscribed" (134:9). "...we do not believe that any religious society has authority to try men on the right of property or life… or to inflict any physical punishment upon them. They can only excommunicate them from their society, and withdraw from them their fellowship" (134:10).
- Citizenship + self-defense: "We believe that all men are bound to sustain and uphold the respective governments in which they reside, while protected in their inherent and inalienable rights" (134:5); "all men are justified in defending themselves, their friends, and property… in times of exigency, where immediate appeal cannot be made to the laws" (134:11).

**Thoughts:**

- *★ Government exists to protect agency (134:1-4).* The declaration's whole frame is that government is "instituted of God for the benefit of man" (134:1) — and the *benefit* is the protection of agency: "the free exercise of conscience, the right and control of property, and the protection of life" (134:2). (This is the §101:77-80 inspired-Constitution doctrine in *declarative* form — "that every man may act according to the moral agency which I have given… not in bondage one to another" [§101:78] restated as a creed: the legitimate purpose of the state is to *secure freedom*, especially "the freedom of the soul" [134:4]. The magistrate's bound is sharp: "restrain crime, but never control conscience; punish guilt, but never suppress the freedom of the soul." For the project's agency thread [§58:26/§93:30/§101:77]: the order that protects agency is sacred *because agency is sacred* — government is legitimate exactly insofar as it guards the moral agency God gave; a government that controls conscience has exceeded its instituted purpose.)

- *★ The church holds NO coercive power — only fellowship (134:10).* The most pointed article for the presiding covenant: "we do not believe that any religious society has authority to try men on the right of property or life… or to inflict any physical punishment upon them. **They can only excommunicate them from their society, and withdraw from them their fellowship**." (The §121:41 "no power… only by persuasion" applied to the *institution*: even the church — holding real priesthood authority — has *no* civil, physical, or coercive power over persons; its *only* sanction is the withdrawal of fellowship. This is the presiding covenant made *structural* — the church governs by persuasion and association, never by force on body or property [the §134:9 church-state separation guards it from borrowing the state's sword]. For the project's presiding discipline: an institution that presides rightly holds *no* coercive power over its members — its only lever is fellowship, offered or withdrawn; the moment a religious body reaches for physical or proprietary force, it has stepped outside its authority [§121:37 "Amen to the priesthood"]. The §121:41 covenant and the §134:10 limit are the same doctrine — persuasion, never compulsion, all the way up to the institution.)

- *The bond-servants article — of its time, noted not adjudicated (134:12).* The declaration's final article counsels missionaries not to "interfere with bond-servants" or preach to them "contrary to the will… of their masters" (134:12). (This is a 1835 accommodation to the slavery-era legal context of Missouri and the South — a prudential caution written into a moment when the Saints were being accused of abolitionist agitation that endangered lives. It is historically conditioned and morally uncomfortable to modern eyes; per the walk's discipline I note it factually as *of its time* — a declaration adopted by vote, not a revelation, reflecting the legal and political peril of 1835 — and leave its weighing to Michael and the larger arc of the Restoration [the priesthood-lineage thread that lands at OD 2, 1978]. I record; I do not defend or adjudicate.)
