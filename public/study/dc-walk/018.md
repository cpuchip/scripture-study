# D&C 18 — the worth of souls

**Setting:** **Fayette, New York, June 1829** — to Joseph, Oliver Cowdery, and David Whitmer, answering Oliver's desire to know more. Per Joseph, it made known "the calling of twelve apostles in these last days" and instruction for building up the Church — *before the Church was even organized* (that comes in §20, April 1830). The Twelve are foreseen ten months early.

**Binding idea:** "Remember the worth of souls is great in the sight of God" (18:10) — and the *measure* of that worth is the Atonement itself: "the Lord your Redeemer suffered death in the flesh; wherefore he suffered the pain of all men" (18:11). You learn what a soul is worth by what was paid for it. And the payment was *everything*, for *each* one — so the joy over a single soul is immense (18:15).

**Read:** Because Oliver *knows* the things he wrote are true (18:2 — the §6 witness), he's commanded to "rely upon the things which are written" (18:3), for in them is "the foundation of my church, my gospel, and my rock" (18:4). "I speak unto you, even as unto Paul mine apostle, for you are called even with that same calling with which he was called" (18:9). Then the heart: "Remember the worth of souls is great in the sight of God; for, behold, the Lord your Redeemer suffered death in the flesh; wherefore he suffered the pain of all men, that all men might repent and come unto him" (18:10-11). "How great is his joy in the soul that repenteth!" (18:13). "If it so be that you should labor all your days… and bring, save it be one soul unto me, how great shall be your joy with him in the kingdom of my Father!" (18:15). "Take upon you the name of Christ, and speak the truth in soberness" (18:21); "there is none other name given whereby man can be saved" (18:23); "all men must take upon them the name which is given of the Father, for in that name shall they be called at the last day" (18:24). The Twelve "are they who shall desire to take upon them my name with full purpose of heart" (18:27); Oliver and David are to "search out the Twelve… by their desires and their works you shall know them" (18:37-38). "These words are not of men nor of man, but of me" (18:34) — "you can testify that you have heard my voice, and know my words" (18:36).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ The worth of souls is measured by the Atonement (18:10-11).* The two verses are welded: *remember the worth of souls* — then immediately, *the Redeemer suffered the pain of all men.* The logic is that you cannot know what a soul is worth by looking at the soul; you know it by looking at the *price*. Christ "suffered the pain of all men" — so the worth of any one man is the whole suffering of God. There is no cheap soul, because there was no cheap redemption. (And §19, the very next section, is about to show what that suffering *was* — §18 names the worth, §19 shows the cost. Read together they're a single teaching: souls are worth *that*.)
- *The math of joy: one soul = great joy; many = greater (18:13-16).* The Lord builds an explicit ladder: His joy over *one* repentant soul is "great" (18:13); your joy with *one* soul you bring is "great" (18:15); your joy with *many* is greater still (18:16). The striking thing is the *floor*: "if it so be that you should labor all your days… and bring, save it be one soul." A whole life of labor for a single soul is not failure — it is "great joy." The Lord pre-emptively dignifies the missionary who converts almost no one. The worth isn't in the count; it's in the soul, and one is already worth a life. (This completes §§15-16: the most-worth thing is bringing souls, and here even *one* makes a life's labor worth it.)
- *"Take upon you the name of Christ" — identity by the name (18:21-25).* Salvation is framed as *bearing a name*: "there is none other name given whereby man can be saved" (18:23, Acts 4:12); "in that name shall they be called at the last day" (18:24); "if they know not the name by which they are called, they cannot have place in the kingdom" (18:25). This is the temple/sacrament thread — taking *upon* oneself the name of Christ (Mosiah 5, the §6:36 "look unto me in every thought"). To be saved is to be *called by His name* — to belong to Him so fully that His name is the one you answer to. (This connects to the new-name temple-truth Michael named before his walk: names are load-bearing in the Restoration; the name you bear at the last day determines your place. I flag the broader name-theology as the reserved/temple-adjacent thread it is — the saving mechanics of "the name given of the Father" run into territory that is properly Michael's + the Spirit's to weigh.)
- *Desire is the qualification for the Twelve (18:27-28,37-38).* "The Twelve are they who shall desire to take upon them my name with full purpose of heart." The apostolic call, like every call in this cluster (§4 onward), turns on *desire* — and Oliver and David are to *recognize* the future apostles "by their desires and their works" (18:38). The desire-as-entry-point thread reaches the highest office: even apostleship is, at root, granted to those who *want* Christ's name with full purpose of heart. (And it's the §11:21 obtain-before-declare logic applied to selection: you know the called ones by the convergence of *desire* and *works* — wanting, made visible by doing.)
- *"You have heard my voice, and know my words" (18:35-36).* The section ends by grounding its own authority in §8-9's revelation theology: "it is my voice which speaketh them unto you… by my power you can read them one to another." Oliver can *testify* not because he reasoned to it but because he *heard the voice* in mind and heart (8:2). The witness of the words is the same as the witness of everything else in the cluster — the Spirit on the receiver.
