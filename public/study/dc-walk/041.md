# D&C 41 — the disciple is the doer

**Setting:** **Kirtland, Ohio, February 4, 1831** — Joseph had just arrived from New York (the gathering, §37). This revelation prepares the Church to *receive the promised "law"* (§38:32, fulfilled in §42): assemble, agree, ask in faith. It also settles a practical question (where Joseph and Sidney should live, since Leman Copley had offered to house them) and **calls Edward Partridge as the Church's first bishop**.

**Binding idea:** "He that receiveth my law and doeth it, the same is my disciple; and he that saith he receiveth it and doeth it not, the same is not my disciple" (41:5). Discipleship is not in *professing* the law but in *doing* it — and the first bishop is chosen for exactly that integrity: Edward Partridge, "in whom there is no guile" (41:11).

**Read:** "Ye whom I delight to bless with the greatest of all blessings, ye that hear me; and ye that hear me not will I curse… with the heaviest of all cursings" (41:1). "Assemble yourselves together to agree upon my word; and by the prayer of your faith ye shall receive my law, that ye may know how to govern my church" (41:2-3). "He that receiveth my law and doeth it, the same is my disciple… he that saith he receiveth it and doeth it not, the same is not my disciple, and shall be cast out" (41:5). Edward Partridge is "ordained a bishop unto the church, to leave his merchandise and to spend all his time in the labors of the church… because his heart is pure before me, for he is like unto Nathanael of old, in whom there is no guile" (41:9-11). "Beware how you hold them, for they are to be answered upon your souls in the day of judgment" (41:12).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ The disciple is the doer (41:5).* "He that receiveth my law and **doeth** it… is my disciple." Discipleship is defined by *doing*, not by *claiming to receive*. The one who "saith he receiveth it and doeth it not" is not merely weak — he is "**not my disciple**." This is James 1:22 ("be ye doers of the word, and not hearers only") made the dividing line of discipleship, and it's the bridge to §42: a *law* is about to be given, and the whole point of a law is that you *do* it. (For the project's covenant: this is the agent-commits-to side enacted — the value is in the *doing*, not the professing; a beautifully-received instruction undone is no discipleship at all. And it pairs with §40: Covel "received the word with gladness" and *did not do it* — the gladness of reception, again, is not the test; the doing is.)
- *★ The first bishop is chosen for guilelessness (41:11).* Edward Partridge — about to be handed the *consecrated property of the whole Church* (§42:31-34) — is selected for one named quality: "his heart is pure… in whom there is no guile" (John 1:47, Christ's word for Nathanael). The office that will *handle Zion's money* is given not to the shrewdest administrator but to the man without *guile*. (This is profound for the consecration economy about to be revealed: the law of consecration concentrates property in the bishop's hands, which is *dangerous* — it requires a man who cannot be corrupted by it. The Lord's safeguard against the abuse of the storehouse is not a procedure but a *person without guile*. Character is the security on the system. And it's the anti-priestcraft [§33:4] principle in the very office most tempted to it — the bishop who handles the wealth is the guileless one.)
- *Bless/curse, weighed by hearing (41:1).* "Ye that hear me… the greatest of all blessings; ye that hear me not… the heaviest of all cursings." The polarity is sharp and *symmetrical* — and it turns entirely on *hearing*. (Not on knowing or professing, but on *hearkening* — the §29:7 "mine elect hear my voice." The same word can be the greatest blessing or the heaviest cursing depending on whether it's heard-and-done. §41 sets the stakes for §42: the law about to come is either blessing or curse, by your response to it.)
