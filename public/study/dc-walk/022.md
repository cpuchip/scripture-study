# D&C 22 — a new and everlasting covenant

**Setting:** **Manchester, New York, April 16, 1830** — ten days after the Church was organized. Some recent converts who had already been baptized in other churches wanted to join *without* being rebaptized — surely their earnest prior baptism counted? The Lord's answer is four verses, and it is firm.

**Binding idea:** Baptism is not a sincere personal act that any minister can perform; it is entry into "a new and an everlasting covenant" (22:1), and it requires *authority*. "Though a man should be baptized an hundred times it availeth him nothing" (22:2) without it — because the issue was never the convert's sincerity but the *authority* by which the ordinance is administered (the §13 conferral, now applied to every member).

**Read:** "All old covenants have I caused to be done away in this thing; and this is a new and an everlasting covenant, even that which was from the beginning" (22:1). "Although a man should be baptized an hundred times it availeth him nothing, for you cannot enter in at the strait gate by the law of Moses, neither by your dead works" (22:2). "It is because of your dead works that I have caused this last covenant and this church to be built up unto me, even as in days of old" (22:3). "Enter ye in at the gate, as I have commanded, and seek not to counsel your God" (22:4).

**Thoughts:**
- *Authority, not sincerity, is the issue (22:2).* The convert's logic is reasonable and humane: *I was baptized in good faith; why redo it?* The Lord's answer relocates the question entirely. The prior baptism's defect isn't the convert's heart — it's that it was administered without the authority just restored (§13). "A hundred times" of sincere-but-unauthorized baptism "availeth nothing," because baptism is not the convert *expressing* faith but God *receiving* him through an authorized ordinance. (This is the hard edge of the §13 "I confer" doctrine: if authority must be conferred, then ordinances done without it are *dead works* — sincere, but not binding. It's not a judgment on the other churches' people; it's a claim about what makes an ordinance *valid*.)
- *"Dead works" — sincere but lifeless (22:2-3).* The phrase (Moroni 8:23, Heb 6:1) is stark. A "dead work" is not a *bad* work; it's a *lifeless* one — a true form without the living authority that animates it. The prior baptisms were *works* (real, intended, religious) but *dead* (lacking the conferred power). And the remedy isn't to fix the convert; it's that "this last covenant and this church" had to "be built up… *because of* your dead works" — the whole Restoration exists, in part, because the ordinances had gone lifeless. (This is the apostasy diagnosed at the level of *ordinance*: not that everyone became wicked, but that the *living authority* drained out, leaving sincere dead forms — the same loss §13 repairs.)
- *"Seek not to counsel your God" (22:4) — the sharp close.* The convert's instinct to negotiate ("surely my baptism counts") is named as *counseling God* — trying to instruct Him on His own terms. The footnote points to Jacob 4:10: "seek not to counsel the Lord, but to take counsel from his hand." It's a humbling redirection: the covenant's terms are His to set, not ours to argue. (Gently, this is the §9 lesson again — you don't bring God your conclusion and ask Him to ratify it; you *receive* His terms. The convert wanted his prior baptism ratified; the Lord asks him to receive the new one.)
