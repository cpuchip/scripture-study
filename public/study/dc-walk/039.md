# D&C 39 — prepared for a greater work

**Setting:** **Fayette, New York, January 5, 1831** — to **James Covel**, a Methodist minister of about forty years who had "covenanted with the Lord that he would obey any command" given through Joseph. This is the Lord's response to that covenant: a warm, expansive call, naming Covel's sorrow, forgiving his past, and commissioning him to "a greater work." It must be read with §40 — its three-verse sequel, in which Covel *breaks* the covenant. Together they are the parable of the sower enacted in real time.

**Binding idea:** "Thine heart is now right before me at this time" (39:8) — *now*, *at this time*. The Lord meets Covel exactly where he is, names the pride and worldliness that made him "reject me many times" (39:9), declares "the days of thy deliverance are come" (39:10), and offers him "a greater work" and "a blessing so great as you never have known" (39:10-11). The whole of it hangs on one condition: "if thou wilt hearken to my voice" (39:10) — and §40 records that he didn't.

**Read:** Christ, "the light… which shineth in darkness" (39:2), "to as many as received me, gave I power to become my sons… and even so will I give unto as many as will receive me" (39:4). "He that receiveth my gospel receiveth me; and he that receiveth not my gospel receiveth not me" (39:5). "This is my gospel—repentance and baptism by water, and then cometh the baptism of fire and the Holy Ghost, even the Comforter, which… teacheth the peaceable things of the kingdom" (39:6). "James, I have looked upon thy works and I know thee. Thine heart is now right before me at this time; and… I have bestowed great blessings upon thy head; nevertheless, thou hast seen great sorrow, for thou hast rejected me many times because of pride and the cares of the world" (39:7-9). "The days of thy deliverance are come, if thou wilt hearken to my voice… Arise and be baptized… and you shall receive my Spirit, and a blessing so great as you never have known" (39:10). "If thou do this, I have prepared thee for a greater work" (39:11); "power shall rest upon thee; thou shalt have great faith, and I will be with thee and go before thy face" (39:12). "Thou art not called to go into the eastern countries, but… to the Ohio" (39:14), where "I have kept in store a blessing such as is not known among the children of men" (39:15).

**Thoughts:**
- *"Thine heart is now right before me at this time" (39:8) — the precision of "now."* The Lord's affirmation is carefully *time-stamped*: Covel's heart is right *now, at this time*. Not "you are a faithful man" but "at this moment your heart is turned." It reads, in hindsight, as exact: the Lord names the *present* truth of Covel's heart without overstating its permanence — because He knows (39:9) the pattern of pride and worldliness that has made Covel "reject me many times." The affirmation is genuine *and* qualified by the man's history. (The Lord doesn't flatter Covel into a fixed sainthood; He meets the real, *currently*-turned heart and offers it deliverance — *if* it will hearken. The "now" carries the whole tension: a heart can be right now and not stay so.)
- *"Thou hast rejected me many times because of pride and the cares of the world" (39:9) — the fault named in advance.* The Lord tells Covel, *before* the call, the exact weakness that endangers it: pride and "the cares of the world." This is the §23:1 / §31:9 pattern — the fault named at the call (Oliver's pride, Marsh's household, now Covel's worldliness). And it is *precisely* what §40 says undid him: "the fear of persecution and the cares of the world caused him to reject the word" (40:2). The Lord diagnosed the disease and offered the cure in the same breath; Covel took neither. (The naming is mercy, not fatalism — Covel was *warned* of the very thing that would catch him; the warning didn't compel the avoiding. Agency, again, is real: God's foresight [38:2, "all things present"] does not override the choice.)
- *A "greater work… prepared" — the Sidney pattern, offered (39:11).* Covel is told, like Sidney (§35:3), that the Lord has "prepared thee for a greater work" — his forty years of ministry could have been the runway for the fulness. The retrospective-recognition offer (your past was preparation) is extended to Covel too. *But* — and this is the difference §40 makes — Sidney *received* it and Covel *rejected* it. The same offer, the same reframing of a life's preparation, met with opposite responses. (The offer is genuine for both; the divergence is the man's. Two ministers, two prepared-for-greater-works, one Restoration apostle and one who returned to his "former principles and people.")
- *"The peaceable things of the kingdom" (39:6) — the §36:2 phrase again.* The Comforter "teacheth the peaceable things of the kingdom" — repeated verbatim from §36:2 (Edward Partridge). The Spirit's curriculum is consistently *peace*; the irony is that Covel rejected it for "fear of persecution" (40:2) — he traded the *peaceable* things for the *fear* of men (the §30 fear-of-man thread). The peace was offered; the fear won.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off — read with **§40** (the sequel). **Walk's contribution:** **§39 is the warm, full call that §40 will show rejected** — and read together they're the parable of the sower in real time. The Lord names Covel's heart right *"now, at this time"* (39:8) — genuine but carefully time-stamped, qualified by the history of pride and worldliness (39:9) that He names *before* the call (the §23:1/§31:9 fault-at-the-call pattern). He offers Covel the *same* "prepared for a greater work" reframing He gave Sidney (§35:3) — your forty years could be the runway. The difference is the man: Sidney received it, Covel (§40) rejected it for the very fault named. For Michael: the call where the Lord diagnoses the disease *and* offers the cure in one breath — "your heart is right now; you've rejected me before through pride and worldliness; here is your deliverance *if* you'll hearken" — preserving in the canon both the genuine call and (next section) the genuine refusal.
