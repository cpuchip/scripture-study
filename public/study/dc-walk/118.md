# D&C 118 — let men be appointed to supply the place of those who are fallen

**Setting:** **Far West, Missouri, July 8, 1838** — in answer to Joseph's supplication, "Show us thy will, O Lord, concerning the Twelve." The Quorum had been gutted by the Kirtland apostasy; several apostles had fallen. The revelation reorganizes the Twelve, names their replacements, and commands a *departure date* — April 26, 1839, from the Far West temple site — that history would make famous.

**Binding idea:** "Let a conference be held immediately; let the Twelve be organized; and let men be appointed to supply the place of those who are fallen" (118:1). The §114:2 promise enacted: the fallen are *replaced*, the Quorum *refilled* — and the new apostles are named.

**Read:** "Let a conference be held immediately; let the Twelve be organized; and let men be appointed to supply the place of those who are fallen" (118:1). "If they will do this in all lowliness of heart, in meekness and humility, and long-suffering… I will provide for their families; and an effectual door shall be opened for them" (118:3). "Next spring let them depart to go over the great waters, and there promulgate my gospel" (118:4). "Let them take leave of my saints in the city of Far West, on the twenty-sixth day of April next, on the building-spot of my house" (118:5). "Let my servant John Taylor, and also my servant John E. Page, and also my servant Wilford Woodruff, and also my servant Willard Richards, be appointed to fill the places of those who have fallen" (118:6).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ The Quorum refilled — the office outlasts the fallen (118:1,6).* The §114:2 "others shall be planted in their stead" *enacted*: the fallen apostles are replaced, and the new ones are *named* — John Taylor, John E. Page, Wilford Woodruff, Willard Richards (118:6). (The kingdom's resilience [§114] in action: an apostle's fall does not leave a permanent hole; the Lord *names the successors*. And the men named would prove the wisdom — Taylor and Woodruff later *led* the Church as Presidents; the Quorum refilled after the apostasy became, in time, *stronger* than before. The office outlasts the man [§81/§107:99/§114]; and the *replacement* is often where the future leadership is found [as Zion's Camp, §105, had also forged leaders]. For the project: a vacancy from a failure is not a wound but an *opening* — "men appointed to supply the place" — and the new steward may exceed the fallen one.)
- *★★ The Far West departure — a prophecy kept to the letter against the odds (118:4-5).* The Lord commands the Twelve to "take leave of my saints in the city of Far West, on the **twenty-sixth day of April next**, on the building-spot of my house" (118:5), then depart for England. (History made this a *test*: within months of this revelation, the Saints were *driven out of Missouri* by the extermination order [winter 1838-39]; Far West was *abandoned*, in enemy hands, and the apostates publicly *mocked* the prophecy, vowing it would be the one revelation that *failed* — the Twelve could not possibly return to hostile territory on a *named day*. But on April 26, 1839, before dawn, the Twelve *secretly returned* to the deserted Far West temple site, held their meeting on the cornerstone, ordained Wilford Woodruff and George A. Smith, rolled a stone into place, and departed — *fulfilling the revelation to the letter* in the teeth of mortal danger. The §82:10 "I am bound when ye do what I say" and the §1:38 voice-of-servants: the word was kept *exactly*, against every reason not to, because the Lord had spoken a *date and a place*. For the project's covenant-keeping: when the Lord [or the covenant] names a specific commitment, faithfulness keeps it *to the letter* — even when circumstances make it costly or seemingly impossible; the obscure, dangerous, literal obedience is where the covenant is proven. I note the Far West departure as a famous instance of obedience-against-the-odds, historical.)
- *Provide-for-families-if-lowly (118:3).* The condition on the promise: "if they will do this in all lowliness of heart, in meekness and humility, and long-suffering… I will provide for their families" (118:3). (The §100:1 "your families are well… in mine hands" given a *condition*: the provision for the missionary's family is tied to the missionary's *lowliness* — meekness and humility and long-suffering. The §112:10 "be thou humble" again: the apostle's power and the family's care both rest on *humility*. For the missionary leaving family for the work: the Lord provides — *if* the heart is lowly.)
