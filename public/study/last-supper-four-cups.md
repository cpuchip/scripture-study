# The Four Cups: The Passover, the Last Supper, and the Cup He Finished

> *"For even Christ our passover is sacrificed for us."*
> — [1 Corinthians 5:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-cor/5?lang=eng&id=p7#p7)

**Binding question:** How does the Passover — and specifically its four cups — illuminate what Jesus did at the Last Supper, in Gethsemane, and on the cross?

**Catalyst:** A video on the four cups of the Passover seder, pulled as a study thread on a traveling Sunday.

---

## I. The Last Supper was a Passover

Every synoptic gospel says it plainly. Matthew: "Now the first day of the feast of unleavened bread the disciples came to Jesus, saying unto him, Where wilt thou that we prepare for thee to eat the passover?" ([Matthew 26:17](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/26?lang=eng&id=p17#p17)). Mark: "the first day of unleavened bread, when they killed the passover" ([Mark 14:12](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/mark/14?lang=eng&id=p12#p12)). Luke: "Then came the day of unleavened bread, when the passover must be killed" ([Luke 22:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/22?lang=eng&id=p7#p7)).

The meal in the upper room was not a generic supper. It was the oldest feast Israel kept — older than the Mosaic law it would later sit inside, instituted in Egypt on the night the destroying angel passed through ([Exodus 12:1–13](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ex/12?lang=eng&id=p1-p13#p1)). For roughly fifteen centuries Israel had killed a lamb, eaten it with unleavened bread and bitter herbs, and told the children the story of the night God brought them out.

Jesus wanted this one. Luke records the strongest possible Hebraism on His lips: "With desire I have desired to eat this passover with you before I suffer" ([Luke 22:15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/22?lang=eng&id=p15#p15)). Not "I would like to." *With desire I have desired.* He had been to many Passovers. This was the Passover He had been moving toward.

And inside it, He changed it. "As they were eating, Jesus took bread, and blessed it, and brake it, and gave it to the disciples, and said, Take, eat; this is my body" ([Matthew 26:26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/26?lang=eng&id=p26#p26)). Then the cup: "Drink ye all of it; For this is my blood of the new testament, which is shed for many for the remission of sins" ([Matthew 26:27–28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/26?lang=eng&id=p27-p28#p27)).

The footnote on "testament" reads simply: *GR covenant.* The new testament is the new covenant. And the gospel-library footnote on "blood" sends you to [Exodus 24:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ex/24?lang=eng&id=p8#p8), where Moses sprinkled the blood of sacrifice on Israel and said, "Behold the blood of the covenant." Jesus took the elements of the old covenant meal and made them the emblems of the new. Old covenant: blood on the people. New covenant: "Drink ye all of it."

He did not abolish the Passover. He fulfilled it and folded it forward. President Dallin H. Oaks taught that "the sacrament is the ordinance that replaced the blood sacrifices and burnt offerings of the Mosaic law" (quoted in the [New Testament Scripture Helps](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/manual/scripture-helps-new-testament/23-matthew-26-mark-14-john-13?lang=eng)). The Passover was a memorial: "this day shall be unto you for a memorial; and ye shall keep it a feast to the Lord throughout your generations" ([Exodus 12:14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ex/12?lang=eng&id=p14#p14)). The sacrament is also a memorial: "this do in remembrance of me" ([Luke 22:19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/22?lang=eng&id=p19#p19)). The Scripture Helps manual cites a striking observation about what *remembering* meant to ancient Israel. It "is not merely a mental exercise. Rather, it is participation in the event remembered." The Passover did not merely recall the exodus. It put each generation inside it. The sacrament does the same with Calvary.

Paul says it without hedging: "Christ our passover is sacrificed for us" ([1 Corinthians 5:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-cor/5?lang=eng&id=p7#p7)). Webster's 1828 dictionary catches why the sentence works. *Passover*, sense one: the feast. Sense two: "The sacrifice offered at the feast of the passover." *(Requoted 2026-06-12 — the earlier "; the paschal lamb" tail was the 1913 edition's, though Noah's own first sense tells the story with "the blood of the paschal lamb.")* The word names both the meal and the victim. When Paul writes "Christ our passover," he is using Webster's second sense. Christ is the Lamb.

## II. The four cups and the four "I will" promises

The video Michael watched traces a structure the gospels do not number for us but that runs underneath the meal: the four cups.

In the Jewish seder, the meal is punctuated by four cups of wine. The tradition attaches each cup to one of four promises God made through Moses in [Exodus 6:6–7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ex/6?lang=eng&id=p6-p7#p6):

> "I am the Lord, and **I will bring you out** from under the burdens of the Egyptians, and **I will rid you** out of their bondage, and **I will redeem you** with a stretched out arm... And **I will take you to me for a people**, and I will be to you a God."

Four "I will" verbs: *bring out, deliver, redeem, take as a people.* Sanctification, deliverance, redemption, and the cup of covenant — the cup of belonging. The deliverance is not the end; the end is "I will take you to me for a people, and I will be to you a God." Israel was brought out of Egypt *so that* they could be brought into covenant with the Lord. The fourth promise is the point of the first three.

Here is where honesty matters, and Michael asked for it. Exodus does not mention any cups at the first Passover. The first Passover, as Exodus 12 records it, has a lamb, blood, hyssop, unleavened bread, and bitter herbs — no wine, no four cups. The four-cup structure, and the mapping of the cups onto the four verbs of Exodus 6, is rabbinic. It is codified in the Mishnah, in the tractate Pesachim, compiled around 200 AD — generations after the upper room. We cannot say Jesus was working through a fixed, numbered, four-cup liturgy the way a modern Jewish family does, because the codified form we know postdates Him.

What the New Testament *does* give us is suggestive. Luke records not one cup at the supper but two. Early, before the bread: "And he took the cup, and gave thanks, and said, Take this, and divide it among yourselves" ([Luke 22:17](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/22?lang=eng&id=p17#p17)). Then, after the bread: "Likewise also the cup after supper, saying, This cup is the new testament in my blood" ([Luke 22:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/22?lang=eng&id=p20#p20)). Two cups, at distinct points in the meal — the sacramental cup is explicitly the one "after supper." Multiple cups were in use. And the meal ended with singing: "when they had sung an hymn, they went out into the mount of Olives" ([Matthew 26:30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/26?lang=eng&id=p30#p30); [Mark 14:26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/mark/14?lang=eng&id=p26#p26)). The Scripture Helps manual notes that the hymn was almost certainly the Hallel — Psalms 113 through 118, the songs sung at Passover.

So the four-cup framing is a true and ancient tradition, even if its codified numbering came after Christ. It is a lens. Held loosely, it illuminates. Held too tightly, it overclaims. The rest of this study uses it the first way.

## III. The cup He did not drink

Three gospels record the same arresting sentence. Matthew: "I will not drink henceforth of this fruit of the vine, until that day when I drink it new with you in my Father's kingdom" ([Matthew 26:29](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/26?lang=eng&id=p29#p29)). Mark: "I will drink no more of the fruit of the vine, until that day that I drink it new in the kingdom of God" ([Mark 14:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/mark/14?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)). Luke: "I will not drink of the fruit of the vine, until the kingdom of God shall come" ([Luke 22:18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/22?lang=eng&id=p18#p18)).

He stopped. In a meal built around cups, He announced a cup He would not drink — not now, not henceforth, not until a future kingdom. The video reads this as Jesus deliberately stopping before the fourth cup, the cup of covenant and rejoicing, leaving the seder unfinished.

The text supports a deferral. It does not, by itself, support the specific number. None of the three gospels says "this was the third of four" or "He skipped the fourth." What all three say is that Jesus declared a cup He would not drink *until the kingdom*. The deferral is in the text. The numbering comes from the seder tradition laid over it.

But the deferral itself is real, and it is strange enough to demand attention. He is at a feast. He has just blessed bread and cup. And He announces He is pausing — leaving something undrunk, pointing it forward to "that day" in "my Father's kingdom." The Joseph Smith Translation of [Luke 22:16](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/22?lang=eng&id=p16#p16) sharpens it. Where the King James reads "until it be fulfilled in the kingdom of God," the JST expands: "until it be fulfilled *which is written in the prophets concerning me. Then I will partake with you,* in the kingdom." Joseph Smith's revision makes the deferral explicit and ties it to prophecy: He will not finish this meal until what the prophets wrote of Him is fulfilled. And [D&C 27:5–14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/27?lang=eng&id=p5-p14#p5) — cross-referenced in the footnote — promises that in a yet-future day He will drink of the fruit of the vine again with His prophets on the earth.

So the cup He did not drink in the upper room points forward. It points, in His own words, to a future kingdom event. Hold that. It will matter when we ask where the bitter cup is finished, because His stated timeline runs past the cross, not to it.

## IV. The bitter cup of Gethsemane

They sang the Hallel and walked out to the Mount of Olives, to a garden called Gethsemane. And there a different cup appears.

> "O my Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me: nevertheless not as I will, but as thou wilt." ([Matthew 26:39](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/26?lang=eng&id=p39#p39))

Luke: "Father, if thou be willing, remove this cup from me: nevertheless not my will, but thine, be done" ([Luke 22:42](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/luke/22?lang=eng&id=p42#p42)). The gospel-library footnote on "remove" sends you directly to [D&C 19:18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/19?lang=eng&id=p18#p18). The footnote on "cup" in Matthew sends you to the same place. The editors of the scriptures want you to read Gethsemane's cup and D&C 19 together.

D&C 19 is the risen Christ speaking to Martin Harris in 1829, and it is the only place in scripture where the Lord names the cup Himself:

> "Which suffering caused myself, even God, the greatest of all, to tremble because of pain, and to bleed at every pore, and to suffer both body and spirit—and would that I might not drink the bitter cup, and shrink— Nevertheless, glory be to the Father, and I partook and finished my preparations unto the children of men." ([D&C 19:18–19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/19?lang=eng&id=p18-p19#p18))

*The bitter cup.* That is His word for it. And notice what He admits. "Would that I might not drink the bitter cup, and shrink." He names the wish to recoil. He does not pretend He wanted it. In the garden He had asked three times for it to pass; in D&C 19 He tells us, looking back, that He felt the pull to shrink. The honesty of that line is the doctrine. The sinless God, the greatest of all, *wished not to drink it* — and drank it anyway.

This is where the bitter cup of Gethsemane meets the cups of the Passover. The Passover meal already held bitterness in it. "With bitter herbs they shall eat it" ([Exodus 12:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ex/12?lang=eng&id=p8#p8)) — bitter herbs eaten alongside the lamb, recalling the bitterness of bondage. At the table, bitterness sat next to the lamb. In the garden, the Lamb drank the bitterness Himself. The herbs on the seder plate were a memorial of someone else's affliction. The cup in Gethsemane was His own.

And the bitter cup is the inverse of the fourth cup. The fourth cup of the seder is the cup of covenant and rejoicing — "I will take you to me for a people." The cup in Gethsemane is the cup of God's wrath against sin, the cup the Old Testament prophets describe being handed to the nations ([Jeremiah 25:15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/jer/25?lang=eng&id=p15#p15), "Take the wine cup of this fury at my hand"). Jesus drinks the cup of fury so that we can be handed the cup of belonging. He takes the bitter so the covenant cup can come to us sweet.

Elder Bruce R. McConkie put the scene in D&C 19's own words: "We know that he lay prostrate upon the ground as the pains and agonies of an infinite burden caused him to tremble and would that he might not drink the bitter cup" ([The Purifying Power of Gethsemane](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1985/04/the-purifying-power-of-gethsemane?lang=eng)). He sweat blood "as he drained the dregs of that bitter cup his Father had given him."

## V. "I thirst" — the hyssop and the cup He finished

Michael's question: is the bitter cup finished in Gethsemane, or on the cross?

The garden was not the end. McConkie answers it directly: "while he was hanging on the cross for another three hours, from noon to 3:00 p.m., all the infinite agonies and merciless pains of Gethsemane *recurred*." His testimony at the end of that talk: the Atonement "took place in Gethsemane *and* at Golgotha." Not Gethsemane *or* Golgotha. One cup, drunk in the garden and drained on the cross. Elder Jeffrey R. Holland names the same continuity from the other end — the anguish, he says, "began in the Garden of Gethsemane" ([This Do in Remembrance of Me, quoted in the Scripture Helps manual](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/manual/scripture-helps-new-testament/23-matthew-26-mark-14-john-13?lang=eng)). Began there. The verb says it did not end there.

So follow the cup to its last drop. John takes us to Golgotha:

> "After this, Jesus knowing that all things were now accomplished, that the scripture might be fulfilled, saith, I thirst. Now there was set a vessel full of vinegar: and they filled a sponge with vinegar, and put it upon hyssop, and put it to his mouth. When Jesus therefore had received the vinegar, he said, It is finished: and he bowed his head, and gave up the ghost." ([John 19:28–30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/19?lang=eng&id=p28-p30#p28))

Read the sequence carefully, because it carries the answer. "All things were now accomplished" comes *first.* The atoning suffering is already complete when He says "I thirst." Then the drink. Then "It is finished." Then death. The drink is not the agony; the agony is done. The drink is a final, deliberate act — John says Jesus speaks "I thirst" specifically "that the scripture might be fulfilled." It fulfills [Psalm 69:21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ps/69?lang=eng&id=p21#p21): "in my thirst they gave me vinegar to drink." He asks for the drink in order to complete what was written.

Now the hyssop. Michael asked whether the hyssop means something. It does. Of the four gospels, only John names hyssop at the cross — Matthew and Mark say the sponge was put on "a reed." John, the gospel most saturated with Passover, chose the word *hyssop*. And hyssop has one unforgettable scriptural job. At the first Passover:

> "And ye shall take a bunch of hyssop, and dip it in the blood that is in the basin, and strike the lintel and the two side posts with the blood that is in the basin." ([Exodus 12:22](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ex/12?lang=eng&id=p22#p22))

Hyssop is the applicator. It is the instrument that takes the lamb's blood and puts it where it saves — on the doorposts, so that "when I see the blood, I will pass over you" ([Exodus 12:13](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ex/12?lang=eng&id=p13#p13)). At the first Passover, hyssop carried the lamb's blood *to* the people. At the last Passover, hyssop carried a drink *to* the Lamb. The plant that applied the saving blood is the plant that lifts the final cup to the One whose blood it all pointed to.

And John makes the Lamb identification unmistakable. A few verses later: "they brake not his legs... For these things were done, that the scripture should be fulfilled, A bone of him shall not be broken" ([John 19:33, 36](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/19?lang=eng&id=p33#p33)). The footnote sends you to [Exodus 12:46](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ex/12?lang=eng&id=p46#p46): "neither shall ye break a bone thereof" — the law of the paschal lamb. John times the crucifixion to "the preparation of the passover" ([John 19:14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/19?lang=eng&id=p14#p14)), the very hours the lambs were slain in the temple. He is telling us: this is the Passover lamb, dying on the day of Passover lambs, with not a bone broken, exactly as the law required.

Is the vinegar the deferred fourth cup? This is the study's most beautiful claim and its most fragile, and Michael asked for honesty over a satisfying ending.

What can be said with confidence: the death is steeped in Passover. The Lamb, the unbroken bones, the hyssop, the timing — John lays them out deliberately. And the Greek word behind "It is finished" is *tetelestai* — finished, completed, paid in full. The same root carries D&C 19:19's "I partook and *finished* my preparations." The gospel-library footnote on D&C 19:19's "finished" sends you to [John 19:30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/19?lang=eng&id=p30#p30). The scriptures themselves tie the bitter cup's "finished" to the cross's "It is finished." On the cross, with a drink lifted on Passover hyssop, the bitter cup is drained and the work is done.

What cannot be said as confidently: that the vinegar *is* specifically the seder's fourth cup, completing the meal interrupted in the upper room. Here is the tension, and it is a real one. In the upper room Jesus said He would not drink of the fruit of the vine "until that day when I drink it new... in my Father's kingdom" ([Matthew 26:29](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/26?lang=eng&id=p29#p29)) — and [D&C 27](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/27?lang=eng) confirms that points to a still-future day. His own stated timeline for finishing the cup of the *vine* runs past the cross, not to it. Also, John never calls the vinegar a cup; it is "a vessel full of vinegar," sour wine, the drink of soldiers and the poor. The cleanest reading of the vinegar is not the seder's cup of rejoicing. It is Psalm 69:21 fulfilled, and it is the bitter cup's last bitter swallow.

So the typology is true at the level it is true. Christ is the Passover. The bitter cup He drank is what makes the covenant cup available to us. He stopped before a cup of rejoicing in the upper room and pointed it forward — and on the cross He finished the *other* cup, the bitter one, so that the cup of rejoicing could one day be poured. The fourth-cup-on-the-cross framing edifies because it points at something real: a deferral, a draining, and a "finished." It overreaches only if it forces John's vessel of vinegar to be the literal fourth cup of a liturgy codified two centuries later. The Lamb is real whether or not the numbering holds.

## VI. What the text says, what tradition says, what the type says

Michael asked to hold the tension honestly. Here is the honest accounting, in three tiers.

**What the New Testament states explicitly.** The Last Supper was a Passover meal. Jesus took the bread and the cup and instituted the sacrament as the new covenant memorial. He declared a cup of the vine He would not drink until a future kingdom. In Gethsemane He prayed for a cup to pass and did not shrink. On the cross, "knowing that all things were now accomplished," He received a drink lifted on hyssop and said "It is finished." His bones were not broken, fulfilling the law of the paschal lamb. Paul names Him "our passover... sacrificed for us." All of this is in the text, verifiable, certain.

**What ancient tradition supplies.** The four-cup structure of the seder, the mapping of the cups to the four "I will" promises of Exodus 6, the Hallel sung after the meal. These are real and ancient — the Hallel is named in the manual as the likely hymn — but the codified four-cup form comes from the Mishnah, compiled around 200 AD. It runs parallel to and after the New Testament, not provably before it. The tradition is a faithful lens. It is not chapter and verse.

**What the type says.** A type is true when it points to Christ, even when its details are devotional rather than doctrinal. The four-cup reading points at things that are unmistakably true: that the Passover was always about a Lamb; that deliverance was always *for* covenant belonging; that Jesus deferred something at the table and finished something at the cross; that bitterness and the Lamb were on the same plate from the first Passover, and that on the last one the Lamb drank the bitterness alone. The type teaches truly even where the numbering is loose, because the thing it points to — the Lamb of God, slain — is the most solid fact in the account.

The binding question asked how the four cups illuminate the Last Supper, Gethsemane, and the cross. The honest answer: the *Passover* illuminates all three, overwhelmingly and certainly. The *four cups specifically* give a lens that is moving and largely true to the spirit, while running ahead of the explicit letter at one point — the claim that the cross's vinegar is the literal fourth cup. Take the lens for what it gives. It gives a Lamb, a bitter cup, a hyssop branch, and a "finished."

And on the question Michael most wanted answered — where is the bitter cup finished — the witnesses agree. Not in Gethsemane alone. The agonies of the garden, McConkie testified, *recurred* on the cross. Holland: the anguish *began* in the garden. D&C 19:19: "I partook and finished my preparations" — and the scriptures' own footnote ties that "finished" to "It is finished" in John 19:30. The bitter cup is one cup. It is lifted to His lips in Gethsemane and drained to the last drop on the cross. *Tetelestai.* It is finished there.

## Becoming

This study began with a question about a number — how many cups, which one He skipped. It ends somewhere else. It ends at a Lamb who drank the cup He asked to have pass.

The line that will not leave me is D&C 19:18: "would that I might not drink the bitter cup, and shrink." He did not want it. He said so, plainly, looking back on it years later from resurrected glory. He still calls it bitter. And He drank it anyway, and the next word is "Nevertheless." The whole Atonement turns on that one word. *Nevertheless.* The wish to shrink was real; the drinking happened past it.

I keep my own bitter cups small and I still ask them to pass. The hard conversation, the assignment I did not want, the day that asks more of me than I planned to give. My instinct is His first instinct — let this pass from me. What I do not do well is His second move. The *nevertheless.* I want the cup removed; He shows me the cup drunk.

**Questions to sit with:**
- Where in my life right now am I praying "let this cup pass" — and have I yet prayed the second half of His prayer, "nevertheless, not as I will"?
- The Passover only worked when the blood was actually applied — hyssop dipped, doorposts struck. Am I letting the Lamb's blood be *applied* to me, through the sacrament, weekly and deliberately? Or am I remembering it the way I'd remember a fact?
- Holland asked it directly: do I see the sacrament as *my* Passover — my safety, my deliverance, my redemption? Or as a quiet four minutes I pass through?

**This week:**
- At the sacrament, before the bread comes, name one specific bitterness I am carrying and one specific sin I want passed over. Hand both to the Lamb on purpose. Treat the cup as applied, not merely observed — the hyssop touching the doorpost of an actual week.
- When the next small bitter cup comes — the conversation or task I want to pass — pause before reacting and say the second half out loud: *nevertheless, not as I will, but as thou wilt.* Practice the word the whole Atonement hangs on.

**Connected to:** [becoming/plan-of-salvation.md](../becoming/plan-of-salvation.md) — the Atonement is the center of the plan, and this study locates it concretely: a garden, a cross, a cup begun and a cup finished. The sacrament is where that finished work is applied to an unfinished person.

## A note on this study

This study followed the project's phased workflow with externalized notes (`study/.scratch/last-supper-four-cups.md`). The critical-analysis phase changed the study's center of gravity. The catalyst video's thesis — that Jesus completes the seder's fourth cup on the cross — is emotionally compelling, and an earlier instinct was to write toward it. Stress-testing against the actual text surfaced two honest problems: the codified four-cup seder form is Mishnaic and postdates Christ, and Jesus' own stated timeline for the cup of the vine ([Matthew 26:29](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/26?lang=eng&id=p29#p29); [D&C 27](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/27?lang=eng)) runs to a future kingdom, not to Golgotha. The study keeps the four-cup framing as a devotional lens and is explicit about where the lens outruns the letter. The answer to the binding question — that the bitter cup is one cup, begun in Gethsemane and finished on the cross — rests on Read-verified scripture (D&C 19:16–19, John 19:28–36) and two apostolic witnesses (McConkie 1985, Holland 2009) whose talks were read in full.
