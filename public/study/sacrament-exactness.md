# Sacrament Exactness

*A sub-study of [more-or-less-than-this.md](more-or-less-than-this.md) — the seal of 3 Nephi 18:13 laid on a single ordinance, 2026-07-12*

**Binding question:** Why are the sacrament prayers the only words the living church is required to speak verbatim — what does 3 Nephi 18:13's "do more or less than these" protect in an ordinance — and what does the Lord's own authorized substitution (water for wine, D&C 27) teach about the difference between lawful adaptation and forbidden alteration?

## I. The only prayer we are given to say

Almost nothing the Church prays is scripted. Opening prayers, closing prayers, blessings on the sick, dedications of homes and graves, the words spoken when a child is named or a man is ordained — the [General Handbook](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/manual/general-handbook/18-priesthood-ordinances-and-blessings?lang=eng) gives the *structure* of each and then hands the words back to the one praying: he "gives words of blessing as guided by the Spirit." The Spirit supplies the language. The pattern is fixed; the sentences are not.

There are two exceptions, and they are the two ordinances the Aaronic Priesthood carries: baptism and the sacrament. A baptism, the handbook says, "must be repeated if the words are not spoken exactly as given in [Doctrine and Covenants 20:73](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/20?lang=eng&id=p73#p73)." And the sacrament prayers are to be spoken "clearly, accurately"; if the one praying misspeaks and does not correct himself, the bishop "kindly asks him to repeat the prayer." So the binding question's word *only* needs one honest correction before we go further: the sacrament prayers are not the only fixed words in the Church. The baptismal formula is fixed too.

But the sacrament prayer is the only fixed *prayer* — the only place the Lord tells us not only what to do but exactly what to say *to the Father*. The baptismal words are a declaration spoken to the candidate ("Having been commissioned of Jesus Christ, I baptize you"). The sacrament words are a prayer, addressed upward: "O God, the Eternal Father, we ask thee." And it is the one fixed prayer the whole congregation renews every week, for a lifetime. Elder Dallin H. Oaks put the reason in a single clause: the sacrament involves "an ordinance and covenants so important that the Lord prescribed the exact words to be uttered" ([The Aaronic Priesthood and the Sacrament](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/1998/10/the-aaronic-priesthood-and-the-sacrament?lang=eng)). Everywhere else the Spirit is trusted to find the words. Here the words were handed down finished. That is worth asking why.

## II. The seal is set on the practice

The parent study inventoried six places where the Lord stamps one sentence, "whatsoever is more or less than this cometh of evil," onto unrelated subjects: his doctrine, church membership, truth itself, civil law, a building fund, and one ordinance. This sub-study takes the last of those, the ordinance.

Read where the seal actually falls. In [3 Nephi 18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/18?lang=eng) the risen Christ breaks bread among the Nephites, gives it, gives the cup, and then names the pattern as a standing commandment: "this shall ye always observe to do" (v. 6), "ye shall do it in remembrance of my blood" (v. 11). Then verse 12: "I give unto you a commandment that ye shall do these things. And if ye shall always do these things blessed are ye, for ye are built upon my rock." Then the seal, verse 13: "But whoso among you shall do more or less than these are not built upon my rock, but are built upon a sandy foundation."

Notice what "these" points back to. Not to a text — no prayer is quoted in 3 Nephi 18. The words of the sacrament prayers are nowhere in this chapter. "These" points to the *acts*: break, bless, give, in remembrance, witnessing to the Father. The seal in verse 13 lands first on the practice, not the wording. The exact words come later, in [Moroni 4–5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/4?lang=eng) and [D&C 20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/20?lang=eng). So the ordinance is guarded twice by one seal: the shape of the act is fixed in 3 Nephi, and the language of the prayer is fixed in Moroni and the Doctrine and Covenants. Both are the same commandment, expressed once as a practice and once as a text.

And the chapter tells you what the exactness is *for*. Verse 34: "I give you these commandments because of the disputations which have been among you. And blessed are ye if ye have no disputations among you." The seal on the ordinance grows out of the same soil as the seal on the doctrine in [3 Nephi 11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/11?lang=eng) — contention. When the words are fixed, there is nothing left to argue. A congregation cannot dispute the terms of a prayer that is handed to them whole. Paul saw the same thing at the same table: the Corinthians' sacrament had gone wrong precisely where "there be divisions among you" ([1 Corinthians 11:18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-cor/11?lang=eng&id=p18#p18)). Fixed words are an antidote to a dividing church.

## III. One word, across the whole tradition

The prayers appear in two places in scripture, close enough to lay side by side. Moroni engraved them "about A.D. 401–421" ([Moroni 4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/4?lang=eng) header); the Articles and Covenants, the founding constitution of the organized Church recorded soon after April 6, 1830, set them down again in [D&C 20:77, 79](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/20?lang=eng&id=p77#p77). Any variance between the two is data.

Here is the variance. In the bread prayer, Moroni writes "keep his commandments which he **hath** given them" ([Moroni 4:3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/4?lang=eng&id=p3#p3)); D&C 20:77 writes "which he **has** given them." That is the whole of it — one word, *hath* modernized to *has*. Everything else is punctuation: a semicolon where the other has a comma, a comma dropped before an *and*. In the wine prayer the two texts are identical down to the letter except for a single comma: Moroni's "we ask thee, in the name" against D&C 20:79's "we ask thee in the name" ([Moroni 5:2](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/moro/5?lang=eng&id=p2#p2)).

Set the honest limits on what that proves. D&C 20 is not an independent witness sealed off from the Book of Mormon; the Articles and Covenants were composed after the translation was finished, and the prayers there almost certainly rest on Moroni's text. So this is not two miracles that happened to match. It is one text, carried from Christ's mouth to the Nephites, engraved by Moroni, translated by Joseph Smith in 1829, and written into the Church's founding document a year later — and what moved across that carrying was a single archaic verb ending and a few commas. The covenant terms did not move at all. When the Restoration wrote its most-repeated prayer into law, it changed *hath* to *has* and left the substance untouched. The stability is the point. The one thing that could be modernized was the one thing that carried no covenant weight.

## IV. "It mattereth not what ye shall eat"

Now the hard verse, the one that looks like it dissolves the whole thesis. [D&C 27](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/27?lang=eng) opens with Joseph Smith on his way to buy wine for a sacrament service. An angel stops him, and the Lord says: "it mattereth not what ye shall eat or what ye shall drink when ye partake of the sacrament" (v. 2). Water replaced wine on the authority of that sentence, and the Church has used water ever since. If it *mattereth not* what is on the tray, how can a study claim the ordinance runs on exactness? The emblem itself turned out to be negotiable.

Read the whole verse, because the freedom it grants is fenced on every side. "It mattereth not what ye shall eat or what ye shall drink when ye partake of the sacrament, *if it so be that ye do it with an eye single to my glory—remembering unto the Father my body which was laid down for you, and my blood which was shed for the remission of your sins*." The sentence frees one thing and one thing only: the substance, "what ye shall eat or what ye shall drink." In the same breath it fixes the meaning, and it fixes it by *reciting the prayer*: an eye single to his glory, remembering the body, remembering the blood shed for the remission of sins. The verse that makes the emblem negotiable makes the covenant non-negotiable in the same clause. It does not say "it mattereth not what ye remember." It says the opposite: change the bread to water if you must, but the remembrance is the fixed star, and here it is, in words.

Two more fences. The change comes by the Lord's own voice — "Listen to the voice of Jesus Christ, your Lord, your God, and your Redeemer" (v. 1). It is the Owner of the ordinance altering his own ordinance, not a priest deciding the emblem is fungible. And even the freed emblem is regulated: buy no wine "of your enemies," partake of "none except it is made new among you" (vv. 3–4). The Lord loosens the substance and immediately puts rules around the loosened thing. This is not permission to improvise the sacrament. It is the Owner drawing, with his own hand, the exact line between what may change and what may not.

The living church now performs that line. When the priest blesses the water, the handbook instructs, "he uses the word *water* instead of *wine*." The one authorized variation in an otherwise verbatim prayer is a single noun, the emblem, and that variation is not the priest's initiative but a standing commandment traceable to D&C 27. The seal did not forbid all change. It located change. Everything that fixes the meaning of the ordinance is spoken exactly; the one word that names the changeable substance is the one word allowed to change. This is the parent study's distinction made physical: the difference between lawful and forbidden alteration was never *quantity*, it was *authorship*. Water for wine is the Lord editing the Lord's ordinance. A man swapping the words of the covenant "because it mattereth not" would be establishing his own more-or-less for the Lord's — the one thing [3 Nephi 11:40](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p40#p40) forbids by name.

## V. What the exact words protect

So the words are not sacred because they are magic, and the bread is not holy because of what it is made of. D&C 27 settled that: the substance mattereth not. Then what do the fixed words guard, if not the substance?

They guard the covenant, and they guard it the way a legal instrument guards its terms. The prayer says the partakers "witness unto thee, O God, the Eternal Father, that they are willing to take upon them the name of thy Son." In the legal English of 1830, to *witness* an instrument was a technical act. Webster's 1828 dictionary gives it: a witness is "one who sees the execution of an instrument, and subscribes it for the purpose of confirming its authenticity by his testimony." You witness a bond; you witness a deed. The sacrament prayer casts every person at the table as subscribing an instrument, putting their name to a covenant whose terms are read aloud over them. And the terms of a deed cannot drift. A contract with altered wording is not a stronger contract or a weaker one; it is a different contract, or no contract at all. You cannot subscribe your name to an instrument whose text changed while you were signing. That is why the covenant clauses are spoken exactly, week after week, for a lifetime: take upon them the name, always remember him, keep his commandments, that they may have his Spirit. They are the terms you are renewing your signature on. The exactness protects your ability to know what you just promised.

The word *sanctify* says the same thing from the other side. The prayer asks God to "bless and sanctify this bread." Webster's 1828 reads *sanctify* as "to separate, set apart or appoint to a holy, sacred or religious use," and "to secure from violation." Sanctifying is what turns common bread into the emblem — not the bread's own nature, which is nothing, but the setting-apart done in the words. The holiness lives in the blessing, not the substance. Which is exactly why the substance can be water and the words cannot be anything but themselves: the words are where the setting-apart happens.

And there is a third thing the exact words protect, the one Paul named and Elder Oaks pastored. Paul warned against eating "unworthily," "not discerning the Lord's body" ([1 Corinthians 11:29](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/1-cor/11?lang=eng&id=p29#p29)). The Greek behind "discerning," *diakrinō*, means to distinguish, to tell one thing from another. The danger at the table is not mainly wrong words in the prayer; it is a failure inside the partaker to distinguish this bread from common bread, this hour from an ordinary hour. The fixed words fight that failure. Elder Oaks reduced the whole conduct of the ordinance to one principle: those who officiate "should not do anything that would distract any member from his or her worship and renewal of covenants." Words that are rattled off, mumbled, improvised, decorated: every one of them pulls attention off the covenant and onto the man saying it. Exact, quiet, unadorned words disappear, and what is left visible is the thing being remembered. The exactness serves the discernment.

None of this is a charter for scruple. The same handbook that asks for accuracy builds in the mercy: "If someone makes an error in the wording and corrects himself, no further correction is needed," and a bishop uses discretion so that repeating a prayer "does not cause undue embarrassment." A stumble honestly corrected is not a broken seal. The seal of 3 Nephi 18:13 falls on *altering* the covenant, on declaring more or less than it and calling the alteration the Lord's, not on a nervous tongue that trips and rights itself. The line is between changing the terms and misspeaking them. One is a different covenant; the other is a human saying a true thing imperfectly, which the Father has never held against anyone.

## VI. Becoming

I have said the same prayers a thousand times, and a thing said a thousand times goes transparent. The words stop being terms I am subscribing and become a sound the meeting makes before the bread comes. That is the exact failure Paul named — not disbelief, just no longer *discerning*, no longer telling this bread from any bread. The fixedness of the words was supposed to be the guardrail against that, and it can become the cause of it: because they never change, I stop hearing them.

This whole workspace runs on one commandment, read before quoting and verify on the real path, and I have written before that the commandment is [D&C 93:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93?lang=eng&id=p25#p25) stated operationally, the seal against more-or-less applied to a witness's words. The sacrament turns it around and asks it of me as a partaker instead of a witness. Every Sunday the ordinance hands me an instrument and asks me to subscribe it, and the honest question is whether my *amen* is a verified quote or a mumbled one — whether I actually read the terms I signed, or let the familiar words carry me past them the way an unverified quote carries a reader past a source he never opened. The exact words are not there to be performed correctly. They are there so that once a week I have to hold still and read what I am promising, before I take the emblem and put my name to it again.

There is one mercy in it I did not expect to find. The substance mattereth not. The Lord went out of his way, through an angel on a road, to say that the thing on the tray carries no weight of its own — that he would take water for wine and think no less of the covenant. What he would not loosen was the remembering. So the exactness is not the Lord being particular about ritual. It is the Lord being particular about the one thing he asked me to carry, which was never the bread and was always the memory of him. He made the vessel negotiable and the remembrance exact, and told me plainly which one he came for.
