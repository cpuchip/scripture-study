# Moroni 3 — the manner of ordination: plain, by prayer, hands, the name of Christ, and the Spirit

**Binding idea:** "The manner which the disciples, who were called the elders of the church, ordained priests and teachers— After they had prayed unto the Father in the name of Christ, they laid their hands upon them, and said: In the name of Jesus Christ I ordain you to be a priest (or if he be a teacher, I ordain you to be a teacher) to preach repentance and remission of sins through Jesus Christ, by the endurance of faith on his name to the end. Amen" (3:1-3). "And they ordained them by the power of the Holy Ghost, which was in them" (3:4).

**Read:** Moroni 3 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 4 verses. The exact form of ordaining priests and teachers.

## Thoughts

Moroni records the ordination of priests and teachers in its bare, exact form, and the *plainness* is the point. There is no elaborate ceremony, no required liturgy beyond the essentials: pray to the Father in the name of Christ; lay on hands; speak the ordaining words — "In the name of Jesus Christ I ordain you to be a priest" (or teacher); and it is done "by the power of the Holy Ghost." That is all. Moroni is building the record so the church can be *reconstituted precisely* in a later day, and what he preserves is the irreducible core — the authority, the prayer, the hands, the words, the Spirit — without ornament. The simplicity is itself a testimony: the true church does not require elaborate machinery; it requires authority exercised in Christ's name by the power of the Holy Ghost. (This is exactly the form the restored Church would later use, as D&C 20:39, 60 attests — the pattern preserved across fourteen centuries by a hunted man writing in hiding.)

But the verse worth slowing on is the *purpose* embedded in the ordaining words (3:3): a priest or teacher is ordained "to preach repentance and remission of sins through Jesus Christ, by the endurance of faith on his name to the end." The office is not an honor or a status; it is a *charge*, and the charge is the whole gospel in a sentence. The ordained man exists to preach two things — repentance, and remission of sins through Christ — and to do it by means of "the endurance of faith on his name to the end." Every priest and teacher is, by the very words of his ordination, pointed at the same single task: turn people to Christ for the forgiveness of their sins, and to enduring faith in His name. The ordination does not make a man important; it makes him an instrument of repentance and remission. And the closing detail — "they ordained them by the power of the Holy Ghost, which was in them" — confirms what chapter 2 established: the authority is real but it is not the men's own. They do not ordain by their own dignity or office but "by the power of the Holy Ghost." The whole act, from the opening prayer to the laying on of hands, is the Spirit working through men who have first called on the Father. Authority in Christ's church is plain, purposeful, and powered by the Holy Ghost — not by the men who hold it.
