# Moroni 6 — baptism for the broken heart; the church to nourish and keep; unlimited forgiveness; Spirit-led meetings

**Binding idea:** Baptism is for the repentant: "none were received unto baptism save they came forth with a broken heart and a contrite spirit, and witnessed unto the church that they truly repented of all their sins... save they took upon them the name of Christ, having a determination to serve him to the end" (6:2-3). The baptized "were numbered among the people of the church of Christ; and their names were taken, that they might be remembered and nourished by the good word of God... relying alone upon the merits of Christ, who was the author and the finisher of their faith" (6:4). The church "did meet together oft, to fast and to pray, and to speak one with another concerning the welfare of their souls" and "to partake of bread and wine, in remembrance of the Lord Jesus" (6:5-6). They guarded against iniquity, but "as oft as they repented and sought forgiveness, with real intent, they were forgiven" (6:8). And "their meetings were conducted... after the manner of the workings of the Spirit, and by the power of the Holy Ghost" (6:9).

## Thoughts

Moroni closes the church-order chapters with the requirements for baptism, and they make clear that baptism in Christ's church is *for the repentant*, never a casual entry. No one was received "save they came forth with a broken heart and a contrite spirit, and witnessed unto the church that they truly repented of all their sins" (6:2), and "save they took upon them the name of Christ, having a determination to serve him to the end" (6:3). Four things stand out: a *broken heart and contrite spirit* (the only sacrifice Christ asks, 3 Ne 9:20), *witnessed repentance* (a public turning, not a private feeling), *taking Christ's name*, and a *determination to serve to the end*. Baptism is the gate that requires something real to pass through it — genuine repentance, public commitment, and a settled resolve for the long road. (This requirement of *fruit* and *witnessed repentance* is precisely why infants are not baptized, which Moroni's father will address head-on in chapter 8 — baptism is for those who can repent and witness.)

But the most beautiful thing about the chapter is what happens *after* baptism (6:4) — the purpose of the church itself. The newly baptized "were numbered among the people of the church... and their names were taken, that they might be *remembered and nourished* by the good word of God, to keep them in the right way, to keep them continually watchful unto prayer, *relying alone upon the merits of Christ*." The church is not a club that enrolls members and forgets them; it is an organism that *nourishes*. The names are taken not for administrative tidiness but "that they might be remembered and nourished" — every member known, fed by the word, kept watchful, helped to stay in the right way. And the whole aim of the nourishing is to keep them "relying alone upon the merits of Christ, who was the author and the finisher of their faith" (Heb 12:2). The church exists to point its members away from themselves and toward Christ — to nourish them not into self-sufficiency but into deeper reliance on Him. This is the answer to what a church is *for*: not to make members feel they belong (though they do), but to nourish them in the word until they rely wholly on the merits of Christ.

The rhythm of church life is simple and communal (6:5-6): meeting "oft" to fast, pray, "speak one with another concerning the welfare of their souls," and partake of the sacrament "in remembrance of the Lord Jesus." Note the phrase "the welfare of their souls" — when they met, they spoke of the things that actually mattered, the state of their souls, not surface concerns. And it was frequent ("oft"), communal ("one with another"), and centered on Christ (the sacrament). The early church was a community of mutual soul-care, gathered often around the emblems of the atonement.

And the chapter holds together two things that can seem in tension — *discipline* and *forgiveness* — and shows they are one (6:7-8). The church was "strict to observe that there should be no iniquity among them," and the unrepentant who would not confess had their "names... blotted out" — real church discipline, by the three-witness process (D&C 42:80), even removal from the body. But: "as oft as they repented and sought forgiveness, with real intent, they were forgiven" (6:8). The discipline and the forgiveness are not opposites; they serve the same end. The discipline exists *for the sake of repentance* — to call the sinner back, not to cast him out permanently — and the moment the sinner repents "with real intent," forgiveness is *unlimited*: "as oft as they repented." This is the same standard the Lord gave Alma: "as often as my people repent will I forgive them" (Mosiah 26:30). The church guards its purity strictly, and forgives its penitent infinitely; the strictness and the mercy are both in service of bringing souls to repentance and keeping them in the body of Christ. "Real intent" is the hinge — forgiveness is unlimited but not automatic; it answers genuine repentance, not mere words.

And the chapter ends on the principle that governs everything in this whole section (6:9): "their meetings were conducted... after the manner of the workings of the Spirit, and by the power of the Holy Ghost; for as the power of the Holy Ghost led them whether to preach, or to exhort, or to pray, or to supplicate, or to sing, even so it was done." The meetings were not run by a fixed program but *led by the Spirit* — whatever the Holy Ghost prompted (preaching, exhortation, prayer, supplication, song), that was done. This completes the thread that has run through chapters 2-6: every part of the church — conferring the Holy Ghost (2), ordaining (3), the sacrament (4-5), and now the meetings themselves (6) — is done "by the power of the Holy Ghost." The church Christ established is, from top to bottom, a Spirit-led church: its authority flows from the Spirit, its ordinances are administered by the Spirit, and even its worship follows the Spirit's lead. Moroni's church-order chapters are, in sum, a portrait of a community held together not by organization but by the Holy Ghost — entered by genuine repentance, sustained by mutual nourishment, kept pure by loving discipline, forgiven without limit, and worshipping as the Spirit leads. That is the church the reader is to rebuild.
