# Moroni 7 — Mormon's great sermon: faith, hope, and charity, and the way to judge good from evil

**Binding idea:** Every good thing comes of Christ, and you have the Light of Christ to judge it — "every thing which inviteth to do good, and to persuade to believe in Christ, is sent forth by the power and gift of Christ" (7:16). By that light you lay hold on every good thing through faith, which produces hope, which flowers into charity — "the pure love of Christ" (7:47) — and "whoso is found possessed of it at the last day, it shall be well with him."

**Read:** Moroni 7 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 48 verses. Moroni preserves a sermon his father Mormon "spake concerning faith, hope, and charity... in the synagogue" (7:1). The capstone teaching of the three theological virtues, paired with the doctrine of how to discern good from evil — the only sermon of Mormon's preserved at length.

## Thoughts

Moroni is laying down the record, and the last thing he wants in it before his own farewell is not his own words but his father's. "I, Moroni, write a few of the words of my father Mormon, which he spake concerning faith, hope, and charity" (7:1). It is a son's act of love — the man who watched his entire people die wants the world to hear his father preach. And what Mormon preached, in the synagogue, to "the peaceable followers of Christ," was the architecture of the whole gospel life: how you know what is good, and how the good you lay hold of grows from faith to hope to charity.

**The sermon opens with discernment, not with faith — because you cannot exercise faith in the good until you can recognize it.** Mormon's first movement (7:5-19) answers a question most sermons skip: *how do I know what is actually of God?* His answer is breathtakingly democratic. "It is given unto you to judge, that ye may know good from evil; and the way to judge is as plain... as the daylight is from the dark night" (7:15). Not reserved for prophets, not requiring special revelation — *plain*, as plain as telling noon from midnight. And the instrument is universal: "the Spirit of Christ is given to every man, that he may know good from evil" (7:16). Every man. The Light of Christ is the birthright conscience in every human being, the inner daylight by which good and evil can be told apart. This is one of the most generous doctrines in all scripture — God did not leave moral knowledge to the lucky or the trained; He wrote the capacity to recognize good into every soul that comes into the world.

**And then the test itself, the single most useful sentence Mormon gives for a lifetime of decisions:** "every thing which inviteth to do good, and to persuade to believe in Christ, is sent forth by the power and gift of Christ; wherefore ye may know with a perfect knowledge it is of God" (7:16). And its mirror: "whatsoever thing persuadeth men to do evil, and believe not in Christ, and deny him, and serve not God... ye may know with a perfect knowledge it is of the devil" (7:17). Two tests bundled into one — does it invite to *do good*, and does it *persuade to believe in Christ*? Both point the same direction. The good and the Christ-ward always run together; you never have to choose between being good and following Christ, because all good *is* of Christ (7:12, "all things which are good cometh of God"). Mormon's confidence in the devil is total and bleak: "he persuadeth no man to do good, no, not one" (7:17). The adversary has zero good in him; therefore anything that genuinely invites you toward goodness cannot be from him. This is a tool you can carry into any decision — not "is this comfortable" or "is this approved," but *does this invite me to do good and to believe in Christ?*

**But the discernment carries a warning Mormon states twice (7:14, 7:18): judge rightly, lest you call good evil and evil good.** "Take heed... that ye do not judge that which is evil to be of God, or that which is good and of God to be of the devil" (7:14 — Isaiah's "woe unto them that call evil good, and good evil," 2 Ne 15:20, standing behind it). And "with that same judgment which ye judge ye shall also be judged" (7:18). The Light of Christ is given to *discern*, not to *condemn*; turn the instrument of discernment into an instrument of condemnation and it turns back on you. The faculty for telling good from evil is sacred, and misusing it — to damn rather than to choose — is itself a misjudgment that rebounds.

**The second movement (7:20-39) is the answer to the practical question: how do I lay hold on every good thing?** Mormon has shown that good things exist and are recognizable; now — "how is it possible that ye can lay hold upon every good thing?" (7:20). The answer is *faith*. "By faith, they did lay hold upon every good thing" (7:25). And here Mormon makes the case that miracles and the ministering of angels have not ceased and never will, "so long as time shall last, or the earth shall stand, or there shall be one man upon the face thereof to be saved" (7:36) — because "it is by faith that miracles are wrought; and it is by faith that angels appear and minister unto men" (7:37). The logic is exact and sobering: if these things have ceased, it is not because God changed but "because of unbelief, and all is vain" (7:37). The God of miracles is unchanging (echoing Mormon 9 and Moroni 7 together — the same God, the same faith, the same access) — so the only thing that can stop the miracles is the failure of faith on our side, never a withdrawal on His. (This is the same theology Mormon's son will close the whole record with in Moroni 10:8-19 and 10:24 — "if these things are done away then has come the apostasy.")

**The third movement (7:40-48) is the ladder: faith → hope → charity, each growing from the last.** Mormon builds it carefully. "How is it that ye can attain unto faith, save ye shall have hope?" (7:40) — and "what is it that ye shall hope for? ... ye shall have hope through the atonement of Christ and the power of his resurrection, to be raised unto life eternal" (7:41). Hope in the Restoration is not wishful optimism; it is the *specific, anchored expectation of resurrection and eternal life through Christ's atonement*. Then the interdependence: "if a man have faith he must needs have hope; for without faith there cannot be any hope" (7:42). And the soil both grow in: "he cannot have faith and hope, save he shall be meek, and lowly of heart" (7:43). Faith and hope require meekness — pride cannot believe, because pride trusts itself; meekness is the posture of the soul that can receive. And from meekness, faith, and hope, and the confession "by the power of the Holy Ghost that Jesus is the Christ," there *must needs* come charity — "for if he have not charity he is nothing" (7:44).

**And then the summit, where Mormon quotes Paul and then surpasses him.** Verse 45 is 1 Corinthians 13 almost word for word — "charity suffereth long, and is kind, and envieth not, and is not puffed up, seeketh not her own, is not easily provoked, thinketh no evil, and rejoiceth not in iniquity but rejoiceth in the truth, beareth all things, believeth all things, hopeth all things, endureth all things." But where Paul defines charity by its behaviors, Mormon adds the definition Paul never gave: **"charity is the pure love of Christ, and it endureth forever; and whoso is found possessed of it at the last day, it shall be well with him"** (7:47). The "pure love of Christ" — both genitives at once: the love that *Christ has* (His pure love, which He bestows), and the love *for Christ* that becomes love like His. It is not a virtue we manufacture; it is His love bestowed on us, which then flows through us. That is why the chapter ends not with a command to *be* more loving by effort but with a command to *pray to be filled*: "pray unto the Father with all the energy of heart, that ye may be filled with this love, which he hath bestowed upon all who are true followers of his Son" (7:48). Charity is asked for, not achieved. You pray to be *filled* with what only He can give.

**And the very last verse reaches all the way back to the chapter's beginning and forward to eternity at once.** The sermon opened with the goal of entering "the rest of the Lord" (7:3); it closes with what that rest will be: "when he shall appear we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is; that we may have this hope; that we may be purified even as he is pure" (7:48, quoting 1 John 3:2-3). The whole ladder — faith, hope, charity — terminates in *becoming like Christ by seeing Him as He is*. Charity is "the pure love of Christ"; being filled with it is being made pure "even as he is pure." The end of the love of Christ is to be made like Christ. That is the rest Mormon invited his people into in verse 3, and it is the same destination his son Moroni will leave the whole world pointing toward three chapters later.

## Connections

- **1 Cor 13 (whole chapter); Ether 12:28-35** — Paul's hymn to charity, which Mormon quotes (7:45) and then crowns with the definition "the pure love of Christ" (7:47); Moroni's own meditation on faith/hope/charity in Ether 12 is the companion piece.
- **2 Ne 15:20; Isa 5:20; D&C 64:16** — "woe unto them that call evil good, and good evil"; the warning behind Mormon's "judge not that which is evil to be of God" (7:14).
- **John 1:9; D&C 84:45-46; D&C 88:7-13; Mosiah 16:9; Ether 3:14** — the Light of Christ "given to every man" (7:16); the universal conscience by which all may know good from evil.
- **Matt 7:16-20; 3 Ne 14:16-20** — "by their works ye shall know them" (7:5); the good tree and the good fruit; Mormon's premise for discernment.
- **Mormon 9:9-21; Moroni 10:7, 19, 24** — miracles and angels have not ceased; the unchanging God of miracles, accessed only by faith — Mormon and Moroni preaching the identical doctrine, father and son.
- **3 Ne 27:27; 1 John 3:2-3** — "we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is... purified even as he is pure" (7:48); the end of charity is to become like Christ — the same "even as I am" of 3 Ne 27.
- **Moroni 10:32-33** — "come unto Christ... and love God with all your might... that ye may be perfected in him"; the charity Mormon preaches here is what Moroni's promise points toward.

## Notable

Yes — Mormon's great sermon, the capstone teaching on faith, hope, and charity, and the chapter is dense with load-bearing doctrine. **The Light of Christ is given to every man to know good from evil, as plain as daylight from dark** (7:15-16); **the test for any decision — does it invite to do good and to believe in Christ? then it is of God** (7:16-17, with the devil who "persuadeth no man to do good, no, not one"); **judge rightly, lest with that same judgment ye be judged — discernment, not condemnation** (7:14, 7:18); **faith lays hold on every good thing, and by faith miracles are wrought** — never ceasing except by unbelief (7:20-37); **the ladder: faith produces hope produces charity, all rooted in meekness** (7:40-44); and the summit — **charity is the pure love of Christ, bestowed not achieved, prayed for to be filled with**, ending in being "purified even as he is pure" (7:47-48).
