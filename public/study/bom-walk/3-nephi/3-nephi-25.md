# 3 Nephi 25 — Malachi 4: the same day that burns the stubble is the dawn that heals; Elijah and the turning of hearts

**Binding idea:** The close of Malachi, given by Christ to the Nephites. One day, two destinies: "the day cometh that shall burn as an oven; and all the proud, yea, and all that do wickedly, shall be stubble... it shall leave them neither root nor branch" (25:1) — "But unto you that fear my name, shall the Son of Righteousness arise with healing in his wings; and ye shall go forth and grow up as calves in the stall" (25:2). The same day is annihilation to the proud and sunrise-with-healing to those who fear His name. Then: "Remember ye the law of Moses, my servant, which I commanded unto him in Horeb" (25:4), and the prophecy that grounds temple and family work — "Behold, I will send you Elijah the prophet before the coming of the great and dreadful day of the Lord; and he shall turn the heart of the fathers to the children, and the heart of the children to their fathers, lest I come and smite the earth with a curse" (25:5-6).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 25 (full, verbatim via gospel_get) alongside Malachi 4 (KJV, via gospel_get) for the variant comparison. 6 verses. The final words of the Old Testament prophets, supplied by Christ.

## Thoughts

The whole chapter turns on one fact that's easy to read past: verses 1 and 2 describe *the same day*. "The day cometh that shall burn as an oven" — and to the proud and the wicked, that day burns them to stubble, "neither root nor branch." But "unto you that fear my name," that very same day is when "the Son of Righteousness" arises "with healing in his wings." It is not two different days, one for the wicked and one for the righteous. It is one day — the day of the Lord's coming — and what it is depends entirely on what you are. To the proud it is the oven; to those who fear His name it is the dawn. The chapter heading calls it "the great and dreadful day" (v5), and the doubling is exact: *great* for one, *dreadful* for the other, the same day wearing both faces. This is the direct continuation of the refiner's fire from chapter 24:2-3. There the fire purified the silver and burned the dross; here the same fire is healing to those who fear His name and ash to those who do not. The fire is constant; what differs is what there is in a person to burn. For the wicked, all there is is dross, and the fire leaves "neither root nor branch." For the faithful, the fire is the sunrise after a long night, and they "go forth and grow up as calves in the stall."

That last image is worth slowing on, because it's a picture of pure released joy. "Calves in the stall" — or as the KJV has it, "calves of the stall" — are penned animals; the image is of the stall door thrown open and the calves bursting out, leaping and kicking in the open field after confinement. The day that terrifies the wicked is, for those who fear God, the day the door opens and they run free. The contrast could not be sharper: the proud reduced to ash under the soles of feet (v3), the faithful leaping like released calves. Both responses to one event. The day of the Lord is not neutral; it is the great revealer, sorting all of humanity by the single question of verse 2 — do you fear His name?

Verse 2 also carries what is probably the most-discussed difference between the Book of Mormon and the King James text, and it's worth real attention. The KJV reads "the *Sun* of righteousness" — the sun, the heavenly body, rising at dawn with healing in its rays. The Hebrew is *shemesh*, sun; the image is the sunrise after night, the warmth and light spreading like outstretched wings (or the rays themselves as "wings"). But 3 Nephi reads "the *Son* of Righteousness" — capital S, the title of a person. Now, the Christian tradition had long read Malachi's "Sun of righteousness" as a figure of Christ (the *sol iustitiae*, the dawn from on high of Luke 1:78). What 3 Nephi does is make explicit, as a title, what was implicit as an image: the dawn *is* the Son. And the stunning thing about the setting is that **Christ Himself is the one reading it**. He stands before the Nephites and reads them the prophecy that "the Son of Righteousness" will arise with healing in his wings — He is naming Himself in the prophecy He is dictating. The risen Lord, the resurrected Son, reads the Nephites the promise of the Son who rises with healing, and He is that Son, arisen, standing in front of them with the healing in His hands (3 Ne 17:7-9, where He had just healed all their sick). The image and the reality are in the same room. (The "wings" image also runs deep — *kanaf* in Hebrew is both "wing" and the "border/hem" of a garment, and the woman who was healed by touching the hem of Christ's garment, Matt 9:20, may be living out exactly this: healing in the *wings*/borders of the Son of Righteousness.)

Verse 4 is a quiet hinge that's easy to overlook: "Remember ye the law of Moses, my servant, which I commanded unto him in Horeb." This is striking because Christ has *already*, earlier in His Nephite ministry, declared the law of Moses fulfilled in Him: "in me is the law of Moses fulfilled" (3 Ne 15:4-5). So why read them "remember the law of Moses"? Because fulfilled is not the same as despised. The Nephites are in the hinge moment — the old covenant fulfilled, the new begun — and Christ does not want them to throw away the law as though it had been worthless. It was His own law, given at Horeb, the schoolmaster that pointed to Him (Gal 3:24). Remember where you came from. The statutes and judgments did their work; they brought Israel to Christ; and now that Christ has come, the law is fulfilled in Him, but its memory is to be honored, not erased. There's a maturity in this — the fulfillment of a thing does not require contempt for it. The law is kept by being remembered even as it is transcended.

And then the close — the final words of the Old Testament prophets, the verses that ground the entire work of temples and family history. "I will send you Elijah the prophet before the coming of the great and dreadful day of the Lord; and he shall turn the heart of the fathers to the children, and the heart of the children to their fathers, lest I come and smite the earth with a curse." Two things land hard here. First, *what Elijah does*: he turns hearts across generations — fathers toward children, children toward fathers — the welding of the family chain across death. For Latter-day Saints this is the sealing power, restored when Elijah appeared in the Kirtland temple on April 3, 1836 (D&C 110:13-16), and the substance of Moroni's first words to Joseph Smith (D&C 2, JS-H 1:38-39). The turning of hearts between the generations is the binding of families forever. Second, and this is the part that always stops me: *why* — "lest I come and smite the earth with a curse." Without the turning of hearts, without the welding of the generations, the Lord's coming would smite the *whole earth* with a curse. The sealing of families across death is not a peripheral comfort; it is so central to the purpose of the earth that, absent it, the entire earth would be "utterly wasted at his coming" (the D&C 2 wording). The family bond is the thing that justifies the earth's existence. The whole creation hangs on whether hearts turn between the generations. And that — not judgment, not fire — is the *last word* of the Old Testament prophets. Malachi 4 ends with the editorial note "The End of the Prophets." The final note of the old covenant is not wrath but family: hearts turning, generations welded, the earth spared because love reaches across death.

There's a fittingness to Christ giving the Nephites *this* as the close of the prophetic record He's supplying. He has spent two chapters tending the record — auditing it (ch 23), supplying what they lacked (ch 24-25). And the very last words He gives them from the prophets are about the turning of hearts and the keeping of the generations. The record itself is an act of turning hearts across generations — Mormon to us, the fathers' words preserved for the children. The book that the resurrected Christ is so carefully completing is, in its deepest purpose, an instrument of exactly what Malachi's last verse describes: hearts turned across time, the fathers speaking to the children, lest the earth be cursed. The Book of Mormon *is* Elijah's work in book form.

## Likening (the four questions)

1. **Which face will that day wear for me?** The day is one day; great and dreadful are the same date. The question is not "will the day come?" but "what am I, when it does?" — stubble with no root, or one who fears His name and runs out like a released calf into the dawn. The likening is uncomfortably direct: the day sorts by a single criterion, and I am being sorted by it now, by whether I fear His name now.

2. **Have I let the Son of Righteousness rise on me — is there healing in His wings for what I carry?** The promise is healing, to those who fear His name. The woman touched the border of His garment and was healed. The likening asks whether I have actually come close enough to the Son of Righteousness to be healed, or only admired the dawn from a distance.

3. **Do I despise what was fulfilled, or remember it?** "Remember ye the law of Moses." The mature posture toward a thing transcended is honor, not contempt — the schoolmaster that brought me to Christ is not to be thrown away once Christ has come. Where am I tempted to despise the lesser thing that did its work and brought me here?

4. **Are my hearts turned — toward the fathers, toward the children?** Elijah's whole work, and the reason the earth is spared, is the turning of hearts between generations. The likening is concrete: do I know my fathers? Am I turned toward my children? Family history and temple work are not hobbies — they are the thing that, Malachi says, keeps the earth from being cursed. My heart's posture toward the generations is load-bearing.

## BoM-vs-KJV variant notes

Like the rest of the Malachi block, 3 Nephi 25 tracks the KJV Malachi 4 closely — but it contains the single most theologically significant variant in the whole quotation:

- **v2 — "Son of Righteousness" (3 Ne) vs "Sun of righteousness" (KJV).** This is the standout. The KJV's *Sun* (the heavenly body, Hebrew *shemesh*, the literal dawn-image) becomes 3 Nephi's *Son* (capital S, the Christological title). The Christian tradition had already read the KJV "Sun of righteousness" as Christ (the *sol iustitiae*); 3 Nephi makes the reading explicit as a *title*. Whether one reads this as the Book of Mormon clarifying the intended referent or as Joseph Smith's translation reflecting the long-standing Christological reading, the effect is the same: the dawn is named as the Son, and — uniquely — the Son Himself is the one reading the prophecy aloud, naming Himself in it. This is the most consequential single-word variant in the Nephite Malachi.
- **v2 — "calves in the stall" (3 Ne) vs "calves of the stall" (KJV).** Minor preposition shift; "in" slightly localizes, "of" slightly characterizes. Negligible doctrinally.
- **v6 — identical to the KJV.** Worth marking precisely because it is *not* changed: the famous *other* variant of Malachi 4:5-6 — Moroni's rendering to Joseph Smith ("plant in the hearts of the children the promises made to the fathers... if it were not so, the whole earth would be utterly wasted," JS-H 1:38-39 / D&C 2) — does *not* appear here. The resurrected Christ quotes Malachi 4:6 in its plain KJV form ("turn the heart of the fathers to the children... lest I come and smite the earth with a curse"). So three forms of this prophecy coexist in the canon: the KJV and 3 Nephi (identical to each other) and the angelic JS-H/D&C 2 rendering (expanded). The 3 Nephi text holds to the received form.

The cumulative tilt of the Malachi block (chs 24-25) matches the meta-finding from the Isaiah blocks: near-verbatim fidelity to the KJV, with the few variants pointing *Christ-ward* — here the dawn explicitly named as the Son. Christ "expounded them" (24:1) but the exposition isn't recorded; the near-verbatim text is what we're given, with the Son-of-Righteousness clarification as the one luminous change.

## Notable

Yes — Malachi 4, the close of the prophets, given by Christ. **One day, two destinies — the same fire burns and heals** (25:1-2, great and dreadful are the same day, sorted by who fears His name); **"the Son of Righteousness arise with healing in his wings"** (25:2 — the KJV "Sun"→"Son" variant, the risen Son reading the prophecy that names Himself, healing in the room where He'd healed their sick); **the faithful grow up as released calves** (25:2-3, the day of the Lord as leaping joy for the righteous, ash for the proud); **"remember the law of Moses" — fulfilled but not despised** (25:4); and **Elijah turns the hearts of the generations lest the earth be cursed** (25:5-6 — the sealing power, the family welded across death so central that without it the whole earth would be smitten; the last word of the prophets is family, not wrath).
