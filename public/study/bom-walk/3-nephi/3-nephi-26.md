# 3 Nephi 26 — the lesser part written to try faith; the babes utter marvelous things; the church established

**Binding idea:** Christ "expound[s] all things... even from the beginning until the time that he should come in his glory... even unto the great and last day" (26:3-4) — the whole sweep of history, creation to consummation to judgment, taught in three days. But Mormon can write "even a hundredth part" of it (26:6); the fuller record is on "the plates of Nephi" (26:7); what we have is "a lesser part" (26:8). And the reason is the chapter's load-bearing doctrine: the lesser is given first "to try their faith, and if it shall so be that they shall believe these things then shall the greater things be made manifest unto them. And if it so be that they will not believe these things, then shall the greater things be withheld from them, unto their condemnation" (26:9-10). "I was about to write them... but the Lord forbade it, saying: I will try the faith of my people" (26:11). Then the tender reversal — Christ "did loose [the children's] tongues, and they did speak unto their fathers great and marvelous things, even greater than he had revealed unto the people" (26:14); "babes did open their mouths and utter marvelous things" (26:16). And the church begins: the disciples baptize, the baptized "were filled with the Holy Ghost" (26:17), "they had all things common among them, every man dealing justly" (26:19), and "they who were baptized in the name of Jesus were called the church of Christ" (26:21).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 26 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 21 verses. The hinge from Christ's personal ministry to the established Church — and Mormon's clearest statement of why we get only the lesser part.

## Thoughts

The chapter opens on the largest possible canvas. Having given the Nephites Malachi, Christ "did expound all things unto them, both great and small" — and the scope is total: "from the beginning until the time that he should come in his glory... even until the elements should melt with fervent heat, and the earth should be wrapt together as a scroll, and the heavens and the earth should pass away," all the way to "the great and last day, when all people, and all kindreds, and all nations and tongues shall stand before God, to be judged of their works." The whole arc of cosmic history — creation, the course of the world, the dissolution of the elements, the final judgment — taught in three days, by the One "who was before the world began." That last phrase (v5) quietly names His premortality: the One expounding all of history from beginning to end is the One who preceded it, the premortal Christ, which is why He can narrate the whole of it. He doesn't expound history as a scholar reconstructs it; He expounds it as its Author, from before its first page.

And then verse 2 explains why He had given them Malachi at all, and it's worth catching: "These scriptures, which ye had not with you, the Father commanded that I should give unto you; for it was wisdom in him that they should be given unto future generations." The Malachi chapters weren't given primarily for the Nephites who heard them — they were given "for... future generations." The Father is building the record deliberately, with us in view. This is the third chapter running on the theme of Christ tending the record (auditing it in 23, supplying it in 24-25), and here the motive is named outright: the record is being assembled, under the Father's command, for readers not yet born. The care taken with the record is care taken with us.

But the doctrinal heart of the chapter is verses 6-11, and it answers a question that hangs over the entire Book of Mormon: why do we get so little? Christ taught for three days and expounded all things from beginning to end — and Mormon can preserve "even a hundredth part." Where did the rest go? The answer is not that it was lost; it's that it was *withheld on purpose*. "I was about to write them, all which were engraven upon the plates of Nephi, but the Lord forbade it, saying: I will try the faith of my people." The lesser part — what we hold — is "expedient that they should have first, to try their faith." And the mechanism is exact: "if it shall so be that they shall believe these things then shall the greater things be made manifest unto them. And if it so be that they will not believe these things, then shall the greater things be withheld from them." Belief in the lesser is the *key* to the greater. The withholding is not God hoarding the good material; it is the same principle by which all revelation has always come — "unto him that receiveth I will give more; and from them that shall say, We have enough... shall be taken away even that which they have" (2 Ne 28:30); "to him that hath, more shall be given" (Matt 13:12); the mysteries given "according to the heed and diligence which they give unto him" (Alma 12:9). The greater things are not a reward dangled for good behavior; they are simply what opens to a heart that has proven it will receive. Faith in what you have been given is the only door to what you have not. The Book of Mormon itself is the test: receive *this* — the lesser part, brought "from the Gentiles" — believe it, and the sealed greater portion (the brother of Jared's full vision, Ether 4:7) opens; reject it, and the greater stays sealed "unto their condemnation." We are, right now, taking the exam that decides whether the rest is unsealed. The hundredth part in our hands is the entrance examination for the other ninety-nine.

There's something bracing about that for anyone who wishes the scriptures said more, answered every question, left no gap. The gaps are not an accident or a tragedy of lost plates — they are the design. God could have given the whole. He withheld it deliberately, because a faith that requires the whole before it will believe any is not faith. The person who says "show me the greater things and then I'll believe" has the logic exactly backwards: the greater things come *after* belief in the lesser, not before. The withholding is mercy in the sense that it protects the very thing — faith — that the greater things require. To hand the greater portion to an unbelieving heart would be to cast pearls before a posture that cannot receive them (Matt 7:6).

Then comes the reversal that I find almost the most moving thing in the chapter, set right against all this talk of withholding. The greater things were withheld from the *adults* — but Christ "did loose [the children's] tongues, and they did speak unto their fathers great and marvelous things, *even greater than he had revealed unto the people*." The babes "did open their mouths and utter marvelous things." The very revelation withheld from the believing adults flowed freely through the children — and what the children uttered was so far beyond the adults that it was "forbidden that there should not any man write them." These are the same children Christ had set in the midst, encircled with fire, and prayed over until His joy was full (3 Ne 17:11-24). Now they are the revelators. The least become the channel of the greatest. It is "out of the mouth of babes" (Matt 11:25, the footnote) made literal — the children who could not yet have reasoned their way to deep doctrine become the vessels through which the things "greater than he had revealed unto the people" are spoken to their own fathers. The reversal is the whole gospel in miniature: the kingdom belongs to those who become as little children (3 Ne 11:37-38; Matt 18:3), and here the little children are the ones who carry the kingdom's deepest things. The fathers, who had just been told the greater would be withheld until their faith proved out, hear the greater anyway — from the mouths of their own babes. Mercy finds a way around the test even as the test stands.

And verse 18 marks a threshold worth naming: "many of them saw and heard unspeakable things, which are not lawful to be written." This is Paul's experience exactly — "caught up into paradise, and heard unspeakable words, which it is not lawful for a man to utter" (2 Cor 12:4). There is a category of the sacred that is not for the page at all — not because it is secret in the conspiratorial sense, but because some things lose rather than gain by being written, and some experiences are given to be lived, not transcribed. The record, so carefully tended through these chapters, has an edge it does not cross. Even a perfect record-keeper writes only what is lawful to write.

The chapter closes by stepping down from the mountain of revelation into the ordinary, durable work of the church (vv17-21). The disciples "began from that time forth to baptize and to teach"; "as many as were baptized in the name of Jesus were filled with the Holy Ghost"; "they had all things common among them, every man dealing justly, one with another"; "they did do all things even as Jesus had commanded them"; and "they who were baptized in the name of Jesus were called the church of Christ." This is the hinge from Christ's personal three-day ministry to the institution that will carry it forward — and the institution is built on baptism, the Holy Ghost, consecration ("all things common"), justice between people, and the name of Christ. That last detail matters: the church is named after Him (the very issue He'll address head-on in the next chapter, 3 Ne 27). And "all things common among them" is the seed of the golden age — the consecrated Zion society that 4 Nephi will describe in full, where "they had all things common among them; therefore there were not rich and poor" (4 Ne 1:3). The marvelous things and unspeakable visions give way to the plain economics of a just people, and the plainness is not a comedown — it is the marvelous made daily. A people who have seen the heavens opened come down the mountain and deal justly with one another and hold all things common. That is what the revelation was *for*.

## Notable

Yes — the hinge chapter, and Mormon's clearest "why so little." **Christ expounds all things from the beginning to the last day** (26:1-5, the whole cosmic arc by its premortal Author); **the record given for future generations by the Father's command** (26:2); **the lesser part given first to try faith — belief unlocks the greater, unbelief withholds it** (26:6-11, the load-bearing doctrine; the Book of Mormon as the entrance exam for the sealed portion; the gaps are design); **out of the mouth of babes — the children become revelators** (26:14, 16, the withheld revelation flows through the children, greater than the adults received; mercy around the test); **unspeakable things not lawful to be written** (26:18, the threshold the record won't cross); and **the church of Christ established — baptism, Holy Ghost, all things common** (26:17-21, the seed of 4 Nephi's Zion, the marvelous made daily).
