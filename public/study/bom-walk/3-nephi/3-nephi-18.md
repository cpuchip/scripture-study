# 3 Nephi 18 — always remember me, and ye shall have my Spirit: the sacrament and the watch

**Binding idea:** Christ institutes the sacrament. He breaks and blesses bread, gives it to the disciples and the multitude until "they had eaten and were filled" (18:4), and names its meaning: "this shall ye do in remembrance of my body... it shall be a testimony unto the Father that ye do always remember me. And if ye do always remember me ye shall have my Spirit to be with you" (18:7) — repeated for the wine, "in remembrance of my blood" (18:11). The faithful doer is "built upon my rock"; "whoso among you shall do more or less than these" builds on sand (18:12-13). Then the charges: "ye must watch and pray always, lest ye be tempted by the devil... for Satan desireth to have you, that he may sift you as wheat" (18:15, 18); "Pray in your families unto the Father" (18:21); "hold up your light that it may shine unto the world. Behold I am the light which ye shall hold up" (18:24); forbid the unworthy from the sacrament, "nevertheless, ye shall not cast him out from among you, but ye shall minister unto him... ye shall be the means of bringing salvation unto them" (18:30-32). He touches the disciples "one by one" giving them power "to give the Holy Ghost" (18:36-37), then is overshadowed by a cloud and ascends.

## Thoughts

The meaning of the sacrament is stated twice, identically, once for the bread and once for the wine, and the repetition is the point: "it shall be a testimony unto the Father that ye do always remember me. And if ye do always remember me ye shall have my Spirit to be with you" (18:7, 11). The ordinance has a purpose and a promise. The purpose is *remembering* — the sacrament is the weekly act by which I testify to the Father that I "always remember" Christ. And the promise that hangs on it is His Spirit: "if ye do always remember me ye shall have my Spirit to be with you." This is the entire "remember, remember" theme of the whole walk (Helaman 5:9-12, the cure for the forgetting that destroys) crystallized into an ordinance. The Nephites kept falling because they *forgot* the moment they were comfortable; the sacrament is the deliberate, repeated, embodied antidote — a regular act that re-anchors memory before it can drift. And the conditional is precise: the companionship of the Spirit is tied to always remembering. Not occasional remembering, not Sunday remembering — *always*. The bread and wine are the weekly renewal of a continuous remembering, and the fruit of that continuous remembering is the continuous presence of the Spirit. The whole point is to make the remembering stick, because the Spirit stays with the heart that doesn't forget. I lose the Spirit not by some dramatic sin but, usually, by simply forgetting — and the sacrament exists to interrupt the forgetting every single week.

The charge to "watch and pray always" (vv15-18) is grounded in a vivid and sobering reason: "Satan desireth to have you, that he may sift you as wheat." This is the same warning Christ gave Peter (Luke 22:31), and the image is precise — sifting wheat means violent shaking to separate the grain from the chaff, the adversary wanting to shake a soul hard enough to scatter it. And the defense against being "led away captive" is not heroic resistance but constant prayer: "ye must watch and pray always." Not pray when tempted, but pray *always*, because the watching is continuous — the adversary doesn't keep office hours, so neither can the prayer. The "always" recurs through the chapter — always remember, always pray, always in my name — and it builds a picture of discipleship as a continuous posture rather than a series of discrete acts. The Christian life is not a set of religious moments punctuating a secular life; it's a sustained watchfulness and a sustained remembering and a sustained prayer, because the threat is sustained. Satan "desireth to have you" — present tense, ongoing — so the remembering and praying must be ongoing too. And v21 brings it home to the most important place: "Pray in your families unto the Father, always in my name, that your wives and your children may be blessed." The watch is kept first in the home; the family that prays together is the family blessed and guarded, and that's where the continuous prayer begins.

Verse 24 contains one of the most freeing statements about discipleship in the chapter: "hold up your light that it may shine unto the world. Behold I am the light which ye shall hold up—that which ye have seen me do." The disciples are commanded to be a light to the world (12:14) — but here Christ clarifies *whose* light it is. "I am the light which ye shall hold up." They don't generate the light; they hold up His. The job is not to be impressive, not to shine with one's own brilliance, but to hold Christ up where the world can see Him — "that which ye have seen me do." This is the same deflection Nephi made in Helaman 9 ("I know nothing save it were given me by the power of God") and the same economy as the secret-worship of chapter 13 (don't shine to be seen). The disciple's light is borrowed, reflected — the moon, not the sun. And that's liberating, because it removes the pressure of having to be the light myself. I'm not asked to dazzle the world with my own goodness; I'm asked to hold up Christ, to point past myself to Him, to do what I've seen Him do. The light that shines through a faithful disciple is never his own; it's Christ held up high enough for others to see. The failure mode is to start holding up *myself* — my insight, my righteousness, my works — instead of Him; the whole commission is to keep Him, not me, lifted where the world is looking.

The worthiness teaching in verses 28-32 holds a balance I find pastorally profound. On one side, the sacrament must be guarded: "ye shall not suffer any one knowingly to partake of my flesh and blood unworthily... for whoso eateth and drinketh my flesh and blood unworthily eateth and drinketh damnation to his soul" (1 Corinthians 11:27-29). The ordinance is sacred, and to partake of it in unrepentant sin is to harm oneself, so the unworthy are to be forbidden *the sacrament*. But then, immediately, the other side: "Nevertheless, ye shall not cast him out from among you, but ye shall minister unto him and shall pray for him... ye shall not cast him out of your synagogues, or your places of worship, for unto such shall ye continue to minister; for ye know not but what they will return and repent... and I shall heal them; and ye shall be the means of bringing salvation unto them" (vv30-32). The distinction is everything: forbid the unworthy from the *ordinance*, but never cast them out of the *fellowship*. The sacrament is protected; the person is kept close. And the reason is hope — "ye know not but what they will return and repent." You don't expel the struggling sinner, because the whole point of keeping them in the fellowship is to be "the means of bringing salvation unto them." This guards against two opposite failures: cheapening the sacrament by letting anyone partake regardless of state, and abandoning the sinner by casting them out of the community where they might be healed. The church is to be a place where the unworthy are simultaneously kept from harming themselves at the sacrament table and kept near enough to be brought back. The ordinance has a guard; the door has none. That balance — sacred enough to protect the holy things, open enough to never lose a soul — is the exact pastoral wisdom the modern church still tries to live.

And there's the recurring note that ties it all together (v34): "I give you these commandments because of the disputations which have been among you. And blessed are ye if ye have no disputations among you." The whole body of instruction — the sacrament, the watching, the open meetings, the worthiness balance — is given, Christ says, *because of the disputations*. The contention He named as "of the devil" (11:29) is the disease these commandments treat. Order in worship, care in administering the sacrament, ministering to the struggling instead of casting them out — all of it is, in part, the cure for contention. A church that does these things rightly has no need to dispute, because the practices themselves embody the unity. And the chapter ends with the conferral of the Holy Ghost (vv36-37) — Christ touching the disciples "one by one" (the personal touch yet again) and giving them "power to give the Holy Ghost" — and then the cloud, and the ascension. The first day of His ministry among the Nephites closes with the means of His continuing presence put in place: the sacrament to renew the remembering, the prayer to keep the watch, the priesthood to give the Spirit. He ascends, but He has left them everything they need to keep Him with them — which is exactly what the sacrament promises: always remember me, and ye shall have my Spirit to be with you. He goes; the Spirit stays; the remembering is the bridge.

## Notable

Yes — the sacrament instituted and the great charges. **The sacrament — "always remember me, and ye shall have my Spirit"** (18:7, 11, the ordinance crystallizing the whole "remember" theme; the antidote to the forgetting that destroys; the Spirit tied to always-remembering); **watch and pray always — Satan desires to sift you as wheat** (18:15-18, continuous vigilance, constant prayer the defense); **pray in your families** (18:21); **"I am the light which ye shall hold up" — the borrowed light** (18:24, hold up Christ not yourself); and the pastoral balance — **forbid the unworthy from the sacrament but never cast out the person** (18:28-32, guard the ordinance, keep the door open, "be the means of bringing salvation unto them"). Discipleship as a continuous posture, not discrete acts (the recurring "always"); the commandments given as the cure for contention (18:34). The Holy Ghost conferred by the laying on of hands, one by one (18:36-37); the ascension in a cloud.
