# 3 Nephi 24 — Malachi 3: the messenger, the refiner's fire, "prove me," and the book of remembrance

**Binding idea:** Christ gives the Nephites a prophecy they never had. "He commanded them that they should write the words which the Father had given unto Malachi" (24:1) — Malachi prophesied c. 430 BC, long after Lehi left Jerusalem (c. 600 BC), so the Nephite record contained nothing of him. Just as in chapter 23 Christ corrected an *omission* in their record, here He supplies a prophecy they never could have had. The chapter is Malachi 3, nearly verbatim: the messenger who prepares the way and "the Lord whom ye seek" who "shall suddenly come to his temple, even the messenger of the covenant" (24:1); "who may abide the day of his coming?... he is like a refiner's fire" (24:2-3); the swift witness against sorcerers, adulterers, oath-breakers, and those who oppress the hireling, the widow, the fatherless, the stranger (24:5); "For I am the Lord, I change not; therefore ye sons of Jacob are not consumed" (24:6); "Return unto me and I will return unto you" (24:7); "Will a man rob God?... In tithes and offerings... bring ye all the tithes into the storehouse... and prove me now herewith... if I will not open you the windows of heaven" (24:8-10); and the tender close — "a book of remembrance was written before him for them that feared the Lord, and that thought upon his name... they shall be mine... when I make up my jewels; and I will spare them as a man spareth his own son that serveth him" (24:16-17).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 24 (full, verbatim via gospel_get) alongside Malachi 3 (KJV, via gospel_get) for the variant comparison. 18 verses. The prophecy the Nephites lacked, supplied and expounded by Christ.

## Thoughts

The first thing to feel about this chapter is *why* Christ gives it at all. Lehi's family carried the brass plates out of Jerusalem around 600 BC, and the brass plates contained the prophets down to Jeremiah. But Malachi prophesied roughly 430 BC — a century and a half *after* the Nephites left. They simply did not have him. So Christ does something He didn't do with the rest of His ministry: He dictates an entire prophetic book to be added to their record, word for word, "the words which the Father had given unto Malachi." This is the direct continuation of chapter 23. There, Christ *audited* the record and corrected an omission — a fulfilled prophecy that had been left out. Here, He *supplies* a prophecy that was never available to be included. Both motions complete the record: chapter 23 fixes what was wrongly left out, chapter 24 adds what could never have been put in. The risen Lord is curating His own scripture in both directions — correcting and supplying — so that the Nephite record will carry what the Father wanted His people to have. The record matters enough to Him that He gives it His direct attention, line by line.

And there is a quiet dramatic irony running under the whole chapter that makes it almost startling once you see it. Christ is reading aloud, to a people gathered *at the temple* in Bountiful (3 Ne 11:1), the prophecy that "the Lord, whom ye seek, shall suddenly come to his temple, even the messenger of the covenant." He is the Lord. He came suddenly. He came to the temple. The people hearing the prophecy are living its fulfillment in the very act of hearing it — the messenger of the covenant is reading them the prophecy of the messenger of the covenant coming to the temple, while standing in the temple court. But the prophecy is not exhausted by that moment; it reaches forward to the Second Coming, the day no one can abide, the refiner's fire, the judgment. So the chapter holds two horizons at once: Christ's first coming (already, suddenly, to the temple) and His second (still future, refining, judging). For the Nephites it was both memory and prophecy; for us it is all prophecy. The "messenger of the covenant" came once in mercy and will come again in fire.

The refiner's fire (vv2-3) is the image that governs the chapter's tone, and it is worth slowing on, because it reframes what the "day of his coming" is *for*. "Who may abide the day of his coming?... for he is like a refiner's fire, and like fuller's soap. And he shall sit as a refiner and purifier of silver... and purge them as gold and silver, that they may offer unto the Lord an offering in righteousness." The refiner's fire is not primarily destructive — it is *purifying*. The silversmith heats the ore not to annihilate it but to drive off the dross, and he watches the crucible until the surface goes mirror-bright and he can see his own face reflected in the metal. That is the point: the fire is severe, but its object is purity, not ruin. The end of the refining is "an offering in righteousness" — something pure enough to be offered. This connects straight back to chapter 22's everlasting kindness: the affliction is "a small moment," and even the affliction is doing the work of refining, burning out what cannot be offered. So the answer to "who may abide the day of his coming?" is not "no one" in despair — it is the refined, those willing to be purged, who will be left bright when the dross is gone. The fire that consumes the dross is the same fire that purifies the silver; whether it destroys or refines depends on what there is in you to burn.

Then the swift witness (v5), and what strikes me is the *list*. "I will be a swift witness against the sorcerers, and against the adulterers, and against false swearers" — those are the sins we expect on a prophet's list, the religious-moral offenses. But the list does not stop there: "and against those that oppress the hireling in his wages, the widow and the fatherless, and that turn aside the stranger, and fear not me." The judgment sweeps from sorcery and adultery straight into *economic and social* oppression — cheating the hired worker out of his wages, exploiting the widow and the orphan, turning aside the immigrant. God puts wage-theft and oppression of the vulnerable in the same breath as sorcery and adultery, as things He will be a "swift witness" against. The root of all of it is named at the end: "and fear not me." Every item on the list — the occult sin, the sexual sin, the false oath, the exploitation of the powerless — flows from not fearing God. The person who truly fears God does not cheat his workers or prey on widows, because he knows there is a Witness. (The variant here is worth marking: where the KJV reads "turn aside the stranger *from his right*," 3 Nephi reads simply "turn aside the stranger" — slightly broadening it from a legal wrong to any turning-aside of the outsider. More below.)

Verse 6 is the theological hinge, and it is pure mercy disguised as a statement about God's nature: "For I am the Lord, I change not; therefore ye sons of Jacob are not consumed." Notice the logic. God's *unchangingness* is given as the reason His people are *not destroyed*. We might expect immutability to be a threat — an unchanging God who will not bend, will not relent. Malachi says the opposite: because God does not change, His *covenant* does not change, His promises to Jacob hold, and that is precisely why Jacob is "not consumed." The same God who has every reason to consume a people who "are gone away from mine ordinances" (v7) does not, *because* He is unchanging — and what is unchanging in Him is His covenant faithfulness. This is the deepest comfort in the chapter, and it sits right next to the refiner's fire on purpose: the One who refines is the One who will not change toward His people, so the fire is safe — it purifies, it does not annihilate, because the God wielding it cannot stop being faithful. The immutability of God is not the wall we run into; it is the floor we stand on.

The famous tithing passage (vv8-12) carries something I had not weighed properly before: it contains the one place in all of scripture where God *invites* being put to the test. "Bring ye all the tithes into the storehouse... and prove me now herewith, saith the Lord of Hosts, if I will not open you the windows of heaven." Everywhere else the rule is "thou shalt not tempt the Lord thy God" (Deut 6:16; Matt 4:7) — do not put God to the proof. Here, uniquely, God says *prove me* — test me, and watch what I do. And the test is tithing. The structure is the prosperity-covenant that has run through the entire Book of Mormon (keep the commandments and prosper, 1 Ne 2:20; 2 Ne 1:20; the whole Nephite cycle), now narrowed to one specific commandment: bring the tithes, and the windows of heaven open. The image of the "windows of heaven" is deliberately drawn from the flood (Gen 7:11, the footnote's cross-reference — "the windows of heaven were opened" to pour judgment-water on the earth). Malachi inverts it: the same windows that once opened to drown the earth in judgment now open to pour out blessing "that there shall not be room enough to receive it." The flood of wrath becomes a flood of blessing. And the blessing is paired with protection — "I will rebuke the devourer for your sakes" — so that what is given is also kept. Robbing God brings a curse on "this whole nation" (v9); giving God His portion opens the windows. It is the prosperity-covenant in its most concrete, testable form — and the one commandment God stakes His own reputation on, inviting the proof.

But the chapter's true treasure, and the reason its placement after chapter 23 is so striking, is the book of remembrance (vv16-18). Set the scene: the people have been saying "It is vain to serve God... what doth it profit that we have kept his ordinances?" (v14) — the cynicism of the faithful watching the wicked prosper, "we call the proud happy" (v15). Against that backdrop of discouragement, there is a remnant: "Then they that feared the Lord spake often one to another; and the Lord hearkened and heard." That is already tender — God *leans in and listens* to the ordinary conversations of those who fear Him, the way they encourage each other when serving God feels vain. And then: "a book of remembrance was written before him for them that feared the Lord, and that thought upon his name." Here is the stunning continuity with chapter 23. There, Christ audited the *earthly* record and required it be true and complete. Here, God keeps a *heavenly* record — a book of remembrance — of those who fear Him and think on His name. The reason the earthly record must be accurate is that there is a heavenly record being kept with perfect accuracy. God Himself is a record-keeper. He hears the quiet conversations of the faithful and writes them down. And what those written-down ones become is almost too tender: "they shall be mine, saith the Lord of Hosts, in that day when I make up my jewels" — His treasured possession, His *segullah* — "and I will spare them as a man spareth his own son that serveth him." The refiner's fire of verse 2 and the spared son of verse 17 are the same people seen from two ends: refined in the fire, then gathered up as jewels, then spared as a beloved child. The day no one can abide is, for those written in the book of remembrance, the day they are made up as jewels. The fire and the tenderness are one sequence.

## Likening (the four questions)

1. **What does "prove me now herewith" ask of me personally?** It asks for a specific, testable obedience — not general goodwill toward God but the concrete act (the tithe) by which I let God demonstrate His faithfulness. The invitation is unique: God almost never says "test me," but on this point He does. The likening is direct — am I willing to give God His portion *first*, before I can see the windows open, trusting the one commandment He stakes His reputation on?

2. **Where is the dross the refiner means to burn out of me?** The fire is not coming for my destruction but for my purity — "that they may offer an offering in righteousness." The honest question is not "will I survive the day of his coming?" but "what in me is dross, and am I willing to let it be purged so that what remains can be offered?" The fire's severity is mercy aimed at making me offerable.

3. **Do I fear God enough to be just to the powerless?** The swift-witness list puts wage-theft, and the oppression of the widow, the orphan, and the stranger, in the same breath as sorcery and adultery — and roots all of it in "fear not me." The likening: my treatment of the people with the least power over me (the worker I could underpay, the outsider I could turn aside) is the truest measure of whether I actually fear God, because there is a Witness even when no human one is watching.

4. **Am I one of those who "spake often one to another," written in the book of remembrance?** When serving God feels vain and the proud look happy, the remnant's practice is to "speak often one to another" — to encourage one another in the fear of the Lord — and God leans in to listen and writes them down. The likening: do I have those conversations? Am I, with others who fear Him, building the quiet record that God Himself is keeping?

## BoM-vs-KJV variant notes

3 Nephi 24 tracks the KJV Malachi 3 very closely — like the Isaiah blocks (2 Ne 12-24, 3 Ne 22) and the Sermon (3 Ne 12-14), the quotation is near-verbatim, differing mostly in punctuation and the capitalization "Lord of Hosts" (3 Ne) vs "Lord of hosts" (KJV). The substantive variants are few and small, but several point the same direction:

- **v1 — added framing.** 3 Nephi prefaces the prophecy with "Thus said the Father unto Malachi—," absent in the KJV. This is Christ's own attribution: He is careful to mark these as the *Father's* words given to Malachi, not His own — consistent with His habit throughout 3 Nephi of pointing to the Father (3 Ne 11:32-36).
- **v5 — "from his right" omitted.** KJV: "turn aside the stranger *from his right*." 3 Nephi: "turn aside the stranger." The KJV phrase makes it a specifically *legal* wrong (denying the stranger his legal due); 3 Nephi broadens it to any turning-aside of the outsider — slightly more general, slightly more about the heart's posture toward the stranger than a courtroom right.
- **v7 — tense.** KJV "ye said, Wherein shall we return?"; 3 Nephi "ye say." Present tense — the defensiveness is ongoing, not a past episode.
- **v13 — "so much" omitted.** KJV: "What have we spoken *so much* against thee?"; 3 Nephi: "What have we spoken against thee?" Marginally sharpens the denial.
- **v14 — "ordinances" plural.** KJV: "we have kept his *ordinance*" (singular); 3 Nephi: "his *ordinances*" (plural). Broadens from a single ordinance to the whole body of them.

None is doctrinally large. The cumulative tilt — like the meta-finding from the 2 Nephi Isaiah blocks (judgment-passages leaning toward mercy) — is mild *generalization*: the stranger's wrong broadened beyond the legal, the ordinances pluralized. Christ's expounding (24:1, "he expounded them") is not recorded in the text; we get the words but not His commentary, which makes the close fidelity to Malachi itself the point — He let the prophecy stand nearly as given.
