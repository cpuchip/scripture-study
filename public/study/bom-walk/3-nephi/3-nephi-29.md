# 3 Nephi 29 — the coming-forth of the record is the sign; do not deny revelation, do not mock Israel

**Binding idea:** Mormon turns directly to the latter-day reader holding his book. "When the Lord shall see fit... that these sayings shall come unto the Gentiles according to his word, then ye may know that the covenant which the Father hath made with the children of Israel, concerning their restoration to the lands of their inheritance, is already beginning to be fulfilled" (29:1). The Book of Mormon's coming-forth is *itself* the sign that the gathering of Israel has begun. Therefore: "ye need not say that the Lord delays his coming" (29:2); "ye need not any longer spurn at the doings of the Lord, for the sword of his justice is in his right hand" (29:4); "wo unto him that shall deny the revelations of the Lord, and that shall say the Lord no longer worketh by revelation, or by prophecy, or by gifts, or by tongues, or by healings, or by the power of the Holy Ghost!" (29:6); and "ye need not any longer hiss, nor spurn, nor make game of the Jews, nor any of the remnant of the house of Israel; for behold, the Lord remembereth his covenant unto them" (29:8).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 29 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 9 verses. Mormon's direct address to the latter-day Gentile reader.

## Thoughts

The whole chapter is written in the second person to a reader who would not be born for sixteen centuries. That frame is the first thing to feel. Mormon, abridging in the fourth century, knowing his own people are doomed (he has just grieved the fourth generation with Christ in chapter 27), turns from the Nephite story and speaks straight to *us* — the Gentile readers who would one day hold the finished book. And what he tells us is what the book in our hands *means*. It is not merely a religious history or a second witness of Christ, though it is both. It is a *sign*. "When... these sayings shall come unto the Gentiles... then ye may know that the covenant which the Father hath made with the children of Israel, concerning their restoration... is already beginning to be fulfilled." The very fact that you are reading this is evidence that the gathering of Israel has commenced. The Book of Mormon is not just a message about the covenant; its arrival in the world is the covenant going into motion. To hold the book is to hold the proof that God has begun to keep His ancient promise.

That reframes the book entirely. We tend to read it for its content — its doctrine, its testimony of Christ, its narratives. Mormon says: before you even open it to the content, notice the *fact* of it. A record buried for fourteen centuries, brought forth among the Gentiles "according to his word" — that the prophecy of its coming-forth (2 Ne 30, 2 Ne 27) was *fulfilled* is itself a fulfilled prophecy, and a fulfilled prophecy is a witness (the same point chapter 23 made about Samuel's prophecy). So the existence of the book argues for the truth of the book. The Lord said it would come forth to the Gentiles; it did; therefore "ye need not say that the Lord delays his coming," and "ye need not imagine in your hearts that the words which have been spoken are vain." The arrival of the record is God's down payment on every other promise He has made.

Then Mormon turns from the comfort to the warning, and the warning has three specific edges that are striking for how directly they hit our own moment. The first: "ye need not any longer spurn at the doings of the Lord, for the sword of his justice is in his right hand." When you see the book come forth, do not despise it — judgment is poised. The second is the one I find most pointed across the centuries (vv5-7): "wo unto him that shall deny the revelations of the Lord, and that shall say the Lord no longer worketh by revelation, or by prophecy, or by gifts, or by tongues, or by healings, or by the power of the Holy Ghost!" This is Mormon naming, in advance, the precise error of cessationism — the belief that the age of revelation and miracles and spiritual gifts has closed, that God once spoke and worked wonders but no longer does. Mormon (and his son Moroni after him, and Mormon again in Mormon 9) treats this as one of the gravest latter-day errors, because it makes God a changed God — and an unchanging God (Mal 3:6, which Christ just quoted in 3 Ne 24:6) cannot have stopped doing what He has always done. If God worked by revelation and gifts and healings in every prior dispensation, and God changes not, then to say He no longer does is to deny His nature. The Restoration's whole foundation is the reversal of exactly this denial: the heavens are *not* closed; God *does* still speak; the gifts *have* returned. Mormon saw the denial coming and pronounced a woe on it before it arrived.

And he sharpens it further (v7): "wo unto him that shall say at that day, *to get gain*, that there can be no miracle wrought by Jesus Christ; for he that doeth this shall become like unto the son of perdition." The denial of miracles is tied to *priestcraft* — saying it "to get gain." There is a kind of religious teaching that profits from a domesticated God, a God safely confined to the past, who makes no present demands and works no present wonders, because such a God is easier to package and sell. To deny the living, working, miracle-doing Christ for profit is, Mormon says, to become "like unto the son of perdition, for whom there was no mercy." That is the harshest comparison available — and it is reserved not for the honest doubter but for the one who denies Christ's power *for gain*, knowing better. The sin is not weakness of faith; it is the calculated, profitable denial of what one has reason to know is true.

The third edge of the warning is the one that reaches furthest beyond its own time (vv8-9): "ye need not any longer hiss, nor spurn, nor make game of the Jews, nor any of the remnant of the house of Israel; for behold, the Lord remembereth his covenant unto them." A Nephite prophet, writing in roughly AD 385, warns the Gentile reader against *contempt for the Jews*. Given the eighteen centuries of Christian contempt, persecution, and worse that would follow — the "hissing" and "making game" of the Jews that became a settled habit of Christendom — the warning is almost unbearably prescient. Mormon's ground for it is not tolerance as a modern value; it is the *covenant*. The Jews and the scattered remnant of Israel are not cast off; "the Lord remembereth his covenant unto them, and he will do unto them according to that which he hath sworn." To mock the Jews is to mock the people God has covenanted to gather — to set oneself against the very thing the coming-forth of this book signals is beginning. And the chapter's final word makes the covenant irreversible: "ye need not suppose that ye can turn the right hand of the Lord unto the left, that he may not execute judgment unto the fulfilling of the covenant which he hath made unto the house of Israel." No human contempt, no Gentile presumption, can deflect God's right hand from keeping His word to Israel. The covenant will be fulfilled. The only question for the Gentile reader is whether they will stand with it or against it.

There's a unity to the three warnings worth naming: each is a form of saying "God has stopped." The Lord delays His coming — God has stopped acting. He no longer works by revelation or miracles — God has stopped speaking. The Jews are cast off — God has stopped covenant-keeping. And the coming-forth of the book refutes all three at once, because the book is the living evidence that God has *not* stopped — He is acting, speaking, and keeping covenant, all in the very fact of the record arriving in the reader's hands. Mormon's whole exhortation is: the thing you are holding proves God has not stopped. Do not live, or teach, or sneer as though He had.

## Notable

Yes — Mormon's direct address to the latter-day reader. **The coming-forth of the record is the sign the covenant is fulfilling** (29:1-3, the book's arrival is itself evidence the gathering of Israel has begun; a fulfilled prophecy is a witness); **wo to cessationism — the God who changes not still works by revelation and gifts** (29:5-6, Mormon names the denial of continuing revelation/miracles in advance as a grave error); **denying miracles to get gain is priestcraft, likened to the son of perdition** (29:7); **do not mock the Jews or Israel — the covenant stands** (29:8, a prophet's prescient warning against Gentile contempt for the covenant people); **no one can turn the Lord's right hand — the covenant is irreversible** (29:9); and the unifying theme — **the record proves God has not stopped acting, speaking, or covenant-keeping** (each warning refutes a form of "God has stopped").
