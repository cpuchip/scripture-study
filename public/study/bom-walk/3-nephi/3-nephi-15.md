# 3 Nephi 15 — "I am he that gave the law": the Lawgiver of Sinai standing among them, and the other sheep

**Binding idea:** Christ resolves the marveling of those who "understood not the saying that old things had passed away" (15:2). He explains the law of Moses precisely: "the law is fulfilled that was given unto Moses. Behold, I am he that gave the law, and I am he who covenanted with my people Israel; therefore, the law in me is fulfilled... it hath an end" (15:4-5). But the *covenant* continues — "the covenant which I have made with my people is not all fulfilled; but the law which was given unto Moses hath an end in me" (15:8) — and "I am the law, and the light. Look unto me, and endure to the end, and ye shall live" (15:9). Then He reveals the Nephites' identity in the larger flock: "ye are they of whom I said: Other sheep I have which are not of this fold; them also I must bring, and they shall hear my voice; and there shall be one fold, and one shepherd" (15:21). The Jews at Jerusalem "understood me not, for they supposed it had been the Gentiles" (15:22); the Nephites were "separated from among them because of their iniquity" (15:19), and "ye have both heard my voice, and seen me; and ye are my sheep" (15:24).

## Thoughts

The single most important sentence in this chapter, and one of the clearest in all scripture for the question of who Christ is, is verse 5: "I am he that gave the law, and I am he who covenanted with my people Israel." The risen Christ, standing among the Nephites, identifies Himself as the God who gave the law to Moses at Sinai and who made the covenants with ancient Israel — that is, He identifies Himself as Jehovah, the LORD of the Old Testament. The footnote points exactly there: "Jesus Christ, Jehovah." This is the answer to the question Michael and I worked in "Is Jesus Christ God?" stated in the most direct possible terms by Christ Himself. The God who thundered from Sinai, who said "I AM THAT I AM," who covenanted with Abraham and led Israel out of Egypt and gave the Ten Commandments — that God is the Man whose wounds they just touched. The Lawgiver and the Lamb are one Person. This collapses any notion that the OT God of law and the NT Christ of grace are different beings; they are the same Being, and He says so. The God of the burning bush is the God of the manger, the God of the cross, and the God who descended to Bountiful. "I am he that gave the law" is Christ claiming the entire history of Israel's God as His own, because it was His own.

The chapter draws a precise and important distinction that's easy to blur: the *law* of Moses has ended in Christ, but the *covenant* has not. "The law in me is fulfilled... it hath an end" (v5), yet "the covenant which I have made with my people is not all fulfilled" (v8). So when Christ said "old things have passed away, and all things have become new" (12:47), He did *not* mean that all prophecy was now complete or that nothing of the old remains. He meant specifically that the law of Moses — the system of performances and ordinances that pointed forward to His sacrifice — has done its work and ended, while the *covenant* (the promises about the gathering of Israel, the future restoration, the things "which are to come," v7) still stands and awaits fulfillment. This matters because it guards against two errors: thinking the law of Moses is still binding (it isn't — it ended in Christ), and thinking the old covenant promises are void (they aren't — they're still being fulfilled, the gathering of Israel chief among them). The law was the temporary schoolmaster; the covenant is the enduring relationship. Christ ended the one and is still keeping the other. The performances are done; the promises are not.

And then verse 9 makes the most beautiful move: "Behold, I am the law, and the light. Look unto me, and endure to the end, and ye shall live." The law isn't merely fulfilled and discarded — it's *replaced by a Person*. Christ doesn't say "here is a new and better law to keep"; He says "I am the law." The whole point of the law of Moses was always to bring people to Him; it was a pointer, a tutor, a finger directing the eye toward the One who would come. Now He has come, and so the pointer gives way to the thing it pointed at. "Look unto me" — the same gaze of the brazen serpent (8:14-15), the look that heals — replaces "keep the statutes." This is the deepest meaning of the fulfillment: religion is no longer fundamentally about keeping a code; it's about looking to a Person. The law could only ever describe righteousness from the outside; Christ *is* righteousness, and the way to live is not to master the description but to look to Him and endure. A code can be obeyed at arm's length; a Person must be looked to, followed, loved. The shift from "keep the law" to "look unto me" is the shift from religion-as-rule-keeping to religion-as-relationship — which is exactly the "I never knew you" warning of the last chapter from the other side. The law became flesh, and now the flesh is the law.

The "other sheep" revelation (vv15-24) opens Christ's flock to a scope the Jews could not imagine. At Jerusalem He had said, "other sheep I have which are not of this fold; them also I must bring, and they shall hear my voice; and there shall be one fold, and one shepherd" (John 10:16) — and the Jews "understood me not, for they supposed it had been the Gentiles" (v22). They assumed "other sheep" meant Gentile converts who would be brought in *through Jewish preaching*. But Christ corrects: the other sheep were not the Gentiles (who would hear Him only "by the Holy Ghost," not by His voice directly, v23) — the other sheep were these, the Nephites, "a remnant of the house of Joseph," whom He came to in person, who "have both heard my voice, and seen me." The Shepherd's flock is not confined to one hemisphere or one people. He has sheep the Jerusalem fold never knew existed, scattered across the earth — the Nephites, the lost tribes, "whom the Father hath led away out of the land" (v15) — and He ministers to all of them. The vision is "one fold, and one shepherd" — not many folds, not many shepherds, but one flock spanning the whole earth under one Lord, who personally visits each portion of it. This is the Restoration's expansive ecclesiology: the God who came to Jerusalem also came to Bountiful, and will gather "the other tribes" too, until all His scattered sheep are one fold. The most moving thing in it is that He came *in person* to the other sheep — He didn't delegate the Nephites to a lesser revelation; He descended and let them touch His wounds, exactly as He would have done at Jerusalem. The other sheep were not second-class; they heard His voice and saw Him, the same as the first fold.

There's a sober note threaded through it too (vv18-20): the reason the Jerusalem flock didn't know about the other sheep was their own "stiffneckedness and unbelief" — "ye were separated from among them because of their iniquity; therefore it is because of their iniquity that they know not of you." The scattering of Israel was both a judgment on the unfaithful and a providential preservation of the faithful elsewhere. Lehi's family was led away *because of* the iniquity at Jerusalem — the separation that produced the Nephites was, from one angle, a rescue, and from another, a consequence of the wickedness they left behind. And the Jerusalem Jews, in their unbelief, couldn't even receive the knowledge that other sheep existed; Christ "was commanded to say no more of the Father concerning this thing unto them" because they couldn't bear it (v18, echoing "I have yet many things to say unto you, but ye cannot bear them now," John 16:12). Iniquity doesn't just incur judgment; it shrinks the capacity to receive truth. The unbelieving heart can't be told certain things, not because God withholds arbitrarily but because the hardened heart cannot hold them. The other sheep stayed hidden from Jerusalem partly because Jerusalem had made itself unable to hear about them. And the comfort on the other side is total: the scattered sheep, whom the proud center had forgotten or never known, were never forgotten by the Shepherd. He knew exactly where every sheep was, came to them in person, and is gathering them all toward the one fold.
