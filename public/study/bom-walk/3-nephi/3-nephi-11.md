# 3 Nephi 11 — He descended, and they felt the prints one by one: the heart of the book

**Binding idea:** The risen Christ descends. The gathered multitude at the temple in Bountiful hear "a small voice" three times — "it did pierce them to the very soul, and did cause their hearts to burn" — and understand it only the third time, when they "open their ears" and "look steadfastly towards heaven" (11:3-6). The Father introduces the Son: "Behold my Beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased, in whom I have glorified my name—hear ye him" (11:7). Christ descends "clothed in a white robe," declares "I am Jesus Christ... I have drunk out of that bitter cup which the Father hath given me, and have glorified the Father in taking upon me the sins of the world" (11:10-11), and invites the whole multitude — "Arise and come forth unto me, that ye may thrust your hands into my side, and also that ye may feel the prints of the nails in my hands and in my feet, that ye may know that I am the God of Israel, and the God of the whole earth, and have been slain for the sins of the world" (11:14). They go forth "one by one" (11:15), feel the wounds, and cry "Hosanna!" He gives the authority and exact mode of baptism, declares "there shall be no disputations among you," names contention as "of the devil, who is the father of contention" (11:29), and delivers the doctrine of Christ — believe, repent, be baptized, become as a little child — "and whoso buildeth upon this buildeth upon my rock, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against them" (11:39).

## Thoughts

This is the chapter the entire Book of Mormon has been moving toward — the title page promises a record "to the convincing of the Jew and Gentile that JESUS is the CHRIST," and here He is, in person, risen, descending from heaven to a waiting multitude. Everything before this — the prophets who testified of Him for six hundred years, the signs of His birth and death, the voice in the darkness — was prologue to this descent. And the way Mormon tells it is unhurried and physical and astonishingly tender, which is itself a kind of testimony: this is not myth or vision but a Man descending, standing, speaking, holding out His hands to be touched. The God of the whole earth came down and let two and a half thousand people, one at a time, put their fingers in His wounds. If I want to know what the Book of Mormon is *for*, it's for this chapter: to put the risen Christ, bodily and personally, in front of every reader, and let them touch the prints.

The way the voice comes, in verses 3-6, teaches something about how God speaks before it teaches anything else. "It was not a harsh voice, neither was it a loud voice; nevertheless... it being a small voice it did pierce them that did hear to the center... it did pierce them to the very soul, and did cause their hearts to burn." This is the still small voice again (1 Kings 19, Helaman 5:30) — the most important voice in the universe is the quiet one, and its power is not in volume but in penetration; it pierces to the soul and burns in the heart. But the detail I most want to keep is that they didn't understand it the first two times. "They heard the voice, and they understood it not." Only "the third time they did open their ears to hear it; and their eyes were towards the sound thereof; and they did look steadfastly towards heaven" — and *then* "they did understand." The voice was speaking the whole time; what changed was the hearers. They had to stop, open their ears, and look steadfastly toward heaven before the words became intelligible. Revelation, even the Father's own voice from heaven, requires the receiver to attune — to quiet down, turn toward the source, and attend with the whole self. The Father didn't speak louder the third time; the people listened harder. How often I've missed the voice not because it wasn't speaking but because I wasn't yet opening my ears and looking steadfastly toward where it came from.

And then the Father's words, verse 7: "Behold my Beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased, in whom I have glorified my name—hear ye him." These are the same words spoken at Christ's baptism (Matthew 3:17), at the Transfiguration (Matthew 17:5), and in the First Vision (JS–H 1:17). Every time the Father speaks audibly in scripture, He says essentially the same thing: *that is my Son; hear Him.* The Father's role, again and again, is to direct all attention to the Son. He doesn't speak to glorify Himself; He speaks to point to Christ. There's a whole theology of the Father in that pattern — the Father's glory is bound up in the Son's, "in whom I have glorified my name," and His consistent message to humanity is not "look at me" but "hear ye him." When the most important voice in existence finally speaks, it spends its words pointing past itself to the Son.

The center of the chapter, and one of the most moving scenes in all of scripture, is the invitation to touch the wounds (vv14-15). "Arise and come forth unto me, that ye may thrust your hands into my side, and also that ye may feel the prints of the nails in my hands and in my feet." And then: "the multitude went forth... going forth one by one until they had all gone forth, and did see with their eyes and did feel with their hands." *One by one.* Twenty-five hundred people (3 Nephi 17:25), each individually, each taking a turn, while the risen Lord of heaven and earth stood and let every single one of them put their hands in His side and feel the nail-prints in His hands and feet. Consider the patience of that — the condescension of it. He did not address them as a faceless crowd; He gave each person a personal, physical, undeniable encounter with His resurrected body. The God of the whole earth made Himself touchable, and waited, one by one, through every last person. This is how Christ relates to souls: not as a mass to be impressed but as individuals to be met, each given their own witness, each known. And the wounds themselves are the deepest thing here. He is *risen*, glorified, perfected — and He kept the wounds. The marks of the crucifixion remain in the resurrected, immortal body, His permanent and eternal identification: "that ye may know that I am the God of Israel... and have been slain for the sins of the world." The risen Christ will bear the prints of His sacrifice forever. The Atonement is not something He did and moved past; it is written into His glorified flesh for eternity, the one thing He carried out of the tomb. When I see Him, I will know Him by the wounds. And the wounds are simultaneously the proof of His divinity ("the God of Israel") and of His suffering ("slain for the sins of the world") — the same marks declare both that He is God and that this God bled for me.

The first doctrinal thing the risen Christ teaches His people, before the Sermon, before everything, is that contention is of the devil (vv28-30). "There shall be no disputations among you... he that hath the spirit of contention is not of me, but is of the devil, who is the father of contention, and he stirreth up the hearts of men to contend with anger, one with another. Behold, this is not my doctrine, to stir up the hearts of men with anger... but this is my doctrine, that such things should be done away." Notice what He targets: not merely the content of disputes but the *spirit of contention* itself — the impulse to contend with anger. Satan is "the father of contention"; the very disposition to quarrel angrily, regardless of who's right, is from him. This is striking as the risen Lord's opening concern. He doesn't first correct their theology or their politics; He first uproots the spirit of contention, "as there have hitherto been... disputations among you concerning the points of my doctrine." Even disputing about *doctrine* — even arguing about Him — can be the devil's work if it's done in the spirit of contention. The truth can be contended for in a way that is itself satanic. This lands directly on how I engage: it is possible to be right about doctrine and wrong in spirit, to defend the gospel in the father-of-contention's own voice. The risen Christ's first instruction is that the spirit in which I hold the truth matters as much as the truth I hold, and that the angry, quarreling spirit is never His, even in service of His doctrine.

And then the doctrine of Christ itself (vv31-41), given pure and witnessed by the whole Godhead — "I bear record of the Father, and the Father beareth record of me, and the Holy Ghost beareth record of the Father and me." Three witnesses to one doctrine. And the doctrine is stunningly simple: "the Father commandeth all men, everywhere, to repent and believe in me"; "whoso believeth in me, and is baptized, the same shall be saved"; "ye must repent, and become as a little child, and be baptized in my name." Believe, repent, become as a little child, be baptized, receive the Holy Ghost ("he will visit him with fire and with the Holy Ghost"). That's it. That's the rock. "Whoso buildeth upon this buildeth upon my rock, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against them." And the warning: "whoso shall declare more or less than this, and establish it for my doctrine, the same cometh of evil... buildeth upon a sandy foundation." The doctrine is to be neither added to nor subtracted from — both addition and subtraction come "of evil." There's a profound restraint in that: the gospel's power is in its simplicity, and the temptation in every age is to complicate it (declare *more*) or to dilute it (declare *less*), and both miss the rock. The pure doctrine — faith, repentance, baptism, the Holy Ghost, the childlike heart — is the whole foundation, witnessed by Father, Son, and Holy Ghost, against which the gates of hell cannot prevail. After all the destruction, all the darkness, all the centuries of prophecy, the risen Lord stands among His people and gives them the one thing that matters, plainly: believe in me, repent, be baptized, become as a little child, and you are built on the rock. The heart of the book is the heart of the gospel, and it could not be simpler.

## Notable

Yes — **the heart of the entire Book of Mormon**, the descent of the risen Christ. **The Father always points to the Son — "hear ye him"** (11:7, the same words as the baptism/Transfiguration/First Vision); **the still small voice understood only when the hearer attunes** (11:3-6, they opened their ears and looked steadfastly); **the resurrected Christ keeps the wounds — the marks of the Atonement are eternal** (11:14-15, written into His glorified flesh forever, declaring both His divinity and His suffering); **Christ meets souls one by one, not as a mass** (11:15, 2,500 people each personally touching the prints — the patience and condescension); **contention is of the devil, the father of contention** (11:28-30, the risen Lord's first doctrinal concern — the *spirit* of contention, even about doctrine, is satanic); **the doctrine of Christ, the rock** — believe, repent, be baptized, become as a little child (11:31-39); **declare more or less and it comes of evil** (11:40, neither add nor subtract); and **the Godhead are one** (11:27, 36 — central to the "Is Jesus Christ God?" study). The mode of baptism given exact (11:23-27).
