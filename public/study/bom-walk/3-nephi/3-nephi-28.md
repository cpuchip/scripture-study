# 3 Nephi 28 — the heart's desire; the three Nephites; the doctrine of translation; the chain of oneness

**Binding idea:** Christ asks each disciple, one by one, "What is it that ye desire of me, after that I am gone to the Father?" (28:1). Nine ask the blessed, natural thing — that after living "unto the age of man" their ministry would end and they would "speedily come unto thee in thy kingdom" (28:2); He grants it ("after that ye are seventy and two years old ye shall come unto me," 28:3). But three "durst not speak unto him the thing which they desired" (28:5) — they desired what John the Beloved desired: to tarry, to "never taste of death," to "bring the souls of men unto me, while the world shall stand" (28:9). And Christ calls this "more blessed" (28:7). To them He promises "fulness of joy... ye shall be even as I am, and I am even as the Father; and the Father and I are one; and the Holy Ghost beareth record of the Father and me" (28:10-11). They are caught up into heaven, "saw and heard unspeakable things," and undergo what "did seem unto them like a transfiguration" (28:13-15). And Mormon, who first wrote "whether they were mortal or immortal... I know not" (28:17), later records: "since I wrote, I have inquired of the Lord, and he hath made it manifest unto me that there must needs be a change wrought upon their bodies" — the doctrine of translation: a change "that they might not taste of death... that they might not suffer pain nor sorrow save it were for the sins of the world... that Satan could have no power over them... and they were sanctified in the flesh" (28:36-39).

## Thoughts

The chapter begins with the most revealing question a person can be asked: "What is it that ye desire of me?" Christ asks it of each disciple individually, "one by one," at the threshold of His departure — and what each one answers exposes the shape of his heart. The question is the same one He asked Solomon (2 Chr 1:7) and will ask in the latter days (D&C 7); it is the question that, more than any test of works, reveals what a person actually loves. And the answers divide. Nine ask for the blessed and entirely good thing: that after a full life their labor would end and they would "speedily come unto thee in thy kingdom" — rest, reunion, the natural longing of a faithful servant for home. Christ grants it without reproach: at seventy-two they will come to Him and "find rest." There is nothing wrong with that desire. It is holy. But three could not bring themselves to speak what they wanted — "they sorrowed in their hearts, for they durst not speak unto him the thing which they desired." Their desire felt too presumptuous to voice. And Christ, knowing their thoughts, names it for them: they desired what John the Beloved desired (John 21:22) — to tarry, never to taste death, to keep bringing souls to Christ "while the world shall stand." And He calls it "more blessed."

What makes the three's desire "more blessed" is worth sitting with, because it could be misread as a ranking of the disciples themselves. It isn't — the nine are blessed, fully. The difference is in the *direction* of the desire. The nine desired rest; the three desired more labor. The nine wanted to come home soon; the three wanted to stay and work longer, to deny themselves the rest they had earned in order to keep gathering souls. The "more blessed" is the desire that wants to give more rather than receive sooner — the consecrating impulse that asks not "when may I rest?" but "how long may I serve?" Both desires are honored; the Lord grants each man exactly what his heart asked. But the chapter quietly marks the higher love as the one that wants to keep pouring out. It is the same shape as the Savior's own ministry — He who "went about doing good" until the cross, who could have come down and did not. The three want to be like that, and He blesses them to be like that, for the longest time imaginable: until He comes again.

And then verses 10-11 open the deepest thing in the chapter, and it reaches straight into the question of who Christ is. The promise to the three is "fulness of joy" — and Christ defines that fulness by a chain: "your joy shall be full, even as the Father hath given me fulness of joy; *and ye shall be even as I am, and I am even as the Father; and the Father and I are one; and the Holy Ghost beareth record of the Father and me.*" Read the chain carefully. The disciples are to become *as Christ is*. Christ is *as the Father is*. The Father and Christ *are one*. The Holy Ghost *witnesses* both. This is the doctrine of becoming from 27:27 ("even as I am") now extended to its full theology: the path of the disciple runs *into* the oneness of the Godhead. The end of discipleship is not standing near God but being made like Christ, who is like the Father, who is one with Him. And the verse does double work — it is both a statement about the *Godhead's* unity ("the Father and I are one," exactly the language of 3 Ne 11:27 and 2 Ne 31:21) and about the *disciple's incorporation* into that unity ("ye shall be even as I am"). The same oneness that binds the Father and the Son is the oneness the sanctified disciple is brought into. (This is a significant datapoint for the *is-jesus-christ-god* study: Christ here places Himself in the chain of identity with the Father — "I am even as the Father; and the Father and I are one" — while also opening that likeness to the disciple. The oneness is real and shared, not a collapse of persons: the Holy Ghost "beareth record of the Father and me," distinguishing the three even in their unity.)

The translation itself (vv12-15) is described with a careful reverence. Christ "touched every one of them with his finger" — sealing the promise — "save it were the three who were to tarry," whose different destiny required no such touch. Then "the heavens were opened, and they were caught up into heaven, and saw and heard unspeakable things" — Paul's experience again (2 Cor 12:4), and the third time in three chapters that the sacred runs past the edge of what may be written (3 Ne 26:18; 27:23). "Whether they were in the body or out of the body, they could not tell; for it did seem unto them like a transfiguration of them, that they were changed from this body of flesh into an immortal state, that they could behold the things of God." This is the same necessity Moses described — "his glory was upon me; and I beheld his face, for I was transfigured before him... for no man can behold all the glory of God, and afterwards remain in the flesh on the earth" (Moses 1:11). To behold the things of God, a mortal body must be changed, even temporarily. The transfiguration is not a reward; it is the *enabling condition* of seeing God and surviving it.

Then the deliverances (vv18-23), and the allusions are unmistakable and deliberate. The three are cast into prison and the prisons "were rent in twain"; cast into the earth and delivered by smiting it "with the word of God"; "thrice they were cast into a furnace and received no harm" — Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego in Nebuchadnezzar's fiery furnace (Dan 3); "twice were they cast into a den of wild beasts; and behold they did play with the beasts as a child with a suckling lamb" — Daniel in the lions' den (Dan 6). Mormon is reaching for the OT's great deliverance-types on purpose: the furnace and the lions' den, the two iconic images of God preserving His servants intact through what should kill them, are gathered up and multiplied in the three Nephites. The translated beings are invulnerable in exactly the ways Daniel's friends and Daniel himself were — and the image of playing with wild beasts "as a child with a suckling lamb" reaches forward to Isaiah's peaceable kingdom (Isa 11:6), the wolf with the lamb, where the curse of enmity between man and beast is lifted. The three live, already, in a body the last day will give everyone.

But the part of this chapter I find quietly remarkable is Mormon's own conduct as a record-keeper (vv17, 36-40), because it embodies the very discipline these closing chapters keep circling. In verse 17 Mormon writes honestly: "whether they were mortal or immortal, from the day of their transfiguration, I know not." He does not pretend to knowledge he lacks. He marks the uncertainty *in the record*. And then — verses 36-37 — he tells us what he did about it: "as I spake concerning those whom the Lord hath chosen... that I knew not whether they were cleansed from mortality to immortality— But behold, since I wrote, I have inquired of the Lord, and he hath made it manifest unto me." He didn't leave the gap; he inquired; he got the answer; and he transparently updated the record, showing the seam — "since I wrote." This is exactly the posture chapter 23 dramatized when Christ audited the record and Nephi corrected the omission: a found gap, acknowledged honestly, then filled by inquiry, with the correction shown rather than hidden. Mormon is modeling, in the text itself, intellectual and spiritual honesty — say what you don't know, ask, and correct openly when light comes. A prophet who writes "I know not" and then "I have inquired" is teaching, by example, that the honest record does not paper over its own gaps. It is the same standard this whole project runs on: don't claim what you haven't verified; when you find you were uncertain, go to the source; show the correction.

And the answer Mormon receives is the doctrine of translation, stated precisely (vv38-40). The change wrought on the three was so "that they might not taste of death... might not suffer pain nor sorrow save it were for the sins of the world" — the one sorrow they retain is the holy one, grief over sin, the same exception the Savior named for His own joy (3 Ne 27:32). "Satan could have no power over them, that he could not tempt them; and they were sanctified in the flesh, that they were holy." But Mormon is careful to mark the limit: "this change was not equal to that which shall take place at the last day." Translation is not resurrection. It is an intermediate, sanctified state — beyond mortality's pain and Satan's reach, but still awaiting "a greater change" at the judgment day, when they will "be received into the kingdom of the Father to go no more out." The precision matters: the Book of Mormon does not blur translation into resurrection. It names it as its own category — a holy, deathless, untemptable state that nonetheless still looks forward to the final change. Even the most exalted mortal condition is not the end; it is a longer service ending in the same glory promised to all the faithful.
