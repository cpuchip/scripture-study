# 3 Nephi 9 — the same voice says "wo" and "that I may heal you": Christ speaks out of the dark

**Binding idea:** Out of the three-day darkness, the voice of the still-unseen Christ is heard "among all the inhabitants." It begins in judgment — "Wo, wo, wo unto this people" — and names the destroyed cities, each burned or sunk or buried "to hide their iniquities... that the blood of the prophets and the saints shall not come any more unto me against them" (9:2-11). Then, in a single breath, the voice turns from judgment to the tenderest invitation in the book: "O all ye that are spared because ye were more righteous than they, will ye not now return unto me, and repent of your sins, and be converted, that I may heal you?... mine arm of mercy is extended towards you, and whosoever will come, him will I receive" (9:13-14). He declares His full divinity: "I am Jesus Christ the Son of God. I created the heavens and the earth... I was with the Father from the beginning... I am the light and the life of the world. I am Alpha and Omega" (9:15-18). And He announces the new covenant: "your sacrifices and your burnt offerings shall be done away... ye shall offer for a sacrifice unto me a broken heart and a contrite spirit" (9:19-20).

## Thoughts

The structure of this chapter is its deepest teaching: the same voice that pronounces the threefold "wo" and recounts the burning of the cities turns, without a pause, to "will ye not now return unto me... that I may heal you?" Judge and healer are one Person, and the turn between them is instantaneous. The voice that destroyed Jacobugath is the voice that pleads with the survivors. And the purpose of the call is not condemnation but *healing* — "that I may heal you," "mine arm of mercy is extended towards you, and whosoever will come, him will I receive." This is the hardest and most important thing to hold about God in the same hand: He is both the One who let the wicked cities fall and the One whose first words to the survivors are an offer of mercy and healing. We tend to want a God who is either all-justice or all-mercy, and 3 Nephi 9 refuses to let me have either. The same voice does both, in the same speech, and mercy gets the final and longer word. The judgment-voice was never the whole of Him; it was the prelude to the invitation. And the timing matters — He speaks in the *darkness*, before He is seen, before the light returns. The first thing the survivors hear from their God, in the blackest moment, is not "you deserved this" but "will ye not return, that I may heal you." Even the wo was in service of the healing — the destruction cleared away the wicked who would have killed the survivors too, and now the survivors, "spared because ye were more righteous," are called home. The arm of mercy is extended into the dark.

Verses 15-18 are the most comprehensive declaration of Christ's divinity in the Book of Mormon, and they come from His own mouth: "I am Jesus Christ the Son of God. I created the heavens and the earth, and all things that in them are. I was with the Father from the beginning. I am in the Father, and the Father in me; and in me hath the Father glorified his name... I am the light and the life of the world. I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the end." Read the claims. He is the *Creator* — "I created the heavens and the earth" (John 1:3, Colossians 1:16). He is *eternal* — "I was with the Father from the beginning." He is *one with the Father* — "I am in the Father, and the Father in me." He is the *light and life* of the world (John 8:12, 1:4). He is *Alpha and Omega*, beginning and end (Revelation 1:8). This is the answer to the question Michael and I worked in "Is Jesus Christ God?" — not inferred from prophets' testimony but spoken by Christ Himself, out of the darkness, after His death and resurrection: the full divinity, the Creator-God who was with the Father from the beginning. And it sits right beside "I came unto my own, and my own received me not" — the God who made everything came to His own creation and was rejected by it. The Creator of the heavens and the earth was stoned and crucified by the creatures He made. The vastness of the divinity claim and the humility of the rejection are held in adjacent verses, and the contrast is the gospel: the God who made all things submitted to being slain by the things He made, to redeem them.

The new covenant in verses 19-20 is one of the great hinge-points of all scripture: the end of animal sacrifice and its replacement by the broken heart. "Ye shall offer up unto me no more the shedding of blood; yea, your sacrifices and your burnt offerings shall be done away, for I will accept none... And ye shall offer for a sacrifice unto me a broken heart and a contrite spirit." For thousands of years, since Adam, the law of sacrifice had required the shedding of blood — the slain lamb pointing forward to the Lamb of God. Now the Lamb has been slain; the thing all the sacrifices pointed to has happened; and so the outward sacrifice is "done away" and replaced by the inward one. From this moment, the sacrifice God accepts is not the death of an animal but the breaking of a proud heart — "a broken heart and a contrite spirit" (Psalm 51:17). This is the deepest possible reframing of what "sacrifice" means. It was never really about the animal; the animal was a teaching device, a pointer. What God always wanted, and now requires directly, is the surrendered heart — the heart broken of its pride, contrite, willing to be remade. The blood-sacrifice ends not because sacrifice ends but because the *real* sacrifice it always symbolized — the offering up of my own will and pride — is now what's asked. And the gentleness of what follows is striking: "whoso cometh unto me with a broken heart and a contrite spirit, him will I baptize with fire and with the Holy Ghost, even as the Lamanites... were baptized with fire and with the Holy Ghost, and they knew it not." The fire comes to the broken heart — and it can come so gently that, like the Lamanites, you don't even know it happened. The baptism of fire is not always a dramatic burning; sometimes it's a quiet filling you only recognize in retrospect. The condition is the broken heart; the fire is God's response, and it may come unfelt.

And the close gathers it all into the simplest possible terms (vv17, 22): "as many as have received me, to them have I given to become the sons of God" (John 1:12) — receiving Christ is a new birth into sonship — and "whoso repenteth and cometh unto me as a little child, him will I receive, for of such is the kingdom of God." After the cosmic divinity claims, after the convulsion of the whole earth, the way in is to come "as a little child" (Mark 10:15) — in the humility, trust, and teachability of a child. The Creator of the heavens and the earth, Alpha and Omega, asks to be approached the way a child approaches a parent. And the reason He can ask it is in the last verse: "for such I have laid down my life, and have taken it up again" (John 10:18). The whole invitation rests on the resurrection — He can offer healing and eternal life and sonship because He has Himself died and risen. The voice in the darkness is the voice of a God who has already conquered death, speaking three days into a darkness that will lift when, on the other side of the world, He rises. "Repent, and come unto me ye ends of the earth, and be saved" — the universal call, from the Creator who became the crucified-and-risen, out of the dark, to all who will come as little children. It is the last thing the Nephites hear before, in the next chapters, the light returns and He descends among them in person.

## Notable

Yes — a peak chapter, the voice of the resurrected Christ before His descent. **The same voice pronounces the wo and extends the arm of mercy** (9:2-14 — judge and healer one Person, mercy the final word, spoken into the darkness: "that I may heal you"); **Christ's full divinity declared in His own voice** (9:15-18 — Creator, eternal, one with the Father, light and life, Alpha and Omega; the most comprehensive divinity claim in the book, central to the "Is Jesus Christ God?" study); **the broken heart and contrite spirit replace animal sacrifice** (9:19-20 — the great new-covenant hinge, the real sacrifice always the surrendered heart); **the baptism of fire can come unfelt** (9:20, the Lamanites "knew it not"); **come as a little child** (9:22); and **the Creator came to His own and was rejected by what He made** (9:15-16).
