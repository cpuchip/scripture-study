# 3 Nephi 13 — worship in secret, the heart that follows its treasure, and "take no thought" — to the twelve

**Binding idea:** The Sermon turns to the inward life of worship. Alms, prayer, and fasting are to be done "in secret," because "thy Father who seeth in secret, himself shall reward thee openly" (13:4, 6, 18) — while the hypocrites who do them "to be seen of men" already "have their reward" (13:2, 5, 16). Christ gives the Lord's Prayer (13:9-13), expands the one petition that conditions the rest — "if ye forgive men their trespasses your heavenly Father will also forgive you; but if ye forgive not... neither will your Father forgive" (13:14-15) — and teaches the heart's economy: "lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven... for where your treasure is, there will your heart be also" (13:20-21), "ye cannot serve God and Mammon" (13:24). Then the chapter's distinctive Nephite turn: "when Jesus had spoken these words he looked upon the twelve whom he had chosen, and said unto them... ye are they whom I have chosen to minister unto this people. Therefore I say unto you, take no thought for your life, what ye shall eat" (13:25) — the famous "take no thought" addressed not to the whole multitude but specifically to the called twelve, with "seek ye first the kingdom of God... and all these things shall be added unto you" (13:33).

## Thoughts

The first half of this chapter is about the economy of reward, and it's stated three times with the same structure — alms, prayer, fasting — to make sure it lands: the hypocrites who do righteous things "to be seen of men" already "have their reward" (13:2, 5, 16), while those who do them "in secret" are rewarded by the Father who "seeth in secret." The phrase "they have their reward" is quietly devastating. It doesn't say the hypocrites get *no* reward; it says they get *theirs* — the human applause they were after. They wanted to be seen, and they were seen; that was the whole transaction, and it's complete. They spent their righteousness to purchase admiration, and they got exactly what they paid for, with nothing left over for heaven. The choice is between two audiences and two paymasters: do it for people, and people's notice is your full and final wage; do it in secret, and God Himself becomes the rewarder. You cannot draw from both accounts — the moment the human audience pays you in applause, the divine account is settled at zero. This cuts at a temptation I know well, the wish to be *seen* doing the good thing, and Christ names it precisely: the seeing is the wage, and it's a small one. The reward of the unseen good is that God, who needs no audience, becomes your audience. The secret act has only one possible rewarder, and He is the one whose reward actually lasts.

Verse 21 is one of the most psychologically penetrating lines in scripture: "for where your treasure is, there will your heart be also." Notice the direction of the causation. We assume the heart comes first — that we invest in what we already love. Christ reverses it: the heart *follows* the treasure. Where you put your treasure is where your heart will *go*. This is both a diagnostic and a tool. As a diagnostic: if I want to know where my heart actually is, I don't consult my feelings — I look at where my treasure is, where my time and money and attention actually go, because my heart is wherever those are. But as a tool, it's even more useful, because if the heart follows the treasure, then I can *move my heart* by moving my treasure. I don't have to wait until I feel devoted to God to invest in His kingdom; I can lay up treasure in heaven — give, serve, consecrate — and the heart will follow it upward. The order is liberating: I'm not stuck waiting for my affections to change before I act; I act, I invest, and the affections follow the investment. Lay up treasure in heaven and your heart relocates to heaven. This is also the bright inverse of Helaman 13's slippery treasures: there, hearts set on earthly riches lost everything; here, treasure laid up in heaven draws the heart to where "neither moth nor rust doth corrupt."

The chapter's most important interpretive contribution, though, is the framing of "take no thought," and the Nephite version makes explicit what's ambiguous in Matthew. In Matthew 6:25, "take no thought for your life, what ye shall eat" reads as general counsel to everyone, and has caused genuine confusion — does Christ forbid all planning, all prudence, all saving for the future? But here, verse 25 sets the scene unmistakably: "when Jesus had spoken these words he looked upon the twelve whom he had chosen, and said unto them... ye are they whom I have chosen to minister unto this people. Therefore I say unto you, take no thought for your life." The "take no thought" is spoken *to the twelve*, the called ministers, as the charge of consecration. They are being asked to leave their livelihoods and give themselves wholly to the ministry, and the "take no thought" is the trust that vocation requires — the promise that if they consecrate themselves to the kingdom, God will provide their bread and clothing (the fowls, the lilies). This recontextualizes the whole passage. It is not a blanket prohibition on prudence for every disciple; it is the specific faith demanded of those called to full-time service, who must trust God for temporal needs precisely because they've surrendered the means of providing for themselves. The elders later sent "without purse or scrip" (D&C 84) are the same pattern. The principle generalizes — every disciple is to "seek first the kingdom" — but the radical "take no thought for the morrow" is, in its sharpest form, the missionary's and the minister's charge, the trust of those who have left all to serve. That distinction matters, because it frees the ordinary disciple from a false guilt about prudent planning while preserving the genuine radical call to those who are sent.

And the climax of the whole second half is verse 33, which reorders every priority: "seek ye first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things shall be added unto you." This is the resolution of the take-no-thought charge, and it's the principle that does generalize to everyone. The temporal needs — food, clothing, the morrow — are real, and the Father "knoweth that ye have need of all these things" (v32). But the ordering is everything: seek the kingdom *first*, and the rest is "added." Not "seek the kingdom and ignore the temporal," but "seek the kingdom first and trust that the temporal follows." The anxiety the chapter keeps naming — "what shall we eat? what shall we drink? wherewithal shall we be clothed?" — is dissolved not by securing the temporal first but by putting the kingdom first and letting God add the rest. The divided heart that "cannot serve God and Mammon" (v24) is healed by a single priority: the kingdom first. And the "single eye" of verse 22 is the same teaching in another image — the eye fixed singly on God's glory fills the whole body with light, while the divided gaze fills it with darkness. The whole chapter, from the secret alms to the take-no-thought, is one sustained call to undivided devotion: do the good for God's eyes alone, put your treasure where you want your heart to be, fix your eye singly on Him, serve one Master, seek the kingdom first — and the anxieties about everything else fall away, because a heart wholly given to God has nothing left to be anxious with.
