# 3 Nephi 7 — the dog to its vomit: a nation fragments, and one man's faith opens heaven daily

**Binding idea:** The chief judge is murdered and "they did destroy the government of the land" — the people "separate one from another into tribes, every man according to his family and his kindred and friends" (7:2). The republic dissolves into tribalism in a single year, "because of the secret combination" (7:6). And the speed of the relapse is named without mercy: "six years had not passed away since the more part of the people had turned from their righteousness, like the dog to his vomit, or like the sow to her wallowing in the mire" (7:8). The tribes patch together "some degree" of civil peace with strict laws — yet "their hearts were turned from the Lord their God, and they did stone the prophets" (7:14): order without righteousness. Into this anarchy steps Nephi, "having been visited by angels," grieved for the people's hardness, ministering "with power and with great authority" — and "they were angry with him, even because he had greater power than they, for it were not possible that they could disbelieve his words, for so great was his faith on the Lord Jesus Christ that angels did minister unto him daily" (7:18). He casts out devils, and "even his brother did he raise from the dead, after he had been stoned" (7:19).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 7 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 26 verses. The collapse into tribes; the relapse; Nephi's ministry and miracles.

## Thoughts

The political collapse in verses 1-6 follows directly from the spiritual collapse of the last chapter, and the order matters. In chapter 6 the inequality broke up the *church*; here the secret combination destroys the *government*, and the people "separate one from another into tribes, every man according to his family and his kindred and friends." When the larger unifying order fails — first the covenant community, then the civil one — people retreat to the smallest reliable loyalty: blood and kin. The nation doesn't just weaken; it *fragments*, dissolving into clan-tribes each with its own leader and laws. This is the deep human default when the center cannot hold: fall back to family, kindred, friends — to the tribe. And it's worth seeing as the political form of the spiritual disease. The church broke up over inequality; the body politic breaks up over the same loss of any binding loyalty higher than kinship. A people united by covenant can be one body across family lines; a people who've lost the covenant have nothing left to unite them but blood, and blood divides as easily as it bonds. The fragmentation into tribes is what's left when the things that made them one people — the faith, the law, the shared covenant — have all dissolved.

Verse 8 is the cruelest image in the chapter, and it's deliberately revolting: "six years had not passed away since the more part of the people had turned from their righteousness, like the dog to his vomit, or like the sow to her wallowing in the mire." This is the language of 2 Peter 2:22, and Peter draws the conclusion explicitly: "it had been better for them not to have known the way of righteousness, than, after they have known it, to turn from the holy commandment." The horror here is not merely that they fell, but that they *returned* — to the very wickedness they had repented of and been cleansed from, the way a dog goes back to eat what its own body rejected. There's a special wretchedness in the relapse that's worse than never having been clean, because the relapse despises the cleansing. They had been delivered (3 Ne 4), had wept tears of joy, had given all glory to God — and within six years they went back to it, like a washed sow returning to the mud. The image is meant to disgust me, and it should, because it's a mirror: every time I return to a sin I'd genuinely repented of, I am the dog and the vomit, the washed sow and the mire. The speed — six years — is the "space of not many years" theme at its sharpest, but the *vomit* image adds the dimension of revulsion that the bare speed lacks. It's not just fast; it's foul.

The most chilling diagnosis is verse 14: the tribes establish "very strict laws that one tribe should not trespass against another, insomuch that in some degree they had peace in the land; nevertheless, their hearts were turned from the Lord their God, and they did stone the prophets and did cast them out." Order without righteousness. They achieved a functional civil peace — laws, restraint, a workable inter-tribal stability — *while* stoning the prophets and rejecting God entirely. This is one of the most important and least comfortable observations in the chapter, because it shatters the assumption that social order and righteousness travel together. A society can be peaceful, law-abiding, and orderly, and simultaneously be killing the prophets and turned from God. Outward stability is not a measure of spiritual health; a people can have peace and law and still be spiritually dead. In some ways this is a *more* dangerous condition than open chaos, because the functioning order masks the inward apostasy — everything looks fine, the laws work, there's no war, and meanwhile the prophets are being stoned and the hearts are turned away. The lesson for any orderly, prosperous, peaceful society, including my own, is sobering: the presence of civil order proves nothing about the presence of righteousness. You can have a well-run society that is stoning its prophets.

Against all of this stands one man, and what's said of Nephi in verses 15-19 is staggering. "So great was his faith on the Lord Jesus Christ that angels did minister unto him daily." Daily. The heavens were continuously open to him — not in occasional crises but as a constant condition of his life, because his faith was that complete. This is the fruit of the "unwearyingness" God commended in the earlier Nephi (Helaman 10); a faith sustained without wavering opens heaven not once but continuously. And his power was so great that "it were not possible that they could disbelieve his words" — the evidence was undeniable. He cast out devils, healed, and "even his brother did he raise from the dead, after he had been stoned and suffered death by the people." He raised his own martyred brother. And here is the chapter's hardest truth about the human heart: faced with a man who raises the dead, "they were angry with him because of his power." Not converted — *angry*. When the evidence becomes impossible to deny, the hardened heart doesn't surrender; it turns to rage. You cannot argue with a man who raises the dead, so they hated him instead. This is the same pattern as Helaman 16:6 and 3 Nephi 1:22 — the undeniable miracle provokes anger rather than faith in a heart that has already chosen. The greatest possible evidence (resurrection of the dead, daily angelic ministry) produced, in most, not belief but fury. It confirms again that the obstacle was never insufficient evidence; it was a will already set against God. And it sets up the tragedy of the next chapter: a people who had every reason to believe — signs, prophets, a man among them who raised the dead and was visited by angels daily — and who, "but few" excepted, chose anger instead, until the destruction came.

There's one quiet, important detail in verses 24-25: even in the total collapse of the government and the church, "there were none who were brought unto repentance who were not baptized with water," and Nephi "ordained... men unto this ministry" to baptize "as a witness and a testimony before God." The forms held. Amid anarchy and tribalism and stoned prophets, the ordinance of baptism was established and required — repentance sealed by the covenant act, the priesthood ordained to administer it. When everything external had collapsed, Nephi didn't abandon the order of the gospel; he maintained it, baptizing the few who came, ordaining men to carry it on. There's something steadying in that for any season of collapse: the gospel's forms — baptism, the priesthood, the witness-ordinances — don't depend on a functioning society to remain in force. When the nation fragments and the church breaks up, the covenant ordinances still stand, administered by the faithful few, for the few who will come. The kingdom of God is not the same as the order of the land, and it can persist, in true and full form, even when the land has dissolved into warring tribes.

## Notable

Yes — the collapse into anarchy and Nephi's ministry. **Society fragments into tribalism when the binding loyalty is lost** (7:2-6, the political form of ch 6's church-breakup); **the relapse is worse than never having known — the dog to its vomit** (7:8, the special wretchedness of returning to cleansed-from sin, six years from the golden age); **order without righteousness** (7:14, civil peace with stoned prophets — outward stability no measure of spiritual health, and more dangerous than open chaos); **undeniable power provokes anger, not faith, in the hardened** (7:18-20, they hated the man who raised the dead); **faith so great that angels minister daily** (7:15, 18 — the continuously open heaven, the fruit of unwearyingness; Nephi raises his martyred brother); and **the gospel's ordinances hold even when society collapses** (7:24-25, baptism maintained in the anarchy). Sets up the destruction of ch 8.
