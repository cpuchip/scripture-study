# 3 Nephi 21 — the sign is the book in your hands: the Book of Mormon as the marker that the gathering has begun

**Binding idea:** Christ gives "a sign, that ye may know the time when these things shall be about to take place — that I shall gather in, from their long dispersion, my people, O house of Israel" (21:1). And the sign is the coming forth of the very record being made: "when these things which I declare unto you... shall be made known unto the Gentiles... and shall come forth of the Father, from them unto you" — that is, when the Book of Mormon reaches the Gentiles and through them the scattered remnant — "it shall be a sign unto them, that they may know that the work of the Father hath already commenced unto the fulfilling of the covenant" (21:2-7). For this, "it is wisdom in the Father that they should be established in this land, and be set up as a free people... that these things might come forth from them unto a remnant of your seed" (21:4). The servant who brings it forth "shall be marred because of them. Yet I will heal him, for I will show unto them that my wisdom is greater than the cunning of the devil" (21:10). The unrepentant Gentiles will be cut off (21:14-21); but if they repent, "I will establish my church among them, and they shall come in unto the covenant and be numbered among this the remnant of Jacob... that they may build a city, which shall be called the New Jerusalem" (21:22-23), where "the power of heaven come down among them; and I also will be in the midst" (21:25).

## Thoughts

The most remarkable thing in this chapter is that the sign Christ gives is the very book I am holding. He says He will give "a sign, that ye may know the time when these things shall be about to take place" — the gathering of Israel and the establishment of Zion — and the sign turns out to be the coming forth of the Book of Mormon to the Gentiles in the latter days (21:2-7). "When these things which I declare unto you... shall be made known unto the Gentiles... it shall be a sign unto them, that they may know that the work of the Father hath already commenced." This is extraordinary to sit with as a reader. The Book of Mormon is not merely a record *about* the gathering; its own existence in my hands is the prophesied *sign* that the gathering has begun. Christ, standing in Bountiful around AD 34, points across two thousand years and says: the day My words come forth to the Gentiles, that is the marker. And they have come forth. I am reading them. Which means, if Christ's word is true, the work of the Father has "already commenced" — the gathering of Israel is underway, and the proof is the book itself. There's a self-authenticating quality to this that's hard to overstate: the very act of reading the Book of Mormon is participating in the fulfillment of the sign it prophesies. The reader is not an observer of the prophecy; the reader is *inside* it.

Verse 4 carries a reading of history that is distinctively the Restoration's: "it is wisdom in the Father that they [the Gentiles] should be established in this land, and be set up as a free people by the power of the Father, that these things might come forth from them unto a remnant of your seed." The establishment of a free nation in this land — read in the Restoration as the founding of the United States and the religious liberty it secured — was, according to this verse, *providential*, and its purpose was to enable the Book of Mormon to come forth. The freedom wasn't an end in itself; it was the condition God arranged "that these things might come forth." This reframes the whole sweep of American history through a single lens: the discovery, the settlement, the revolution, the constitution, the liberty — all of it, in this reading, was the Father preparing a free people among whom the record could be brought forth and published to the world without being suppressed. It's a humbling and sobering view of a nation's purpose: the freedom was given for the gospel's sake, which is precisely why the warnings to the Gentiles in this chapter (and chapter 16) are so grave — a free people raised up to carry the gospel, who then reject it in pride, has betrayed the very purpose of its freedom. The liberty was instrumental to the covenant; to keep the liberty and discard the gospel is to keep the gift and refuse the reason for it.

The marred servant in verse 10 is one of the chapter's tender and mysterious notes: "the life of my servant shall be in my hand; therefore they shall not hurt him, although he shall be marred because of them. Yet I will heal him, for I will show unto them that my wisdom is greater than the cunning of the devil." The servant who brings forth the book — in the Restoration's reading, Joseph Smith — will be "marred because of them," yet preserved in the Lord's hand until his work is done, and ultimately healed. The phrase "marred... yet I will heal him" echoes the suffering servant of Isaiah 52-53 ("his visage was so marred," quoted just a chapter earlier in 20:44), drawing a parallel between Christ's own marring and the marring of His servant. And the point of preserving the marred servant is to demonstrate that "my wisdom is greater than the cunning of the devil." The adversary opposes the work with all his cunning — and God's preservation of the servant, the bringing forth of the book despite all opposition, is the proof that divine wisdom overmatches satanic cunning. The work goes forward not because the servant is invulnerable (he's "marred") but because his life is "in my hand" until the purpose is accomplished. There's comfort in that for anyone doing the Lord's work against opposition: you may be marred, but if your life is in His hand, you cannot be stopped until the work He gave you is done.

The two Gentile destinies (vv14-23) repeat the conditional of chapter 16 but with sharper imagery of what gets "cut off." For the unrepentant Gentiles: horses, chariots, cities, strongholds, witchcrafts, soothsayers, graven images, groves, and "all lyings, and deceivings, and envyings, and strifes, and priestcrafts, and whoredoms, shall be done away" (vv14-19). This is Micah 5's catalog — the dismantling of everything a proud, idolatrous, deceitful people trusts in. The things cut off are revealing: military power (horses, chariots, strongholds), false religion (witchcrafts, images, groves, priestcrafts), and social corruption (lyings, deceivings, whoredoms) — the whole apparatus of a civilization that has rejected God. But for the repentant: "I will establish my church among them, and they shall come in unto the covenant and be numbered among this the remnant of Jacob" (v22). The same Gentiles who could be cut off can, by repentance, be grafted permanently into Israel — and not as second-class members but as builders of Zion: "they shall assist my people, the remnant of Jacob... that they may build a city, which shall be called the New Jerusalem" (v23). The repentant Gentiles become co-builders of the New Jerusalem alongside the remnant. There's a beautiful reciprocity in the gathering: the Gentiles brought the book to the remnant (the sign), and now the repentant Gentiles join the remnant to build Zion. Neither does it alone; the covenant family is rebuilt from both, "and I also will be in the midst" (v25).

And the chapter widens at the end to the full scope of the gathering (vv26-29): "the work of the Father commence... among all the dispersed of my people, yea, even the tribes which have been lost... with the Father to prepare the way whereby they may come unto me." The gathering isn't only the Nephite remnant and the Gentiles — it's "all the dispersed," "all nations," even the lost tribes, all being prepared to "call on the Father in my name." And the manner of it echoes Isaiah 52:12: "they shall not go out in haste, nor go by flight, for I will go before them, saith the Father, and I will be their rearward." The gathering is not a panicked flight but an orderly homecoming with God before and behind — the Lord going ahead to prepare the way and guarding the rear so none is lost. The whole vision is staggering in scope and gentle in execution: every scattered portion of Israel, across all nations and all the centuries of dispersion, gathered home, not in haste or fear, but led by God Himself who goes before and behind. And the sign that the whole vast work has begun is the book in my hands. I read it, and in reading it, I am living inside the fulfillment of a prophecy Christ spoke two thousand years ago to a people who have long since vanished — except that, by this very record, they have not vanished at all, but are being gathered home.

## Notable

Yes — the sign of the gathering. **The Book of Mormon's coming forth IS the sign the gathering has begun** (21:1-7 — the reader is inside the prophecy, the book in your hands the self-authenticating marker that the work has "already commenced"); **a free nation established providentially so the book could come forth** (21:4 — the Restoration reading of the American founding, liberty instrumental to the covenant); **the marred-but-preserved servant** (21:10 — Joseph Smith, marred yet preserved, "my wisdom is greater than the cunning of the devil"); **the two Gentile destinies — cut off or numbered among Jacob** (21:14-22, Micah 5); **the repentant Gentiles become co-builders of the New Jerusalem** (21:23-25, the covenant family rebuilt from both); and **the gathering of all the dispersed — not in haste, God before and behind** (21:26-29, Isa 52:12).
