# 3 Nephi 14 — the golden rule, the fruits, "I never knew you," and the house on the rock

**Binding idea:** The Sermon closes (compare Matthew 7). Christ turns "again to the multitude" with the warnings and promises that complete the discourse: "Judge not, that ye be not judged" (14:1) — cast the beam from your own eye first, "and then shalt thou see clearly to cast the mote out of thy brother's eye" (14:5); "Ask, and it shall be given... seek, and ye shall find; knock, and it shall be opened" (14:7); the golden rule, "all things whatsoever ye would that men should do to you, do ye even so to them, for this is the law and the prophets" (14:12); the strait gate and narrow way "which leadeth unto life, and few there be that find it" (14:14); "by their fruits ye shall know them" (14:20); the terrible "Not every one that saith unto me, Lord, Lord, shall enter into the kingdom of heaven... I never knew you; depart from me, ye that work iniquity" (14:21-23); and the closing parable — "whoso heareth these sayings of mine and doeth them" is the wise man whose house, founded on a rock, stood when "the rain descended, and the floods came, and the winds blew" (14:24-27).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 14 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 27 verses. Compare Matthew 7. The Sermon at the Temple, part 3 (close).

## Thoughts

The judge-not teaching (vv1-5) is often misread as a blanket prohibition on all moral discernment, but the parable of the mote and the beam shows it's something more precise. Christ doesn't say "never help your brother with the mote in his eye"; He says, "first cast the beam out of thine own eye; and then shalt thou see clearly to cast the mote out of thy brother's eye." The goal is still to help the brother — but the prerequisite is self-correction. The hypocrite isn't condemned for caring about his brother's fault; he's condemned for fixating on a speck in another's eye while blind to a plank in his own. Self-examination comes first not because other people's faults don't matter but because you literally cannot see clearly to help until your own larger fault is dealt with. The beam distorts your vision; remove it, and then — clear-eyed and humbled by your own repentance — you can actually serve your brother rather than condemn him. This reframes correction entirely: it's not forbidden, it's *sequenced*. Deal with your own sin first, and the helping that follows will come from humility instead of hypocrisy, from a clear eye instead of a self-righteous one. The person who has just pulled a beam from his own eye approaches his brother's mote very differently than the person who has never examined himself at all.

The ask/seek/knock promise (vv7-11) rests on an argument from the lesser to the greater that I find quietly tender: "If ye then, being evil, know how to give good gifts unto your children, how much more shall your Father who is in heaven give good things to them that ask him?" Even flawed, sinful human parents don't hand their hungry child a stone when he asks for bread, or a serpent when he asks for a fish — they give good gifts, however imperfectly. And Christ reasons: if *you*, being evil, do that much, how much more will the perfect Father do? The argument works by making me consider my own instinct toward my children — the reflexive desire to give them what's good — and then telling me that instinct is a faint, fallen echo of God's infinitely greater desire toward me. God's goodness toward His children isn't less than mine toward my children; it's incomparably more. So the asking, seeking, knocking is not pleading with a reluctant deity; it's a child asking a Father who already wants to give good things and only waits to be asked. "Every one that asketh, receiveth" — not because asking twists God's arm but because asking is the form in which a Father who delights to give meets a child willing to receive.

The golden rule in verse 12 is the whole law in one sentence: "all things whatsoever ye would that men should do to you, do ye even so to them, for this is the law and the prophets." What strikes me is that it's stated *actively* — not the negative "don't do to others what you wouldn't want done to you" (which only requires refraining) but the positive "do to others what you would want done to you" (which requires acting). It's not enough to avoid harming; I'm to actively do for others the good I'd want for myself. And Christ says this single principle *is* "the law and the prophets" — the entire moral content of scripture distilled to one operational rule. Every commandment about how to treat others is an application of it. If I want to know how to act toward anyone in any situation, I have a complete answer: imagine myself in their place, and do what I'd want done. The whole law fits in that.

The two most sobering passages in the chapter, and maybe in the whole Sermon, are the fruits and "I never knew you." "By their fruits ye shall know them" (v20) — the test of anything claiming to be of God is not its appearance, its words, or its credentials, but its fruit. The wolf wears sheep's clothing; the false prophet sounds like a true one; the test that cuts through the disguise is what it actually *produces*. A good tree cannot bring forth evil fruit, and a corrupt tree cannot bring forth good — so watch the fruit, not the foliage. And then the chilling escalation in verses 21-23: "Not every one that saith unto me, Lord, Lord, shall enter into the kingdom of heaven... Many will say to me in that day: Lord, Lord, have we not prophesied in thy name, and in thy name have cast out devils, and in thy name done many wonderful works? And then will I profess unto them: I never knew you; depart from me, ye that work iniquity." Read what these people did — they prophesied, cast out devils, performed "many wonderful works," all *in His name*. By any external measure they were spiritual successes. And Christ says, "I never knew you." The works, however impressive, were not the thing. What saves is not what I accomplish for Christ but whether Christ *knows* me — whether there is a real relationship, whether I "doeth the will of my Father." This is the same truth as King Benjamin's "how knoweth a man the master whom he has not served" (Mosiah 5:13): salvation is relational, not transactional. It is terrifyingly possible to build an impressive religious résumé — even with genuine miracles — and remain, at the core, unknown to Christ, because the works were never accompanied by the surrendered heart that does the Father's will. "I never knew you" is the most frightening sentence in the Sermon, and it's spoken to the religiously accomplished, not the obviously wicked. The question at the judgment is not "what did you do in my name?" but "do I know you?"

And the Sermon closes, fittingly, on the same image Helaman gave his sons — the house on the rock (vv24-27). But notice the precise condition: "whoso heareth these sayings of mine *and doeth them*" is the wise builder; "every one that heareth these sayings of mine *and doeth them not*" is the foolish one. The difference between the two houses is not hearing — *both* heard the sayings. The difference is doing. The whole Sermon, with all its teachings, is only a foundation for the one who acts on it; for the one who merely hears and admires, it's sand. And the storm comes to both houses identically — "the rain descended, and the floods came, and the winds blew, and beat upon that house" — exactly as Helaman 5:12 promised, the storm beats on the rock-builder too. The foundation doesn't prevent the storm; it determines whether the house stands. This is the Sermon's final and most practical word, and it's aimed straight at me as I finish reading it: I have now *heard* these sayings — the Beatitudes, the higher law, the secret worship, the golden rule, the fruits, "I never knew you." The only question that matters is whether I will *do* them. To hear the Sermon and not do it is to have spent the whole discourse building on sand. The doing is the rock; the storm is coming to every house; and the house that stands is the one whose hearer became a doer. The risen Christ ends the greatest sermon ever given not with a doctrine but with a choice: hear and do, or hear and don't — and the floods will reveal which.
