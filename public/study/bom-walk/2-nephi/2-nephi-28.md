# 2 Nephi 28 — "All Is Well in Zion": The Devil's Three Strategies

*(Nephi's own prophecy of the last days — false churches, false doctrines, and the devil's methods. Mini-study. The chapter the "grind the poor" thread (13:15) was pointing toward.)*

**Binding idea:** Nephi maps how souls are lost in the last days — and the surprise is that the devil's most effective tool is not rage but a lullaby. What are his three strategies, and why is "all is well" the deadliest?

**Read:** In the last days the churches "built up, and not unto the Lord" contend — "I, I am the Lord's; and the others shall say: I, I am the Lord's" (v3); they "teach with their learning, and deny the Holy Ghost" (v4); they say "there is no God today, for the Lord and the Redeemer hath done his work... this day he is not a God of miracles" (vv5-6). False gospels spread: "Eat, drink, and be merry, for tomorrow we die; and it shall be well with us" (v7), and the subtler one — "he will justify in committing a little sin; yea, lie a little, take the advantage of one because of his words, dig a pit for thy neighbor; there is no harm in this... God will beat us with a few stripes, and at last we shall be saved" (v8). "**They rob the poor because of their fine sanctuaries; they rob the poor because of their fine clothing; and they persecute the meek and the poor in heart, because in their pride they are puffed up**" (v13). Even the sincere are at risk: "they have all gone astray save it be a few, who are the humble followers of Christ; nevertheless, they are led, that in many instances they do err because they are taught by the precepts of men" (v14). Then the devil's methods: he will "**rage in the hearts of the children of men, and stir them up to anger against that which is good**" (v20); "others will he pacify, and lull them away into carnal security, that they will say: **All is well in Zion; yea, Zion prospereth, all is well — and thus the devil cheateth their souls, and leadeth them away carefully down to hell**" (v21); "others he flattereth away, and telleth them **there is no hell**... I am no devil, for there is none" (v22). The woes: "wo be unto him that is at ease in Zion! Wo be unto him that crieth: All is well!... wo be unto him that saith: We have received, and we need no more!" (vv24-27). And the answer: "I will give unto the children of men **line upon line, precept upon precept, here a little and there a little**... unto him that receiveth I will give more; and from them that shall say, We have enough, from them shall be taken away even that which they have" (v30). "**Cursed is he that putteth his trust in man, or maketh flesh his arm**" (v31). "Wo be unto the Gentiles... nevertheless, I will be merciful unto them... for **mine arm is lengthened out all the day long**" (v32).

**Thoughts:**
- *The devil's three strategies (vv20-22) — a taxonomy of deception.* Nephi names three distinct methods, and they are still the three: **(1) rage** — "stir them up to anger against that which is good" (the angry, the contentious, those whom truth offends); **(2) carnal security** — "lull them away... All is well in Zion... and leadeth them away *carefully* down to hell" (the complacent, the comfortable); **(3) denial** — "there is no hell... I am no devil, for there is none" (the dismissive, the sophisticated). The genius of the middle one is the word *carefully*. The devil does not always shove; sometimes he soothes. The soul lulled by "all is well" is not dragged to hell — it is *led carefully*, gently, while believing everything is fine. The lullaby is deadlier than the rage because no alarm ever sounds.
- *"All is well in Zion" — the indictment of complacency (vv21, 24-25).* The deadliest deception is aimed not at the world but at *Zion* — at the believing, prospering, comfortable religious people who say "all is well." "Wo be unto him that is at ease in Zion!" The danger is not that they are doing evil; it is that they are *at ease*, satisfied, certain there is nothing more to do. Complacency among the faithful is a sharper target than wickedness among the wicked.
- *"Rob the poor because of their fine sanctuaries" (v13).* The grind-the-poor thread from the Isaiah block (13:14-15) and chapter 26 (v20) reaches its sharpest form here: religion's *fine sanctuaries* and *fine clothing* built on robbing the poor, while persecuting "the meek and the poor in heart." The buildings and the vestments are the visible spoil — exactly the daughters-of-Zion finery (13:16-24) now worn by churches. A standing indictment of any prosperous religion whose grandeur rests on the backs of the poor.
- *The "little sin" gospel (v8).* "He will justify in committing a little sin... God will beat us with a few stripes, and at last we shall be saved." This is cheap grace dissected before the term existed — sin minimized ("a little"), consequences discounted ("a few stripes"), salvation presumed ("at last we shall be saved"). It is the most modern-sounding verse in the chapter: the gospel of no real cost.
- *"We have enough" vs "line upon line" (vv27, 29-30).* The complacency has a doctrinal form: "We have received the word of God, and we need no more." Against it, the open heaven: "line upon line, precept upon precept... unto him that receiveth I will give more; and from them that shall say, We have enough, from them shall be taken away even that which they have." Revelation is not a closed book but a flowing line — and the posture that closes it ("we have enough") forfeits even what it had. The receptive gain; the satisfied lose.
- *Even the humble can be led to err (v14).* The most sobering line: the few "who are the humble followers of Christ" are nonetheless "led, that in many instances they do err because they are taught by the precepts of men." Sincerity is not immunity. Good people, following Christ, can still inherit error from the precepts of men around them. The cure is v30-31 — keep receiving line upon line, and do not make flesh your arm.
