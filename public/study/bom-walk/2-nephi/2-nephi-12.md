# 2 Nephi 12 — Isaiah 2: The Mountain That Rises and Everything Proud Brought Low

*(Isaiah block — Isaiah 2, first of Nephi's great block, chs 12-24. Full protocol: exegesis + variants + likening. Compare Isaiah 2.)*

**Binding idea:** Isaiah opens Nephi's block with two elevations — one mountain that rises so all nations flow up to it, and everything proud that gets brought low. Why this as the opening, and what do the Book of Mormon variants do to it?

**Read:** "In the last days, when the mountain of the Lord's house shall be established in the top of the mountains, and shall be exalted above the hills, and all nations shall flow unto it" (v2). The peoples say, "Come ye, and let us go up to the mountain of the Lord... and he will teach us of his ways... for out of Zion shall go forth the law" (v3); "they shall beat their swords into plow-shares, and their spears into pruning-hooks — nation shall not lift up sword against nation, neither shall they learn war any more" (v4). "O house of Jacob, come ye and let us walk in the light of the Lord; **yea, come, for ye have all gone astray, every one to his wicked ways**" (v5). The indictment: forsaken because they hearken to soothsayers, their land "full of silver and gold... full of idols; they worship the work of their own hands, that which their own fingers have made" (vv7-8); "the mean man boweth **not** down, and the great man humbleth himself **not**, therefore, forgive him not" (v9). Then the Day of the Lord: "enter into the rock, and hide thee in the dust" (v10); "the lofty looks of man shall be humbled... and the Lord alone shall be exalted in that day" (v11, repeated v17); the day comes "upon the proud and lofty... and he shall be brought low" (v12) — on cedars, oaks, mountains, towers, walls, "all the ships of the sea, and... all the ships of Tarshish, and... all pleasant pictures" (vv13-16); "the idols he shall utterly abolish" (v18); men cast their idols "to the moles and to the bats" and flee to caves "when he ariseth to shake terribly the earth" (vv19-21). "Cease ye from man, whose breath is in his nostrils; for wherein is he to be accounted of?" (v22).

**Exegesis — the two elevations:**
- The chapter is built on a single vertical contrast. **One thing rises:** the mountain of the Lord's house, "established in the top of the mountains... exalted above the hills," that "all nations shall flow unto" (v2) — flowing *up*, against gravity, which is the image's quiet miracle. **Everything else proud is leveled:** lofty looks, haughty men, cedars of Lebanon, oaks of Bashan, high mountains, towers, fenced walls, ships, "pleasant pictures" (vv11-16). The refrain names the resolution twice: "the Lord alone shall be exalted in that day" (vv11, 17). Two heights compete, and only one is left standing.
- *The latter-day temple + millennial peace (vv2-4).* The opening is the gathering — nations streaming to the house of the Lord to be taught his ways — and the result is the end of war: swords to plowshares. Isaiah opens Nephi's block not with judgment but with the destination: a taught people who stop learning war.
- *Idolatry as self-worship (v8).* "They worship the work of their own hands, that which their own fingers have made." The core sin is not merely statues; it is bowing to what you yourself produced — your own output as your god. In a land "full of silver and gold... full of treasures" (v7), the idol is the achievement.
- *"Cease ye from man" (v22).* The chapter ends where Lehi and Nephi keep landing: stop trusting the arm of flesh. "Whose breath is in his nostrils" — man is borrowed breath; "wherein is he to be accounted of?" The leveling of all human loftiness has a single practical upshot: quit depending on it.

**The Book of Mormon variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- *v5 — the added confession (pastoral).* KJV Isaiah 2:5 ends: "O house of Jacob, come ye, and let us walk in the light of the LORD." Full stop. The Book of Mormon continues: "**yea, come, for ye have all gone astray, every one to his wicked ways**" — importing the language of Isaiah 53:6 ("all we like sheep have gone astray"). The invitation now carries its own indictment: *come walk in the light, because you have all left it.* The likening method in action — the call to repentance is built into the call to come.
- *v9 — the "not" reversal (doctrinal).* KJV Isaiah 2:9: "And the mean man boweth down, and the great man humbleth himself: therefore forgive them not." The Book of Mormon inserts *not* twice: "the mean man boweth **not** down, and the great man humbleth himself **not**." The KJV reads as men bowing *to idols*; the Book of Mormon reads as men refusing to bow *to God* (the footnote: "IE unto God; he worships idols instead"). The variant relocates the sin from misdirected worship to refused humility — high and low alike will not bend.
- *v16 — both ships (textual witness).* KJV (following the Hebrew) Isaiah 2:16: "all the ships of Tarshish." The Septuagint has "ships of the sea." 2 Ne 12:16 has **both**: "all the ships of the sea, and... all the ships of Tarshish." The footnote in both editions notes it — the brass plates preserved a phrase the Hebrew lost and a phrase the Greek lost. A small but pointed evidence that Nephi's source predates the split between the textual traditions.
- *v6 — "O Lord" (address).* KJV: "Therefore thou hast forsaken thy people." BoM: "Therefore, **O Lord**, thou hast forsaken thy people" — turning a flat statement into a direct address to God, which reframes the indictment as a lament spoken *to* the Lord rather than *about* him. (Also v6: KJV "are soothsayers" → BoM "hearken unto soothsayers" — they listen to diviners rather than being diviners.)

**Nephite framing — likening (four questions):**
- *Why did Nephi include Isaiah 2 first?* Because it states the whole arc of the block in miniature: the gathering to the Lord's house (the hope), the indictment of prosperous idolatry (the diagnosis), and the Day of the Lord that levels all pride (the warning). It opens with the destination — a taught, peaceful people — so the dense chapters that follow have somewhere to be going.
- *Was it for us, last-dispensation readers?* Directly. "In the last days... the mountain of the Lord's house shall be established in the top of the mountains" — the Restoration reads this as the latter-day temple(s) and the gathering, "the top of the mountains" carrying obvious resonance for a people who would settle the Rockies (D&C 133:13). The swords-to-plowshares peace is still future to us.
- *How does it apply to us?* The idolatry of v8 — "they worship the work of their own hands, that which their own fingers have made" — is uncomfortably exact for a prosperous, technological age that prizes what it builds. The thing your own fingers made, treated as the thing you bow to: that is a live temptation in a workshop, not an ancient one. And "cease ye from man, whose breath is in his nostrils" (v22) names the cure.
- *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* Nephi just told us (11:4) all things typify Christ and that he reads Isaiah as one who *saw* the Redeemer. So "the Lord alone shall be exalted in that day" (v11, 17) is Christological — the One left standing when all human loftiness falls is the Redeemer Nephi saw. And the v5 variant shows Nephi's likening hand: he reads "come walk in the light" and hears, for his own astray people, "ye have all gone astray" — the text turned toward the listener.

**Notable / flag:** Isaiah block, full protocol. The structural key is the **two competing elevations** — the mountain that rises vs. all pride leveled, resolved in "the Lord alone shall be exalted." Three substantive verified variants: the **v9 "not" reversal** (refused humility — the most doctrinally significant), the **v16 ships doublet** (the cleanest brass-plate textual witness in the block), and the **v5 added confession** (the likening method visible in the text). The chapter intro footnote's own claim — "differences in more than half of the 433 verses of Isaiah quoted in the Book of Mormon" — sets expectations for the chapters ahead.
