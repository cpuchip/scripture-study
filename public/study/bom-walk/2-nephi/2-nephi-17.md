# 2 Nephi 17 — Isaiah 7: Immanuel — The Sign Given to a King Who Wouldn't Ask

*(Isaiah block — Isaiah 7. Full protocol: exegesis + variants + likening. Near-identical to KJV. Compare Isaiah 7.)*

**Binding idea:** A terrified king is offered any sign he wants and refuses it — so why does God give the sign anyway, and why is the sign a child named "God with us"?

**Read:** In the days of Ahaz, Rezin of Syria and Pekah of Israel ("Ephraim") march on Jerusalem; "his heart was moved, and the heart of his people, as the trees of the wood are moved with the wind" (vv1-2). God sends Isaiah and his son Shear-jashub ("a remnant shall return") to Ahaz with the word: "Take heed, and be quiet; fear not, neither be faint-hearted **for the two tails of these smoking firebrands**" (v4). The enemy plot to install a puppet king "shall not stand, neither shall it come to pass" (v7). "If ye will not believe, surely ye shall not be established" (v9). The Lord tells Ahaz: "Ask thee a sign of the Lord thy God; ask it either in the depths, or in the heights above" (v11). Ahaz refuses, in false piety: "I will not ask, neither will I tempt the Lord" (v12). Isaiah's reply: "Hear ye now, O house of David; is it a small thing for you to weary men, but will ye weary my God also? Therefore, the Lord himself shall give you a sign — **Behold, a virgin shall conceive, and shall bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel**" (vv13-14). "Butter and honey shall he eat, that he may know to refuse the evil and to choose the good. For before the child shall know to refuse the evil and choose the good, the land that thou abhorrest shall be forsaken of both her kings" (vv15-16). The rest: the king of Assyria — the "hired razor" — will shave the land bare (vv17-25).

**Exegesis:**
- *Two smoking firebrands (v4).* Ahaz is terrified of two kings; God calls them "the two tails of these smoking firebrands" — burnt-out stubs with little fire left (the footnote: "those two kings have little fire left"). The threat that has the king and his people shaking like wind-blown trees is, in God's read, nearly ash. Fear inflates scale; faith deflates it. The first word of the prophecy is *the thing you fear is smaller than your fear of it.*
- *The faith-wordplay (v9).* "If ye will not believe, surely ye shall not be established." In Hebrew this is a pun on one root (*aman*, the root of "amen"): *if you will not be firm [in faith], you will not be made firm [secure]*. Believing and being established are the same word. Faith is not a feeling added to security; faith *is* the security. A king who will not trust will not stand.
- *The refusal dressed as reverence (vv11-12).* God tells Ahaz to ask for a sign — any sign, depth or height. Ahaz declines: "I will not tempt the Lord." It sounds devout. It is cowardice. He has already decided to trust Assyria (he will hire the Assyrian king as his "razor," vv17, 20), so a sign from God is unwelcome — it might obligate him to a faith he has no intention of exercising. Refusing the sign God *commanded* you to ask for is not humility; it is a decision already made, given a pious mask.
- *Immanuel — the sign given anyway (v14).* "The Lord himself shall give you a sign" — so the sign comes despite the refusal. And the sign is a name: *Immanuel*, "God with us." To a king who will not let God be with him, the sign is precisely God-with-us. The prophecy has a near-term horizon (a child born soon; before he is old enough to choose good from evil, the two kings Ahaz fears will be gone, v16) and a far horizon that Matthew names explicitly: the virgin birth of Christ (Matt 1:23). The immediate sign of a child's brief growth doubles as the ultimate sign of the Incarnation — God literally with us.
- *The dual fulfillment, held honestly.* In Ahaz's day the sign worked on a short clock: within a few years Syria and Ephraim fell. But the language — a *virgin* conceiving, the child named *God with us* — overflows its occasion, and Matthew reads it as fulfilled in Jesus. Nephi, who frames the whole block with "all things typify Christ" (11:4), takes the Christ-reading as the point. Both are true: the sign that reassured a faithless king is the sign that announced the Savior.

**The Book of Mormon variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- This is another **near-identical** chapter. The differences are minor: v6 "yea, the son of Tabeal" for KJV "even"; v14 adds "shall" ("shall bear a son"); v18 drops "the rivers of" ("uttermost part of Egypt"); v20 drops "namely"; v23 restructures slightly. None alters the sense.
- *On "virgin" (v14):* this is **not** a Book-of-Mormon distinctive — the KJV also reads "virgin." The famous debate is between the Hebrew *almah* (young woman / maiden of marriageable age) and the Greek Septuagint *parthenos* (virgin), which Matthew follows (Matt 1:23). Both the KJV and the Book of Mormon land on "virgin," so the Restoration text agrees with the Christological reading the New Testament already established; it does not change the KJV here.

**Nephite framing — likening (four questions):**
- *Why did Nephi include Isaiah 7?* For Immanuel. Reading "all things typify Christ" (11:4), Nephi takes "God with us" as a direct Christ-sign — the same condescension he himself saw in vision (1 Ne 11, God coming down to be born of a virgin). The chapter is the Old Testament's clearest virgin-birth prophecy, and Nephi the Christ-witness would not leave it out.
- *Was it for us, last-dispensation readers?* For us Immanuel is fulfilled, not pending — "God with us" is the Incarnation already accomplished (Matt 1:23). And the faith condition (v9, "if ye will not believe, ye shall not be established") is timeless: trust is what makes a people stand.
- *How does it apply to us?* Ahaz's move is recognizable — a spiritual-sounding refusal ("I will not tempt the Lord") that masks a decision already made to trust something else. We do it when we decline God's offered help in pious language because we have quietly chosen the arm of flesh. And "two smoking firebrands": the crisis that has us shaking is usually smaller than our fear of it.
- *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* Nephi saw the condescension of God — the virgin, the child (1 Ne 11:13-21). So Isaiah 7:14 is, for him, not a cryptic Hebrew oracle but a description of what he watched: God becoming a baby to be *with us*. The sign of presence given to a king who refused presence is the same pattern as the whole gospel — God gives himself to those who would not ask.
