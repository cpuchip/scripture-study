# 2 Nephi 25 — "We Talk of Christ": Nephi's Plainness After Isaiah

*(Nephi's own commentary on the Isaiah block he just quoted — the plainness chapter. Mini-study + block synthesis. Contains 2 Ne 25:23 and 25:26.)*

**Binding idea:** After thirteen chapters of Isaiah's "manner of the Jews," Nephi turns deliberately to *plainness* — and the plain thing is Christ, named and dated. Why does plainness matter, and how does "by grace... after all we can do" sit beside "we talk of Christ"?

**Read:** "Isaiah spake many things which were hard for many of my people to understand; for they know not concerning the manner of prophesying among the Jews" (v1). "The words of Isaiah are not plain unto you, nevertheless they are plain unto all those that are filled with the spirit of prophecy... **my soul delighteth in plainness unto my people, that they may learn**" (v4). They will be understood "in the last days... in that day shall they understand them" (v8). Then Nephi's plain prophecy: the Jews carried to Babylon return (vv10-11); "when the day cometh that the Only Begotten of the Father... shall manifest himself unto them in the flesh, behold, they will reject him... **they will crucify him; and after he is laid in a sepulchre for the space of three days he shall rise from the dead, with healing in his wings**" (vv12-13); "my soul delighteth to prophesy concerning him, **for I have seen his day**" (v13). Scattered and scourged "until they shall be persuaded to believe in Christ, the Son of God, and the atonement, which is infinite for all mankind" (v16); "the Lord will set his hand again the second time to restore his people... a marvelous work and a wonder" (v17). And the naming: "**the Messiah cometh in six hundred years from the time that my father left Jerusalem; and... his name shall be Jesus Christ, the Son of God**" (v19); "**there is none other name given under heaven save it be this Jesus Christ... whereby man can be saved**" (v20). The climax: "we labor diligently to write, to persuade our children... to believe in Christ, and to be reconciled to God; **for we know that it is by grace that we are saved, after all we can do**" (v23). "We keep the law of Moses, and look forward with steadfastness unto Christ" (v24); "the law hath become dead unto us, and we are made alive in Christ" (v25). "**And we talk of Christ, we rejoice in Christ, we preach of Christ, we prophesy of Christ... that our children may know to what source they may look for a remission of their sins**" (v26). "The right way is to believe in Christ and deny him not... Christ is the Holy One of Israel; wherefore ye must bow down before him, and worship him with all your might, mind, and strength" (vv28-29).

**Thoughts:**
- *Plainness is a deliberate choice, not a limitation (vv1-8).* Nephi does not apologize for not writing "after the manner of the Jews" — he chooses against it, because that manner made Isaiah "hard." His own gift is "plainness... that they may learn" (v4). And he is candid that Isaiah's hard words will land later: "in the last days... in that day shall they understand them" (v8). This is the answer to the whole block I just walked: the Isaiah was always meant to be understood eventually, and Nephi supplies the plain key now so his children are not lost in the meantime. The dense thirteen chapters get a plain interpreter in chapter 25.
- *Christ named, dated, and made exclusive (vv19-20).* This is among the most specific Messianic prophecies anywhere: the Messiah comes "in six hundred years," and "his name shall be Jesus Christ, the Son of God" (v19). Five hundred ninety years before Bethlehem, the name is on the plates. And the exclusivity: "there is none other name given under heaven save it be this Jesus Christ... whereby man can be saved" (v20, echoing Acts 4:12). Plainness produces a name, a date, and a claim — not a mood.
- *"By grace... after all we can do" (v23) — the synthesis, read plainly.* The verse people fight over reads, in context, as the resolution of the law-and-Christ tension Nephi has been working: "we labor diligently... to believe in Christ, and to be reconciled to God; for we know that it is by grace that we are saved, after all we can do." The plain sense is the same order he gave in 10:24 — reconcile yourselves, *then* grace saves. "All we can do" is not a quota that earns salvation; it is the laboring, persuading, believing, reconciling that *turns us toward* the grace that does the saving. The next verses guard it: "we are made alive in Christ" (v25), not by the law, which "hath become dead unto us." Effort that turns toward grace, grace that does the work — neither alone.
- *"We talk of Christ" (v26) — plainness distilled.* The most-quoted sentence in the chapter is the program of the whole record: "we talk of Christ, we rejoice in Christ, we preach of Christ, we prophesy of Christ... that our children may know to what source they may look for a remission of their sins." This is what plainness is *for* — not cleverness but a clear pointing finger: *look there, to Christ, for the remission of your sins.* It is the same purpose as the small plates from the start ("persuade men to come unto... Christ," 1 Ne 6:4) and the same as Nephi's "all things typify Christ" (11:4), now stated as the family's daily speech.
- *Law kept, but dead (vv24-25, 27).* The Nephites keep the law of Moses while believing in Christ — but they keep it knowing it is "dead," a finger pointing to the life that is in Christ, "until the law shall be fulfilled." They obey the type while looking past it to the antitype. This is the clearest statement of how a pre-Christian people lived Christianly: keeping the schoolmaster while watching for the Master.
