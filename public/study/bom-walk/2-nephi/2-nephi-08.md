# 2 Nephi 8 — Isaiah 51–52:1-2: "Awake, Awake"

*(Isaiah block — Jacob reading Isaiah 51 + 52:1-2. Exegesis + variants + likening. Compare Isaiah 51.)*

**Binding idea:** Who is asleep — the arm of the Lord, or Zion? And which way does the "Awake" command point?

**Read:** "Hearken unto me, ye that follow after righteousness. Look unto the rock from whence ye are hewn... Look unto Abraham, your father, and unto Sarah, she that bare you; for I called him alone, and blessed him." The Lord will "comfort Zion... make her wilderness like Eden." His salvation is permanent against a perishing creation: "the heavens shall vanish away like smoke, and the earth shall wax old like a garment... But my salvation shall be forever." To those with his law in their heart: "fear ye not the reproach of men... For the moth shall eat them up like a garment." Then the first cry: "**Awake, awake! Put on strength, O arm of the Lord**... Art thou not he that hath cut Rahab, and wounded the dragon? Art thou not he who hath dried the sea... that hath made the depths of the sea a way for the ransomed to pass over? Therefore, the redeemed of the Lord shall return, and come with singing unto Zion." God answers: "**I am he**; yea, I am he that comforteth you. Behold, who art thou, that thou shouldst be afraid of man, who shall die... And forgettest the Lord thy maker?" Then the second cry, now turned outward: "**Awake, awake, stand up, O Jerusalem**, which hast drunk... the cup of his fury." Her sons have fainted; but "I have taken out of thine hand the cup of trembling... thou shalt no more drink it again. But I will put it into the hand of them that afflict thee." Finally the third: "**Awake, awake, put on thy strength, O Zion**; put on thy beautiful garments, O Jerusalem... Shake thyself from the dust... loose thyself from the bands of thy neck, O captive daughter of Zion."

**Exegesis — the three "Awake" calls (the spine of the chapter):**
- The chapter turns on three imperatives, and the *direction reverses* between the first and the rest. **(1) v9 — the people cry to God:** "Awake, awake! Put on strength, O arm of the Lord... as in the ancient days." They appeal to the Exodus power — "cut Rahab," "wounded the dragon," "dried the sea... a way for the ransomed to pass over" (Rahab/the dragon = the chaos-monster, here Egypt; the dried sea = the Red Sea, Ex 14:21). The plea is: *do again what you did at the Exodus.*
- **God's answer (v12-16):** the arm of the Lord was never asleep. "I am he that comforteth you" — and the rebuke is gentle: "who art thou, that thou shouldst be afraid of man, who shall die... and forgettest the Lord thy maker, that hath stretched forth the heavens?" You feared the oppressor and forgot the Creator. The arm that made the heavens has not slept; *you* have, under the cup.
- **(2) v17 + (3) v24 — God turns the command back:** "Awake, awake, stand up, O Jerusalem" / "Awake, awake, put on thy strength, O Zion." The one who needs to wake is not God but the people stupefied by "the cup of his fury." We pray for God to rouse himself and act; he tells us he has been awake the whole time, and it is we who slept. The imperative we aimed at heaven gets handed back to us.
- *The cup transferred (v22-23):* the cup of trembling is taken from the afflicted — "thou shalt no more drink it again" — and "put... into the hand of them that afflict thee." The cup of wrath does not vanish; it changes hands (the same cup Christ will drink to the dregs so we need not, Matt 26:39; D&C 19:18).
- *The rock and the one man (v1-2):* comfort to a remnant who feels too small to matter — "Look unto Abraham... for I called him alone, and blessed him." God built a nation from one couple; a tiny colony is not too small for the same hand.

**The BoM variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- *v9-10, "it" → "he/who":* KJV reads "Art thou not **it** that hath cut Rahab... Art thou not **it** which hath dried the sea." The Book of Mormon reads "Art thou not **he**... Art thou not **he who**." The arm of the Lord is personalized — not an *it* (an attribute, a force) but a *he* (a person). The Christology surfaces in a pronoun: the arm that made a way through the sea is the same Lord who comforts.
- *v19, "two things" → "two sons" (the significant one):* KJV Isaiah 51:19 reads "These two **things** are come unto thee... desolation, and destruction, and the famine, and the sword." The Book of Mormon reads "These two **sons** are come unto thee, **who shall be sorry for thee**." And v20 adds the matching clause: "Thy sons have fainted, **save these two**." The KJV's abstract pair of calamities becomes two persons who mourn over Jerusalem and are spared the general fainting — pulling the verse toward the two-witnesses motif the footnotes themselves flag (Rev 11:3; Zech 4:14). The BoM reading is the harder, more specific, and more prophetic one.

**Nephite framing — likening (Isaiah block, four questions):**
- *Why did Jacob include it?* He is preaching to a broken-off remnant who feel both small and abandoned. Isaiah 51's first word is *look to the one man I called and blessed* — you are not too few — and its refrain is *the arm of the Lord is not asleep, you are; wake up and put on your strength.* That is precisely the medicine for a scattered colony tempted to think God's mighty acts are all in the past tense.
- *Was it for us, the last-dispensation readers?* The "Awake, O Zion... put on thy beautiful garments" of v24-25 is read by the Restoration as a latter-day gathering text — Joseph Smith's D&C 113:7-10 is the revealed commentary on these exact verses ("put on thy strength, O Zion" = Zion clothing herself with the authority of the priesthood; "loose thyself from the bands of thy neck" = scattered Israel freeing itself from the curses by coming to the knowledge of the gospel). So yes: the command to wake is aimed down the centuries at us.
- *How does it apply to us?* The same reversal: we pray for God to intervene, to wake up and fix what frightens us — and the answer is that he is awake, has always been, and the sleeper is us. "Put on thy strength" is an imperative *to Zion*, not a request *of God*. The waking is ours to do.
- *How do we read it better through Jacob's framing?* Jacob places this right after the suffering-Servant of ch 7 (Isaiah 50). The arm that "gave his back to the smiter" (7:6) is the same arm summoned to "awake... as in the ancient days" (8:9) — and God's answer ("I am he") identifies the comforting arm with the suffering Servant. Read in sequence, the Servant who is struck and the Arm that delivers are *one person*: the Holy One of Israel Jacob keeps naming.
