# 2 Nephi 26 — "All Are Alike Unto God": Inclusivity Against Priestcraft

*(Nephi's own prophecy — the Nephite destruction foreseen, the last-days Gentile churches, priestcraft defined, and the book's flagship inclusivity verse 26:33. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** The chapter ends with God denying *no one*. What is the priestcraft it sets that inclusivity against — and how does 26:33 answer the hard verse back in chapter 5?

**Read:** After Christ "shall have risen from the dead he shall show himself unto you, my children" (v1); signs of his birth, death, and resurrection will come, and "great and terrible shall that day be unto the wicked" (v3); the righteous who "look forward unto Christ with steadfastness... shall not perish" (v8); "the Son of Righteousness shall appear unto them; and he shall heal them, and they shall have peace with him, until three generations... and many of the fourth" (v9). Nephi grieves: "**O the pain, and the anguish of my soul for the loss of the slain of my people!**... but I must cry unto my God: Thy ways are just" (v7); "**the Spirit of the Lord will not always strive with man**" (v11). The book itself is foreseen: the destroyed "shall speak unto them out of the ground... their voice shall be as one that hath a familiar spirit... whisper out of the dust" (v16). In the last days the Gentiles "are lifted up in the pride of their eyes... they put down the power and miracles of God, and **preach up unto themselves their own wisdom and their own learning, that they may get gain and grind upon the face of the poor**" (v20); secret combinations rise, the devil leading "them by the neck with a flaxen cord, until he bindeth them with his strong cords forever" (v22). Then the turn to mercy: "the Lord God worketh not in darkness. He doeth not anything save it be for the benefit of the world; for **he loveth the world, even that he layeth down his own life that he may draw all men unto him**" (v24); "Come unto me all ye ends of the earth, buy milk and honey, **without money and without price**" (v25); "all men are privileged the one like unto the other, and **none are forbidden**" (v28). The definition: "**priestcrafts are that men preach and set themselves up for a light unto the world, that they may get gain and praise of the world; but they seek not the welfare of Zion**" (v29); "the laborer in Zion shall labor for Zion; **for if they labor for money they shall perish**" (v31). And the climax: "he doeth nothing save it be plain... and **he denieth none that come unto him, black and white, bond and free, male and female; and he remembereth the heathen; and all are alike unto God, both Jew and Gentile**" (v33).

**Thoughts:**
- *26:33 answers 2 Ne 5:21 — same author, same book.* Back in chapter 5, the hard complexion/curse language sat unresolved (flagged for Michael). Here, twenty-one chapters later, the same prophet writes the corrective in the plainest possible terms: God "denieth none that come unto him, **black and white**, bond and free, male and female... **all are alike unto God, both Jew and Gentile**." This is not a later editor patching Nephi; it is Nephi himself, declaring that God's invitation runs across every line that humans draw — color, status, sex, ethnicity. The book contains its own answer to its hardest verse, and the answer is unambiguous. (This strengthens the 5:21 flag: the doctrine of the book, stated by its first author, is radical equality before God.)
- *Priestcraft, precisely defined (v29).* "Men preach and set themselves up for a light unto the world, that they may get gain and praise of the world; but they seek not the welfare of Zion." Three marks: (1) setting *yourself* up as the light (rather than pointing to Christ, the light — contrast 25:26, "we talk of Christ"); (2) the motive is gain and praise; (3) the tell is the *absence* of seeking Zion's welfare. The antidote is exact: "the laborer in Zion shall labor for Zion; for if they labor for money they shall perish" (v31). This is the standing test for anyone who teaches publicly — including teaching that reaches an audience: is the self the light, or is Christ? Is the motive gain and praise, or the welfare of others? It is the Ben Test in Nephi's words.
- *The inclusivity is grounded in the Atonement (v24).* God "loveth the world, even that he layeth down his own life that he may draw all men unto him" (John 3:16 + John 12:32). The reason none are forbidden is that the price was already paid for all — "without money and without price" (v25, Isa 55). You cannot buy what was bought for you; you can only come. Priestcraft sells what God gives free.
- *The voice from the dust (vv15-16).* Nephi describes the future emergence of his own record — the destroyed people "speak unto them out of the ground... whisper out of the dust" (Isa 29:4). The Book of Mormon is a buried people's whisper, surfacing in the last days. The very book I am walking is, in its own pages, foreseen as a voice from the dust.
- *Nephi's grief is honest (v7).* "O the pain, and the anguish of my soul for the loss of the slain of my people!" He has seen his nation's end (the Mormon-at-Cumorah destruction), and it "well nigh consumeth" him — yet he still says "Thy ways are just." Foreknowledge of the worst does not cancel grief; it coexists with trust. The prophet weeps and worships in the same breath.
