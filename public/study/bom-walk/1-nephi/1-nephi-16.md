# 1 Nephi 16 — The Liahona, the Broken Bow, and a Son Who Hands the Role Back

**Binding idea:** How does divine guidance actually work on the trail — and what does Nephi do when even his father murmurs?

**Read:** When the brothers complain that Nephi has "declared unto us hard things," he answers: "the guilty taketh the truth to be hard, for it cutteth them to the very center." After marriages to Ishmael's daughters, the Lord commands the journey, and at the tent door Lehi finds "a round ball of curious workmanship... of fine brass," with spindles, "the one pointed the way." They follow "the directions of the ball" to the fertile parts. Then Nephi's "fine steel" bow breaks; the others' bows "lost their springs"; there is no food, and "even my father began to murmur against the Lord." Nephi makes a new wooden bow and arrow — and asks his father, "Whither shall I go to obtain food?" Lehi "did inquire of the Lord," "was truly chastened... brought down into the depths of sorrow," and is told, "Look upon the ball, and behold the things which are written." Nephi observes that the pointers "did work according to the faith and diligence and heed which we did give unto them," with new writing appearing "from time to time, according to the faith and diligence which we gave unto it" — "and thus we see that by small means the Lord can bring about great things." Nephi climbs the mountain, slays food, and they give thanks. Later Ishmael dies and is buried "in the place which was called Nahom"; his daughters mourn and murmur to return; Laman urges his brothers to "slay our father, and also our brother Nephi," charging him with "cunning arts" and the ambition "to make himself a king." The voice of the Lord chastens them; they repent.

**Thoughts:**
- *"The guilty taketh the truth to be hard" (v2).* Nephi's diagnostic: the same truth that "justifies" the righteous "cutteth [the guilty] to the very center." The discomfort is information about the hearer, not a flaw in the message. When truth feels like an assault, that is conscience, not cruelty (cf Acts 7:54, "cut to the heart"; Hel 13:24-27). The brothers' "hard things, more than we are able to bear" is a confession dressed as a grievance.
- *The Liahona works by faith — "by small means... great things" (v28-29).* The chapter's doctrine. The compass is not a mechanical GPS; the pointers "did work according to the faith and diligence and heed which we did give unto them," and the writing changed "according to the faith." When faith flagged, it stopped (Alma 37 makes this explicit). A tiny brass ball directs a continental migration — "by small means the Lord can bring about great things." Guidance is real but conditional; the instrument runs on the faith of those who carry it.
- *Even Lehi murmurs — and Nephi hands him the role back (v20-24).* The most quietly remarkable moment. Hungry, the *prophet* Lehi "murmur[s] against the Lord." Nephi — himself a seer, who could simply inquire on his own — instead makes a new bow and asks his *father*, "Whither shall I go?" He returns the prophetic inquiry to Lehi rather than going around him. Lehi inquires, is chastened, repents, and is restored to his office. Nephi is the designated "ruler" (2:22), but here he refuses to usurp the faltering leader; he lifts him by giving him the role back. Leadership that restores its leader instead of replacing him.
- *The cycle recurs — the broken bow (the ch-7 template).* Crisis (no food) → murmuring (even Lehi this time) → Nephi acts in faith (new bow) and asks → guidance (the Liahona's writing) → repentance → blessing (food) → thanks. The wheel from ch 7 turns again; each hardship resets it. The escalation: now the *father* murmurs, and Laman openly plots murder (v37).
- *Laman's accusation is a self-portrait (v38).* He charges Nephi with "cunning arts" and the wish "to make himself a king and a ruler." Laman cannot see revelation; he sees only manipulation and ambition — because ambition is his own lens. The unbelieving heart reads the prophet as a schemer (same root as the reason-from-obstacle blindness of chs 3, 7).
- *Nahom, the place of mourning (v34).* Ishmael is buried at Nahom — the footnote gives the Hebrew *naham*, "to console, be sorry." They mourn at a place whose name means mourning. A small, sad piece of wordplay sitting in the narrative.
