# 1 Nephi 20 — Isaiah 48: Refined in the Furnace, Called Out of Babylon

*(Isaiah block — full exegesis + Nephite framing + the four likening questions, per the walk's prophetic-quotation protocol. Compare Isaiah 48.)*

**Binding idea:** Why does Nephi hand his broken-off family *this* chapter — God's word to a nominal, afflicted, exile-bound Israel?

**Read (the text):** The Lord addresses "the house of Jacob, who are called by the name of Israel, and are come forth out of the waters of Judah, or out of the waters of baptism," who "swear by the name of the Lord... yet they swear not in truth nor in righteousness" — a covenant people in name only. God recalls that He "declared the former things from the beginning... before it came to pass I showed them thee," so Israel could not credit its idols. Because Israel is "obstinate, and thy neck is an iron sinew, and thy brow brass," and "a transgressor from the womb," yet "for my name's sake will I defer mine anger... that I cut thee not off. For, behold, I have refined thee, I have chosen thee in the furnace of affliction." He declares Himself "the first... and also the last," who "laid the foundation of the earth," "thy Redeemer, the Holy One of Israel... who leadeth thee by the way thou shouldst go." Then the lament: "O that thou hadst hearkened to my commandments—then had thy peace been as a river, and thy righteousness as the waves of the sea." The call: "Go ye forth of Babylon, flee ye from the Chaldeans... say ye: The Lord hath redeemed his servant Jacob," for "he caused the waters to flow out of the rock for them." And the verdict: "there is no peace, saith the Lord, unto the wicked."

**Exegesis + the BoM-vs-KJV variant:**
- Isaiah 48 sits in the prophet's "former things / new things" message to exilic Israel: God's argument is that He alone foretold history (the "former things"), so the idols get no credit, and now He declares "new things." The historical hinge is deliverance from Babylon — v14-15's "him" whom "the Lord hath loved," who will "do his pleasure on Babylon," is the Cyrus-figure who frees the exiles. Dual horizon: the literal return (538 BC) and the latter-day gathering out of spiritual Babylon (D&C 133:5, 14, "Go ye out from Babylon").
- *The signature variant (v1), now verified against both texts this session:* KJV Isaiah 48:1 reads only "come forth out of the waters of Judah"; 1 Nephi 20:1 adds "or out of the waters of baptism." The gloss reads the lineage-water as covenant-water — "Israel" defined by the baptismal covenant, not bloodline alone. It is the most-cited 1 Nephi 20 variant, and it sets the chapter's terms: who is the true Israel? Those in the covenant, not merely those of the name. (A second, minor v1 difference: KJV "but not in truth"; BoM "yet they swear not in truth.")
- *Furnace of affliction (v10):* "I have chosen thee in the furnace of affliction." Affliction is framed as refining, not rejecting — the exile is the furnace, and God's restraint ("for my name's sake will I defer mine anger") is mercy staked on His own covenant glory ("I will not give my glory unto another," v11).
- *The divine lament (v18):* "O that thou hadst hearkened... then had thy peace been as a river." God grieves the peace Israel forfeited — the "if only" of God, the cost of half-obedience. Its inverse closes the chapter: "there is no peace... unto the wicked" (v22).
- *Christology (v12-17):* the speaker is "the first... and the last" (Rev 1:17; 22:13), who "laid the foundation of the earth" (the Creator) and is "thy Redeemer" — Jehovah, the premortal Christ (continuing 19:7-10). The one who speaks Isaiah 48 is the same God of Israel who will yield Himself to be crucified.

**Why Nephi set it here — the four likening questions:**
1. *Why this chapter, here?* Because Isaiah 48 is *about Nephi's exact situation*: a covenant people, nominal and "obstinate," refined "in the furnace of affliction," and called to "flee Babylon." Nephi's family is a broken-off remnant of Israel, afflicted through a wilderness-furnace, fled from a doomed Jerusalem already falling to Babylon. He could hardly find a closer mirror — and 19:24 said exactly this: "liken them unto yourselves... ye who are a remnant of the house of Israel, a branch who have been broken off."
2. *Was it for us, the latter-day readers?* Yes. "Flee Babylon" is read across the dispensations — D&C 133 reuses it for the latter-day call to "come out" of the world. The nominal-religion charge ("called by the name... yet not in truth") is a mirror for any covenant people, the latter-day Saints and Gentiles included.
3. *How does it apply to us?* As a diagnostic and a promise. The diagnostic: do we "swear by the name of the Lord... yet not in truth" — covenant in name, not in heart? The promise: affliction is the furnace where the chosen are refined, not discarded; God defers His anger "for his name's sake." And the lament names the personal cost — the peace "as a river" we forfeit by half-hearkening. The wicked simply have no peace (v22).
4. *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* Nephi reads Babylon not as a dead empire but as the doomed world the covenant people must leave — he and his family literally did. And the "waters of baptism" gloss makes the covenant the boundary of Israel: you are of Israel by the covenant you keep, not the name you carry. Read with your own name in it, Isaiah 48 asks every reader — are you Israel in truth, or only by title; and will you come out of Babylon?
