# 1 Nephi 13 — The Great and Abominable Church, and the Book That Establishes the Book

**Binding idea:** What does Nephi see of the Gentile future — and what does the Book of Mormon say it exists *to do*?

**Read:** The angel shows Nephi the Gentile nations and, among them, "a great church... most abominable above all other churches," founded by the devil, which "slayeth the saints of God" and yokes them; its desires are "gold, and silver, and silks, and scarlets... and the harlots," and "for the praise of the world do they destroy the saints." Across "many waters," the Spirit of God "wrought upon" a man who "went forth upon the many waters" to the seed of Nephi's brethren, and on "other Gentiles" who "went forth out of captivity" across the sea. The Gentiles prosper and obtain the land; the seed of the brethren is "scattered before the Gentiles and were smitten." When the Gentiles' "mother" nation gathers to battle them, "the power of God was with them" and delivered them. Nephi sees a book carried among them — "a record of the Jews," like the brass plates "save there are not so many" — which "contained the fulness of the gospel" when it left the Jews, but after passing "by the hand of the twelve apostles... unto the Gentiles," the abominable church "have taken away... many parts which are plain and most precious; and also many covenants," so "an exceedingly great many do stumble." Yet the Lamb promises mercy: "I will manifest myself unto thy seed, that they shall write many things... and after thy seed shall be destroyed and dwindle in unbelief... these things shall be hid up, to come forth unto the Gentiles, by the gift and power of the Lamb." These "last records... shall establish the truth of the first... and shall make known the plain and precious things which have been taken away... that all men must come unto him, or they cannot be saved"; "they both shall be established in one; for there is one God and one Shepherd."

**Thoughts:**
- *The Book of Mormon states its own mission (v40-41).* "These last records... shall establish the truth of the first [the Bible]... and shall make known the plain and precious things which have been taken away." The book exists to *restore the plainness lost from the Bible*, to *confirm* the Bible (not replace it), and to testify "that all men must come unto him." "They both shall be established in one; for there is one God and one Shepherd" — Ezekiel's stick of Judah and stick of Joseph joined (Ezek 37). This is the clearest in-text statement of *why the book is*, companion to its own title page.
- *"Plain and precious things" — the apostasy as subtraction (v26-29).* The book's account of how Christianity fractured is not heresy added but truth *removed*: the abominable church "taken away... many parts which are plain and most precious; and also many covenants." Crucially, the loss happens *after* the record leaves the apostles' hands (v26) — it is the transmission, not the original, that was stripped. So "an exceedingly great many do stumble." Plainness is the casualty; restoring plainness is the cure.
- *The abominable church is defined by desire, not by creed (v6-9).* Its marks are worldliness — "gold, and silver, and silks... the harlots" — and persecution "for the praise of the world." It is not a denomination; ch 14:10 will reduce all churches to two, "the church of the Lamb of God, and... the church of the devil," the latter being whatever fights the Lamb. The diagnostic is appetite and the praise of the world, not a doctrinal label — which keeps the warning aimed at *everyone*, including the covenant Gentiles.
- *Providence and tragedy stated in the same breath (v12-15).* The Spirit drives the discovery and settling of the Americas — and the very same passage names "the wrath of God... upon the seed of thy brethren," who are "scattered... and smitten" before the Gentiles. Nephi's vision of the founding is not triumphalist; the blessing of the Gentiles and the displacement of the people already on the land are one event seen from heaven. The book refuses to celebrate the one without naming the other.
- *The complexion-language recurs (v15).* The prospering Gentiles are "white, and exceedingly fair and beautiful, like unto my people before they were slain" — the same descriptor flagged at 12:23 (cf 2 Ne 5:21), now applied to the Gentiles and indexed to the righteous covenant people. The thread continues; I keep the tension named (study territory with 2 Ne 5).
- *Mercy precedes the warning (v30-34).* The Gentiles who "humble themselves" receive the power of God; the Lord "will not suffer that the Gentiles shall forever remain in that awful state of blindness" — He restores the plain gospel *to them*. Chapter 14 brings the conditional warning; chapter 13 leads with mercy.
