# 1 Nephi 21 — Isaiah 49: "I Have Graven Thee Upon the Palms of My Hands"

*(Isaiah block — full exegesis + Nephite framing + the four likening questions. Compare Isaiah 49.)*

**Binding idea:** Why does Nephi give his family — just told their seed will be destroyed — the chapter where God swears He will never forget the scattered?

**Read (the text):** The chapter opens (in the Book of Mormon) to "all ye that are broken off and are driven out because of the wickedness of the pastors of my people... all ye that are broken off, that are scattered abroad." The Servant speaks: "the Lord hath called me from the womb... hath made my mouth like a sharp sword... made me a polished shaft; in his quiver hath he hid me... Thou art my servant, O Israel, in whom I will be glorified." Though the Servant says "I have labored in vain," God answers that it is "a light thing" merely to "raise up the tribes of Jacob" — "I will also give thee for a light to the Gentiles, that thou mayest be my salvation unto the ends of the earth." To "him whom man despiseth... kings shall see and arise." The Servant is given "for a covenant of the people... that thou mayest say to the prisoners: Go forth; to them that sit in darkness: Show yourselves." Israel is gathered "from far... from the north and from the west; and... the land of Sinim." Then Zion's doubt and God's answer: "Zion hath said: The Lord hath forsaken me... but he will show that he hath not. For can a woman forget her sucking child...? Yea, they may forget, yet will I not forget thee. Behold, I have graven thee upon the palms of my hands; thy walls are continually before me." The Gentiles "shall bring thy sons in their arms... and kings shall be thy nursing fathers." And "all flesh shall know that I, the Lord, am thy Savior and thy Redeemer, the Mighty One of Jacob."

**Exegesis + the BoM-vs-KJV variant:**
- *The signature variant (v1), verified against both texts this session:* KJV Isaiah 49:1 begins "Listen, O isles, unto me; and hearken, ye people, from far." 1 Nephi 21:1 *prepends* an entire address: "Hearken, O ye house of Israel, all ye that are broken off and are driven out because of the wickedness of the pastors of my people; yea, all ye that are broken off, that are scattered abroad." The expansion frames the whole chapter toward the *scattered remnant* — precisely whom Nephi is reading it to. The Book of Mormon hands Isaiah 49 a new opening that makes its audience Nephi's own people.
- Isaiah 49 is the second Servant Song. The Servant is named "Israel" (v3) yet has a mission *to* Israel ("to raise up the tribes of Jacob," v6) — the classic dual identity: the Servant is corporate Israel *and* the individual Messiah who restores it. The mission overflows national bounds: "a light to the Gentiles... my salvation unto the ends of the earth" (v6, the words Simeon will speak over the infant Christ, Luke 2:32). The despised Servant is exalted before kings (v7, anticipating Isaiah 53).
- *"Graven thee upon the palms of my hands" (v16):* anciently a memory-engraving, but for the reader who knows the cross, the nail-wounds (Zech 13:6, the footnote — "wounds in thine hands"). God's covenant memory is cut into His own hands; He cannot forget Israel without looking past His scars. Read against ch 19's Christology (the God of Israel crucified), the palms are literal.
- *"Can a woman forget her sucking child... yet will I not forget thee" (v15):* God's faithfulness measured against — and exceeding — a nursing mother's, with the sober admission "yea, they may forget" (human mothers can fail), "yet will I not." A tender, maternal image of divine love.
- *The prisoners freed (v9):* "say to the prisoners: Go forth; to them that sit in darkness: Show yourselves" — read latter-day as the redemption of the dead (D&C 138 cites this), and as spiritual deliverance generally.
- *The JST note (v24-25):* the footnote preserves the JST reading — "the mighty God shall deliver his covenant people" — clarifying that the "lawful captives" are the covenant people God reclaims even from the mighty.

**Why Nephi set it here — the four likening questions:**
1. *Why this chapter, here?* Because Nephi has just shown his family their seed's destruction (ch 12) and read them Israel's nominal failure (ch 20, Isaiah 48) — and now he needs to give them *hope* (19:24, "that ye may have hope"). Isaiah 49 is the answer: the broken-off and scattered are not forgotten; they are gathered, comforted, graven on God's hands. The BoM v1 expansion ("all ye that are broken off") makes the addressee unmistakably his people.
2. *Was it for us, the latter-day readers?* Yes. The gathering "from the four quarters," the light to the Gentiles, the Gentiles bringing Israel's children "in their arms," kings as "nursing fathers," the prisoners freed — all read as the latter-day gathering and the redemption of the dead (D&C 133, 138). The chapter is one of the book's load-bearing gathering texts.
3. *How does it apply to us?* The center is v14-16: to anyone who says "the Lord hath forsaken me... hath forgotten me," the answer is the nail-prints. God's memory of you is wounded into His palms; your "walls are continually before me." For the broken-off — anyone in exile, cut off, feeling forgotten — this chapter is addressed *by name*.
4. *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* Nephi reads the Servant as both Israel and the Messiah, and the gathering as his own family's hope after the verdict of destruction. He gives a despairing remnant the chapter that says the scattered come home and the forsaken are engraved on God's hands. And Jacob will take this very chapter (Isaiah 49) and preach it at length (2 Ne 6-10) — the family's hope, expounded into a sermon.
