# 1 Nephi 15 — "Have Ye Inquired of the Lord?" (The Seek-and-Find Payoff)

**Binding idea:** Why can Nephi see what his brothers cannot — and what does he finally tell them the dream means?

**Read:** Nephi returns from his vision to find his brothers "disputing one with another" about Lehi's words on the olive tree and the Gentiles — words "hard to be understood, save a man should inquire of the Lord," which "they being hard in their hearts" did not do. Nephi is first "overcome because of my afflictions... great above all, because of the destruction of my people, for I had beheld their fall." Recovered, he asks the cause of their dispute; they "cannot understand" the olive tree. He asks: "Have ye inquired of the Lord?" Their answer: "We have not; for the Lord maketh no such thing known unto us." Nephi presses the promise — "If ye will not harden your hearts, and ask me in faith, believing that ye shall receive, with diligence in keeping my commandments, surely these things shall be made known unto you" — and then explains: Israel is the olive tree; their family a broken-off branch; in the latter days, after the seed dwindles in unbelief, the fulness of the gospel comes to the Gentiles and "from the Gentiles unto the remnant of our seed," who will then know "the very points of his doctrine, that they may know how to come unto him and be saved." When they ask the dream's symbols, he gives them: the tree is the tree of life; the rod is the word of God, which whoso holds fast "would never perish"; the river is "filthiness" — "so much was his mind swallowed up in other things that he beheld not the filthiness of the water"; the gulf is "that awful hell... prepared for the wicked." Filthy works make a filthy soul, and "the kingdom of God is not filthy... there must needs be a place of filthiness prepared." The fruit of the tree "is the greatest of all the gifts of God."

**Thoughts:**
- *"Have ye inquired of the Lord?" is the payoff of the whole vision sequence (v8-9).* Everything since 10:17 was building to this. Nephi *desired*, *believed*, *asked*, and *saw* (10:17 → 11:1-6). His brothers: "We have not [inquired]; for the Lord maketh no such thing known unto us." Two failures in one sentence — they did not ask, *and* they had pre-decided that God does not reveal. The unbelief is self-sealing: assume no revelation, so don't ask, so receive none, and conclude "see, no revelation." Nephi's diagnosis (v11) names the missing three: faith, the belief that you will receive, and diligence (James 1:5). The difference between the brothers was never access. It was the asking — and the believing they would be answered.
- *Lehi "beheld not the filthiness of the water" (v27) — a detail not in ch 8.* Nephi adds something striking: Lehi's "mind was swallowed up in other things" — the tree, the fruit — so that "he beheld not the filthiness of the water" running right beside it. The man fixed on the fruit did not even register the filth. It is a quiet discipleship key: attention is selective, and where it rests shapes what you see. The building-dwellers obsess over the river and the gulf; the fruit-eater barely notices them. You become aware of what you face toward.
- *The kingdom of God is not filthy, so a hell is necessary (v33-34).* Nephi reasons it tight: filthy works → a filthy soul; a filthy soul cannot enter, "if so, the kingdom of God must be filthy also"; since it is not, "there must needs be a place of filthiness prepared." Hell here is not cruelty but the logical consequence of heaven's cleanness — the clean place stays clean precisely because the unclean are elsewhere. The "no unclean thing" of 10:21 is now a syllogism. "The final state of the souls of men is to dwell in the kingdom of God, or to be cast out."
- *Nephi is crushed before he teaches (v4-5).* He returns from the vision "overcome... because of the destruction of my people, for I had beheld their fall." The ch-12 foreknowledge lands as grief; the seer is wounded by what he saw. And only "after I had received strength" does he turn to instruct his quarreling brothers. The prophet who labors for a doomed people (ch 12) pays for the vision in sorrow first.
- *The olive tree explained — graft-back through the Gentiles (v12-18).* The latter-day arc the brothers could not parse: the seed dwindles, the fulness goes to the Gentiles, and "from the Gentiles unto the remnant of our seed," who learn they are covenant Israel and "the very points of his doctrine." This is the book's stated errand toward the remnant (the title page) and the seed of Jacob 5. The covenant under it: "In thy seed shall all the kindreds of the earth be blessed" (Gen 12:3).
- *The rod, the word, the fiery darts (v24).* Nephi re-gives 11:25 to his brothers and adds the promise: hold fast to the word and "neither could the temptations and the fiery darts of the adversary overpower them" (Eph 6:16). Holding fast is the defense; letting go is how the darts land.
- *The fruit is "the greatest of all the gifts of God" (v36).* The block closes on the supremacy of the gift. The tree's fruit — the love of God (11:22), eternal life — is "the greatest of all the gifts of God" (the phrase D&C 14:7 reserves for eternal life). So: tree = love of God = eternal life = the greatest gift. The vision that began as a father's dream (ch 8) ends here naming what the fruit was worth all along.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. 15:8-11 is the seek-and-find capstone — the clearest statement that the brothers' difference from Nephi was *the asking*, not access. The "beheld not the filthiness" detail (15:27) is a small gem worth its own teaching. This closes the first block (chs 1–15: exodus + plates + Ishmael + the two visions + their interpretation). Ch 16 returns to the trail — the Liahona and the broken bow.
