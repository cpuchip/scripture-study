# 1 Nephi 7 — The Cycle Set: Rebellion, Bands, Deliverance, Forgiveness

**Binding idea:** What recurring pattern does the journey for Ishmael's family establish — and how does Nephi diagnose his brothers' unbelief?

**Read:** The Lord tells Lehi it is "not meet" to go into the wilderness alone — the sons must take wives "that they might raise up seed unto the Lord" — so Nephi and his brothers return again to Jerusalem for Ishmael and his household. The Lord "did soften the heart of Ishmael," and the family joins them. On the road, Laman, Lemuel, two of Ishmael's daughters, and two of his sons rebel, wanting to return to Jerusalem. Grieved, Nephi exhorts: "how is it that ye are so hard in your hearts, and so blind in your minds, that ye have need that I, your younger brother, should... set an example for you?" He presses a litany of *forgettings* — the angel, the deliverance from Laban, the record, that "the Lord is able to do all things... if... they exercise faith" — and warns that Jerusalem will be destroyed, that "the Spirit of the Lord ceaseth soon to strive with them" (they have cast Jeremiah into prison and driven out Lehi), and that to return is to perish. They bind him with cords to leave him for wild beasts. Nephi prays — "according to my faith which is in thee, wilt thou deliver me... give me strength that I may burst these bands" — and the bands loosen. When the brothers rage again, Ishmael's daughter, her mother, and a son plead until "they did soften their hearts." The brothers bow, beg forgiveness; Nephi "did frankly forgive them all" and urges them to pray for God's forgiveness. They reach the tent and offer sacrifice.

**Thoughts:**
- *This chapter is the template for the whole Laman-and-Lemuel cycle.* Rebellion → Nephi's exhortation → violence against Nephi → divine deliverance → hearts softened (often by intercession) → repentance and forgiveness → family sacrifice. The same sequence recurs at the broken bow (ch 16), the ship-building (17), and the voyage (18). Ch 7 is the first full turn of a wheel that spins through 1 Nephi — and prefigures the Nephite pride-cycle of the whole book. The drama is not new each time; it is a pattern hardening.
- *Nephi diagnoses unbelief as forgetting.* His exhortation is built on one verb: "how is it that ye have *forgotten*" — the angel, Laban, the record, God's power (v10-12). The opposite of murmuring is remembrance. Laman and Lemuel's defect is not a lack of evidence — they saw an angel — but amnesia; they cannot hold the evidence for a week. This is why "remember" becomes the book's most-pressed imperative, already loaded at 2:24's "ways of remembrance."
- *Soften-heart, twice, and once through people (v5, v19).* The Lord softens Ishmael's heart (v5); then Ishmael's daughter, wife, and son soften Laman and Lemuel's (v19). The same motif from 2:16 — but here the Lord softens hearts *through human intercession*. Mercy arrives by way of the people willing to plead. (Links to `softening-what-i-cannot-soften`; a new facet — intercessory softening.)
- *Deliverance by faith bursts the bands (v17-18).* Bound, Nephi prays "according to my faith," and the cords loose. This is the first instance of a pattern that returns at Ammonihah (Alma 14:28) and with the three Nephites (3 Ne 28:20); the footnote even reaches to Samson (Judg 14:6). Faith here is not just belief but a power that acts on the body's chains.
- *The younger sets the example (v8).* "ye have need that I, your younger brother, should... set an example for you?" The birthright inversion (2:22; 3:29) restated as moral indictment: the younger must teach the elder. The footnote sends to D&C 1:19, 23 — the weak things confound the wise. The Lord's recurring reversal of natural precedence.
- *"Frankly forgive."* After an attempted murder, Nephi forgives "frankly" — freely, without exacting terms (the same word and posture as Luke 7:42, where the creditor "frankly forgave them both"). And he does not stop at forgiving; he urges them to seek God's forgiveness too. The chapter's ethical peak is not the miracle of the bands but the immediacy of the pardon.
- *History confirms the plates (v14).* "Jeremiah have they cast into prison" — a real event (Jer 37:15), and a quiet corroboration of ch 5's claim that the brass plates carried "many prophecies... by the mouth of Jeremiah." Nephi reads current events through a prophet whose words he now physically holds.
