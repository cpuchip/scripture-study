# Jacob 3 — "More Righteous Than You": The Despised Held Up as the Standard

*(Jacob's sermon concludes — comfort to the pure in heart; the Lamanites' fidelity shames the Nephites. Mini-study. Contains skin-curse language — see flag.)*

**Binding idea:** After the daggers of chapter 2, Jacob turns to comfort the wounded — then does something jarring: he holds up the despised, "cursed" Lamanites as *more righteous* than the proud Nephites. What does that reversal do to the skin-curse language the chapter itself uses?

**Read:** "I, Jacob, would speak unto you that are pure in heart. **Look unto God with firmness of mind, and pray unto him with exceeding faith, and he will console you in your afflictions**" (v1); "feast upon his love" (v2). But "wo, wo, unto you that are not pure in heart" (v3). Then the reversal: "the Lamanites your brethren, whom ye hate because of their filthiness and the cursing which hath come upon their skins, **are more righteous than you**; for they have not forgotten the commandment of the Lord... that they should have save it were one wife, and concubines they should have none" (v5). "**Their husbands love their wives, and their wives love their husbands; and their husbands and their wives love their children**; and their unbelief and their hatred towards you is because of **the iniquity of their fathers**" (v7). "I fear that unless ye shall repent of your sins that **their skins will be whiter than yours, when ye shall be brought with them before the throne of God**" (v8). "Wherefore, a commandment I give unto you... that ye **revile no more against them because of the darkness of their skins**; neither shall ye revile against them because of their filthiness; but ye shall remember your own filthiness" (v9). "Remember your children, how that ye have grieved their hearts because of the example that ye have set before them" (v10); "**arouse the faculties of your souls; shake yourselves that ye may awake from the slumber of death**" (v11). Jacob warns "against fornication and lasciviousness, and every kind of sin" (v12); "a hundredth part of the proceedings of this people... cannot be written upon these plates" (v13); "these plates are called the plates of Jacob" (v14).

**Thoughts:**
- *The reversal: the despised are the standard (vv5-7).* Jacob takes the people the Nephites "hate" and holds them up as "more righteous than you." The measure is not privilege, prosperity, or covenant lineage — it is marriage fidelity and family love: "their husbands love their wives... and their husbands and their wives love their children." The Lamanites kept the one-wife commandment the Nephites broke (ch 2). On the scale that actually weighs, the looked-down-upon outrank the looking-down. This is the proud Nephite's worst nightmare: the people he despises are his moral superiors, and God says so.
- *The skin-curse language, and how Jacob himself empties it (vv5-9) — flagged.* This chapter carries the same hard language as 2 Ne 5:21 ("the cursing which hath come upon their skins," "the darkness of their skins," v8 "their skins will be whiter than yours"). I do **not** resolve the nature or origin of that curse here — that remains for a with-Michael study (bin-4). But I note plainly what Jacob's *own text* does with it, because it is a striking in-text corrective and a third data point for that study (alongside 2 Ne 26:33 and 30:6):
  - He **decouples skin from righteousness**: the dark-skinned are "more righteous" (v5), and the proud light-skinned will be the more filthy "before the throne of God" (v8). Skin is explicitly *not* the measure of worth.
  - He treats their "filthiness" as **inherited circumstance, not personal guilt**: it "came because of their fathers" / "the iniquity of their fathers" (vv7, 9) — the present generation bears a condition it did not choose.
  - He issues a **command against skin-based contempt**: "revile no more against them because of the darkness of their skins... remember your own filthiness" (v9). Reviling by skin is forbidden, and the Nephite is told to look at his own state instead.
  So the chapter that most uses the skin-curse language is also the one that most subverts skin as a marker of worth — the despised are righteous, the reviling is forbidden, the filthiness is moral and inherited, not pigmentary and personal. (For the with-Michael study; not autonomously resolved.)
- *Comfort to the wounded (vv1-2).* Jacob does not leave the tender wives of chapter 2 bleeding. He turns first to "you that are pure in heart" — the wounded faithful — and gives the balm: look to God, pray with exceeding faith, "feast upon his love." The same sermon that wielded daggers (2:9) binds the wounds it did not make. The bold rebuke and the tender consolation are two halves of one pastoral act.
- *"Awake from the slumber of death" (v11).* The awake-call returns (Lehi's "awake, arise from the dust," 2 Ne 1; Isaiah's "awake, awake, O Zion," 2 Ne 8). "Arouse the faculties of your souls; shake yourselves." The same summons threads the whole record: the danger is not only sin but sleep — and the cure is to rouse yourself before the slumber becomes the second death.
