# Jacob 4 — "Looking Beyond the Mark": How a People Loses Plainness

*(Jacob's record continues — the law as a type of Christ; the Jews' blindness; the rejected foundation stone. Mini-study. Sets up Jacob 5.)*

**Binding idea:** Jacob diagnoses how a covenant people went blind — not by ignorance but by *looking beyond the mark*. What is that, and how does it sit against the plainness he keeps preaching?

**Read:** Jacob writes that what is engraved on plates "must remain" (vv1-2), "that they may know that we knew of Christ... many hundred years before his coming," as did "all the holy prophets which were before us" (v4). "We keep the law of Moses, **it pointing our souls to him**" — and Abraham offering Isaac "is a **similitude of God and his Only Begotten Son**" (v5). By searching the prophets and revelation, faith becomes unshaken, so that "the very trees obey us, or the mountains, or the waves of the sea" (v6) — "nevertheless, the Lord God showeth us our weakness **that we may know that it is by his grace, and his great condescensions**... that we have power to do these things" (v7). "How unsearchable are the depths of the mysteries of him... despise not the revelations of God" (v8). "**Seek not to counsel the Lord, but to take counsel from his hand**" (v10). "**Be reconciled unto him through the atonement of Christ**" (v11). "Why not speak of the atonement of Christ, and attain to a perfect knowledge of him?" (v12). "He that prophesieth, let him prophesy to the understanding of men; for **the Spirit speaketh the truth and lieth not. Wherefore, it speaketh of things as they really are, and of things as they really will be**" (v13). "The Jews were a stiffnecked people; and they despised the words of plainness, and killed the prophets... **because of their blindness, which blindness came by looking beyond the mark, they must needs fall; for God hath taken away his plainness from them**... because they desired it" (v14). By their stumbling "they will reject the **stone** upon which they might build and have safe foundation" (v15); "this stone shall become the great, and the last, and the only sure foundation" (v16). "I will unfold this mystery unto you" (v18 — → Jacob 5).

**Thoughts:**
- *"Looking beyond the mark" (v14) — the master diagnosis.* Jacob names the precise mechanism of the Jews' blindness: not too little knowledge but a wrong appetite — "they despised the words of plainness... and sought for things that they could not understand." Blindness "came by looking beyond the mark." The mark is Christ, plainly given; looking beyond it is preferring the obscure, the complex, the clever — and missing the target standing right in front of you. And the judgment fits the appetite: "God hath taken away his plainness from them... because they desired it." A people that disdains the plain truth is given the obscurity it wanted, and stumbles on it. This is the whole plainness thread (2 Ne 25, 31, 32; Jacob 2:11) given its negative: the alternative to plainness is not depth but blindness.
- *Truth defined (v13).* "The Spirit speaketh the truth and lieth not. Wherefore, it speaketh of things as they really are, and of things as they really will be." This is the seed of the Restoration's definition of truth (D&C 93:24, "things as they are, and as they were, and as they are to come"). Truth is not consensus or sentiment; it is *things as they really are* — reality itself, present and future, spoken by the Spirit. The cure for looking beyond the mark is a Spirit that reports reality plainly.
- *Power by grace, shown beside weakness (v7).* Jacob and his brethren can command trees and waves "in the name of Jesus" — and immediately he disarms the boast: "the Lord God showeth us our weakness that we may know that it is by his grace, and his great condescensions... that we have power to do these things." The power that flows *through* the instrument is grace, and God deliberately keeps the instrument aware of its weakness so it cannot mistake the power for its own. This is the positive form of the axe (2 Ne 20:15) and the made-thing (27:27): not "I did this by my strength" but "weakness shown, that we may know it is grace." The condescension makes the conduit, and keeps the conduit humble.
- *"Take counsel from his hand," don't counsel him (v10).* "Seek not to counsel the Lord, but to take counsel from his hand." The posture toward God is receptive, not advisory — we do not improve God's plan; we receive it. (The inverse of "looking beyond the mark": the mark-looker thinks he sees further than the plain word; the counsel-giver thinks he sees better than God.) Both are pride dressed as insight.
- *The rejected stone (vv15-17).* Jacob foresees the Jews rejecting "the stone upon which they might build" — Christ, the cornerstone (Ps 118:22; Isa 28:16) — and asks the question Jacob 5 will answer: "how is it possible that these, after having rejected the sure foundation, can ever build upon it?" The mystery of how the rejecters can yet be grafted back is the cliffhanger that sets up Zenos's olive-tree allegory.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off (Jacob 5 next is the flagged one). The chapter's spine is **"looking beyond the mark"** (v14) — the diagnosis of how a covenant people goes blind: not ignorance but a disdain for plainness and an appetite for the obscure, answered by God removing the plainness they despised. It is the negative of the whole plainness thread, and a sharp warning against over-intellectualizing faith past its plain center (Christ). The chapter also gives **the Restoration's definition of truth** ("things as they really are, and... as they really will be," v13 → D&C 93:24) and the positive form of the instrument theme (**power by grace, weakness shown "that we may know,"** v7). v18 sets up Jacob 5.
