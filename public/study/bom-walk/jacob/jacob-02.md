# Jacob 2 — The Sermon That Wounds the Guilty to Spare the Wounded

*(Jacob's temple sermon — pride/riches, then chastity. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Jacob must rebuke two sins — pride in riches and unchastity — and both turn out to be the strong wounding the weak. Why does he grieve so over a sermon he is commanded to give, and what is the order he sets for riches?

**Read:** Jacob comes to the temple "that I might rid my garments of your sins" (v2), but it grieves him: the bold rebuke he must give the guilty will "enlarge the wounds of those who are already wounded" — the tender, chaste wives who "have come up hither to hear the pleasing word of God" now get "daggers placed to pierce their souls" (vv7-9). Still, he must "tell the truth according to the plainness of the word of God" (v11). **First sin — pride in riches:** they have sought gold and silver, and "because some of you have obtained more abundantly... ye are lifted up in the pride of your hearts, and wear stiff necks and high heads because of the costliness of your apparel, and persecute your brethren because ye suppose that ye are better than they" (v13). The teaching: "**Think of your brethren like unto yourselves, and be familiar with all and free with your substance, that they may be rich like unto you**" (v17); "**before ye seek for riches, seek ye for the kingdom of God. And after ye have obtained a hope in Christ ye shall obtain riches, if ye seek them; and ye will seek them for the intent to do good — to clothe the naked, and to feed the hungry, and to liberate the captive, and administer relief to the sick and the afflicted**" (vv18-19). "The one being is as precious in his sight as the other" (v21). **Second, "grosser" sin — unchastity:** they excuse "whoredoms" by David and Solomon (v23). "David and Solomon truly had many wives and concubines, which thing was **abominable before me**, saith the Lord" (v24). "There shall not any man among you have save it be **one wife; and concubines he shall have none**" (v27); "I, the Lord God, **delight in the chastity of women**" (v28); "if I will... raise up seed unto me, I will command my people; otherwise they shall hearken unto these things" (v30). The Lord has heard "the mourning of the daughters of my people... because of the wickedness... of their husbands" (v31). "**Ye have broken the hearts of your tender wives, and lost the confidence of your children, because of your bad examples**" (v35).

**Thoughts:**
- *Both sins are the strong wounding the weak.* Pride-in-riches makes the wealthy "persecute your brethren" (v13); unchastity "broke the hearts of your tender wives" and "lost the confidence of your children" (v35). The thread connecting the two halves of the sermon is the same: the strong using their advantage (wealth, power over women) to wound those beneath them. Jacob is not preaching two unrelated vices; he is preaching one — *the abuse of the vulnerable by the advantaged.*
- *The sermon's anguish: bold for the sake of the wounded (vv6-10).* Jacob "shrink[s] with shame" to give this rebuke, because the same boldness that pierces the guilty also wounds the innocent women in the room "whose feelings are exceedingly tender and chaste." This is the watchman's burden of 1:19 in live action: he must wound, knowing it costs the broken-hearted more pain — but silence would let the wounding go on. His fierceness toward the perpetrators is *driven by* tenderness for the victims. The sermon is hard on the men precisely because it is for the women.
- *The order of riches (vv17-19) — the clearest scriptural wealth ethic.* Riches are not condemned; two things are tested: **order** and **intent**. *Order:* "before ye seek for riches, seek ye for the kingdom of God." *Intent:* once you have a hope in Christ, "ye will seek them for the intent to do good — to clothe the naked, and to feed the hungry." Wealth sought first, for itself, breeds the pride of v13; wealth sought after the kingdom, for the poor, is sanctified. And the social vision is startling: "be familiar with all and free with your substance, **that they may be rich like unto you**" — not charity that keeps the gap, but generosity that closes it.
- *"The one being is as precious as the other" (v21).* The antidote to the pride of v13 ("ye suppose that ye are better than they") is the doctrine that runs through the whole walk: equality before God. "All flesh is of the dust; and for the selfsame end hath he created them." The rich man's supposed superiority is an illusion the Creator does not share. (Same root as 2 Ne 26:33, "all are alike unto God.")
- *The chastity teaching (vv24-30), on the text.* Jacob states the standard plainly: monogamy — "one wife; and concubines he shall have none" — because "I, the Lord God, delight in the chastity of women." David and Solomon's many wives were "abominable." And v30 names the lone exception as God's own prerogative: "if I will... raise up seed unto me, I will command my people; otherwise they shall hearken unto these things." The text's logic is clear: the standard is one wife; any departure is by God's explicit command for raising seed, never by men excusing themselves from scripture (v23, "they understand not the scriptures"). The motive Jacob exposes is *self-justification* — using David and Solomon as a license — and that is exactly what God forbids.
- *The victims named (vv31-35).* The sermon ends not on the men's sin in the abstract but on its measured cost: "the mourning of the daughters of my people," "the sobbings of their hearts," "ye have broken the hearts of your tender wives, and lost the confidence of your children." God hears the women's weeping and will not "suffer" it. The sin is weighed on the scale of the broken hearts it produced.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The chapter's spine: **both sins are the strong wounding the weak** (pride persecutes the poor; unchastity breaks the wives' hearts), and Jacob's anguish is the watchman's burden in action — bold for the sake of the wounded. The **order-and-intent wealth ethic** (vv17-19: kingdom first, then riches to do good, "that they may be rich like unto you") is one of the clearest in scripture. The **chastity teaching** (vv24-30) is handled on the text: monogamy is the standard ("one wife; concubines none"; "I delight in the chastity of women"), with v30 as God's own seed-raising exception — and the sin Jacob exposes is *self-justification from scripture* (v23).
