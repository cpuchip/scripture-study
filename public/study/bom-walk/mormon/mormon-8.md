# Mormon 8 — Moroni alone; the record from the dust; "Jesus Christ hath shown you unto me"

**Binding idea:** Moroni takes up the record alone. "I, Moroni, do finish the record of my father, Mormon... my father also was killed by them, and I even remain alone to write the sad tale of the destruction of my people... And whether they will slay me, I know not" (8:1-3). "I have not friends nor whither to go; and how long the Lord will suffer that I may live I know not" (8:5). He testifies the record will come forth "out of darkness unto light... by the power of God" (8:16), that "if there be faults they be the faults of a man" (8:17), and that "the eternal purposes of the Lord shall roll on, until all his promises shall be fulfilled" (8:22). Then the prophecy of our day: "it shall come even as if one should speak from the dead... in a day when the power of God shall be denied, and churches become defiled and be lifted up in the pride of their hearts" (8:26-28). And the staggering address: "Behold, I speak unto you as if ye were present, and yet ye are not. But behold, *Jesus Christ hath shown you unto me, and I know your doing*" (8:35) — "ye do love money, and your substance, and your fine apparel, and the adorning of your churches, *more than ye love the poor and the needy, the sick and the afflicted*" (8:37).

**Read:** Mormon 8 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 41 verses. The last man of the Nephites, the record from the dust, and the prophecy of the latter day.

## Thoughts

The chapter opens on the loneliest voice in scripture. "I, Moroni... even remain alone to write the sad tale of the destruction of my people." His father is dead, killed by the Lamanites. Every Nephite who escaped Cumorah has been hunted down "until they were all destroyed." Moroni is the last — one man, of a people that once numbered like the sands of the sea, sitting with the plates, writing. "I have not friends nor whither to go; and how long the Lord will suffer that I may live I know not. And whether they will slay me, I know not." Read those sentences slowly. This is a man with no one left — no family, no nation, no home, no future he can see — performing the one task still given him: finishing the record. He does not know if he will be alive tomorrow. He does not know where he will go. And he keeps writing. There is a particular kind of faithfulness in that which the rest of us rarely have to summon: to do the work you were given when every external reason for hope is gone, when there is no community to encourage you and no certainty you will even survive the night. Moroni writes into the dark, for readers he cannot see, with no guarantee he will finish. The Book of Mormon's final voice is a man utterly alone, kept going by nothing but the commandment and the love of God.

And what sustains him is not denial of his situation but a fierce clarity about what the record *is*. "Whoso receiveth this record, and shall not condemn it because of the imperfections which are in it, the same shall know of greater things than these" (8:12). The record has imperfections — Moroni is honest about that — and the condition of receiving its greater things is humility, the willingness not to dismiss it over its human faults. "If there be faults they be the faults of a man... nevertheless God knoweth all things" (8:17). This is a stunning posture for a prophet writing scripture: he does not claim the record is flawless; he claims it is *true*, and warns that the reader who condemns it over its human imperfections endangers himself, because the faults are the writer's and the truth is God's. And the worth is precisely located: "the plates thereof are of no worth... but the record thereof is of great worth" (8:14). The gold is worthless; the words are priceless. Anyone seeking the plates "to get gain" will be disappointed (the very warning Moroni would later give Joseph Smith, JS-H 1:53); the treasure was never the metal. And the coming-forth is certain: "it shall be brought out of darkness unto light... out of the earth, and it shall shine forth out of darkness, and come unto the knowledge of the people; and it shall be done by the power of God" (8:16). A book buried by the last man of a dead people will rise from the ground fourteen centuries later — and Moroni, alone with the plates, knows it.

The reason he is so certain is verse 22, and it is the bedrock under the whole chapter: "the eternal purposes of the Lord shall roll on, until all his promises shall be fulfilled." Nothing can stop the work — not the destruction of the Nephites, not the burial of the record, not the wrath of those who "breathe out wrath and strifes against the work of the Lord" and say "We will destroy the work of the Lord." The work of God cannot be frustrated (D&C 3:1-3). Moroni, who has just watched his entire civilization destroyed, who has every external reason to conclude that God's purposes *can* be defeated, declares the opposite from the ruins: the purposes of the Lord roll on. The destruction of his people was not the defeat of God's work; it was a chapter in it. The record would come forth; the covenant would be kept; the promises would be fulfilled. From inside the total wreckage of everything he loved, Moroni sees that the wreckage is not the end of the story.

And then the chapter turns to the most direct prophet-to-reader moment in all of scripture (8:26-35). Moroni describes the day the record will come forth, and it is unmistakably *our* day: "it shall come even as if one should speak from the dead" (8:26) — the voice from the dust of Isaiah 29:4, the destroyed people crying a warning to the living. He sees "secret combinations and the works of darkness" (8:27); "the power of God denied, and churches... lifted up in the pride of their hearts," with "leaders of churches and teachers" rising "in the pride of their hearts, even to the envying of them who belong to their churches" (8:28); "fires, and tempests, and vapors of smoke in foreign lands... wars, rumors of wars, and earthquakes" (8:29-30); "great pollutions upon the face of the earth" (8:31); the antinomian lie that "it mattereth not, for the Lord will uphold such at the last day" (8:31, the "all is well in Zion" deception of 2 Ne 28:8); and "churches built up that shall say: Come unto me, and for your money you shall be forgiven of your sins" (8:32) — forgiveness for sale. And then the verse that should stop every reader cold: "Behold, I speak unto you as if ye were present, and yet ye are not. But behold, *Jesus Christ hath shown you unto me, and I know your doing*" (8:35). Moroni *saw us*. Alone in the ruins of his nation, Christ showed him the latter-day reader — showed him us, by name and deed, "I know your doing" — and what he wrote, he wrote *to* people he had been shown in vision fifteen centuries before they would be born. The intimacy and the weight of that are almost unbearable. The book is not a generic message that happens to apply to us; it was written by a man who was shown our faces and our deeds and addressed us directly. When we read Mormon 8, we are reading a letter written specifically to us by someone who saw us.

And what he saw, he names without flinching (8:36-41), and it is a portrait of pride. "I know that ye do walk in the pride of your hearts; and there are none save a few only who do not lift themselves up in the pride of their hearts, unto the wearing of very fine apparel, unto envying, and strifes, and malice... and your churches, yea, even every one, have become polluted because of the pride of your hearts" (8:36). The same pride that destroyed his own people, the same pride that began the fall of Zion through "costly apparel" (4 Ne 1:24) — Moroni sees it in us, and he sees it as nearly universal: "none save a few only." But the heart of the indictment is verse 37, and it is the measuring line the entire Book of Mormon keeps returning to: "ye do love money, and your substance, and your fine apparel, and the adorning of your churches, *more than ye love the poor and the needy, the sick and the afflicted*." That is the test. Not what we profess, not how we adorn our worship, but whether we love our possessions more than we love the poor. Moroni's vision of the latter-day church is not of heresy in the abstract but of a specific, concrete failure: beautiful buildings and fine clothes and full accounts, and the hungry and the sick and the naked passing by unnoticed (8:39). "Why do ye adorn yourselves with that which hath no life, and yet suffer the hungry, and the needy, and the naked, and the sick and the afflicted to pass by you, and notice them not?" We adorn the lifeless — gold, fine cloth, ornamented buildings — while ignoring the living who suffer. This is the indictment of *us*, from a man who watched a civilization die of exactly this disease, who was shown our day and grieved it.

There is a terrible mercy in this chapter. Moroni did not have to see us; Christ showed him, and he wrote down what he saw, *for us* — not to condemn us for sport but to warn us, because he had watched where this road ends. The voice from the dust is the voice of a people who took this exact road to annihilation, calling out to the people walking it again: *we did this; here is where it goes; turn.* The pride, the love of money over the poor, the polluted churches, the selling of forgiveness, the envying leaders — Moroni saw all of it consume the Nephites, and he saw it again in us, and his whole purpose in writing is that we might not end as his people ended. The last man of a dead nation spends his lonely strength warning the living. That is why the book exists. And the reader who receives it "and shall not condemn it" (8:12) — who has the humility to hear a dead man's warning rather than dismiss the messenger — is the one who will know the greater things.
