# Mormon 9 — the God of miracles who changes not; the misery of the unclean; Moroni's farewell

**Binding idea:** Moroni turns to "those who do not believe in Christ" (9:1). To them, the doctrine of guilt: "ye would be more miserable to dwell with a holy and just God, under a consciousness of your filthiness before him, than ye would to dwell with the damned souls in hell... it will kindle a flame of unquenchable fire upon you" (9:4-5) — so "turn ye unto the Lord; cry mightily unto the Father in the name of Jesus, that perhaps ye may be found spotless, pure, fair, and white, having been cleansed by the blood of the Lamb" (9:6). Then the great argument against cessationism: "God is the same yesterday, today, and forever, and in him there is no variableness neither shadow of changing" (9:9); "if there were miracles wrought then, why has God ceased to be a God of miracles and yet be an unchangeable Being?... he changeth not; if so he would cease to be God" (9:19); "the reason why he ceaseth to do miracles among the children of men is because that they dwindle in unbelief" (9:20). And his farewell: "I speak unto you as though I spake from the dead... Condemn me not because of mine imperfection... but rather give thanks unto God that he hath made manifest unto you our imperfections, that ye may learn to be more wise than we have been" (9:30-31).

**Read:** Mormon 9 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 37 verses. The appeal to the unbeliever, the case for a God of miracles, and the close of the book of Mormon.

## Thoughts

Moroni opens this final chapter of his father's book by turning to the hardest audience: "those who do not believe in Christ." And he does not begin with proofs; he begins with a question about the day of judgment that reframes what hell actually is. "Do ye suppose that ye could be happy to dwell with that holy Being, when your souls are racked with a consciousness of guilt that ye have ever abused his laws? Behold, I say unto you that ye would be *more miserable to dwell with a holy and just God, under a consciousness of your filthiness before him, than ye would to dwell with the damned souls in hell*" (9:3-4). This is one of the most important things the Book of Mormon teaches about damnation, and it overturns the common picture. Hell is not primarily a place God sends people to torture them; it is the unbearable misery of an unclean soul in the presence of perfect holiness. The wicked person, placed in heaven, would not be comforted — he would be in agony, because "your nakedness before God, and also the glory of God, and the holiness of Jesus Christ... will kindle a flame of unquenchable fire upon you" (9:5). The fire is the consciousness of guilt itself, burning hotter the brighter the holiness it stands before. This is exactly Alma's experience — "the very thoughts of coming into the presence of my God did rack my soul with inexpressible horror" (Alma 36:14). The damned are not kept out of God's presence as an arbitrary penalty; they could not *bear* His presence, and that inability is the punishment. Which means the gospel is not God lowering a bar but God *changing the soul* so that it can finally bear, and even delight in, the holiness it once could not endure. The call follows naturally: "turn ye unto the Lord; cry mightily unto the Father... that perhaps ye may be found spotless, pure, fair, and white, having been cleansed by the blood of the Lamb." The only way to be able to stand in the fire of holiness is to be made clean before you get there.

Then comes the heart of the chapter and the climax of an argument that has run through this whole closing section — Mormon 1:13-14 (the gifts ceased through the people's unbelief), 3 Nephi 29:6 (wo to him who says the Lord no longer works by gifts) — now given its fullest and most rigorous form. Moroni addresses "you who deny the revelations of God, and say that they are done away, that there are no revelations, nor prophecies, nor gifts, nor healing, nor speaking with tongues" (9:7). And his argument is a clean syllogism built on the nature of God. The premise: "God is the same yesterday, today, and forever, and in him there is no variableness neither shadow of changing" (9:9; Heb 13:8; Jas 1:17). The implication: "if ye have imagined up unto yourselves a god who doth vary... then have ye imagined up unto yourselves a god who is not a God of miracles" (9:10) — a changeable god is, by definition, a false one. Then the historical fact: God *did* work miracles — creation itself ("by his word the heaven and the earth should be," 9:17), Christ's mighty works, the apostles' (9:18). And the conclusion, inescapable: "if there were miracles wrought then, *why has God ceased to be a God of miracles and yet be an unchangeable Being? And behold, I say unto you he changeth not; if so he would cease to be God; and he ceaseth not to be God, and is a God of miracles*" (9:19). The logic is airtight: God does not change; God once worked miracles; therefore God still works miracles — because to say He stopped is to say He changed, and a God who changes is no God at all. Cessationism does not merely deny a doctrine; it denies the *immutability of God*, and with it, God's deity.

But Moroni does not stop at the syllogism — he answers the obvious objection. If God still works miracles, why do we see so few? Verse 20: "the reason why he ceaseth to do miracles among the children of men is *because that they dwindle in unbelief, and depart from the right way, and know not the God in whom they should trust*." This is the resolution that the whole section has been building toward. Miracles do cease — but not because God changed. They cease because *people* change, because faith withers, because a generation dwindles in unbelief and no longer knows the God who could work them. The variable is never God; it is always us. The heavens are not closed from above; they are shuttered from below, by unbelief. And the corollary is full of hope: where faith returns, so do the miracles — "whoso believeth in Christ, doubting nothing, whatsoever he shall ask the Father in the name of Christ it shall be granted him; and this promise is unto all, even unto the ends of the earth" (9:21). The gifts did not expire with the apostles; they wait, on God's unchanging side, for the faith that can receive them. Moroni cites Christ's own commission — "these signs shall follow them that believe — in my name shall they cast out devils; they shall speak with new tongues... they shall lay hands on the sick and they shall recover" (9:24; Mark 16:17-18) — and the condition is always the same: "whosoever shall believe in my name, doubting nothing" (9:25). The signs follow the faith; they do not precede it or substitute for it.

There is a sharp word in verse 14 worth pausing on, because it sets the stakes: at the judgment, "he that is filthy shall be filthy still; and he that is righteous shall be righteous still; he that is happy shall be happy still; and he that is unhappy shall be unhappy still." This is the fixity of character (Rev 22:11; 2 Ne 9:16) — the recognition that at some point what we have *become* is set, that the self we built across a lifetime of choices is the self we carry into eternity. It is not that God refuses to change the filthy; it is that the day comes when the filthy no longer *will* to be changed, when character has hardened into permanence. This is why Moroni's exhortation is so urgent: "Be wise in the days of your probation; strip yourselves of all uncleanness" (9:28). The days of probation are the days when the soul is still soft enough to be remade. "Work out your own salvation with fear and trembling before him" (9:27; Phil 2:12) — not in terror of an arbitrary judge, but in sober awareness that what you are becoming now is what you will be then.

And then Moroni's farewell, and the humility in it is breathtaking (9:30-37). "I speak unto you as though I spake from the dead; for I know that ye shall have my words" — the voice from the dust again, a man addressing the future as one already gone. And then: "Condemn me not because of mine imperfection, neither my father, because of his imperfection, neither them who have written before him; *but rather give thanks unto God that he hath made manifest unto you our imperfections, that ye may learn to be more wise than we have been*." This is an astonishing thing for a writer of scripture to say. He does not claim perfection for the record; he acknowledges its imperfections — and then asks the reader not merely to forgive them but to *be grateful for them*, because the imperfections are themselves instructive: see what we got wrong, and be wiser. The flaws in the record are not a defect to be apologized away; they are a gift, a chance for the reader to learn from the writers' failures. (He even names the mechanical cause — they wrote in "reformed Egyptian" because the plates were too small for Hebrew, and Hebrew itself had been altered; "if we could have written in Hebrew... ye would have had no imperfection in our record," 9:32-33.) This is the same posture as Mormon 8:17, but warmer: not "condemn it not, lest you be in danger," but "give thanks for the imperfections." It is the humility of a man who knows the treasure is the truth, not the polish — and who trusts God to carry the truth through the imperfect vessel ("the Lord knoweth the things which we have written... he hath prepared means for the interpretation thereof," 9:34).

The book closes on the watchman's discharge and a prayer (9:35-37). "These things are written that we may rid our garments of the blood of our brethren, who have dwindled in unbelief" — the Ezekiel-watchman duty (Ezek 3:18-19; 2 Ne 9:44): the prophet who warns has cleared his garments of the blood of those who would not hear. Mormon and Moroni wrote so that they would not bear the blood of the unbelieving — they discharged their duty by testifying, whatever the people did with it. And the final words of the book of Mormon are not about its writers or even its readers but about *the covenant*: "may God the Father remember the covenant which he hath made with the house of Israel; and may he bless them forever, through faith on the name of Jesus Christ. Amen." The book that began with a family leaving Jerusalem and ends with the last man of their posterity writing alone in the ruins closes not on the destruction but on the covenant — the same covenant that outlasts the fall (Morm 5:20), the promise that the scattered remnant will yet be remembered and blessed. The last word of Moroni's father's book is a prayer for the descendants of the people who destroyed them, that God would remember His covenant and bless them forever. From the ashes, a blessing; from the dust, a prayer for the future.

## Notable

Yes — the close of the book of Mormon, and the great anti-cessationism argument. **Hell is the misery of the unclean soul in the presence of holiness** (9:3-5, more miserable in God's presence than with the damned; the guilt is the fire); **a god who changes is no God — immutability is deity** (9:9-10, 19); **God still works miracles because He changes not** (9:15-19, the syllogism: to deny it denies His deity; the climax of the argument from Morm 1 and 3 Ne 29); **miracles cease only because people dwindle in unbelief** (9:20, the variable is never God; the heavens are shuttered from below); **signs follow those who believe doubting nothing** (9:21-25); **the fixity of character at judgment** (9:14, what we become is set; probation is when the soul is still soft); **condemn not the record's imperfections — give thanks and be wiser** (9:31-34); **the watchman's discharge** (9:35); and **the book closes on the covenant, not the destruction** (9:36-37, a prayer that God remember Israel and bless them forever).
