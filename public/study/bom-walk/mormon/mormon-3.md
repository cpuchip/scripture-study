# Mormon 3 — boasting in their own strength; Mormon refuses to lead; "vengeance is mine"; the turn to the reader

**Binding idea:** The Lord gives one more chance — "Cry unto this people—Repent ye, and come unto me, and be ye baptized, and build up again my church, and ye shall be spared" (3:2) — but Mormon's cry is "in vain," and they "did not realize that it was the Lord that had spared them" (3:3). After two victories, "because of this great thing which my people, the Nephites, had done, they began to boast in their own strength, and began to swear before the heavens that they would avenge themselves... and would cut them off from the face of the land" (3:9-10). At that, "I, Mormon, did utterly refuse from this time forth to be a commander and a leader of this people, because of their wickedness" (3:11) — though "I had loved them, according to the love of God which was in me, with all my heart; and my soul had been poured out in prayer unto my God all the day long for them; nevertheless, it was without faith, because of the hardness of their hearts" (3:12). The Lord declares: "Vengeance is mine, and I will repay; and because this people repented not after I had delivered them, behold, they shall be cut off" (3:15). Mormon stands "as an idle witness" (3:16) — and turns to address the latter-day reader, that "the Jews, the covenant people of the Lord, shall have other witness... that Jesus, whom they slew, was the very Christ and the very God" (3:21).

**Read:** Mormon 3 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 22 verses. The fatal turn to vengeance, the prophet's refusal, and the witness to the reader.

## Thoughts

The chapter opens on mercy that should not, by the logic of the previous chapter, still be available. Mormon had seen "the day of grace was passed" (2:15) — and yet here the Lord commands him to cry repentance *again*: "come unto me, and be ye baptized, and build up again my church, and ye shall be spared." This is worth holding next to the "day of grace passed." The Lord's mercy outruns even the prophet's grim assessment; as long as there is breath, the offer is renewed. But the tragedy of verse 3 is total: the cry was "in vain," and — the saddest detail — "they did not realize that it was the Lord that had spared them, and granted unto them a chance for repentance." They did not even recognize their reprieves as mercy. Every deliverance, every quiet decade between wars, was God holding the door open, and they read it as their own good fortune or their own strength. Ingratitude here is not merely failing to say thanks; it is failing to *perceive the source* — to see God's hand in the very mercies that should have turned them. A people who cannot recognize when they are being spared cannot repent, because they do not know there is anything to repent toward.

And then the fatal turn, verses 9-10, and it is a precise inversion of the Nephite covenant. After two defensive victories, "because of this great thing which my people, the Nephites, had done, they began to boast in their own strength" — not "the Lord delivered us" but "look what we did" — "and began to swear before the heavens that they would avenge themselves... and would cut them off from the face of the land." Two sins compound here. First, the boasting: crediting their own arm for a deliverance that, even now, was the Lord's mercy (the exact opposite of Captain Moroni's people, who never gave offense and always acknowledged God). Second, and worse, the *vengeance-oath*: they swear "by the heavens, and also by the throne of God" — forbidden oaths (3 Ne 12:34, "swear not at all") — to go on the *offensive*, to "cut off" their enemies. This crosses the bright line the Nephites had been taught for centuries: war was permitted only in defense, "they were not to... give an offense... save it were against an enemy, save it were to preserve their lives" (Alma 48:14; 43:46-47). The moment they swore to go up and avenge and exterminate, they abandoned the only ground on which a righteous people may fight. They became the aggressors, swearing by God's throne to do what God forbids.

Mormon's response is one of the most striking moral acts in the Book of Mormon: "I, Mormon, did utterly refuse from this time forth to be a commander and a leader of this people, because of their wickedness and abomination." He resigns. The general who has led them since sixteen lays down his command rather than lead an offensive war of vengeance. This is conscientious objection at the highest level — a refusal to participate in evil even when the evil is being done by your own people, even when refusing means leaving them leaderless before their enemies. And crucially, it is not a refusal *of love*. Verse 12 is careful to insist on this: "I had loved them, according to the love of God which was in me, with all my heart; and my soul had been poured out in prayer unto my God all the day long for them." Mormon's withdrawal is not the abandonment of a man who has stopped caring; it is the limit a man of conscience reaches when the people he loves demand he help them do evil. He kept loving, kept praying — but "it was without faith, because of the hardness of their hearts." There is a heartbreaking honesty in that admission: Mormon prayed for them all day long, but he could not pray *in faith*, because faith requires some basis for expecting the thing prayed for, and their hardness gave him none. He prayed as one prays for the dying he loves but knows will not recover — the prayer is real, the love is real, the faith cannot be manufactured. A prophet can love perfectly and still pray without faith, when the object of the prayer has made itself unreachable.

The Lord's word in verse 15 names the deepest reason the vengeance-oath was the final sin: "Vengeance is mine, and I will repay; and because this people repented not after I had delivered them, behold, they shall be cut off." The Nephites' oath to avenge was not merely bloodthirsty; it was *usurpation*. Vengeance belongs to God alone (Deut 32:35; Rom 12:19) — not because vengeance is wrong in the absolute, but because only a being of perfect justice and perfect knowledge can be trusted with it. For a man to swear vengeance is to claim a prerogative that is God's, to set himself in God's seat. The Nephites swearing "by the throne of God" to take vengeance were, almost literally, trying to sit on that throne. And the Lord's response is not "I forbid it" but something more terrible: very well — "vengeance is mine," and the vengeance I will repay is upon *you*, "because this people repented not after I had delivered them." The people who swore to cut off their enemies will themselves be "cut off from the face of the earth." The usurpation of God's vengeance becomes the occasion of God's vengeance.

And then Mormon's role transforms (v16): "I did stand as an idle witness to manifest unto the world the things which I saw and heard." From commander to *witness*. The word "idle" is exact and a little startling — he does nothing, militarily; he stands aside; he watches. But this is not passivity for its own sake. He stands as an "idle witness" precisely so that he can "manifest unto the world the things which I saw and heard." The sober child who was "quick to observe" (1:2) has come full circle: his life's work was never the army but the *record*. When he could no longer lead them in righteousness, he could still witness them truthfully for us. The withdrawal from command is the taking-up of the role he was actually born for. And it is a profound reframing of what faithfulness looks like when you cannot fix a situation: sometimes the faithful act is not to keep fighting a losing battle but to *witness truthfully* — to stand aside from the evil and record what happened, so that those who come after can learn what you could not make your contemporaries learn.

Which is exactly what Mormon does in the rest of the chapter (vv17-22): he turns from the dying Nephites and speaks straight to *us*. "I write unto you, Gentiles, and also unto you, house of Israel... unto you, twelve tribes of Israel." He lays out the judgment structure — the twelve tribes judged by the Jerusalem Twelve, the Nephite remnant by the Nephite Twelve who are themselves judged by the Jerusalem Twelve (1 Ne 12:9-10; 3 Ne 27:27) — and states the universal reach: "ye must all stand before the judgment-seat of Christ, yea, every soul who belongs to the whole human family of Adam." But the purpose of the whole record is named in verse 21, and it is the heart of why the Book of Mormon exists: "that the Jews, the covenant people of the Lord, shall have other witness besides him whom they saw and heard, that Jesus, whom they slew, was *the very Christ and the very God*." The Book of Mormon is the second witness — the "other witness" — to the divinity of Christ. And note the doubled claim, exactly the language of the question I worked on yesterday: not merely "the very Christ" (the Messiah, the anointed one) but "the very *God*." Mormon, like Nephi and Abinadi and Moroni, places Jesus squarely in identity with God — "the very Christ and the very God." The whole point of the record he is "idle witness" to is to add a second witness, across an ocean and fifteen centuries, to the testimony the Jews rejected: that the Jesus they slew was God. (This is a clean datapoint for the *is-jesus-christ-god* study — Mormon's statement of the record's very purpose hinges on Jesus being "the very God.") And the chapter ends in the missionary's universal yearning: "I would that I could persuade all ye ends of the earth to repent and prepare to stand before the judgment-seat of Christ." Even as his own people march to destruction, Mormon's heart reaches past them to everyone who would ever read — the idle witness is, in the act of writing, the most active missionary of all.
