# Mormon 2 — the sorrowing of the damned; the day of grace passed; the prophet's lifelong grief

**Binding idea:** At sixteen, "large in stature," Mormon is made "the leader of their armies" (2:1-2) — a boy general over a doomed people. Amid blood and carnage, the Nephites finally "began to repent... and began to cry even as had been prophesied by Samuel" (2:10), and Mormon's heart rejoices, "knowing the mercies and the long-suffering of the Lord" (2:12). "But behold this my joy was vain, for their sorrowing was not unto repentance, because of the goodness of God; but it was rather the *sorrowing of the damned*, because the Lord would not always suffer them to take happiness in sin" (2:13). "They did not come unto Jesus with broken hearts and contrite spirits, but they did curse God, and wish to die" (2:14). And Mormon sees "that the day of grace was passed with them, both temporally and spiritually" (2:15). He has taken the plates of Nephi per Ammaron's charge (2:17), and writes the prophet's lifelong lament: "a continual scene of wickedness and abominations has been before mine eyes ever since I have been sufficient to behold the ways of man... my heart has been filled with sorrow because of their wickedness, all my days; nevertheless, I know that I shall be lifted up at the last day" (2:18-19).

## Thoughts

The chapter opens on a detail that should give any reader pause: Mormon, at sixteen, is made commander of the entire Nephite army. "Notwithstanding I being young, was large in stature; therefore the people of Nephi appointed me that I should be their leader." A boy bears the weight of leading a dying civilization in total war. The sober child of chapter 1, chosen for character, is now the general of a people he cannot save. There is something almost unbearable in the trajectory: the Lord visited him at fifteen, forbade him to preach at — and now, at sixteen, the people who would not hear him preach hand him their armies. He can lead them in war but not call them to repentance; they will follow him into battle but not to Christ. The whole tragedy of Mormon's life is set in that contrast. He is trusted with their bodies and refused with their souls.

The doctrinal heart of the chapter is the false repentance of verses 10-15, and it turns on one of the most important distinctions in all of scripture. The Nephites, ground down by war and the slippery treasures Samuel prophesied (they "could not keep that which was their own"), finally begin to mourn and lament. And Mormon — who knows "the mercies and the long-suffering of the Lord" — lets his heart rejoice, supposing this is the turn, that they will "again become a righteous people." It is the hope of every faithful watcher: maybe now, maybe this affliction will bring them back. But the hope is "vain," and the reason is exact: "their sorrowing was *not unto repentance, because of the goodness of God*; but it was rather the *sorrowing of the damned*, because the Lord would not always suffer them to take happiness in sin." This is Paul's distinction — "godly sorrow worketh repentance to salvation... but the sorrow of the world worketh death" (2 Cor 7:10). Godly sorrow grieves over the *sin itself*, because it has offended a good God; it is drawn toward repentance by the goodness of God, not merely driven by the pain of consequences. The sorrowing of the damned grieves only that the *consequences hurt* — that the sin can no longer be enjoyed. The Nephites did not mourn that they had betrayed God; they mourned that God "would not always suffer them to take happiness in sin." They wanted to keep the sin and keep being happy in it, and when the Lord finally would not allow that, they wept — not in contrition but in frustrated appetite.

And the tell, the unmistakable mark of which sorrow it was, is verse 14: "they did not come unto Jesus with broken hearts and contrite spirits, but they did *curse God, and wish to die*." That is the diagnostic. Godly sorrow turns toward Christ — broken heart, contrite spirit, the only sacrifice He asks (3 Ne 9:20). The sorrowing of the damned turns *against* God — cursing Him, wishing to die, because the source of the lost pleasure is identified, correctly, as God's refusal to let sin be happy. The same affliction that softens one heart hardens another, and the direction of the turn reveals everything. A person sorrowing over consequences will, if the consequences are removed, return to the sin; a person sorrowing over the sin will, even if the consequences remain, turn to Christ. The Nephites' cursing of God proved their sorrow was the wrong kind — they were angry at the God who would not let them be comfortable in their wickedness. This is the lived data behind Alma's "wickedness never was happiness" (Alma 41:10): the wicked cannot be made happy in their sin, and when that becomes undeniable, they do not repent — they rage.

Then verse 15, and it is one of the most desolate sentences in the Book of Mormon: "my sorrow did return unto me again, and I saw that the day of grace was passed with them, both temporally and spiritually; for I saw thousands of them hewn down in open rebellion against their God, and heaped up as dung upon the face of the land." The "day of grace passed" is Jeremiah's lament made real — "the harvest is past, the summer is ended, and we are not saved" (Jer 8:20). It does not mean God's grace ran out or that He stopped offering; it means the *people* had so wholly and wilfully hardened that the season in which the offer could take effect in them had closed. Grace was still grace; they were no longer soil it could grow in. And the image of the dead — "heaped up as dung upon the face of the land" (Jer 8:2) — is deliberately dehumanizing, because that is what their choices made of them: thousands dying "in open rebellion against their God," not as tragic victims but as men who chose the rebellion that killed them, their bodies left like refuse. Mormon's joy of verse 12 collapses into the sorrow of verse 15, and the collapse is the whole emotional arc of his life in miniature — hope that the affliction will turn them, then the recognition that it has only hardened them.

And out of that recognition comes the prophet's lifelong lament, verses 18-19, which is among the most personal passages in the record. Mormon, having taken the plates of Nephi exactly as the ten-year-old was charged (the fidelity across fourteen-plus years is its own quiet testimony), writes: "a continual scene of wickedness and abominations has been before mine eyes ever since I have been sufficient to behold the ways of man. And wo is me because of their wickedness; for my heart has been filled with sorrow because of their wickedness, all my days." This is what it costs to be the righteous witness in a wicked age — not occasional grief but a heart "filled with sorrow... all my days," a life spent watching what he loved destroy itself. He has never known a time of peace; from the moment he was old enough to perceive, he has seen only wickedness. And yet the verse does not end in despair: "nevertheless, I know that I shall be lifted up at the last day." Mormon holds, simultaneously, the deepest grief over his people and an unshaken assurance of his own redemption. The two are not in tension; the same faith that lets him see his people clearly (and grieve) lets him see his own end clearly (and hope). The righteous can carry lifelong sorrow over others without losing their own anchor — indeed, the anchor is what makes the sorrow bearable. A man with no hope could not survive seeing what Mormon saw; it is precisely because he knows he "shall be lifted up" that he can keep watching and keep recording.

One more line carries weight, verse 26: after a victory, Mormon notes, "nevertheless the strength of the Lord was not with us; yea, we were left to ourselves, that the Spirit of the Lord did not abide in us; therefore we had become weak like unto our brethren." This is the quiet horror beneath the military narrative. The Nephites still win battles — but by their own arm, not the Lord's strength. They are "left to themselves." For a people whose entire history ran on the covenant "inasmuch as ye keep my commandments ye shall prosper" (1 Ne 2:20), to be "left to ourselves" is the covenant's reversal — not actively cursed in every battle, but simply abandoned to their own strength, which is no match for what is coming. The most frightening judgment is not always a thunderbolt; sometimes it is God's withdrawal, leaving a people to their own resources. "We had become weak like unto our brethren" — the distinction between Nephite and Lamanite, which once was the distinction between the covenant-keeping and the fallen, has collapsed; both are now equally without the Spirit, equally "left to themselves," equally weak. The day of grace passing is, at bottom, the Spirit ceasing to abide — and a people left to themselves, however large their armies, are already lost.
