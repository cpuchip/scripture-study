# Alma 41 — restoration as reciprocity: "wickedness never was happiness"

**Binding idea:** Corianton had "wrested the scriptures" (41:1) into a self-justifying misreading — that "restoration" meant everyone would be restored to happiness regardless of how they lived, which would make his sin harmless. Alma dismantles it: restoration means "to bring back again evil for evil, or carnal for carnal, or devilish for devilish — good for that which is good; righteous for that which is righteous... merciful for that which is merciful" (41:13). You get back what you sent out, what you became. So "do not suppose... that ye shall be restored from sin to happiness... wickedness never was happiness" (41:10) — not "won't be" but *never was*, because the carnal state is "contrary to the nature of happiness" (41:11). The practical edge: "see that you are merciful unto your brethren... and ye shall have mercy restored unto you again... for that which ye do send out shall return unto you again" (41:14-15). Restoration "more fully condemneth the sinner, and justifieth him not at all."

## Thoughts

The key to the chapter is recognizing what Corianton had done with the doctrine. Alma says "some have wrested the scriptures, and have gone far astray because of this thing" — and the "thing" is the word *restoration*. Corianton seems to have heard "all things shall be restored" (40:23) and concluded that in the resurrection everyone gets restored to a happy state, sin notwithstanding — a kind of universalism that would make his behavior with Isabel inconsequential. It's the oldest move there is: find a doctrine, bend it until it licenses the thing you already wanted to do. Alma's whole chapter is the un-bending. And it matters that the sin came *after* the doctrinal distortion — Corianton didn't sin and then rationalize; he rationalized (wrested the scripture) and then sinned on the strength of it. Bad theology cleared the way for bad behavior. That's why Alma spends three chapters on doctrine to reclaim one wayward son: the belief had to be corrected before the behavior could be.

The correction is that restoration is *reciprocity*, not reset. "The meaning of the word restoration is to bring back again evil for evil, or carnal for carnal, or devilish for devilish — good for that which is good; righteous for that which is righteous; just for that which is just; merciful for that which is merciful" (v13). Restoration doesn't take a thing and put it in a state opposite its nature (v12) — it gives back the same kind. You are restored to *yourself*, to what your works and "the desires of your hearts" (v3) made you. And desire is the currency Alma keeps naming: "raised to happiness according to his desires of happiness... the other to evil according to his desires of evil" (v5). You'll get back, intensified and made permanent, what you spent your life wanting. That reframes judgment entirely — it's less a sentence imposed from outside and more a revealing of what you already chose to become. "They are their own judges, whether to do good or do evil" (v7). God ratifies the self you built.

And then verse 10, one of the most quoted lines in the book, and far deeper than it sounds: "wickedness never was happiness." Notice the tense. Not "wickedness will not lead to happiness" (a future penalty), but "never *was*" — a statement about the present and permanent nature of reality. Wickedness and happiness are not sequential (sin now, suffer later); they're contradictory categories that cannot occupy the same space. The carnal state is, in v11's exact phrase, "a state contrary to the nature of happiness." Which means the sinner isn't being denied a happiness he could otherwise have had through sin — he's pursuing happiness by a route that, by its nature, leads the opposite direction, like trying to get warmer by walking deeper into the cold. This is the deepest possible answer to "why not sin?" It isn't mainly "because you'll be caught and punished." It's "because the thing you're seeking — happiness — cannot be reached by the thing you're doing." Corianton went to Isabel looking for something; Alma's answer is that whatever he was looking for, it was never there to find, because wickedness *never was* happiness. The lie under every sin is that it's a shortcut to joy; "never was happiness" is the four-word demolition of that lie.

The practical turn in vv14-15 makes the doctrine livable and even hopeful. If restoration is reciprocity, then it's also a tool you can use: "see that you are merciful unto your brethren; deal justly, judge righteously, and do good continually... and ye shall have mercy restored unto you again." Send out mercy and mercy comes back; send out righteous judgment and you'll be judged righteously. "That which ye do send out shall return unto you again, and be restored" (v15). It's Galatians' "whatsoever a man soweth, that shall he also reap" and the Beatitude's "blessed are the merciful, for they shall obtain mercy," stated as a law of the universe rather than a reward scheme. For Corianton this cuts both ways and Alma names which way it cuts for an unrepentant sinner: "the word restoration more fully condemneth the sinner, and justifieth him not at all." The doctrine Corianton hoped would excuse him is in fact the doctrine that most surely indicts him — because if you get back what you send out, then the man sending out lust and abandonment of duty is storing up exactly that for himself. But the same law is the sinner's hope the moment he turns: start sending out mercy, justice, and good, and that is precisely what will be restored. Restoration condemns the unrepentant and saves the repentant by the identical mechanism — you get yourself back, so change the self you're building.
