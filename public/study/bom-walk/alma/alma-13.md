# Alma 13 — Called on Account of Faith Foreknown: The Holy Order and the Rest

*(The holy order of the priesthood — ordained "from the foundation of the world according to the foreknowledge of God," on account of exceeding faith; the sanctified who abhor sin; Melchizedek's converted city; the pleading call to enter the rest. ~82 BC. Mini-study — completes the 12-13 discourse; spin-off `alma12-13` running.)*

**Binding idea:** Having shown that the heart either hardens into the chains of hell or softens toward the rest (ch 12), Alma now shows *who* God calls to bring people into that rest, and *why* — and the "why" is a doctrine of foreordination that turns entirely on foreknown choice. How were the high priests called, and what is the goal of the whole order?

**Read:** "The Lord God **ordained priests, after his holy order, which was after the order of his Son**, to teach these things" (v1), "that thereby the people might know in what manner to look forward to his Son for redemption" (v2). "And this is the manner after which they were ordained—being **called and prepared from the foundation of the world according to the foreknowledge of God, on account of their exceeding faith and good works; in the first place being left to choose good or evil**; therefore they having chosen good, and exercising exceedingly great faith, are called with a holy calling" (v3); "called... on account of their faith, while **others would reject the Spirit of God on account of the hardness of their hearts**... while, if it had not been for this they might have had as great privilege" (v4); "**in the first place they were on the same standing with their brethren**; thus this holy calling being prepared... for such as **would not harden their hearts**" (v5); ordained "to teach his commandments... that they also might **enter into his rest**" (v6); the order "without beginning of days or end of years... according to his foreknowledge of all things" (v7). Many "became high priests... on account of their exceeding faith and repentance... **choosing to repent and work righteousness rather than to perish**" (v10); "**sanctified, and their garments were washed white through the blood of the Lamb**" (v11); "being pure and spotless before God, **could not look upon sin save it were with abhorrence**; and there were many... who were made pure and **entered into the rest of the Lord their God**" (v12). "Humble yourselves... bring forth fruit meet for repentance, that ye may also enter into that rest" (v13). "**Melchizedek**... a king over the land of Salem; and his people had waxed strong in iniquity... all gone astray" (v17); "Melchizedek having **exercised mighty faith**... did preach repentance unto his people. And behold, **they did repent**; and Melchizedek did establish peace in the land... therefore he was called the **prince of peace**" (v18); "there were many before him, and also there were many afterwards, but **none were greater**" (v19). "The scriptures are before you; if ye will **wrest them** it shall be to your own destruction" (v20). "**Now is the time to repent**, for the day of salvation draweth nigh" (v21); angels "declare it unto all nations... glad tidings of great joy" (v22), preparing hearts for his coming (v24); "**Would to God that it might be in my day; but let it be sooner or later, in it I will rejoice**" (v25). "I wish from the inmost part of my heart, yea, **with great anxiety even unto pain**, that ye would hearken... and **not procrastinate the day of your repentance**" (v27); "watch and pray continually, that ye may not be tempted above that which ye can bear" (v28); "**having faith on the Lord; having a hope that ye shall receive eternal life; having the love of God always in your hearts**, that ye may be lifted up at the last day and **enter into his rest**" (v29); "that ye may not be bound down by the **chains of hell**, that ye may not suffer the **second death**" (v30).

**Thoughts:**
- *Foreordination on account of foreknown faith — not unconditional election (vv3-5).* This is the chapter's hardest and most precise doctrine, and the qualifiers are everything. The high priests were "called and prepared from the foundation of the world according to the foreknowledge of God" — *but* "on account of their exceeding faith and good works," and "in the first place being **left to choose good or evil**." God's foreknowledge does not *cause* the calling; it *foresees the choice* on which the calling rests. This is the Restoration's careful line against Calvinist predestination: it is **fore-ordination**, not pre-destination — election grounded in agency, not bypassing it. Verse 5 makes it unmistakable: "in the first place they were on the **same standing** with their brethren," and the holy calling was "prepared... for such as **would not harden their hearts**." Everyone began equal; the called are simply those whom God foreknew would *choose good* and *not harden* — the very choice ch 12 just made the hinge of everything. The others "might have had as great privilege" (v4); they were not denied it by decree but forfeited it by hardening. Foreordination, rightly understood, magnifies agency rather than erasing it: God calls in advance those He sees will freely answer.
- *The sanctified abhor sin — the new heart completed (vv11-12).* The fruit of the holy order is not merely forgiveness but transformation: those sanctified "could not look upon sin save it were with **abhorrence**." This is the mighty-change thread (Mosiah 5:2, "no more disposition to do evil") brought to its terminus. The fully sanctified heart does not merely *resist* sin by discipline; it *recoils* from it by nature — sin has become repugnant to it, as it is to God. This is the difference between a man who refrains from sin he still wants and a man whose wants themselves have changed. The new birth, completed, remakes the appetites; "pure and spotless before God" is not just a clean record but a clean *desire*. And the reward is the discourse's goal: "**entered into the rest of the Lord their God**" (v12).
- *Melchizedek converted a wicked city — the model held up to Ammonihah (vv17-19).* Alma's choice of example is pointed. Melchizedek's people "had waxed strong in iniquity... all gone astray" — exactly Ammonihah's condition. And Melchizedek "exercised mighty faith... preached repentance... and behold, **they did repent**," and he "established peace... the prince of peace." Alma holds up, to a city gone astray, the greatest of all high priests (v19, "none were greater") *succeeding at the very task Alma is attempting* — converting a wicked city. The message is hope: it *can* be done; Salem proves a city full of wickedness can repent and find peace. The poignancy is that Ammonihah will *not* repent as Salem did — the model is offered and refused, and the city is destroyed (ch 16). The same word that converted Salem is preached to Ammonihah, and the difference is, once again, the hardening of hearts.
- *"Enter into the rest" — the destination of the whole apparatus (vv6, 12, 13, 16, 29).* The phrase recurs through both chapters like a tolling bell — the priesthood, the ordinances (a "type of his order," v16), the sanctification, the faith-hope-charity — all of it exists for one end: "that they might **enter into the rest of the Lord**." The holy order is not an end in itself; it is the means God provides to bring fallen people into His rest (Heb 3-4; D&C 84:24). Everything in the discourse — the warning of the chains, the mercy of the flaming sword, the foreordained teachers, the sanctifying blood — converges on this single destination: the rest of the Lord. (And v29 gives the path in: the faith-hope-charity triad of Alma 7:24, here aimed squarely at the rest.)
- *The discourse bookended — chains of hell to the rest (12:11 ↔ 13:30).* Alma closes exactly where he opened, confirming the two chapters are one argument. Chapter 12 began with "the **chains of hell**" (12:11); chapter 13 ends pleading "that ye may not be bound down by the **chains of hell**, that ye may not suffer the **second death**" (13:30). The whole discourse is framed between the two destinations a heart can reach — the chains (hardening) or the rest (softening) — and every doctrine in between (the Fall, the probation, the holy order, the sanctification) is about which way a heart will go. The structure *is* the message: from the Fall, two roads, and the hinge is whether you harden your heart.
- *"With great anxiety even unto pain" (v27).* Alma's close is not detached doctrine but raw pleading — "I wish from the inmost part of my heart, yea, with great anxiety even unto pain, that ye would hearken... and not procrastinate the day of your repentance." This is the same anguish-for-souls that drove the sons of Mosiah (Mosiah 28:3) and Mosiah 25:11 — the prophet feels the people's danger as physical pain. And his personal longing surfaces: "**Would to God that it might be in my day**" — the prophet who knows Christ is coming aches to see it himself, yet submits the timing — "but let it be sooner or later, in it I will rejoice." Bold hope, submitted timing (the same posture as 7:8's honest "I do not know"). The doctrine is delivered by a man whose heart is breaking to be heard.

Folded-back findings: (1) **the chains of hell are defined epistemically, not morally** — 12:11 is an explicit *definition* ("this is what is meant by..."); the captivity is the end-state of a narrowing of received word, and the devil "inherits a vacancy" rather than seizing from outside; (2) **the cherubim/flaming sword are the *enabling condition* of redemption** (12:26) — without them "the plan of redemption would have been frustrated"; mercy, not wrath; (3) **"enter into my rest" is named first in 12:34**, before the ch-13 priesthood apparatus — ch 12 names the destination, ch 13 the institutional means (8 occurrences of "rest of the Lord" across the two chapters; the rest = "the fulness of his glory," D&C 84:24); (4) **the foreordination passage works against Calvinist election three times** (13:3-5 insist on equal standing thrice — Webster's Milton: "Foreknowledge had no influence on their fault"); (5) **the discourse is a verbal ring** — "chains of hell" opens ch 12 (vv6,11) and closes ch 13 (v30), a textual fact framing everything between as one argument. This chapter completes the discourse with the doctrine of **foreordination on account of foreknown faith** (vv3-5): the high priests were called "from the foundation of the world according to the foreknowledge of God," *but* "on account of their exceeding faith... in the first place left to choose good or evil," and "on the same standing with their brethren" — *fore-ordination grounded in agency, not Calvinist election*; God calls in advance those He foresees will freely answer and "would not harden their hearts" (the ch-12 hinge). The fruit of the order is the **new heart completed** — the sanctified "could not look upon sin save it were with abhorrence" (v12): not just forgiven but with the appetites remade (Mosiah 5:2's terminus). **Melchizedek is the model held up to Ammonihah** (vv17-19): the greatest high priest converted a city that "had waxed strong in iniquity... all gone astray" — proof a wicked city *can* repent (the model Ammonihah will refuse, ch 16). And the whole apparatus aims at one end — **"enter into the rest of the Lord"** (vv6, 12, 13, 16, 29) — with the **12-13 discourse bookended** between the chains of hell (12:11) and the warning against them (13:30): from the Fall, two roads, the hinge being whether you harden your heart. Alma closes pleading "**with great anxiety even unto pain**" (v27) and longing "**would to God it might be in my day**" (v25 — bold hope, submitted timing).
