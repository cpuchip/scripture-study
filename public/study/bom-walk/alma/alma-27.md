# Alma 27 — the joy that exhausts strength: the people of Ammon

**Binding idea:** The grievance-cure arc closes by weaving the cured people into the nation they once raided. The Anti-Nephi-Lehies, facing a second slaughter they again refuse to resist, are commanded by the Lord to flee — and the Nephites, by "the voice of the people," give their former enemies the land of Jershon and pledge to "set our armies between" them and danger (27:22-23), bearing the sword *for* the pacifists. They become "the people of Ammon," named for the missionary who brought them, "firm in the faith of Christ, even unto the end" (27:27). And Ammon, meeting Alma again, is "swallowed up in the joy of his God, even to the exhausting of his strength" (27:17) — the joy that "none receiveth save it be the truly penitent."

**Read:** Alma 27 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Closes the sons-of-Mosiah arc (chapters 17-27).

## Thoughts

The covenant holds under the worst test. The Amalekites, unable to revenge themselves on the Nephites, turn back on the converts, and "this people again refused to take their arms, and they suffered themselves to be slain according to the desires of their enemies" (v3). It happens *twice*. The buried-weapons covenant wasn't a one-time emotional high — it survives a second massacre. And this is what finally moves Ammon to act: not to arm them, which would break their covenant, but to remove them from the killing ground entirely. The Lord confirms it — "Get this people out of this land, that they perish not; for Satan has great hold on the hearts of the Amalekites" (v12). There's a wisdom here about protecting the vulnerable that doesn't violate their conscience: you don't hand the pacifist a sword; you build them a refuge and stand guard yourself.

The king's response to the plan is pure repentance-logic, and it's worth noticing. When Ammon proposes fleeing to the Nephites, the king's fear isn't death — it's that "the Nephites will destroy us, because of the many murders and sins we have committed against them" (v6). And his counter-offer: "we will be their slaves until we repair... the many murders and sins" (v8). A king volunteering his whole people into slavery as restitution. That's the depth of their sense of what they owe. And Ammon refuses it on principle — "It is against the law of our brethren... that there should be any slaves among them" (v9, the Mosiah 29 reign-of-judges principle). The repentant want to pay; the law of the free society won't take payment in bondage. They must "rely upon the mercies of our brethren" instead — which is the only currency repentance can actually offer: not restitution that squares the account, but throwing yourself on mercy. You cannot repay murder. You can only be received.

Then the Nephites receive them, and the arrangement is remarkable. The chief judge puts it to "the voice of the people" (v21, the reign-of-judges in action), and the people vote to give Jershon to their former raiders and to "set our armies between the land Jershon and the land Nephi, that we may protect our brethren... on account of their fear to take up arms against their brethren lest they should commit sin" (v23). The Nephites agree to bleed and die in battle so that the people of Ammon never have to break their covenant. That's an extraordinary thing — one people bearing the literal sword so another people can keep their vow not to. It sets up everything that follows: the war chapters, and eventually the stripling warriors (the sons of these very people, not bound by the parents' oath, who fight to defend the nation that sheltered them). The cure didn't just save souls; it created a new kind of interdependence — the armed protecting the unarmed-by-conscience.

The emotional center is Ammon's collapse. He meets Alma — the reunion chapter 17 opened with, now reached in the forward narrative — and "the joy of Ammon was so great even that he was full; yea, he was swallowed up in the joy of his God, even to the exhausting of his strength; and he fell again to the earth" (v17). Mormon notes, almost tenderly, that Alma's and the others' joy "was not that to exceed their strength" (v19) — only Ammon's did. The man who felt the despair hardest ("we were about to turn back," 26:27) now feels the joy hardest. And Mormon names what kind of joy it is: "joy which none receiveth save it be the truly penitent and humble seeker of happiness" (v18). It's the same epistemology as 26:21 — only the penitent knows these things. The depth of the joy is proportional to the depth of the repentance behind it. Ammon, who "went forth even in wrath to destroy" the church, has the most to be forgiven of and therefore the most joy when the forgiven become the forgiving. The collapse isn't weakness; it's the body unable to contain what the soul received.

The chapter ends with the people of Ammon's permanent character, and every line is a reversal of who they were. The people whose grievance "delighted in murdering" now "look upon shedding the blood of their brethren with the greatest abhorrence" (v28). The people the Nephites wanted to exterminate are now "perfectly honest and upright in all things... firm in the faith of Christ, even unto the end" (v27) — the "never did fall away" of 23:6 restated as settled fact. And the line that completes the arc theologically: "they never did look upon death with any degree of terror, for their hope and views of Christ and the resurrection; therefore, death was swallowed up to them by the victory of Christ over it" (v28, Paul's 1 Cor 15:54-57). The people who buried their weapons and lay down to die could do it because resurrection had emptied death of its terror. The new name — "the people of Ammon" (v26) — completes the identity reconstruction begun at Anti-Nephi-Lehi: they are named, finally, for the love that found them.

## Connections

- **Alma 17:1** — the meeting with Alma (v16); the arc circles back to the reunion chapter 17 opened with.
- **Alma 24:21-26** — the first slaughter; the second (v3) proves the covenant wasn't a one-time high.
- **Mosiah 29:32** — no slaves in the reign of judges; Ammon refuses the king's offer of bondage-as-restitution (v9).
- **Mosiah 27:24** — Alma relates his own conversion (v25); the persecutor-turned-prophet parallel to the Lamanite turning.
- **Alma 18:42; 19:14; 22:18** — the falling-from-joy/Spirit pattern; now Ammon himself (v17, "fell again").
- **1 Cor 15:54-57** — "death was swallowed up... by the victory of Christ" (v28); the resurrection emptying death of terror.
- **Alma 23:6; Hel 15:8** — "firm in the faith of Christ, even unto the end" (v27); the never-fall-away permanence confirmed.
- **Alma 43:11-13; 53; 56** — the people of Ammon / Jershon become the setting for the war chapters; the protection arrangement (vv22-24) seeds the stripling warriors.
- **Mosiah 28:1-9** — the original missionary commission; "you cannot repay murder, only be received" answers the whole arc.
