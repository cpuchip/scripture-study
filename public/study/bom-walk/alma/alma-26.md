# Alma 26 — "in his strength I can do all things": Ammon's psalm

**Binding idea:** The fourteen-year mission gets its doxology, and its theology is the line between holy joy and self-glory. When Aaron warns "thy joy doth carry thee away unto boasting" (26:10), Ammon draws the distinction exactly: "I do not boast in my own strength... I know that I am nothing; as to my strength I am weak; therefore I will not boast of myself, but I will boast of my God, for in his strength I can do all things" (26:11-12). The joy is real and enormous, but its whole content is *God did this through nothing*. The brethren who were mocked as fools for going to "as stiffnecked a people" as the Lamanites (26:24) now look on thousands loosed "from the chains of hell" (26:14), and the converts' love exceeds anything "even among the Nephites" (26:33).

**Read:** Alma 26 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

The hinge of the whole psalm is the exchange with Aaron, because it answers the question every joyful success raises: when does rejoicing become bragging? Ammon is exultant — harvest imagery pouring out, sheaves gathered into garners safe from the storm (vv5-7), thousands brought "into the marvelous light of God" (v3) — and Aaron, the brother who spent the same years in a dungeon, says plainly, "Ammon, I fear that thy joy doth carry thee away unto boasting" (v10). It's a real rebuke from a peer, and Ammon doesn't dismiss it; he refines on it. "I do not boast in my own strength, nor in my own wisdom... I know that I am nothing; as to my strength I am weak; therefore I will not boast of myself, but I will boast of my God, for in his strength I can do all things" (vv11-12). That last clause is Paul's "I can do all things through Christ which strengtheneth me" (Philip 4:13, the footnote confirms it) — but Ammon front-loads the condition Paul leaves implicit: *I am nothing, I am weak, therefore.* The "all things" is only available to the man who has located himself at zero. Boasting in God isn't false modesty about real achievement; it's the accurate report of who actually did the work. The test of whether joy has curdled into pride is simple — does the praise terminate on me or pass through me to God? Ammon's passes through. "I cannot say the smallest part which I feel" (v16) is not the language of a man impressed with himself.

And the chains-of-hell motif comes home. Back in Alma 12, the chains of hell were the binding that filled a heart emptied of the word (the alma12-13 study found the vacancy mechanism). Here Ammon says God "has loosed our brethren from the chains of hell" (v14) — the exact phrase, now reversed. "They were encircled about with everlasting darkness and destruction; but behold, he has brought them into his everlasting light... and they are encircled about with the matchless bounty of his love" (v15). The word that, refused, lets the chains in is the same word that, received, strikes them off. The two encirclements — darkness and love — are set against each other, and conversion is the move from one circle to the other. The whole Ammonihah-and-Lamanite arc has been a study in those two encirclements; here Ammon names them in one breath.

Ammon grounds his right to speak of mercy in his own past. "We went forth even in wrath, with mighty threatenings to destroy his church" (v18) — the Mosiah 27 persecutor, the man the angel stopped. "Why did he not let the sword of his justice fall upon us... he did not exercise his justice upon us, but in his great mercy hath brought us over that everlasting gulf" (vv19-20). He preaches mercy to the Lamanites as someone who got mercy he had no claim to. And he names the epistemology of it: "what natural man is there that knoweth these things? ... none... save it be the penitent" (vv21-22). The mysteries aren't withheld arbitrarily; they're simply invisible to the unrepentant, the way 1 Corinthians 2 says the things of God are foolishness to the natural man. Repentance is the organ of this kind of knowing. Which is exactly why the Amalekites, who'd theologized away repentance, couldn't be reached, and the penitent Lamanites could.

The remembered mockery (vv23-25) is worth sitting with, because it exposes the Nephite version of the grievance. The brethren back in Zarahemla "laughed us to scorn": "Do ye suppose that ye can bring the Lamanites to the knowledge of the truth?... as stiffnecked a people as they are; whose hearts delight in the shedding of blood?" And their proposed alternative: "Let us take up arms against them, that we destroy them" (v25). That's the grievance running the *other* direction — the Nephites' contempt for the Lamanites as irredeemable savages, with extermination as the reasonable policy. Ammon's answer is the whole thesis: "we came into the wilderness not with the intent to destroy our brethren, but with the intent that perhaps we might save some few of their souls" (v26). The sword would have been the sensible Nephite move; the saving was the impossible one — and the saving worked where the sword never had. He even admits they nearly quit: "when our hearts were depressed, and we were about to turn back, the Lord comforted us" (v27, recalling 17:10-11). The fourteen years included the moment they almost went home.

And the verse that lands the cure's proof: "has there been so great love in all the land? ... Nay, there has not, even among the Nephites. For behold, they would take up arms against their brethren; they would not suffer themselves to be slain. But behold how many of these have laid down their lives" (vv33-34). The despised Lamanites now out-love the covenant Nephites — the same inversion as the queen out-believing all the Nephites (ch 19), now in love rather than faith. The people the Nephites wanted to exterminate became the people whose love exceeded the Nephites' own. That's the grievance dissolved from both ends.

The psalm closes on universal mercy, and it's another datapoint for the curse question. God "has been mindful of this people, who are a branch of the tree of Israel, and has been lost from its body in a strange land" (v36, the Jacob 5 olive-tree allegory, branch lost and remembered). Then the widest statement: "God is mindful of every people, whatsoever land they may be in; yea, he numbereth his people, and his bowels of mercy are over all the earth" (v37, footnote → 2 Ne 26:33, Jonah 4:11, Acts 10:35 — Peter's "God is no respecter of persons"). The Lamanites were never outside God's reach; he was mindful of them as a lost branch of his own tree the whole time. (Logged for the 2 Ne 5:21 study.)

## Connections

- **Philippians 4:13** — "I can do all things through Christ which strengtheneth me"; "in his strength I can do all things" (v12), with the "I am nothing... I am weak, therefore" condition made explicit.
- **Alma 12:11** — the chains of hell (the alma12-13 study's vacancy mechanism); "loosed... from the chains of hell" (v14), the exact phrase reversed.
- **Alma 5:7, 26** — the marvelous light; "sing redeeming love" (v13, footnote → 5:26); the two encirclements (darkness/love, v15) echo Alma 5's deliverance.
- **1 Pet 2:9** — "out of darkness into his marvellous light" (v3).
- **John 4:35; D&C 4:4** — the field white/ripe to harvest (v5); the sickle (Joel 3:13).
- **1 Cor 2:11, 14** — the natural man cannot know; "none... save it be the penitent" (vv21-22); repentance as the organ of knowing.
- **Mosiah 27:10** — Ammon the persecutor "went forth... to destroy his church" (v18); mercy preached by one who received it.
- **2 Cor 11:23-29** — Paul's catalog of apostolic sufferings; Ammon's parallel catalog (v29).
- **Jacob 5:25, 40-45** — the olive-tree allegory; "a branch of the tree of Israel... lost from its body" (v36); ties to the jacob5 spin-off.
- **2 Ne 26:33; Jonah 4:11; Acts 10:35** — God mindful of every people (v37); universal mercy. **(5:21-study datapoint.)**
- **Alma 17:10-11** — the Lord's comfort + patience command; recalled at v27 (they almost turned back).
