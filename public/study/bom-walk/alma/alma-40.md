# Alma 40 — the space between: resurrection certain, the intermediate state, the perfect frame

**Binding idea:** Corianton's "mind is worried concerning the resurrection of the dead" (40:1) — and a worry about the resurrection is, underneath, a worry about whether his deeds finally matter. Alma answers with what he "inquired diligently of God" to know: there is a "time appointed that all shall come forth from the dead" (40:4), and a "space betwixt the time of death and the time of the resurrection" (40:6) in which the spirit goes "home to that God who gave them life" (40:11) — the righteous to "paradise, a state of rest" (40:12), the wicked to "outer darkness... awful, fearful looking for the fiery indignation" (40:13-14). Then the resurrection is literal and total: "every limb and joint shall be restored... even a hair of the head shall not be lost; but all things shall be restored to their proper and perfect frame" (40:23). And Alma models intellectual honesty throughout, separating what God revealed from his own opinion (40:5, 20).

## Thoughts

The pastoral logic connects this to chapter 39 more tightly than it first appears. Corianton's sexual sin grew partly from doubt, and a doubt about the resurrection is functionally Korihor's doctrine from chapter 30 — "when a man was dead, that was the end thereof." If death is the end, sin doesn't ultimately matter; there's no one to answer to, no record to face. So Alma's first work in reclaiming his son is to re-establish that death is *not* the end: there is a space, a state, a resurrection, and a judgment. Re-laying that floor is what makes repentance make sense again. You can't call a man to repent of what he'll never answer for. Alma gives Corianton back the thing his sin had quietly denied — that his deeds follow him past the grave.

What strikes me most is Alma's intellectual honesty, which is itself a teaching. He's unfolding "a mystery," but he immediately bounds it: "there are many mysteries which are kept, that no one knoweth them save God himself" (v3). On the timing of the resurrection — "whether there shall be one time, or a second time, or a third time... it mattereth not; for God knoweth all these things" (v5). And later, on whether the righteous rise at Christ's resurrection: "I do not say... but behold, I give it as my opinion" (v20). A prophet, mid-revelation, explicitly flags the line between what was revealed to him and what is his best inference. That's a model worth keeping for anyone who teaches sacred things: claim certainty only where God has given it, and label opinion as opinion. Alma doesn't pad his authority by pretending to know the timing; he gives Corianton the rock-solid certainty (there *is* a resurrection and a judgment) and honest agnosticism about the details God hasn't disclosed. The honesty makes the certainty more trustworthy, not less.

The doctrine of the intermediate state (vv11-14) is the chapter's substance, and it's tender and sobering at once. At death "the spirits of all men... are taken home to that God who gave them life" (v11, Ecclesiastes' "the spirit shall return unto God who gave it"). The righteous enter "a state of happiness, which is called paradise, a state of rest, a state of peace, where they shall rest from all their troubles and from all care, and sorrow" (v12). That word *rest* lands — the dead in Christ aren't in suspense or sleep; they're at rest, the troubles and cares of mortality finally set down. The wicked, by contrast, are in "outer darkness... awful, fearful looking for the fiery indignation of the wrath of God" (vv13-14) — note it's a state of *looking-for*, of dread anticipation, the same forward-leaning fear Alma 34:34 described (the spirit you die with persists). Neither state is the final judgment; both are the waiting room, holding "until the time of their resurrection" (v14). It's the Luke 16 picture (the rich man and Lazarus) given doctrinal shape, and D&C 138 will later fill it in further.

And the resurrection itself, when Alma finally defines it against the intermediate state, is gloriously physical: "the soul shall be restored to the body, and the body to the soul; yea, and every limb and joint shall be restored to its body; yea, even a hair of the head shall not be lost; but all things shall be restored to their proper and perfect frame" (v23). This is not the spirit floating free of the body — it's the *reuniting* of spirit and body, permanent and complete. And the detail "not a hair lost... perfect frame" is pure comfort: whatever mortality did to the body — disease, age, injury, loss — the resurrection restores it whole, to its proper and perfect form. The body isn't discarded; it's redeemed. For a young man tangled in the misuse of his body, there's a quiet dignity restored here: the body matters enough to God to be raised perfect and kept forever. The flesh is not the enemy of the soul; in the resurrection they're made one and glorified together.

The chapter ends on the fork it's been building toward (v25-26): "the righteous shall shine forth in the kingdom of God" (Daniel's image), but "an awful death cometh upon the wicked... no unclean thing can inherit the kingdom of God... they drink the dregs of a bitter cup." The resurrection is universal but its outcomes diverge — everyone rises in a perfect frame, but to different destinies, "judged according to their works" (v21). Which loops straight back to Corianton: the perfect frame you'll be raised in stands before God either to shine or to drink the dregs, and which one is decided by what you do now. The resurrection doesn't erase the moral stakes of mortality; it makes them eternal. That's the answer to the worried son — not "don't worry about the resurrection" but "the resurrection is exactly why your life right now matters forever."
