# Alma 28 — the two mournings: fear and hope over the same grave

**Binding idea:** Mormon stops to weigh the cost. After "a tremendous battle... such an one as never had been known" (28:2), with tens of thousands slain, the land fills with "the cry of widows" (28:5). And he draws the distinction that the whole bloody span has been driving toward: of those who mourn their dead, some "have reason to fear... that they are consigned to a state of endless wo" (28:11), while others "rejoice and exult in the hope, and even know... that they are raised to dwell at the right hand of God" (28:12). Same death, two mournings — and the only difference is whether the gospel reached the one who died. From which: "the great call of diligence of men to labor in the vineyards of the Lord" (28:14).

**Read:** Alma 28 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Closes the account of Alma 17-28.

## Thoughts

This is a pause for grief, and it earns it. The Lamanite armies pursue the people of Ammon, and the Nephites who pledged to defend them pay the price they promised — "a tremendous slaughter among the people of Nephi" (v3). Mormon doesn't rush past the human cost the way a triumphalist would. He lingers on the sound of it: "the cry of widows mourning for their husbands, and also of fathers mourning for their sons, and the daughter for the brother, yea, the brother for the father" (v5). The protection arrangement of chapter 27 wasn't free; somebody's husband died so a person of Ammon could keep his vow. Mormon makes you hear the widows. That honesty about cost is part of why the record is trustworthy — the rejoicing in Ammon's psalm and the weeping here are held in the same hand.

The heart of the chapter is verses 11-12, and it's one of the most piercing things Mormon does as editor. He looks at all the dead and divides the mourning, not the corpses. Some mourners weep "because they have reason to fear... that they are consigned to a state of endless wo" — they grieve a death and dread an eternity. Others "truly mourn for the loss of their kindred, yet they rejoice and exult in the hope, and even know... that they are raised to dwell at the right hand of God, in a state of never-ending happiness." The same event — a body in the ground — produces despair in one home and grief-shot-through-with-hope in another. And the variable is not how good the person was at dying or how much they were loved; it's whether they had Christ. This is exactly why the sons of Mosiah's fourteen years mattered, and why the chapter ends on a missionary call: the difference between the two mournings is the difference the gospel makes, and so men are under "the great call of diligence... to labor in the vineyards of the Lord" (v14). The labor of conversion is, finally, the labor of changing which mourning a family will one day do.

Verse 13 is worth pausing on for its precision: "thus we see how great the inequality of man is because of sin and transgression, and the power of the devil." The "inequality" here isn't God playing favorites — it's the self-made disparity of eternal outcomes, traceable to "the cunning plans which he hath devised to ensnare the hearts of men." It sits in deliberate tension with 1 Nephi 17:35, where God "esteemeth all flesh in one." God's regard is equal; the outcomes are not, because agency and the devil's snares produce real divergence. The inequality is authored below, not above — the same point Alma 3 made about the curse being self-brought (a thread I'm keeping for the 2 Ne 5:21 study). God esteems all flesh alike; what men do with that esteem diverges enormously.

The chapter closes the way the whole arc has felt — two-sided. "The great reason of sorrow, and also of rejoicing — sorrow because of death and destruction among men, and joy because of the light of Christ unto life" (v14). Not joy *instead of* sorrow, not sorrow *swallowing* joy — both, held together. The mature response to a world like this one isn't to choose between grief and gladness; it's to carry both, because both are true. People really die, and Christ really conquers death. Mormon won't let you have either without the other.
