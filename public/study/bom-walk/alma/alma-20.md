# Alma 20 — the grievance at full volume, and the love that disarms it

**Binding idea:** The road to free Aaron runs straight into the grievance tradition's highest exponent. Lamoni's father, king over all the land, recites the Mosiah 10 grievance almost verbatim — "he robbed our fathers... his children are also come amongst us that they may, by their cunning and their lyings, deceive us, that they again may rob us" (20:13) — and tries to kill first his own son, then Ammon, for siding with the truth. What disarms him is not Ammon's sword (though Ammon wins the fight) but Ammon's *terms*: he asks nothing for himself, only freedom for his brethren and the kingdom kept for Lamoni. The king is "astonished" at "the great love he had for his son Lamoni" (20:26). Selfless love, not victory, plants the seed that flowers in chapter 22.

**Read:** Alma 20 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

This chapter gives you the disease in its purest, most powerful form, and it's worth hearing it straight. The over-king sees Ammon and calls him "one of the children of a liar" (v10); then, when Lamoni explains, his father erupts: "thou art going to deliver these Nephites, who are sons of a liar. Behold, he robbed our fathers; and now his children are also come amongst us that they may, by their cunning and their lyings, deceive us, that they again may rob us of our property" (v13). The footnote runs straight to Mosiah 10:16 — this is the grievance tradition I studied in mos10, recited by the man at the top of it, nearly word for word, centuries after the supposed robbery. The whole structure of the lie is here: a historical injury ("he robbed our fathers"), a permanent ethnic indictment ("sons of a liar"), and a present paranoia ("that they again may rob us"). The grievance is total, inherited, and load-bearing for the entire culture. You cannot reason with it; the king's son has just been raised from a coma by these "liars" and the king still calls them robbers.

What breaks it is staged in two moves, and both matter. First, Lamoni — the son — refuses to obey the grievance. "I will not slay Ammon... for I know that they are just men and holy prophets of the true God" (v15). The convert's loyalty has migrated from his father's story to the truth, and he says so to the most dangerous person he could say it to. The grievance tradition is broken precisely when a son will no longer enforce it. That's how multi-generational hatred actually ends — not when the elders relent, but when the next generation refuses to carry it.

Second, and this is the part that moves me, Ammon intervenes for the king's *soul*. When the father draws his sword on Lamoni, Ammon doesn't say "don't hurt him." He says: "thou shalt not slay thy son; nevertheless, it were better that he should fall than thee, for behold, he has repented of his sins; but if thou shouldst fall at this time, in thine anger, thy soul could not be saved" (v17). The calculus is entirely eternal. Better the repentant son die than the unrepentant father, because the father killed in his rage is damned. Ammon is more concerned for the murderer's salvation than for the victim's life — which is a genuinely Christlike inversion of how I'd weigh it. He even teaches the doctrine: innocent blood "would cry from the ground to the Lord his God, for vengeance" (v18, the Abel echo, Gen 4:10). He's trying to save the man who's trying to kill him.

The fight itself is the Sebus move again — the king strikes, Ammon "withstood his blows, and also smote his arm that he could not use it" (v20). Restraint inside power, the signature. But the conversion-hinge is the negotiation, not the fight. Ammon could kill the king, and the king knows it, and offers "even to half of the kingdom" (v23, the Esther/Herod phrase — the desperate bribe of a cornered ruler). Ammon refuses the bribe. His terms cost him nothing and gain him nothing personal: free my brethren, let Lamoni keep his kingdom, don't be displeased with him (v24). And *that* is what undoes the king — "when he also saw the great love he had for his son Lamoni, he was astonished exceedingly" (v26). The grievance said Nephites are greedy cunning robbers who want "our property." Standing in front of the king is a Nephite who, holding a sword at the king's throat and offered half a kingdom, wants only his friends' freedom and another man's crown secured. The story cannot survive the man. So the king grants everything, releases Lamoni's kingdom "from this time and forever," and — the seed for chapter 22 — becomes "desirous to learn" the words that produced this love (v27).

There's a Saul-and-Jonathan shadow over the whole scene, and the footnote on the missed feast (v9 → 1 Sam 20:27) confirms it's deliberate. Saul, enraged that Jonathan sided with David the anointed, cast a javelin at his own son at the feast. Here the over-king, enraged that Lamoni sided with the prophets, draws his sword on his own son. The king who turns murderous on his child over loyalty to the Lord's servant is an old, old figure. But this story bends the other way: the father is spared, and converted, by the love he came to destroy.

The chapter ends with the contrast that defines the whole mission. Aaron and his brethren are freed from Middoni — "naked, and their skins were worn exceedingly because of being bound with strong cords," having "suffered hunger, thirst, and all kinds of afflictions" (v29). Ammon's field flourished; Aaron's began in a dungeon. They "had fallen into the hands of a more hardened and a more stiffnecked people" (v30). Same gospel, same calling, radically different soil — and the line that redeems it is "nevertheless they were patient in all their sufferings" (v29), the footnote pointing back to the Lord's command in 17:11 ("ye shall be patient in long-suffering and afflictions"). Aaron lived the exact instruction. Faithfulness isn't measured by Ammon's spectacular fruit; it's measured by whether you endured your assigned field. Some fields are kings' courts. Some are prisons.

## Connections

- **Mosiah 10:12-17** — the grievance tradition; the over-king recites it almost verbatim at v13 (footnote → Mosiah 10:16). The disease in its highest, most powerful voice.
- **1 Sam 20:27-33** — Saul casts a javelin at Jonathan for siding with David; the over-king draws his sword on Lamoni for siding with the prophets (the missed-feast footnote at v9 marks the parallel deliberately).
- **Gen 4:10** — innocent blood crying from the ground; Ammon teaches the doctrine to deter the murder (v18).
- **Philippians 4:13** — "in the strength of the Lord thou canst do all things" (Lamoni, v4); the theme that becomes Ammon's psalm at 26:12.
- **Esther 5:3 / Mark 6:23** — "even to half of the kingdom" (v23); the cornered ruler's desperate bribe, which Ammon refuses.
- **Alma 17:11** — the commanded patience; Aaron lived it in prison (v29, footnote).
- **Alma 22** — the over-king's full conversion; the seed planted here ("desirous to learn," v27) flowers there.
- **Alma 17:37** — the arm-smiting at Sebus; the same restraint repeated (v20).
