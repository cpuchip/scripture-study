# Alma 32 — the seed: faith as the experiment you run before you can know

**Binding idea:** To the poor cast out of the synagogues "because of the coarseness of their apparel" (32:2), Alma gives the gospel's most accessible doctrine of faith. Faith "is not to have a perfect knowledge of things... if ye have faith ye hope for things which are not seen, which are true" (32:21). And the entry bar is the lowest imaginable: "even if ye can no more than desire to believe, let this desire work in you" (32:27). Plant the word as a seed; if it is good it "will begin to swell within your breasts... enlarge my soul... enlighten my understanding" (32:28) — and that swelling is real, discernible evidence (32:35). But the seed must be nourished "with great diligence, and with patience" or it withers (32:37-41); only the nourished tree springs up "unto everlasting life" (32:41). Against the Zoramites' fixed, dead, exclusive religion, faith here is living, growing, and open to anyone who can muster a desire.

## Thoughts

Who Alma preaches to is half the sermon. The poor — cast out of synagogues they "labored abundantly to build with our own hands" (v5), esteemed "as dross," with "no place to worship our God." And Alma's reaction is the opposite of the Rameumptom's contempt: he "beheld with great joy; for he beheld that their afflictions had truly humbled them, and that they were in a preparation to hear the word" (v6). The exclusion that the Zoramites meant as damnation, Alma reads as preparation. Their poverty made them teachable; the rich on the stand were not. This is the great reversal the whole chapter rides on — the cast-out are the ready, and the elect are the closed.

The first move is to break the Zoramite cage around worship. "Do ye suppose that ye cannot worship God save it be in your synagogues only?... that ye must not worship God only once in a week?" (vv10-11). The poor had concluded that being barred from the building barred them from God. Alma frees worship from the building and the calendar in two questions — exactly the cage the Rameumptom built. You don't need their stand; you don't need their day.

Then the humility teaching, with its careful gradation. "Because ye are compelled to be humble blessed are ye; for a man sometimes, if he is compelled to be humble, seeketh repentance" (v13). Compelled humility — humility forced by circumstance — is still a blessing, because it can open the door to repentance. But: "blessed are they who humble themselves without being compelled to be humble" (v16). The poor were humbled by poverty; better is the person who humbles themselves voluntarily, by the word, before they're forced to. This matters because it dignifies the poor's situation (your affliction is genuinely a blessing) without romanticizing it (the higher path is to choose humility before life imposes it). Affliction is a teacher of last resort; the wise don't wait for it.

Verse 21 is the definition the whole church has memorized, and the qualifier is the part I keep relearning: "faith is not to have a perfect knowledge of things; therefore if ye have faith ye hope for things which are not seen, which are true." Not "things not seen" full stop — "things which are not seen, *which are true*." Faith isn't credulity about anything invisible; it's hope directed at unseen realities that are actually true. The object matters. You can have intense belief in a false thing — that's not faith, that's the Zoramite prayer. Faith is hope aimed at what's real but not yet visible. And then v23 widens the door all the way: God gives his word "by angels unto men, yea, not only men but women also... little children do have words given unto them many times, which confound the wise and the learned." The cast-out poor, women, children — the people the Rameumptom would never let on the stand — are exactly the ones God speaks through. The faith Alma teaches is democratized; it bypasses the whole hierarchy of the elect.

The seed experiment (vv27-43) is the operational heart, and what strikes me is that it's an *experiment*, with a falsifiable design. Start with the minimum — "if ye can no more than desire to believe, let this desire work in you" (v27). You don't need belief to begin; you need only a desire to believe, and a willingness to give the word room. Plant it. If it's a good seed, it will "swell," "enlarge my soul," "enlighten my understanding," become "delicious." And here's the epistemology that answers Korihor's empiricism from the previous chapter: "is not this real? Yea, because it is light; and whatsoever is light, is good, because it is discernible" (v35). The swelling is *data*. It's discernible, observable evidence — not blind faith but evidence of a kind Korihor's "ye cannot know what ye cannot see" can't account for, because the seeing happens inside. The experiment yields real knowledge — "your knowledge is perfect in that thing" (v34) — but only of *that thing*, not of everything; faith remains necessary for the rest.

And the warning that keeps it honest: the seed must be nourished or it dies. "If ye neglect the tree, and take no thought for its nourishment... it withers away" — and Alma is careful: "this is not because the seed was not good... but it is because your ground is barren, and ye will not nourish the tree" (vv38-39). A failed faith isn't proof the word was false; it's usually proof the ground went untended. Faith isn't a one-time experiment with a permanent result; it's a tree that needs "diligence, and patience" continually (v41), or the heat of the sun scorches it. The fruit at the end — "most precious... sweet above all that is sweet, and white above all that is white" (v42) — is Lehi's tree-of-life fruit (1 Ne 8, 11), the love of God, eternal life. So the whole arc is: desire → plant → swell (faith confirmed) → nourish → tree → fruit. It's a process, alive and ongoing, which is precisely what the Zoramites' once-a-week recitation could never be. They had a dead creed on a high stand; Alma hands the poor a living seed for the heart.

## Connections

- **Heb 11:1; Ether 12:6** — "faith is the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen"; v21's definition, with the crucial "which are true."
- **Matt 13:3-8, 18-23 (parable of the sower)** — the seed, the barren ground, the scorching sun, the fruit; Alma works the same agricultural parable (vv28, 38-39, footnotes).
- **1 Ne 8:10-12; 11:21-25** — the tree of life and its fruit "sweet above all"; the fruit Alma's tree bears (vv40-42).
- **Alma 31:13-23** — the Rameumptom's worship-in-one-place-one-day; Alma frees worship from the building and the week (vv10-11).
- **Luke 18:9-14** — the poor as the ready, the self-elected as closed; the great reversal (vv2-6).
- **Matt 5:3-5** — "blessed are the poor in spirit... the meek"; the Beatitude behind "blessed are ye" (vv8, 16).
- **Alma 30:43-48** — Korihor's sign-demand and empiricism; v35's "it is light... discernible" answers it — inward evidence is real evidence.
- **Alma 33-34** — the seed to plant is specifically Christ (33:22-23) and his atonement (34); the spin-off works this out.
- **D&C 41:1; John 15:22-24** — "more cursed is he that knoweth the will of God and doeth it not" (v19); knowledge raises accountability.
