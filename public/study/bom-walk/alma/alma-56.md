# Alma 56 — "we do not doubt our mothers knew it": the faith taught at home

**Binding idea:** Helaman's epistle to Moroni recounts the stripling warriors' first battle, and the source of their astonishing courage is named precisely. Facing their first fight, these untested young men say, "Father, behold our God is with us, and he will not suffer that we should fall... we would not slay our brethren if they would let us alone" (56:46). And Helaman explains why: "they never had fought, yet they did not fear death... they had been taught by their mothers, that if they did not doubt, God would deliver them. And they rehearsed unto me the words of their mothers, saying: We do not doubt our mothers knew it" (56:47-48). Their faith was not their own discovery — it was *inherited and trusted*: they believed because their mothers believed and taught them, and that borrowed-yet-real faith was enough. After the battle, the miracle: Helaman numbers them "fearing lest there were many of them slain. But behold, to my great joy, there had not one soul of them fallen to the earth... never were men known to have fought with such miraculous strength" (56:55-56).

**Read:** Alma 56 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Helaman's epistle; the stripling warriors' campaign continues in chs 57-58.

## Thoughts

The chapter is Helaman's own letter, and its emotional center is the moment before the young men's first battle. Helaman has been using his two thousand as a decoy — bait to draw the strongest Lamanite army away from Antipus — and now, uncertain whether the pursuing Lamanites have halted to spring a trap, he turns to the boys and asks, "what say ye, my sons, will ye go against them to battle?" (v44). Their answer stops him: "never had I seen so great courage, nay, not amongst all the Nephites" (v45). But what's striking is that the courage is *not bravado*. They don't say "we're strong, we'll win." They say "our God is with us, and he will not suffer that we should fall" (v46) — courage that is entirely a function of faith. They are fearless because they trust God's deliverance, not because they trust their own arms. And even in that fearless moment they keep the family-not-enemy frame their people are known for: "we would not slay our brethren if they would let us alone" (v46). They'd rather not fight at all; they fight only because Antipus's army will be destroyed if they don't. The same reluctance that defines Moroni's whole war (48:23) lives in these untested teenagers.

And then verses 47-48, which are among the most beloved in the Book of Mormon and deserve their fame. "They had been taught by their mothers, that if they did not doubt, God would deliver them. And they rehearsed unto me the words of their mothers, saying: We do not doubt our mothers knew it." Two things here are profound. First, the faith was *maternal and taught*. These boys' extraordinary trust didn't come from a battlefield conversion or a personal vision — it came from their mothers, drilled into them at home before there was any war to test it. The most formidable faith in the war record was cultivated in childhood by believing women. (And remember who these mothers were: the people of Ammon, the converted Lamanites who "never did fall away," who buried their weapons rather than risk their souls. The mothers who taught "if you do not doubt, God will deliver you" had themselves staked everything on that exact trust. They taught what they had lived.) Second, the form of the sons' faith: "we do not doubt *our mothers knew it*." They don't claim independent knowledge — they claim confidence in their mothers' knowledge. This is the gift the Doctrine and Covenants names (D&C 46:14): "to others it is given to believe on their words." The stripling warriors had not yet had their own confirming experience; what they had was utter trust that their mothers had had theirs, and that borrowed faith was *real enough to march into battle on*. It's a tender and important doctrine — faith that rests on the trustworthy witness of someone who loves you and has lived it is not a lesser faith. It's a true beginning, and for these young men it was sufficient to win a war. (It echoes Timothy, whose "unfeigned faith" dwelt "first in thy grandmother Lois, and thy mother Eunice," 2 Tim 1:5.)

The decoy itself (vv30-49) is worth noting because it shows the cost and the trust together. Helaman is ordered to march his "little sons" as bait — to lead "the most powerful army of the Lamanites" on a chase, which is a desperately dangerous role; if the trap fails, the boys die first. And Helaman's protectiveness is real — "I would not suffer that my little sons should fall into their hands" (v39), he calls them "my sons (for they are worthy to be called sons)" (v10). But the boys' faith makes the dangerous stratagem possible: they march willingly into the bait role because they don't doubt God will deliver them. There's a partnership in it — Helaman's strategic care and the young men's fearless faith, working together. Faith doesn't make Helaman reckless with their lives; it makes the young men willing to be used where they're needed.

And the headline miracle closes the account (vv55-56): after a "terrible battle" in which Antipus himself falls and his army nearly breaks, the two thousand turn the tide by striking the Lamanite rear — and not one of them is killed. Helaman numbers them "fearing lest there were many of them slain," and his joy is overwhelming: "there had not one soul of them fallen to the earth; yea, and they had fought as if with the strength of God; yea, never were men known to have fought with such miraculous strength." The preservation is the divine signature on their faith. They were taught "if they did not doubt, God would deliver them" — they did not doubt, and God delivered them, every one. Mormon isn't claiming such preservation is guaranteed to all faithful soldiers (Antipus and many others died righteous, v51, and "they are happy," v11). The miracle of the unscathed two thousand is a particular sign, a vindication of a particular promise taught and trusted. But the sign is unmistakable, and its meaning points straight back to the mothers: the faith they planted in their sons was not naive — it was *true*, and the empty casualty list among the boys is the proof their mothers' knowledge was real. "We do not doubt our mothers knew it." After the battle, they had no reason to.

Yes — among the most beloved chapters in the book: the stripling warriors' first battle and **"we do not doubt our mothers knew it" (56:47-48)** — faith taught by believing mothers, and the doctrine of believing on a trusted witness's words (D&C 46:14).
