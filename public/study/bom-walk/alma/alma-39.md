# Alma 39 — the grieved father: confronting Corianton without abandoning him

**Binding idea:** Alma's charge to Corianton is the hard one — the son who "didst go on unto boasting in thy strength and thy wisdom" (39:2) and then "didst forsake the ministry... after the harlot Isabel" (39:3). Alma names the sin without flinching ("an abomination in the sight of the Lord; yea, most abominable above all sins save it be the shedding of innocent blood or denying the Holy Ghost," 39:5) and names its consequence without softening it: "when they saw your conduct they would not believe in my words" (39:11) — Corianton's private sin cost the Zoramites their belief. But the whole rebuke is framed by love: "I would not dwell upon your crimes, to harrow up your soul, if it were not for your good" (39:7). The path back is repentance, restitution ("return unto them, and acknowledge your faults," 39:13), and humbly taking counsel from his steadier brothers (39:10).

## Thoughts

The chapter opens by holding up Shiblon as the mirror: "have ye not observed the steadiness of thy brother... has he not set a good example for thee?" (v1). It would be easy to read this as a father playing favorites, but it's doing something more careful — Alma is showing Corianton that the alternative was right there, lived by his own brother under the same conditions (both were missionaries among the Zoramites; Shiblon endured bonds and stoning faithfully, Corianton bolted). And the root Alma names first isn't the sexual sin — it's the pride that preceded it: "thou didst go on unto boasting in thy strength and thy wisdom" (v2). That's the exact thing Alma had just warned Shiblon against ("do not boast in your own wisdom, nor of your much strength," 38:11) — except Shiblon heeded and Corianton didn't. The sin didn't start in Siron with Isabel; it started in the heart that trusted its own strength and stopped heeding counsel. Pride cleared the ground; the harlot was what grew in it.

Alma confronts the sin directly, and the project's commitment to honest engagement means not flinching here. Sexual sin is ranked "most abominable above all sins save it be the shedding of innocent blood or denying the Holy Ghost" (v5) — third, after the sin against the Holy Ghost and murder. The gravity isn't arbitrary prudishness; the three gravest sins all involve the misuse or destruction of life and light at their source: denying the Holy Ghost (rejecting received light), shedding innocent blood (destroying a life), and the misuse of the power by which life is created. Procreation is the one power humans share most directly with God — the power to call a new soul into mortality — and its abuse ranks where it does because of what it is, not because the body is shameful. Alma treats it with exactly that weight: serious, not squeamish. And he refuses to let Corianton minimize it by pointing at Isabel — "she did steal away the hearts of many; but this was no excuse for thee" (v4). The other person's sin is never your alibi.

But what makes this fatherhood and not just condemnation is verse 7: "I would not dwell upon your crimes, to harrow up your soul, if it were not for your good." Alma, who was himself "harrowed up" for three days (ch 36), knows the harrowing is not cruelty — it's the necessary pain that opens the door to the cry for mercy. He's deliberately causing his son discomfort because he's been to the bottom himself and knows the discomfort is the way up. That's love with a spine: not the love that excuses, and not the wrath that crushes, but the love that says the true hard thing because the soul's good requires it. "Ye cannot hide your crimes from God" (v8) is not a threat from a distant judge; it's a father telling his son the thing the son most needs to stop pretending isn't true.

The consequence in verse 11 is the heaviest, and it reframes how I think about a representative's sin. "How great iniquity ye brought upon the Zoramites; for when they saw your conduct they would not believe in my words." Corianton wasn't a private citizen sinning privately — he was a missionary, and his behavior became the reason a whole people rejected the gospel. His sin had a body count measured in unbelief. This is the Nathan-to-David truth ("thou hast given great occasion to the enemies of the Lord to blaspheme," 2 Sam 12:14): when you carry the name of the Lord, your failures don't stay yours. They become other people's excuse to reject what you represent. That's a sobering weight on anyone who teaches or leads — the sin is never just between you and God when you're standing for God in front of others. The Zoramites who wouldn't believe "because of your conduct" are part of what Corianton has to repent of, and part of why the repentance must include returning "and acknowledge your faults" (v13). Restitution, not just remorse.

And the way back is specific and humbling. "Take it upon you to counsel with your elder brothers in your undertakings; for... thou art in thy youth, and ye stand in need to be nourished by your brothers" (v10). The proud son who trusted his own strength is told to go submit himself to the counsel of the brothers he'd been outshone by. "Turn to the Lord with all your mind, might, and strength" (v13), "seek not after riches nor the vain things of this world" (v14). Then Alma eases toward the doctrine, because he discerns the sin had roots in doubt — Corianton "marvels" why Christ's coming is prophesied so long beforehand (v17), and Alma's answer ("is not a soul at this time as precious unto God as a soul will be at the time of his coming?") opens onto the three doctrinal chapters that follow. The pastoral genius is that Alma doesn't only rebuke the behavior; he rebuilds the belief that collapsed under it. A son doesn't usually fall into Isabel's arms purely from appetite; he falls because the framework that would have held him stopped seeming real. So Alma reinforces the framework — resurrection, restoration, justice and mercy — in 40-42. The rebuke and the doctrine are one act of reclamation.

## Notable

Yes — the pastoral model of confronting grave sin without abandoning the sinner: name it honestly (39:5), name its cost to others (39:11), but frame the whole as "for your good" (39:7) and point to repentance + restitution + counsel. And the key insight that the sin started in pride (39:2) and had roots in doubt that Alma rebuilds in 40-42. The flagged Corianton spin-off (39-42) is running.
