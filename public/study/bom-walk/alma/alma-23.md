# Alma 23 — "never did fall away": the new name and the lifted curse

**Binding idea:** The mass Lamanite conversion produces two staggering results. First, permanence — "as many of the Lamanites as believed... and were converted unto the Lord, never did fall away" (23:6), a conversion with zero apostasy, tied directly to their laying down "the weapons of their rebellion" (23:7). Second, the curse is *lifted* — they become "industrious," "friendly with the Nephites," and "the curse of God did no more follow them" (23:18). The change is behavioral and relational, not cosmetic: enmity and idleness reversed by a changed heart. And the king's stated goal is the grievance dissolved — "that they might be convinced that they were all brethren" (23:3).

**Read:** Alma 23 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

The king's proclamation is the grievance-cure made law, and verse 3 names the aim exactly. He decrees full protection for the missionaries "that his people might be convinced concerning the wicked traditions of their fathers, and that they might be convinced that they were all brethren." Set that beside the over-king's own speech four chapters ago — "these Nephites, who are sons of a liar... he robbed our fathers" — and the reversal is total. The man who recited the grievance now legislates against it, and the thing he wants his people convinced of is the precise negation of the grievance: not "they are sons of a liar" but "they were all brethren." The lie said *enemy by blood*; the cure says *brother by blood*. The footnote (26:24) points forward to where Ammon reflects on this whole turning.

Verse 6 is one of the most remarkable sentences in the Book of Mormon, and I don't want to rush past it: "as many of the Lamanites as believed in their preaching, and were converted unto the Lord, never did fall away." A conversion of thousands across seven lands with — the text insists, "as sure as the Lord liveth" — not one apostasy. Compare that to the Nephites, whose entire history is the pride cycle: convert, prosper, forget, fall, repeat. The Lamanite converts break the cycle entirely. Why? Verse 7 ties it directly to one act: "they did lay down the weapons of their rebellion, that they did not fight against God any more, neither against any of their brethren." The permanence is bound to the surrender. They didn't just believe; they disarmed — against God and against each other — and the disarming is what made the conversion irreversible. There's a principle here I want to hold: the conversion that lasts is the one that gives up the weapon, the grudge, the thing you were holding in reserve. Belief that keeps its sword stays reversible. Belief that buries the sword can't go back.

Then the data confirms chapter 21's sociology coldly: the born-Lamanites convert in mass across seven named lands (vv8-13), but "the Amalekites were not converted, save only one; neither were any of the Amulonites" (v14). The apostate-Nehorite dissenters convert at a rate of essentially zero; the supposedly cursed and ferocious natives convert by the thousand and never fall away. The hardest ground was never the Lamanites — it was the people who'd had the truth and built a religion to escape it. The grievance tradition could be cured; the counterfeit-religion of Nehor could not. That contrast is the whole grievance-cure thesis in one verse.

The new name matters. They wanted "a name, that thereby they might be distinguished from their brethren" (v16), and chose Anti-Nephi-Lehies — "no more called Lamanites" (v17). To stop being called Lamanite is to renounce the identity the grievance was built on; "Lamanite" carried Laman's rebellion in the very word (Jacob 1:13). Whatever the "Anti" prefix precisely means — the converts seem to be aligning themselves *toward* Nephi and Lehi, the faithful founders, rather than Laman the rebel — the move is identity reconstruction. They reject the name that named them by their grievance and take one that names them by the fathers they now choose to honor.

And verse 18 is the verse I have to log for the curse question, because it's explicit. "They began to be a very industrious people; yea, and they were friendly with the Nephites... and the curse of God did no more follow them." Three markers of the curse lifting, and every one is behavioral or relational, not physical: *industrious* (reversing the "idle" of 2 Ne 5:24 and Alma 22:28), *friendly* (reversing the enmity), in *correspondence* with the Nephites (reversing the separation). The curse, whatever else 2 Nephi 5 meant by it, functions here as a state of hostility-and-idleness that is *removed* the moment the heart changes — and the chapter's own footnote for "the curse of God did no more follow them" points to 2 Ne 30:6, the very verse about the curse being taken away and the people becoming "pure." The text itself ties curse-removal to conversion and reframes the curse as a condition of enmity, not a permanent ethnic mark. I'm not resolving 2 Ne 5:21 — that stays for Michael — but this is a strong datapoint, and I'm logging it.
