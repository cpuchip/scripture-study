# Alma 17 — the prince who chose to be a servant: Ammon at Sebus

**Binding idea:** The great Lamanite mission opens by naming exactly what it intends to cure — "the baseness of the traditions of their fathers, which were not correct" (17:9), the inherited grievance studied in mos10. And the method is set before the miracle: the Lord commands *patience, long-suffering, good example* (17:11), and Ammon enacts it by refusing a king's daughter to become a flock-herding *servant* (17:25). The prince inverts the very power-relation the grievance was built on. The arm-smiting at Sebus is not conquest — it is, by Ammon's own stated motive, a way "to win the hearts of these my fellow-servants, that I may lead them to believe in my words" (17:29).

**Read:** Alma 17 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Note the superscription: this record "comprising chapters 17 through 27" — the sons-of-Mosiah account runs 17–27.

## Thoughts

The frame is set by the reunion. Alma, journeying from Gideon to Manti, meets the sons of Mosiah after fourteen years apart — and his joy is specifically that "they were still his brethren in the Lord" (v2). They were all converted together by the angel (Mosiah 27); the question after fourteen years was whether they stayed converted, and they did. But more than stayed — they "waxed strong... had searched the scriptures diligently... given themselves to much prayer, and fasting; therefore they had the spirit of prophecy, and the spirit of revelation, and when they taught, they taught with power and authority of God" (vv2-3). The *therefore* is the engine: the diligence produced the power. They didn't go to the Lamanites on raw zeal. They went prepared. That's a rebuke to the idea that a good heart is enough — these men studied for the work.

And they refused the kingdom for it (v6). This is the human cost behind Mosiah 29's abolition of kings. The monarchy didn't just end as a constitutional decision — the heirs *declined the crown* because they wanted "that perhaps they might bring [the Lamanites] to know of the plan of redemption" (v16) more than they wanted to reign. The kingdom of Nephi, traded for fourteen years of hunger, thirst, fatigue, and "much labor in the spirit" (v5) among a people who "delighted in murdering the Nephites" (v14). Their brethren mocked it as folly (we'll get the line in ch 26). They went anyway.

Verse 9 is the thesis of the whole arc, and the footnote proves it: they prayed to bring the Lamanites "to the knowledge of the baseness of the traditions of their fathers, which were not correct" — footnote points to Alma 3:11, the verse from three chapters ago where the "tradition" is what the mark tracks, not the blood. The mos10 study diagnosed the disease: a grievance narrative (Mosiah 10:12-17) taught father-to-son until hatred calcified. Alma 17 names the cure's target precisely — not the people, the *tradition*. You don't kill Lamanites; you dislodge a story.

Then the method. The Lord's instruction in vv10-11 is striking for what it's *not*: not "confound them," not "prove them wrong," but "be patient in long-suffering and afflictions, that ye may show forth good examples unto them in me." The conversion strategy is endurance and example, not argument. And Ammon embodies it immediately. Offered the king's daughter — offered, that is, to be made family, to rise — he answers "Nay, but I will be thy servant" (v25). The son of the Nephite king becomes a Lamanite king's herdsman. Every term of the grievance ("the Nephites think they're better, they robbed us, they lord over us") is contradicted by a Nephite prince voluntarily watching another man's flocks. You cannot easily hate the servant who chose to serve you.

Sebus is usually read as the action sequence, and it is dramatic — the sling, six men down, the clubs, the smitten arms, only the leader slain by the sword (vv36-38). But verse 29 disarms any reading of it as mere heroism. When the flocks scatter and the servants weep in terror of the king, Ammon's "heart was swollen within him with joy" — not bloodlust, but opportunity: "I will show forth my power... that I may win the hearts of these my fellow-servants, that I may lead them to believe in my words." The whole point of the rescued flocks and the severed arms is *relationship*, and relationship is for the sake of *belief*. The defense is a sermon's prologue. He notably "slew none save it were their leader" — he disables rather than kills where he can, smiting off the arms that lift to smite him. Restraint inside the power. The arms carried in to the king (v39) become "a testimony," and that testimony opens chapter 18.

It's a deliberate echo of Moses at the well of Midian, defending the daughters of Reuel against the shepherds who drove them off (Ex 2:17, the chapter's own footnote). The well-deliverance is a biblical type-scene — the stranger who proves himself at the water, and is brought into the household. Ammon is the new Moses at a new well, and the household he's about to enter is a Lamanite king's.

## Connections

- **Mosiah 27:11-34** — the angel's appearance; the sons of Mosiah converted with Alma. "Still his brethren in the Lord" (v2) measures the fourteen-year fruit of that conversion.
- **Mosiah 28:1-9; 29:3** — they sought the mission and refused the kingdom; Mosiah 29's abolition of kings has its human root here.
- **Mosiah 10:12-17** — "a wild and hardened and ferocious people" (v14, footnote → Mosiah 10:12) is the grievance tradition as lived behavior; the disease mos10 diagnosed.
- **Alma 3:11** — "the baseness of the traditions of their fathers" (v9) targets the *tradition*, not the blood — the same distinction Alma 3 drew about the mark.
- **Ex 2:17** — Moses defends the daughters at the well of Midian; Sebus is the type-scene, and the deliverer is brought into the household.
- **2 Kgs 5:8** — "that he may know there is a prophet" (footnote to v29) — the miracle as credential for the message.
- **Mosiah 28:7; Alma 19:22-23** — the Lord's promise to Mosiah to "deliver his sons out of their hands"; why the Sebus attackers could not kill Ammon.
- **Alma 26** — Ammon's later psalm answers the brethren who mocked this mission; "instrument in the hands of God" (vv9,11) recurs at 26:3.
- **Philippians 4:13 / Alma 26:12** — "in his strength I can do all things" — the theology behind the strength of his arm at Sebus, made explicit in ch 26.
