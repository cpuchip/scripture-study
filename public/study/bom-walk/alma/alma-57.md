# Alma 57 — "with exactness," and the faith that preserves through wounds, not from them

**Binding idea:** Helaman's epistle continues — Antiparah and Cumeni retaken, the prisoner crisis, and another battle the stripling warriors turn. Their defining behavior is named: "they did obey and observe to perform every word of command with exactness; yea, and even according to their faith it was done unto them" (57:21). And the miracle deepens from chapter 56: this time *all* of them are wounded — "there was not one soul among them who had not received many wounds," two hundred faint from loss of blood — yet still "there was not one soul of them who did perish" (57:25). Helaman ascribes it "to the miraculous power of God, because of their exceeding faith in that which they had been taught to believe—that there was a just God, and whosoever did not doubt, that they should be preserved" (57:26). Their faith didn't spare them the wounds; it preserved them through them. "They are young, and their minds are firm, and they do put their trust in God continually" (57:27).

## Thoughts

Verse 21 names the trait that, alongside their faith, makes the stripling warriors what they are: "they did obey and observe to perform every word of command with exactness." Exactness. Not approximately, not mostly, not the parts that made sense to them — every word of command, precisely and completely. And Helaman immediately links it to faith: "yea, and even according to their faith it was done unto them" (Matt 9:29, "according to your faith be it unto you"). The exactness and the faith are the same thing seen from two sides. Their obedience isn't anxious legalism — it's the outward form of a settled inward trust. Because they truly believed God would deliver them, they could obey orders completely, without the second-guessing and self-preserving hesitation that makes most soldiers (and most of us) shave the corners off hard commands. Faith produces exactness; exactness expresses faith. This is the same virtue introduced in 53:20 ("true at all times in whatsoever thing they were entrusted") — reliability — now shown in action under fire. You could give these young men any command and know it would be done, fully, because their trust in God freed them from the fear that makes people cut corners.

But the chapter's deepest contribution is how it *corrects* a too-easy reading of chapter 56's miracle. In 56, none of the two thousand were slain, and one might conclude that faith means escaping harm. Chapter 57 will not let that stand. This time, after the battle, Helaman finds that "there were two hundred, out of my two thousand and sixty, who had fainted because of the loss of blood... neither was there one soul among them who had not received many wounds" (57:25). *Every single one was wounded.* Many wounds, two hundred unconscious from blood loss. Faith did not put a force field around them. They bled, they suffered, they nearly died. And yet "there was not one soul of them who did perish." That's the precise shape of the miracle, and it's far more honest and useful than "the faithful don't get hurt." Faith preserved them *through* the wounds, not *from* them. It's the three Hebrews walking through the fire rather than around it (Dan 3); it's "yea, though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death" — through, not around. The promise the mothers taught was "if ye do not doubt, God will deliver you" — and deliverance turned out to mean carried through the slaughter alive, covered in wounds, not exempted from the battle. That's a truer and harder doctrine of faith than the prosperity version, and it's the one I need: faith is not a guarantee against suffering; it's the assurance of being preserved through it for God's purposes. Helaman makes the contrast explicit — they were spared "while there was a thousand of our brethren who were slain" (v26). The thousand who died were not less faithful; the preservation of the two thousand sixty was a *particular sign*, not a universal rule. Sometimes God carries some through untouched-by-death as a witness; the others who fall righteous are no less His ("I trust that the souls of them who have been slain have entered into the rest of their God," v36).

The chapter also contains genuinely hard material that I won't smooth over. The prisoner crisis (vv13-16, 32-33) is brutal: the Nephites have more prisoners than they can feed or guard, the prisoners repeatedly "break out" and attack with stones and clubs, and "we did slay upwards of two thousand of them after they had surrendered themselves prisoners of war" (v14); later, when the prisoners under Gid "rise up in rebellion," they "run upon our swords" and most are killed (v33). Surrendered prisoners slain — it reads harshly to a modern conscience, and it should. The text frames it as forced rather than chosen: the killing came on the prisoners' own armed rebellion, the Nephites lacked provisions and men to hold them safely, and their first resolve was to send them to Zarahemla, not to execute them. Held honestly, this is the grim arithmetic of an army at the edge of its capacity in an existential war — not an atrocity the record celebrates, but a hard reality it reports plainly. The Book of Mormon's war isn't sanitized; men in desperate circumstances make desperate, bloody choices, and Mormon records them without applause. It's a reminder that even a righteous war is full of horror, and that the record's honesty includes the parts that don't flatter anyone.

The note Helaman ends on (v27) is the steady heart of it: "they are young, and their minds are firm, and they do put their trust in God continually." *Continually* — not just in the crisis, but as a settled disposition. Their firmness of mind (Jacob 3:2's "if your minds are firm") and continual trust are what the mothers' teaching produced — not a one-time act of courage but a permanent orientation. And the exactness of v21 is its behavioral expression. Put together, the stripling warriors are a portrait of formed character under maximum pressure: faith taught from childhood → minds firm and trusting continually → obedience exact under fire → preserved through (not from) the wounds. Mormon, who named his own son after Captain Moroni, clearly treasured this account — and the lesson he draws is not "be brave" but "do not doubt, and obey with exactness, and you will be carried through." Not unscathed. Through.
