# Alma 19 — "to me he doth not stink": the queen's faith and Abish the hidden disciple

**Binding idea:** The conversion of Lamoni's house turns on two women the grievance tradition would have written off. The queen's quiet faith — "to me he doth not stink" (19:5), believing her husband lives when the court says he's a rotting corpse — draws from Ammon the verdict that should reorder everything: "there has not been such great faith among all the people of the Nephites" (19:10). And Abish, a Lamanite servant secretly converted years before by her father's vision, is the one who gathers the crowd that becomes the first Lamanite church. The despised people produce the greatest faith in the record, and "his arm is extended to all people who will repent" (19:36).

**Read:** Alma 19 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

The chapter is built on a Lazarus frame, and the footnotes know it. Lamoni has lain "two days and two nights"; some say "he is dead and that he stinketh" and should be entombed (v5, footnote → John 11:39, Martha at Lazarus's tomb). Ammon asks the queen "Believest thou this?" (v9, footnote → John 11:26, Christ to Martha: "Believest thou this?"). The king "sleepeth in God, and on the morrow he shall rise" (v8) — Lazarus is "not dead but sleepeth." Mormon is telling a resurrection story in miniature: the king goes down as if dead, and rises declaring "I have seen my Redeemer" (v13). The man who'll preach the resurrection is first walked through a figure of it.

But the verse that stops me is the queen's. The whole court has pronounced her husband a corpse; the smell is the evidence; the sepulchre is ready. And she says, simply, "as for myself, to me he doth not stink" (v5). That's faith stated as plainly as faith ever gets — not a theological claim, just a refusal to accept the verdict of death over someone the Spirit tells her still lives. She has, as she admits, "no witness save thy word, and the word of our servants; nevertheless I believe that it shall be according as thou hast said" (v9). Belief on testimony alone, without her own sign. And Ammon's response is the hinge of the whole grievance arc: "there has not been such great faith among all the people of the Nephites." It's the centurion all over again — "I have not found so great faith, no, not in Israel" (Luke 7:9, the chapter's own footnote). The outsider, the supposedly cursed Lamanite woman, out-believes the entire covenant nation. The grievance said the Nephites think themselves better. Ammon, a Nephite prince, declares a Lamanite queen the greatest believer he's met. The inversion is total.

The conversion itself is described as light overcoming darkness (v6) — "the dark veil of unbelief was being cast away from his mind, and the light... which was the light of the glory of God... had infused such joy into his soul, the cloud of darkness having been dispelled, and the light of everlasting life was lit up in his soul." This is the exact inverse of the chains-of-hell mechanism from Alma 12: there, refusing the word let darkness fill and bind the mind; here, receiving it lets light flood and free it. Same architecture, opposite direction. And it overwhelms the body — "carried away in God" — the way the joy at Sebus "swelled" Ammon's heart. The pattern across this arc is that real conversion is too big for the frame; it knocks people to the earth.

Then Abish, who I always underweight. She "having been converted unto the Lord for many years, on account of a remarkable vision of her father" — and "never having made it known" (vv16-17). A secret disciple in a hostile court, holding her faith silently for years. When the household falls, she alone stays standing because she already knew this power, and she sees the opening: gather the people, let them behold the scene, "it would cause them to believe." So she runs house to house. She is the catalyst — the first Lamanite church assembles because a hidden convert finally acted on the moment. But it nearly goes wrong: the crowd she gathers erupts in contention (the grievance tradition speaking — Ammon is "a monster, who had been sent from the Nephites to torment them," v26), and Abish, seeing the contention she triggered, weeps "even unto tears" (v28). Then she does the small physical thing that breaks the deadlock — takes the queen by the hand, and the queen rises crying "O blessed Jesus, who has saved me from an awful hell! O blessed God, have mercy on this people!" (v29). The hidden disciple's quiet faith, held for years and finally spent, is what turns the room.

The Sebus reckoning lands here too: the brother of a man Ammon slew draws his sword to kill the prostrate Ammon and "fell dead" (v22) — the promise to Mosiah fulfilled, "I will spare him... according to thy faith" (v23). And the chapter ends on the change-of-heart mark and the universal reach: those converted "declare... that their hearts had been changed; that they had no more desire to do evil" (v33, the Mosiah 5:2 sign), many "had seen angels and conversed with them" (v34), and the closing line answers the curse question directly — "his arm is extended to all people who will repent and believe on his name" (v36, footnote → 2 Ne 26:33). The Lamanites receive the Spirit in full. Whatever the curse was, it was never a wall the gospel couldn't cross.

## Connections

- **Luke 7:9** — "I have not found so great faith, no, not in Israel"; the centurion. "Not such great faith among all the Nephites" (v10) is the same inversion — the outsider out-believes the covenant people. The grievance overturned.
- **John 11:26, 39** — "Believest thou this?" and "he stinketh"; Lamoni as a Lazarus figure, the resurrection-in-miniature.
- **2 Cor 4:4** — the veil of unbelief blinding the mind (v6); conversion as the veil cast away.
- **Alma 12:9-11** — the chains of hell; v6's light dispelling darkness is the exact inverse mechanism.
- **Mosiah 5:2** — "no more disposition to do evil"; the change-of-heart mark restated at v33.
- **Mosiah 28:7; Alma 17:35** — the promise to Mosiah, "I will spare him... according to thy faith" (v23); why the Sebus avenger fell dead.
- **2 Ne 26:33** — "his arm is extended to all people who will repent" (v36, footnote) — "all are alike unto God"; the Lamanites' full reception of the Spirit. **(Datapoint for the OPEN-for-Michael 2 Ne 5:21 study — the cursed people receive everything; the curse was never a barrier to the Spirit.)**
- **Moses 1:9-10** — "prostrate upon the earth"; the natural frame overcome by glory (also Moses 6:37-39, "monster," v26).
- **Alma 22:22** — the queen-by-the-hand raising recurs when Aaron raises Lamoni's father; the gesture repeats.
