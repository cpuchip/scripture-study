# Alma 21 — Aaron's harder field: the religion that removed repentance

**Binding idea:** Aaron's mission is the dark mirror of Ammon's. Where Ammon found a king with a fragment of true belief ("a Great Spirit") to build on, Aaron finds the Amalekites and Amulonites — Nehorite apostates with full-blown counterfeit religion: synagogues, assemblies, worship, and a doctrine ("God will save all men," 21:6) engineered to remove any need to repent. The hardest soil is not irreligion; it's a self-justifying faith that has already answered the question repentance asks. They are "still harder" (21:3) than the Lamanites who never believed.

**Read:** Alma 21 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Superscription: "the preaching of Aaron... comprising chapters 21 through 25."

## Thoughts

The chapter opens with a sociology of hardness, and it's exact. The city is called Jerusalem, built by "the Lamanites and the Amalekites and the people of Amulon" (v2) — and the gradient runs: "Now the Lamanites of themselves were sufficiently hardened, but the Amalekites and the Amulonites were still harder; therefore they did cause the Lamanites that they should harden their hearts" (v3). The dissenters are harder than the natives, and they make the natives worse. The Amulonites descend from Amulon, Noah's wicked priest who infiltrated the Lamanites (Mosiah 23-24); the Amalekites are Nephite dissenters. These are people who had the truth, or proximity to it, and left — and apostates are reliably the hardest ground in this record. The man who never knew can be taught; the man who knew and rejected has built defenses. It's why the Amalekite synagogues are "after the order of the Nehors" (v4) — the Nehorism that's been the through-line of Nephite apostasy since Alma 1 has now metastasized into a whole Lamanite-territory religion.

And the counterfeit's doctrine is the key. The Amalekite who stands to contend says it plainly: "Behold, we have built sanctuaries, and we do assemble ourselves together to worship God. We do believe that God will save all men" (v6). That last line is Nehor's gospel verbatim (Alma 1:4 — Nehor taught "all mankind should be saved at the last day"). It's universalism deployed as an anti-repentance technology. If God saves everyone regardless, then "except we repent we shall perish" is not just wrong, it's offensive — "How knowest thou that we are not a righteous people?" (v6). They have constructed a religion that affirms them exactly as they are. That is far more resistant to the gospel than atheism, because it occupies the gospel's own ground and has pre-answered its central demand. You can't call someone to repentance who has a theology proving they don't need to.

They also deny prophecy outright — "We do not believe in these foolish traditions... neither do we believe that thy fathers and also that our fathers did know concerning the things which they spake, of that which is to come" (v8). This is the anti-Christ template (Jacob 7:2, Sherem; later Korihor): cut the cord to revelation, and the future can't indict the present. Aaron's response is the right one regardless of reception — he "began to open the scriptures unto them concerning the coming of Christ, and... that there could be no redemption for mankind save it were through the death and sufferings of Christ, and the atonement of his blood" (v9). He preaches the atonement plainly into a room that has theologized it away. And they mock him and won't hear (v10). The same gospel that raised a king two chapters ago bounces off a hardened congregation here. The variable was never the message; it's the soil.

So Aaron's arc is suffering, not triumph — Ani-Anti, then Middoni, "few believed," then prison (vv11-13), then the rescue we already saw from Ammon's side in chapter 20. But verse 16 is the quiet faithfulness: "they went forth whithersoever they were led by the Spirit of the Lord, preaching the word of God in every synagogue... where they could be admitted." Paul's pattern (Acts 16:6) — go where the Spirit sends, preach where they'll let you in, leave where they won't. And eventually "the Lord began to bless them, insomuch that they brought many to the knowledge of the truth; yea, they did convince many of their sins, and of the traditions of their fathers, which were not correct" (v17) — the grievance-cure phrase from 17:9 recurring. Even the hardest field yields, after the prison, after the patience.

The chapter ends on the first decree of religious freedom in the arc, and it's a beautiful inversion. Lamoni "would not suffer that Ammon should serve him, or be his servant" (v19) — the prince who insisted on being a servant in chapter 17 is now refused the servanthood, because the relationship has been transfigured from master/slave into brother/brother. Then Lamoni builds synagogues and declares his people "free from the oppressions of the king, his father" (v21) and grants "the liberty of worshiping the Lord their God according to their desires" (v22). The convert-king's first political act is to legislate religious freedom — the same principle the Restoration enshrines (A of F 1:11). The gospel that freed his soul, he turns into law that frees his people's worship.
