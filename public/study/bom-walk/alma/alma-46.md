# Alma 46 — the title of liberty: the inner threat, the rent coat, and the covenant of freedom

**Binding idea:** The danger now is internal, not external. Amalickiah — "a man of cunning device and a man of many flattering words" — uses ambition and flattery to lead away "the lower judges... seeking for power," to "destroy the foundation of liberty which God had granted" (46:4-10). Moroni answers not with an army first but with a *symbol*: he rends his coat, writes on it "In memory of our God, our religion, and freedom, and our peace, our wives, and our children," raises it as "the title of liberty," and prays "for the blessings of liberty to rest upon his brethren, so long as there should a band of Christians remain" (46:12-13). The people covenant by rending their own garments — a self-maledictory sign: keep faith and be preserved, "or the Lord should rend them even as they had rent their garments" (46:21). And the protection is conditional: "God shall not suffer that we... shall be trodden down and destroyed, until we bring it upon us by our own transgressions" (46:18).

## Thoughts

The chapter's first lesson is that the deadliest threat to a free, faithful people comes from inside, and it wears the face of ambition dressed as flattery. Amalickiah doesn't invade; he *recruits*. He's "a large and a strong man... desirous to be a king" (vv3-4), and his weapon is words — he wins "the lower judges of the land... seeking for power" by promising "that if they would support him and establish him to be their king that he would make them rulers over the people" (vv4-5). Flattery plus the promise of power is the whole mechanism. And Mormon draws the moral explicitly: "we also see the great wickedness one very wicked man can cause to take place among the children of men" (v9). The Nephites just survived a Lamanite invasion by faithfulness; weeks later one charismatic demagogue nearly dissolves them from within. Mormon's lament is the one that recurs through the whole record: "thus we see how quick the children of men do forget the Lord their God, yea, how quick to do iniquity, and to be led away by the evil one" (v8). Quick. Not gradually — quick. The forgetting follows the deliverance almost instantly.

Moroni's response is the iconic image, and what strikes me is the *order* of his actions. He rends his coat and writes the title (v12); he puts on his armor (v13); he calls it the title of liberty — and then, before he waves it before a single person, "he bowed himself to the earth, and he prayed mightily unto his God for the blessings of liberty" (v13). The symbol and the sword come second; the prayer comes first. Moroni doesn't rally the crowd and then ask God to bless what he's already started — he kneels in his armor and pours out his soul before he raises the standard. The cause of liberty is consecrated in prayer before it's proclaimed in public. And the content of the title itself is worth weighing: "our God, our religion, and freedom, and our peace, our wives, and our children." It's the same catalog Moroni gave Zerahemnah (44:5) and the same the Nephites fought for in 43:45 — the title of liberty isn't an abstraction about freedom in general; it's a list of the concrete, beloved things worth defending. Liberty here means the freedom to keep your God, your worship, your peace, and your family. That's what the banner is *for*.

The covenant the people make is the chapter's most arresting ritual (vv19-22). They come "rending their garments in token, or as a covenant, that they would not forsake the Lord their God" — and the meaning they assign to the tearing is self-maledictory: "if they should transgress the commandments of God, or fall into transgression, and be ashamed to take upon them the name of Christ, the Lord should rend them even as they had rent their garments." They cast their torn garments at Moroni's feet, declaring "we shall be destroyed, even as our brethren in the land northward, if we shall fall into transgression... he may cast us at the feet of our enemies, even as we have cast our garments at thy feet to be trodden under foot." This is an ancient covenant form — you enact upon a symbol what you're inviting upon yourself if you break faith. It's sobering: the covenant of liberty is also a covenant of accountability. They aren't asking for unconditional protection; they're binding themselves to faithfulness *on pain of becoming the trodden garment* if they fall. The same conditional protection from chapter 44 (v18 here: destroyed only "by our own transgressions"), now sealed by the people's own hands on their own clothing.

Then the Joseph typology, which Moroni reads off the torn coat (vv23-27). "We are a remnant of the seed of Joseph, whose coat was rent by his brethren into many pieces" — and he recalls Jacob's prophecy that a *remnant* of Joseph's coat was "preserved and had not decayed," signifying that a remnant of Joseph's seed would be preserved by God while the rest perished. The rent coat of Genesis 37 becomes the rent garments of the covenant becomes the rent coat of Moroni — three layers of the same image, all pointing at the remnant doctrine. And Moroni turns it pointed: "who knoweth but what the remnant of the seed of Joseph, which shall perish as his garment, are those who have dissented from us? Yea, and even it shall be ourselves if we do not stand fast in the faith of Christ" (v27). The Amalickiahites might be the perishing piece — or *we* might, if we fall. The typology refuses to flatter the in-group; the remnant that's preserved is the faithful one, whoever that turns out to be. This is also where "Christians" enters the record (v15) — the true believers "took upon them, gladly, the name of Christ, or Christians as they were called." A name first given (here as in Acts) by outsiders, worn gladly by the faithful.

The chapter's hard edge is verse 35: the Amalickiahites who "would not enter into a covenant to support the cause of freedom... he caused to be put to death." I won't soften that — a free government, with Amalickiah already fleeing to raise the Lamanites against them, treated refusal to support the common defense as a capital matter. It reads harshly to a modern eye, and it should be held honestly: this is wartime sedition law in an existential conflict, not peacetime coercion of belief (and the record notes "there were but few who denied the covenant of freedom"). It sits in tension with the chapter's celebration of liberty — the freedom defended by the title of liberty did not extend to the freedom to actively side with the enemy in a war for survival. The Book of Mormon doesn't present its heroes as flawless; Moroni is a wartime commander making wartime judgments, and the record lets the hard parts stand. Liberty and its defense are not simple, and the text doesn't pretend they are. What it does insist on is the frame: the title is hoisted "upon every tower" (v36), the standard of liberty planted, and the peace that follows is the fruit of a people who covenanted to remember their God or be rent like the garment.
