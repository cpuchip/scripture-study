# Alma 51 — the king-men's spite, the door it opened, and the javelin that ended Amalickiah

**Binding idea:** When Pahoran refuses to alter the law, a faction of "high birth" who "sought to be kings" (51:8) — the king-men — try to overthrow the free government; the freemen prevail "by the voice of the people" (51:7). But when they lose, the king-men turn seditious: with Amalickiah invading, they are "glad in their hearts" and "would not take up arms to defend their country," "so wroth with the chief judge... and the people of liberty" (51:13). Their internal division costs the nation dearly — while Moroni is forced to put them down (51:17-21), Amalickiah captures a string of strongly-fortified cities, turning the Nephites' own defenses into Lamanite strongholds (51:22-27). And the chapter ends the treacherous king the way the proverb promises: Teancum steals into Amalickiah's tent by night and "put a javelin to his heart" (51:34). The man who swore the "rash" oath to drink Moroni's blood (51:10) dies in his sleep, far from Moroni — "thus endeth the days of Amalickiah" (51:37).

**Read:** Alma 51 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

The king-men crisis is the Book of Mormon's clearest portrait of sedition born from spite, and it's painfully recognizable. The dispute begins legitimately — a faction wants "a few particular points of the law" altered, Pahoran refuses, and the matter is "settled by the voice of the people" in the freemen's favor (vv2-7). That's the system working: a disagreement resolved by the reign-of-judges' ballot, not by blood. The freemen win fair. But the king-men don't accept the verdict. They were "of high birth... sought to be kings... supported by those who sought power and authority over the people" (v8) — and when they lose the vote, they don't reconcile; they nurse the grudge. So when Amalickiah invades, "they were glad in their hearts; and they refused to take up arms... so wroth with the chief judge, and also with the people of liberty, that they would not take up arms to defend their country" (v13). This is the bitter loser at his worst: so resentful of the side that beat him by lawful means that he would rather see the whole nation conquered than lift a finger to defend it alongside his rivals. Their hatred of their political opponents exceeded their love of their country. That's not a partisan caricature; it's a permanent temptation of factional spite — to prefer the catastrophe that vindicates your grievance over the victory that requires cooperating with people you resent.

And the cost is laid out with brutal cause-and-effect. Mormon flags the timing: "this was a critical time for such contentions to be among the people of Nephi" (v9). While Moroni is forced to turn his army inward to crush the king-men (4,000 hewn down, the leaders imprisoned, vv17-21), Amalickiah pours through the undefended east and takes city after city — Moroni, Nephihah, Lehi, Morianton, Omner, Gid, Mulek — "all of which were strongly fortified after the manner of the fortifications of Moroni; all of which afforded strongholds for the Lamanites" (v27). The bitterest irony in the whole war: the cities Moroni had fortified to protect his people now shelter the invader, because internal division stripped them of defenders. The enemy within enabled the enemy without. A kingdom divided against itself cannot stand (Matt 12:25), and here the division doesn't just weaken the nation — it hands the nation's own strongholds to its destroyer. The king-men's refusal to fight didn't keep them out of the war; it just ensured they'd lose it from the inside first.

Moroni's response is the hard edge the war record keeps showing, and I'll hold it honestly as before. He petitions Pahoran "with the voice of the people" for power to "compel those dissenters to defend their country or to put them to death" (v15), it's granted, and he marches against them — 4,000 killed, leaders jailed "for there was no time for their trials at this period" (v19), the rest "compelled to hoist the title of liberty" (v20). This is martial law in an existential invasion, and it reads harshly. But Mormon frames the necessity precisely: "it was his first care to put an end to such contentions and dissensions among the people; for behold, this had been hitherto a cause of all their destruction" (v16). The internal rot, not the external army, had always been the real threat — and with an invasion underway, Moroni couldn't fight Amalickiah while seditious thousands refused to defend the line and gladdened at the enemy's approach. The record doesn't soften it into something comfortable; it presents a wartime leader making a wartime judgment that the survival of the free government required suppressing armed sedition by force. One can hold both that the measure was severe and that the king-men's treason in the face of invasion was real. The book lets the hard thing stand.

And then Amalickiah's end, which is the chapter's grim poetry. The man rose to power by treachery — luring Lehonti off the mount, poisoning him by degrees, stabbing the trusting king mid-handshake (ch 47). He swore an oath "to drink the blood of Moroni" (v9), which Mormon pointedly calls "rash" (v10) and then proves empty: Amalickiah never gets near Moroni. Instead, Teancum and a single servant slip into his camp at night, into the king's own tent, and Teancum "put a javelin to his heart... that he did not awake his servants" (v34). The murderer who killed sleeping and trusting men dies asleep himself, silently, by a single thrust. "He that diggeth a pit shall fall therein." The boast that outran reality — he'd drink another man's blood — recoils into his own heart on a javelin. There's a sobering economy in it: Amalickiah spent the whole arc weaponizing other people's trust and other people's sleep, and that is exactly how he dies. The pit he dug for everyone else was the grave he fell into. "Thus endeth the days of Amalickiah" — and the record gives him no eulogy, only the date.

Yes — the king-men crisis (51:5-21), the Book of Mormon's clearest study of sedition-from-spite (preferring the nation's defeat to a rival's victory) and of internal division handing the enemy the nation's own fortifications (51:27). Moroni's hard wartime measure held honestly. And Amalickiah's pit-digger end (51:34) — the treacherous king dies asleep on a javelin, the rash oath recoiling. "Thus endeth the days of Amalickiah."
