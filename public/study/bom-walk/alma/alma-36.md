# Alma 36 — the cry at the center: Alma's conversion told whole to Helaman

**Binding idea:** Alma gives Helaman his own conversion as the paradigm of repentance, and the telling is built as a chiasmus that turns on a single point. The descent: angel, three days "racked, even with the pains of a damned soul" (36:16), the memory of every sin harrowing him until "the very thought of coming into the presence of my God did rack my soul with inexpressible horror" (36:14). The hinge: remembering his father's prophecy of "one Jesus Christ... to atone for the sins of the world" (36:17), he cries "O Jesus, thou Son of God, have mercy on me" (36:18) — the literal center of the chapter. The ascent: "I could remember my pains no more" (36:19), and joy "as exceeding as was my pain" (36:20). Pain and joy are exactly symmetrical, and the cry to Christ is the axis between them. The whole experience makes him a missionary — "that I might bring them to taste of the exceeding joy of which I did taste" (36:24).

## Thoughts

This is the retelling of Mosiah 27 in the first person, decades later, to a son — and the form is as much the message as the content. The chapter is a large chiasmus (the pattern A-B-C... pivot ...C'-B'-A'): it opens and closes on "keep the commandments... ye shall prosper in the land" (vv1, 30); just inside, "remember the captivity of our fathers" (vv2, 29); then trust/support in trials (vv3, 27); born of God (vv5, 23-24); going to destroy the church / the angel (vv6-9) mirrored by standing up born again (v23); the racking torment (vv12-16) mirrored by the exquisite joy (vv20-21); and at the dead center, vv17-18 — the memory of Christ and the cry to him. Whether or not one counts every rung, the structure's center is unmistakable and it preaches: the lowest point of the soul and its turning are the same moment, and that moment is the cry to Jesus. Everything in a life mirrors around it. An ancient Hebrew would have heard the architecture; we mostly read past it. But Alma built his testimony so that the climax sits in the middle, not the end, because in the geography of the soul the bottom *is* the turning.

The descent is the most exact description of what "the pains of hell" actually are that I know. Alma doesn't say he felt flames. He says "I did remember all my sins and iniquities, for which I was tormented with the pains of hell" (v13), and "I was harrowed up by the memory of my sins" (v17). Hell, here, is *memory in the presence of God* — every sin recalled with perfect clarity by a soul that now sees what it did and cannot bear to stand before its Maker. "The very thought of coming into the presence of my God did rack my soul with inexpressible horror" (v14). The torment is so total he wishes for annihilation: "Oh, thought I, that I could be banished and become extinct both soul and body, that I might not be brought to stand in the presence of my God, to be judged of my deeds" (v15). He'd rather cease to exist than be judged. That's the pains of a damned soul — not external fire but the unendurable clarity of your own record held up to perfect light. And the chains image confirms it: he is "encircled about by the everlasting chains of death" (v18), the same chains-of-hell vacancy from Alma 12 that a refusing heart lets in.

The hinge is a memory too, and that's the whole doctrine of how anyone gets out. In the middle of remembering his sins, "I remembered also to have heard my father prophesy unto the people concerning the coming of one Jesus Christ, a Son of God, to atone for the sins of the world" (v17). Memory was the torment; a different memory becomes the rescue. What you reach for at the bottom decides everything — and what saved Alma was a seed his father had planted years before, a prophecy heard and apparently ignored that surfaced exactly when he was drowning. (Every parent and teacher should take heart from that: the word you plant in someone who seems to reject it may be the thing they grab when they're going under.) And the cry is stripped to its essentials: "O Jesus, thou Son of God, have mercy on me" (v18). No bargain, no list of amends, no promise to do better — just the name of Christ and a plea for mercy from a man who knows he's earned none. That's the publican's prayer, that's the whole gospel in one sentence, offered from the gall of bitterness.

And the turn is instantaneous and total. "When I thought this, I could remember my pains no more; yea, I was harrowed up by the memory of my sins no more" (v19). The memory that was hell doesn't get easier — it's gone, replaced. And then the symmetry that is the chapter's deepest teaching: "there could be nothing so exquisite and so bitter as were my pains... on the other hand, there can be nothing so exquisite and sweet as was my joy" (v21). The joy is exactly as deep as the pain was. This isn't coincidence; it's how the atonement works. The depth of the godly sorrow becomes the measure of the joy on the other side — the same proportionality Ammon named in his psalm (the most-forgiven feel it hardest, 27:17). Which means the harrowing wasn't pointless cruelty; it was the excavation that made room for a joy of equal depth. You don't get the exquisite joy without first letting the exquisite sorrow do its full work. And the vision that follows — "even as our father Lehi saw, God sitting upon his throne, surrounded with numberless concourses of angels... my soul did long to be there" (v22) — is the inverse of v15: the man who wished to be banished from God's presence now longs to be in it. The cry didn't just remove the pain; it reversed the deepest direction of his desire.

Then the conversion becomes a vocation, and this is the part I want to hold. "From that time even until now, I have labored without ceasing, that I might bring souls unto repentance; that I might bring them to taste of the exceeding joy of which I did taste" (v24). The joy isn't a private possession to be hoarded; it's the engine of a life spent helping others taste it. Alma can't stop preaching because he can't stop wanting others to feel what he felt at v20. And he offers Helaman external verification — "many have been born of God, and have tasted as I have tasted, and have seen eye to eye as I have seen" (v26). His experience isn't a one-off; it's reproducible, confirmed by everyone who's run the same experiment (the seed of Alma 32, now testified to from the far side). The chapter closes where it opened, on the covenant — keep the commandments and prosper, fail to and be cut off (v30) — but now the abstract promise is wrapped around a true story of a man who went all the way down and was carried all the way up by a single cry to the Son of God.
