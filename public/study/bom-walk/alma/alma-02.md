# Alma 2 — The Voice of the People Works, and a Faction Defies It Anyway

*(Amlici — a Nehor-follower — seeks to be king, is rejected by the voice of the people, makes himself king by force, and wars; the Amlicites ally with the Lamanites; Alma slays Amlici in battle. ~87 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Mosiah's reign of judges gets its first political stress-test, and the test is precisely the one he warned about (29:26-27). The voice of the people renders the right verdict — and a faction takes up the sword rather than accept it. What does this reveal about the system Mosiah built?

**Read:** "A certain man... called **Amlici**... a very cunning man, yea, a wise man as to the wisdom of the world, he being **after the order of the man that slew Gideon** [Nehor]" (v1) draws away "much people," who "endeavor to establish Amlici to be a king" (v2). This alarms the church, "for they knew that according to their law that such things must be established by the **voice of the people**" (v3) — and "if... Amlici should gain the voice of the people, he, being a wicked man, would deprive them of their rights and privileges of the church; for it was his intent to destroy the church" (v4). They cast their voices (v6); "the **voice of the people came against Amlici**, that he was not made king" (v7). But Amlici "did stir up those who were in his favor to anger," and they "did **consecrate Amlici to be their king**" anyway (vv8-9), commanding war against their brethren (v10). The Amlicites vs. the Nephites/people of God (v11). At the hill Amnihu east of Sidon, battle joins (v15); Alma, "the chief judge and the governor," leads the army (v16); "the Lord did **strengthen the hand of the Nephites**" (v18) — 12,532 Amlicites and 6,562 Nephites slain (v19). The Amlicites join "a numerous host of the **Lamanites**" (v24) and drive toward Zarahemla. Crossing Sidon against enemies "as numerous almost... as the sands of the sea" (v27), "the Nephites being **strengthened by the hand of the Lord, having prayed mightily**... the Lord did hear their cries" (v28). Alma fights Amlici "with the sword, face to face" (v29), praying: "O Lord, have mercy and spare my life, **that I may be an instrument in thy hands to save and preserve this people**" (v30); "he was strengthened, insomuch that he slew Amlici" (v31). The enemy flees, slain and scattered to the wilderness Hermounts (vv35-38).

**Thoughts:**
- *The voice of the people works — and a faction defies it (vv3-9).* This is the immediate test of Mosiah 29's design, and it cuts both ways at once. On one hand, the system *functions exactly as intended*: the majority rejects the would-be tyrant — "the voice of the people came against Amlici" (v7), confirming Mosiah's claim that "it is not common that the voice of the people desireth anything contrary to that which is right" (29:26). The free people, asked, choose rightly. On the other hand, the system's *limit* is exposed in the very next verse: Amlici and his followers, having lost the vote, simply *reject the verdict* and "consecrate Amlici to be their king" by their own faction, then go to war (vv8-10). This is the sober truth about self-government that Mosiah only hinted at: the voice of the people can protect against a tyrant winning *legitimately*, but it cannot, by itself, stop a tyrant who seizes power *illegitimately* by force. A ballot defeats a demagogue at the polls; it does not defeat his army. The system is righteous and it is not self-enforcing — when a faction will not abide the outcome, the question moves from votes to swords. (The "lesser part of the people" Mosiah named, 29:27, here refuses to be the lesser part and makes war to overturn the majority.)
- *The Nehor heresy becomes armed insurrection (v1).* The continuity is deliberate: Amlici is "after the order of the man that slew Gideon" — a follower of Nehor's priestcraft (ch 1). The *religious* corruption of chapter 1 flows directly into the *political* insurrection of chapter 2. It is the same impulse — the desire to be elevated and obeyed above others, pride seeking power — wearing first a preacher's robe and then a would-be king's crown. The false doctrine did not stay in the chapel; it organized into a faction, lost an election, and took up the sword. Priestcraft and the lust for unrighteous dominion are the same root in two seasons, and they are lethal: nearly 19,000 dead in a single day (v19) trace back to Nehor's flattering gospel. Bad doctrine, left to grow, ends in mass graves.
- *Dissent makes common cause with the enemy (vv24-25).* The Amlicites, unable to win at home, *join the Lamanites* — the internal dissenters ally with the external enemy against their own people. This is a recurring Book of Mormon pattern (the grievance-tradition study noted Alma 60:32, "redoubled by those who have dissented from us"): the faction that cannot prevail among its own brethren strengthens the nation's enemies, and the war becomes far worse for the alliance. Apostasy rarely stays neutral; it tends to find the enemy and enlist. (And in the next chapter the Amlicites will mark themselves and be reckoned Lamanites — the dissent completing into full enmity.)
- *Alma the warrior who asks only to serve (vv16, 29-31).* Alma is now chief judge, governor, *and* the general who leads the army into battle and fights Amlici hand to hand — the one scene of personal combat in his record. And his battlefield prayer reveals the man: "O Lord, have mercy and spare my life, **that I may be an instrument in thy hands to save and preserve this people**" (v30). He asks for his life not for his own sake but to serve — the exact inverse of Amlici, who sought power *over* the people. Two men fight to the death, and the difference between them is the difference between wanting to preserve a people and wanting to rule them. (It is also why Alma will soon lay down the judgment-seat, ch 4 — power was never the point for him; the people were.)
- *Strengthened by the hand of the Lord (vv18, 28, 31).* The recurring battle theology returns, three times in one chapter: the vastly outnumbered Nephites ("sands of the sea," v27) are "strengthened by the hand of the Lord" *after praying mightily*. Victory is not credited to numbers or generalship but to God's strengthening of those who cry to Him — the same theology as Zeniff (Mosiah 9-10). The arm of flesh would have lost; the strength of the Lord wins. Even Alma's personal victory over Amlici comes only after the prayer and the strengthening (vv30-31) — the man of God prevails by borrowed strength, not his own arm.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The chapter is the **first political stress-test of the reign of judges**, and it cuts both ways: **the voice of the people works** — the majority rightly rejects Amlici (v7, confirming Mosiah 29:26) — *but it is not self-enforcing*: his faction defies the verdict, makes him king by force, and goes to war (vv8-10). A ballot defeats a demagogue at the polls; it does not defeat his army. The system is righteous and rests, finally, on a people willing to abide its outcomes. The chapter also shows **heresy becoming insurrection** — Amlici is "after the order of" Nehor (v1); the priestcraft of ch 1 flows into the kingship-bid of ch 2 (the same pride-seeking-dominion in two forms), and it costs nearly 19,000 lives in a day (v19). **Dissent allies with the enemy** (the Amlicites join the Lamanites, vv24-25 — apostasy enlists the foe). And **Alma the warrior-prophet asks only to serve** — his battlefield prayer is to be "an instrument... to save and preserve this people" (v30), the inverse of Amlici's lust to rule — prevailing, like the whole army, only when "strengthened by the hand of the Lord" after prayer (vv28, 31).
