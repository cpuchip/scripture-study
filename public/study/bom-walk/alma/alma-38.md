# Alma 38 — the steady son: "bridle all your passions, that ye may be filled with love"

**Binding idea:** Alma's charge to Shiblon is the shortest and warmest of the three, because Shiblon is the steady one — "I have had great joy in thee already, because of thy faithfulness and thy diligence, and thy patience and thy long-suffering among the people of the Zoramites" (38:3). The counsel is reinforcement, not correction: keep your steadiness, "there is no other way or means whereby man can be saved, only in and through Christ" (38:9), don't boast in your wisdom or strength (38:11), and the gem — "Use boldness, but not overbearance; and also see that ye bridle all your passions, that ye may be filled with love" (38:12). The bridle isn't suppression; it's harness — passions controlled so that love can fill the space. And don't pray the Zoramite prayer ("I thank thee that we are better than our brethren") but the publican's ("O Lord, forgive my unworthiness... remember my brethren in mercy," 38:14).

**Read:** Alma 38 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). The charge to Shiblon (the middle of the three sons).

## Thoughts

The first thing to notice is the tone, because it tells you how to read all three charges together. To Shiblon, Alma opens with approval he doesn't have to manufacture: "I trust that I shall have great joy in you, because of your steadiness and your faithfulness... I have had great joy in thee already" (vv2-3). Shiblon had been "in bonds" and "stoned for the word's sake" among the Zoramites and bore it with patience (v4) — he suffered like Aaron, and he stayed. This is the dependable son, and the charge to him is mostly "keep doing what you're doing." Set beside the records-heavy charge to Helaman (the trusted heir) and the long correction coming to Corianton (the one who strayed), you see Alma practicing the "separately" of 35:16 — three sons, three genuinely different words, because love that's paying attention doesn't say the same thing to every child. Shiblon needs reinforcement; Corianton needs rebuke; Helaman needs trust. A father who gave all three the same speech wouldn't be loving them; he'd be processing them.

Alma compresses his whole conversion into three verses for Shiblon (vv6-8), and the compression is instructive about what he thinks is load-bearing: the angel, the three days of "most bitter pain and anguish of soul," and then the hinge — "never, until I did cry out unto the Lord Jesus Christ for mercy, did I receive a remission of my sins. But behold, I did cry unto him and I did find peace to my soul." Stripped to its essentials, the conversion is: pain, until the cry; then peace. The same center as the Alma 36 chiasmus, just told plain. And he draws the doctrine straight from it: "there is no other way or means whereby man can be saved, only in and through Christ. Behold, he is the life and the light of the world" (v9). Alma's own experience is his proof of the exclusivity of Christ — he tried everything else, including running from God, and found that the only thing that worked was the cry to Jesus.

The gem of the chapter, and one of the most quoted lines in the book, is v12: "see that ye bridle all your passions, that ye may be filled with love." The image is exact and easy to misread. A bridle doesn't kill a horse or even slow it down much; it *directs* the animal's power so it serves the rider instead of bolting. Alma isn't telling Shiblon to extinguish his passions — to become cold or feelingless — he's telling him to harness them. And the purpose clause is the part most people drop: bridle your passions *that ye may be filled with love*. Unbridled passion crowds love out; a passion under the rein leaves room for love to fill. So self-mastery here isn't the enemy of feeling; it's the precondition for the highest feeling. The person ruled by appetite and anger has no room left for charity; the person who has reined those in has the capacity love requires. (And the same verse pairs it with "use boldness, but not overbearance" — bold in the truth, not domineering over people; and "refrain from idleness," the recurring anti-Zoramite, anti-Lamanite-curse virtue.)

The close returns to the Rameumptom one more time, now as explicit instruction to a son. "Do not pray as the Zoramites do, for ye have seen that they pray to be heard of men, and to be praised for their wisdom. Do not say: O God, I thank thee that we are better than our brethren; but rather say: O Lord, forgive my unworthiness, and remember my brethren in mercy" (vv13-14). Alma quotes the Rameumptom prayer (31:16) almost verbatim as the thing to *not* do, and gives the publican's prayer (Luke 18:13) as the model. The instruction "acknowledge your unworthiness before God at all times" isn't a recipe for low self-worth; it's the posture that keeps the heart from curdling into the Zoramite contempt. The steady, faithful son — the one with the most legitimate grounds to feel he's done well — is precisely the one who most needs to be told not to pray "I thank thee that we are better." Faithfulness is the soil pride grows best in. Alma knows it, and he inoculates his best son against his best son's particular danger.

- **Alma 31:16; Luke 18:9-14** — "I thank thee that we are better than our brethren"; the Rameumptom prayer named as the anti-pattern, the publican's prayer ("forgive my unworthiness") as the model (vv13-14).
- **Alma 36:6-22** — the conversion (vv6-8 compressed); pain until the cry, then peace.
- **Alma 36:3** — trust in God → delivered from trials (v5).

- **Alma 21:13-14; 38:4** — Shiblon's suffering among the Zoramites (bonds, stoned); he endured like Aaron.
- **Alma 39** — the contrast: the warm joy in Shiblon vs. the grieved correction of Corianton; the "separately" of 35:16.
- **1 Pet 5:8** — "be sober" (v15).
- **Matt 10:22; 2 Ne 31:16** — "blessed is he that endureth to the end" (v2).

## Notable

Yes — **Alma 38:12** ("bridle all your passions, that ye may be filled with love") is one of the most quoted verses in the book, and the purpose clause (restraint *for* love) is the part usually missed. Also the three-sons-three-words discernment and the publican-vs-Rameumptom prayer instruction.
