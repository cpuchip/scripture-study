# Alma 18 — "Believest thou that there is a God?": meeting the king inside his own cosmology

**Binding idea:** Ammon converts a Lamanite king not by overpowering his worldview but by *entering* it. Lamoni has a tradition of "a Great Spirit" (18:5); Ammon starts there — "Believest thou that there is a Great Spirit? ... This is God" (18:26-28) — and expands the small true thing into the whole plan of redemption. And the faithfulness that earned the hearing is named in verse 10: "even he doth remember all my commandments to execute them." The servant who finishes what he's handed is the servant the king will finally listen to.

**Read:** Alma 18 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

Verse 10 is the hinge, and it's the verse I carry as a working principle. Lamoni, hearing that Ammon — instead of resting on his Sebus victory — is out preparing the king's horses and chariots for the journey he was assigned, marvels: "Surely there has not been any servant among all my servants that has been so faithful as this man; for even he doth **remember all my commandments to execute them**." The thing that cracks the king open is not the severed arms. It's that Ammon did the small assigned task, fully, without being reminded. The miracle got the king's attention; the *faithfulness in the ordinary errand* earned his trust. Ammon does not coast on the spectacular. He tends the horses. That's the whole shape of discipleship in one detail — and it's why this verse anchors the rule about finishing what you're handed (Alma 18:10): the scatter at the well was the opportunity, but so was the chore afterward.

The conversion method in this chapter is a master class, and it begins with restraint. The king is paralyzed — won't speak for "the space of an hour" (v14) — and Ammon, "filled with the Spirit," perceives his thoughts and names them: is it the defense of the flocks "that causeth thy marvelings?" (v16). Then immediately he disclaims the thing the king fears: "Behold, I am a man, and am thy servant" (v17). When Lamoni asks point-blank "Art thou that Great Spirit?" Ammon answers flatly, "I am not" (v19). He will not let a false elevation stand even when it would be useful. The honesty is the foundation.

Then the holy guile. "Now Ammon being wise, yet harmless" (v22 — the footnote sends you to Joseph in Egypt, Gen 41, and the phrasing is Christ's own "wise as serpents, harmless as doves") — he secures the commitment before the content: "Wilt thou hearken unto my words, if I tell thee by what power I do these things?" The king: "Yea, I will believe all thy words. And thus he was caught with guile" (v23). It's the only "guile" Scripture commends — Ammon traps Lamoni into *listening*, nothing more. He gets a yes to the question "will you hear me" before he risks the harder questions.

And then the pedagogy that is the heart of the chapter. "Believest thou that there is a God?" — "I do not know what that meaneth" (vv24-25). The word means nothing to Lamoni. A lesser teacher pushes the word harder. Ammon pivots instantly to the king's own vocabulary: "Believest thou that there is a Great Spirit?" — "Yea." — "This is God" (vv26-28). He doesn't demand Lamoni abandon his frame and adopt a Nephite one; he shows Lamoni that the true God is the thing Lamoni already dimly worshipped, rightly named. Start where the hearer is. Build the new on the foundation of whatever true fragment they already hold. From "Great Spirit" he expands to Creator, to the heavens "above the earth" where God dwells (vv29-32), to the God who "knows all the thoughts and intents of the heart" — which lands, because Ammon just demonstrated exactly that by reading Lamoni's mind.

Verses 34-35 are Ammon's real answer to "by what power": "I am a man; and man in the beginning was created after the image of God, and I am called by his Holy Spirit to teach these things... and a portion of that Spirit dwelleth in me, which giveth me knowledge, and also power according to my faith and desires which are in God." Not a Great Spirit — a man with a portion of the Spirit, with power scaled to faith and desire. That's a doctrine of how anyone accesses God's power, dropped into a missionary lesson.

The most quietly radical move is in vv36-38: Ammon retells the Lamanites' own origin story *truthfully*. He rehearses creation, fall, the scriptures down to Lehi leaving Jerusalem, the journey, "and also... concerning the rebellions of Laman and Lemuel." This is the grievance tradition corrected at the source. The Lamanite version (Mosiah 10) says Nephi robbed them, the Nephites are liars who stole the birthright. Ammon lays the true record in front of the king — including who actually rebelled — not as an accusation but as history. You can't dissolve a false story with a counter-accusation; you dissolve it by laying the true record open and letting the king read it. The king believes (v40), cries "O Lord, have mercy... upon me, and my people" (v41) — praying for his people in the same breath as himself — and falls as if dead (v42), which sets up chapter 19.

## Connections

- **Alma 18:10** — "remember all my commandments to execute them" — the faithfulness in the ordinary task that earns the hearing; the anchor of the "finish what you're handed" principle (cf. Alma 17:25, the servant choice).
- **Matt 10:16** — "wise as serpents, harmless as doves"; "Ammon being wise, yet harmless" (v22); the holy guile of v23.
- **Gen 41:38-39; Dan 2:30; 5:16** — Ammon cast in the Joseph/Daniel mold: the captive made the king's wise man because "the Spirit of God" is in him, interpreting what the king cannot grasp.
- **Mosiah 10:12-17** — the grievance tradition; Ammon's truthful retelling "concerning the rebellions of Laman and Lemuel" (v38) corrects it at the root — the cure mos10 set up.
- **Alma 22:12-14** — Aaron will do the same creation-to-redemption arc for Lamoni's father; the missionary template repeated.
- **Alma 17:15; 18:5** — Lamoni's tradition of "a Great Spirit"; the true fragment Ammon builds on.
- **Alma 22:18** — Lamoni's father will pray the same prayer ("I will give away all my sins to know thee") and fall the same way; v42 sets the pattern. (Connects to the existing give-away-all-my-sins study.)
- **John 20:16** — "Rabbanah" (v13), kin to "Rabboni"; the title the servants give Ammon.
- **Mosiah 7:27; Ether 3:15** — "man... created after the image of God" (v34); the doctrine grounding Ammon's self-description.
