# Alma 37 — small and simple things, counsel with the Lord, and the Liahona as type

**Binding idea:** Alma hands Helaman the sacred records and three intertwined lessons. First, "by small and simple things are great things brought to pass... by very small means the Lord doth confound the wise and bringeth about the salvation of many souls" (37:6-7) — the unimpressive instrument carries the great work (the records that converted thousands of Lamanites, 37:8-9). Second, on handling evil: keep the destroyed Jaredites' secret oaths *from* the people; teach the wickedness so they abhor it, never the method, "lest peradventure they should fall into darkness also" (37:27-32). Third, and most personal: "Counsel with the Lord in all thy doings, and he will direct thee for good" (37:37). It closes on the Liahona as a type — it worked "according to their faith," by small means, and stopped when the fathers grew "slothful... because of the easiness of the way" (37:41-46); the word of Christ is the same compass: "if we will look we may live forever" (37:46).

**Read:** Alma 37 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Continues the charge to Helaman (36-37).

## Thoughts

The famous line — "by small and simple things are great things brought to pass; and small means in many instances doth confound the wise" (v6) — is given in a specific context that's easy to forget: Alma is handing over *the records*, and he anticipates Helaman thinking the fuss over old plates is "foolishness." His answer is that God's signature method is the small instrument. It's Paul's "God hath chosen the foolish things... to confound the wise" (1 Cor 1:27), and Alma applies it concretely: "were it not for these things that these records do contain... Ammon and his brethren could not have convinced so many thousands of the Lamanites" (v9). The entire grievance-cure arc I just walked (17-26) rode on these plates — the true history that corrected the false tradition, the prophecies of Christ, came off the brass plates. A whole civilization's conversion turned on a stack of metal sheets a young man was now responsible to keep bright. The great work hides in the small means, and the wise dismiss the small means precisely because they're small. I want to remember that against my own instinct to measure importance by impressiveness.

The secret-combinations section (vv21-32) is a striking ethic about how to handle evil, and it's more subtle than it first reads. Alma commands Helaman to preserve the record of the destroyed Jaredites' "murders, and robbings, and their plunderings" so the people will "abhor such wickedness" — but to keep "their oaths, and their covenants, and their agreements in their secret abominations... from this people, that they know them not, lest peradventure they should fall into darkness also and be destroyed" (vv27-29). Teach the *fruit* of secret combinations (so people hate them); never teach the *recipe* (so people can't replicate them). It's a recognition that some knowledge is genuinely dangerous to disseminate — not because truth should be hidden, but because the operational details of evil are a seed that grows wherever it's planted. "Teach them an everlasting hatred against sin and iniquity" (v32), not a working knowledge of how to do it. That's a wise distinction for any age that catalogs evil: expose the consequences, withhold the manual. (And it points forward — the 24 plates are Ether, the Jaredite record, and the Gadianton robbers will later prove Alma right by reinventing the secret oaths "from the records" anyway.)

But the heart of the chapter, for me, is the counsel to a young son in verses 35-37, because it's a whole spiritual life in three verses. "Learn wisdom in thy youth" (v35) — don't wait. "Let all thy doings be unto the Lord... let all thy thoughts be directed unto the Lord; yea, let the affections of thy heart be placed upon the Lord forever" (v36) — doings, thoughts, affections, the three layers of a person all oriented one direction. And then the verse I keep returning to: "Counsel with the Lord in all thy doings, and he will direct thee for good; yea, when thou liest down at night lie down unto the Lord... and when thou risest in the morning let thy heart be full of thanks" (v37). Not "pray when you're in trouble" but counsel with him in *all* your doings — make God a partner in the ordinary decisions, bracket every day between a night surrender and a morning of thanks. It's Proverbs 3:5-6 ("in all thy ways acknowledge him") turned into a daily rhythm a teenager could actually live. The promise attached isn't ease; it's direction: "he will direct thee for good." A guided life, not a comfortable one.

The Liahona ties the whole charge together and lands the theme that's been building since the brazen serpent. The compass "did work for them according to their faith in God" (v40) — it ran on faith, not mechanics. And the failure mode is precise: "because those miracles were worked by small means... they were slothful, and forgot to exercise their faith and diligence, and then those marvelous works ceased" (v41). They despised the instrument *because* it was small and easy, stopped giving heed, and got lost in the wilderness. Then Alma names the type outright: "these things are not without a shadow... it is as easy to give heed to the word of Christ, which will point to you a straight course to eternal bliss, as it was for our fathers to give heed to this compass" (vv43-44). The word of Christ is the Liahona — a small daily instrument that points a straight course if you give heed, and goes dark the moment you treat its easiness as license to neglect it. And the warning is the exact one from chapter 33's brazen serpent: "do not let us be slothful because of the easiness of the way... if they would look they might live; even so it is with us. The way is prepared, and if we will look we may live forever" (v46). Three times now in this stretch — the serpent, the seed, the Liahona — the lesson is the same: the way is simple, the means are small, and the human failure is always to despise it *for* being simple. The danger isn't that the gospel is too hard. It's that it's too easy to take seriously. "Look to God and live... be sober. My son, farewell" (v47).
