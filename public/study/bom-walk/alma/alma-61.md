# Alma 61 — "I am not angry, but do rejoice in the greatness of your heart"

**Binding idea:** Pahoran answers Moroni's furious, accusatory letter — and reveals that every harsh assumption was wrong. He wasn't in "thoughtless stupor" or a traitor; he had been violently "driven out" of the judgment seat by king-men rebels who "withheld our provisions" so the freemen could not reach the army (61:3-5). The rebels had taken Zarahemla, crowned a king, and allied with the Lamanites (61:8). And to the man who threatened to march on him and called him a possible traitor, Pahoran responds: "in your epistle you have censured me, but it mattereth not; I am not angry, but do rejoice in the greatness of your heart" (61:9). He looks past the false, wounding words to the good heart behind them and refuses offense entirely. He even finds the letter helpful: "I do joy in receiving your epistle, for I was somewhat worried... whether it should be just in us to go against our brethren. But ye have said, except they repent the Lord hath commanded you that ye should go against them" (61:19-20). The wrongly-aimed letter, because its doctrine was sound, resolved Pahoran's real doubt.

## Thoughts

The chapter first vindicates Helaman over Moroni, gently and completely. Two chapters ago, Helaman — equally abandoned, equally denied help — refused to murmur and reached for the charitable explanation: "we do not know but what ye are unsuccessful... if so, we do not desire to murmur" (58:35). Moroni, in the identical situation, assumed the worst — "thoughtless stupor," "traitors" (60:7, 18). And here is the truth: Helaman was *right*. The government wasn't idle or treasonous; Zarahemla had been overthrown from within, Pahoran driven out, the provisions withheld by rebels. The man who assumed the best had read the situation correctly; the man who assumed the worst had not. Mormon doesn't editorialize, but the structure makes the point unmistakable — when you can't see why someone failed you, the charitable assumption is not only kinder, it's more often *accurate*, because people who let you down are usually drowning in something you can't see rather than indifferent to you. Helaman's instinct ("maybe they're in trouble too") was both more loving and more true.

But the chapter's glory is verse 9, Pahoran's response to being unjustly attacked, and it is one of the most Christlike sentences in scripture. He has just been called a traitor, accused of sitting in idle stupor while thousands die, threatened with armed insurrection — by a man who got the facts entirely wrong. Pahoran has every right to wounded pride, to a defensive recitation of "do you have any idea what I've been through," to counter-accusation. He does none of it. "In your epistle you have censured me, but it mattereth not; I am not angry, but do rejoice in the greatness of your heart." He doesn't merely tolerate the false accusation — he looks straight through the harsh words to the good heart that wrote them, and *rejoices* in it. He recognizes that Moroni's fury came from love of liberty and grief over the dead, and he honors the love rather than resenting the misjudgment. This is charity that "is not easily provoked," that "beareth all things, endureth all things" (1 Cor 13). It's the soft answer that turns away wrath (Prov 15:1). It's Christ "when he was reviled, reviled not again" (1 Pet 2:23). And it's almost unbearably mature: the falsely-accused man making it about the accuser's good heart rather than his own vindicated innocence. Most of us, wrongly accused, can't get past "but I didn't do anything wrong." Pahoran skips his own innocence entirely and goes straight to celebrating the zeal of the man who wronged him. That is the response I most need to learn, because being right and being wronged is exactly the situation where I dig in hardest — and Pahoran shows that the bigger response is to absorb the false charge and bless the heart behind it.

And then the twist that redeems Moroni's error without erasing it (vv19-20): Pahoran says the angry letter actually *helped* him. "I do joy in receiving your epistle, for I was somewhat worried concerning what we should do, whether it should be just in us to go against our brethren. But ye have said, except they repent the Lord hath commanded you that ye should go against them." Pahoran had a genuine moral doubt — was it righteous to fight fellow Nephites, the king-men rebels? And Moroni's letter, though built on a false assumption about Pahoran's heart, contained a true revelation (60:33, the Lord's command to go against the unrepentant) that resolved the doubt. So the letter that was *wrong about the person* was *right about the principle*, and the right principle did real good. This is a generous and important reading: God can use even our flawed, mistaken-in-their-premises communications to carry a true word that helps someone — and Pahoran chose to find the helpful true thing in the letter rather than fixate on the unjust accusation. He could have read Moroni's letter as an insult; he read it as counsel. The difference is entirely in the reader's charity. (And it's a quiet rebuke to my own habit of receiving correction by its tone rather than its truth — Pahoran took the truth and let the harsh tone go.)

The doctrine threaded through Pahoran's reply matters too. "We would subject ourselves to the yoke of bondage if it were requisite with the justice of God, or if he should command us so to do. But behold he doth not command us that we shall subject ourselves to our enemies, but that we should put our trust in him, and he will deliver us" (vv12-13). This is striking: liberty is *not* the highest value for Pahoran. He would surrender his freedom if God commanded it. But God does not command submission here — He commands resistance and trust. So they fight, not because freedom is an absolute, but because *obedience to God* is, and God happens to command the defense of freedom in this case. That keeps the whole war record's liberty-theme properly subordinated: the Nephites don't worship freedom; they worship God, who has told them to defend the freedom He gave. And Pahoran ends as graciously as he began — "tell them to fear not, for God will deliver them, yea, and also all those who stand fast in that liberty wherewith God hath made them free" (v21). The two men, briefly at cross-purposes through a misunderstanding, are united again in the same cause, the same trust, the same God. The exchange becomes a model of how strong, faithful men handle conflict: one rebukes sharply (even mistakenly), the other receives it without offense and finds the truth in it, and the friendship deepens rather than fractures (they fight side by side from here). It's D&C 121:43 lived out — "reproving betimes with sharpness... and then showing forth afterwards an increase of love" — except Pahoran supplies the increase of love in response to a reproof he didn't even deserve.

## Notable

Yes — Pahoran's reply is one of the most Christlike responses in scripture: falsely accused and threatened, he answers "it mattereth not; I am not angry, but do rejoice in the greatness of your heart" (61:9) — blessing the heart behind the harsh words instead of defending his own innocence. The chapter vindicates Helaman's assume-the-best instinct (61:3-8), shows a flawed-premise letter still carrying a true word that helped (61:19-20), and subordinates even liberty to obedience to God (61:12-13). The model of conflict that deepens friendship rather than fracturing it (D&C 121:43 lived out).
