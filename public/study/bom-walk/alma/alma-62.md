# Alma 62 — the same affliction hardens some and softens others

**Binding idea:** The long war ends. Moroni and Pahoran retake Zarahemla and Nephihah (the latter "without the loss of one soul," 62:26), the king-men receive "their trial, according to the law" and the unrepentant are executed (62:9-11), the Lamanite prisoners join the people of Ammon to "become a free people" (62:27), and Teancum dies in a final solo raid that kills Ammoron (62:36-37). And then Mormon names the chapter's deepest truth: "because of the exceedingly great length of the war... many had become hardened... and many were softened because of their afflictions, insomuch that they did humble themselves before God" (62:41). The *same* war hardened some and softened others. Suffering is not a uniform force; it's a catalyst that intensifies whichever direction the heart was already facing. And for once the pride cycle breaks — the people prosper again, "but notwithstanding their riches... they were not lifted up in the pride of their eyes; neither were they slow to remember the Lord" (62:49).

**Read:** Alma 62 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Closes the long war narrative; Moroni retires, Helaman dies.

## Thoughts

The single most important verse in the chapter, and one of the most important in the whole war record, is verse 41: "because of the exceedingly great length of the war between the Nephites and the Lamanites many had become hardened, because of the exceedingly great length of the war; and many were softened because of their afflictions, insomuch that they did humble themselves before God, even in the depth of humility." The *same* affliction — the identical war, the same years of bloodshed and famine and loss — produced opposite results in different hearts. Some it hardened; some it softened. This is a doctrine I need to keep in front of me, because it corrects the lazy assumption that suffering is automatically refining or automatically embittering. It's neither. Affliction is a catalyst, not a cause — it intensifies and reveals whatever direction the heart was already turned. The furnace that hardens clay melts wax; the same sun that ripens the wheat scorches the shallow-rooted seed (Matt 13:6); the same gospel is "a savor of life unto life" to some and "death unto death" to others (2 Cor 2:16); the same rain makes one field bear fruit and another bring forth thorns (Heb 6:7-8). What determines which way you go under affliction isn't the affliction; it's what you do with it — whether you turn toward God or away. Pharaoh saw the same plagues that should have humbled him and hardened with each one. The Nephites lived the same war, and it split them. So when I'm in the furnace, the question is never "is this making me better?" — the furnace does nothing on its own. The question is "which way am I turning in it?" Suffering reveals and amplifies the heart's existing direction; it does not set that direction for me. That's both sobering (the trial won't refine me automatically) and empowering (I'm not at the mercy of my circumstances; I choose, under them, which way to bend).

The pride cycle, which has been the engine of Nephite collapse for the whole book, breaks here — and the chapter tells you exactly why. The people "prosper again... and began to grow exceedingly rich" (v48), which is precisely the setup that bred pride within a single chapter back in Alma 45:24. But this time: "notwithstanding their riches, or their strength, or their prosperity, they were not lifted up in the pride of their eyes; neither were they slow to remember the Lord their God; but they did humble themselves exceedingly before him" (v49). And the reason, in v50: "they did remember how great things the Lord had done for them, that he had delivered them from death, and from bonds, and from prisons." Remembering is the antidote to the pride cycle. The long affliction had taught the softened to remember — and a people who remembers their deliverance can hold prosperity without being corrupted by it. This is the lesson of Deuteronomy 8 ("beware that thou forget not the LORD thy God... lest when thou hast eaten and art full... thine heart be lifted up"). Prosperity isn't the problem; forgetting is. The Nephites who were softened by the war came out of it with the one thing that makes wealth safe — a memory of who delivered them. It won't last forever (4 Nephi and Helaman will show the relapse), but for this generation, scarred and softened, the cycle is broken: rich and humble at once, because they remember.

The chapter closes the great captain's arc with quiet dignity. Teancum, the fierce friend of liberty who killed both Amalickiah and Ammoron, dies in his last raid (vv36-37) — and there's a sober note in it: he went "in his anger," alone, to kill Ammoron, and though he succeeded, the king woke his servants before dying and they slew Teancum. The man of fierce anger died on anger's errand. Mormon honors him without reservation — "a man who had fought valiantly for his country, yea, a true friend to liberty" — and the honor is deserved. But the careful reader notices the pattern: Teancum's righteous fury, which made him heroic, also drove him to the reckless solo raid that cost his life. It's a gentle reminder that even righteous anger, unbridled, carries a cost (the bridle-your-passions principle from Alma 38:12 again). And Moroni — the man of whom "if all men were like unto Moroni the powers of hell would be shaken" — simply retires. He "yielded up the command of his armies into the hands of his son... and he retired to his own house that he might spend the remainder of his days in peace" (v43). No triumph, no monument, just a quiet handoff to the next generation and a peaceful retirement. The greatest captain in the record exits not in glory but in rest — which fits the man who "did not delight in bloodshed" and only ever wanted to save his people. He got his wish: peace, and a son to carry it on.

And the intercessory note in verse 40 is worth marking: "for the righteous' sake, yea, because of the prayers of the righteous, they were spared." The whole people — including the hardened and the wicked — were spared because of the prayers of the faithful few among them. This is the salt-of-the-earth principle (Matt 5:13), Abraham bargaining for Sodom (Gen 18), Alma spared "because of the prayers of his father" (Mosiah 27:14). The righteous don't just save themselves; their prayers hold up the roof over everyone. It's a humbling thought in both directions — that I might be spared because of someone else's faithfulness, and that my own faithfulness might be holding up more than I know. The war ends, Helaman returns to rebuild the church (vv44-46), the people repent and are baptized, and the book of Alma's long, bloody middle resolves into peace — a peace bought by armor and stratagem and blood, yes, but held by a people who, this time, remembered.

## Notable

Yes — closes the long war (43-62) and carries one of the war record's deepest doctrines: **the same affliction hardens some and softens others (62:41)** — suffering as catalyst, not cause, intensifying the heart's existing direction. The pride cycle broken for a generation because they *remembered* their deliverance (62:48-50, the antidote to prosperity-pride). The prayers of the righteous sparing the many (62:40). And the dignified exits — Teancum dying on anger's errand, Moroni retiring in peace, Helaman dying having rebuilt the church.
