# Alma 4 — When the Church Out-Prides the World: Alma Trades the Gavel for Pure Testimony

*(Thousands baptized, then pride enters the church and stops its growth; Alma gives up the judgment-seat to Nephihah to devote himself wholly to the word. ~86–83 BC. Mini-study — sets up the great sermon of Alma 5.)*

**Binding idea:** The most powerful man in the nation — chief judge, governor, and high priest — concludes that he cannot fix his people by governing them, and steps down to *preach*. What did he see that drove the decision, and what is the one remedy he names for a proud church?

**Read:** After the war, no contentions (v1), but the people are "greatly afflicted for the loss of their brethren" (v2); "every soul had cause to mourn; and they believed that it was the judgments of God... therefore they were **awakened to a remembrance of their duty**" (v3). The church grows — 3,500 baptized in the seventh year (vv4-5), continual peace. But in the eighth year "the people of the church **began to wax proud, because of their exceeding riches**... and they began to wear **very costly apparel**" (v6) — a grief to Alma and the teachers (v7). They "set their hearts upon riches and upon the vain things of the world... began to be scornful, one towards another, and... to **persecute those that did not believe according to their own will**" (v8); contentions, envyings, strife, malice, "**pride, even to exceed the pride of those who did not belong to the church**" (v9). "The wickedness of the church was a **great stumbling-block** to those who did not belong... and thus the church **began to fail in its progress**" (v10). Alma saw "the **example** of the church... lead those who were unbelievers on from one piece of iniquity to another" (v11), "**great inequality**... some lifting themselves up with their pride, despising others, **turning their backs upon the needy and the naked** and those who were hungry" (v12) — while others were "succoring those who stood in need... imparting their substance to the poor... thus **retaining a remission of their sins**" (vv13-14). Alma "began to be very sorrowful; nevertheless the Spirit of the Lord did not fail him" (v15); he gave Nephihah power "according to the voice of the people" as chief judge (vv16-17), retaining the high priesthood himself (v18), "that he himself might go forth among his people... to **stir them up in remembrance of their duty**, and that he might **pull down... all the pride and craftiness and all the contentions**... **seeing no way that he might reclaim them save it were in bearing down in pure testimony against them**" (v19); he "**confined himself wholly to the high priesthood**... to the testimony of the word" (v20).

**Thoughts:**
- *The pride cycle, and the church out-priding the world (vv6-10).* The recurring Book of Mormon cycle turns again, and fast: affliction humbles the people (v3, "awakened to a remembrance"), peace brings prosperity, prosperity breeds pride — all within a few years. But Alma 4 adds the sharpest indictment in the cycle: the church's pride grew "**even to exceed the pride of those who did not belong to the church**" (v9). The covenant people out-prided the world. And the consequence is the one that should sober every believing community: "the wickedness of the church was a **great stumbling-block** to those who did not belong... and thus the church began to fail in its progress" (v10). The church's hypocrisy did not merely damage the church; it *stopped the conversion of everyone watching*. The world looks at a proud, scornful, costly-appareled "saint" and is repelled from the gospel itself. Nothing halts the church's growth like the church's own pride — the example of the members preaches louder than the sermons of the priests, and a proud example preaches against the very thing it claims.
- *The split community: closed hands and open hands (vv12-14).* The pride takes one concrete, diagnostic form — "**turning their backs upon the needy and the naked** and those who were hungry... and those who were sick" (v12). This is the exact inverse of everything the record has built (Benjamin's "impart to the poor," Mosiah 4:26; Alma 1:27, "liberal to all"). The proof of the proud heart is the closed hand. And against it stands the faithful remnant, "imparting their substance to the poor... **thus retaining a remission of their sins**" (v14) — Benjamin's exact phrase (Mosiah 4:12, 26): caring for the poor is *how* the remission is retained. So the same church holds two peoples now, distinguished not by doctrine but by what their hands do — the proud who close them and the humble who keep them open. The dividing line within the covenant people is generosity.
- *Alma trades political power for pure testimony — the great pivot (vv15-20).* This is the chapter's pivot and one of the most instructive leadership decisions in the record. Alma is the *chief judge and governor* — he holds the highest civil power in the nation, the power to "enact laws... and to put them in force." And he concludes it is *not enough* — indeed not the right tool — for the disease he faces. So he hands the judgment-seat to Nephihah and "confined himself wholly to the high priesthood... to the testimony of the word." The reason is stated precisely: "**seeing no way that he might reclaim them save it were in bearing down in pure testimony against them**" (v19). Pride and contention cannot be *legislated* away; the law can punish a thief but cannot humble a heart. The only thing that reaches the prideful heart is "**pure testimony**" — the word of God borne with conviction and without guile. So the most powerful man in the nation gives up the more powerful *worldly* office to do the more *necessary* spiritual work, because he has diagnosed the sickness correctly: it is spiritual, and only a spiritual remedy will cure it. (This is a profound ordering of priorities — the word does what the law cannot, and the man who can see that lays down the gavel for the pulpit.)
- *"Pure testimony" — the remedy for pride (v19).* The phrase is the chapter's gift and the hinge of all of Alma's later ministry. Not argument, not coercion, not legislation — "**pure testimony**." The word of God borne purely (for the hearer's good, not the speaker's gain — the opposite of Nehor's priestcraft) is the one force that can "pull down all the pride and craftiness." This decision sets up everything that follows: Alma 5 (the searching-questions sermon, the purest testimony in the Book of Mormon — "have ye experienced this mighty change in your hearts?"), Alma 7, 12-13, 32-34 — the masterwork sermons that are the fruit of this pivot. Alma's greatest contribution to the record comes *after* he gives up power to bear testimony. The word, not the office, was where his real authority lay.
- *Affliction → remembrance → pride → affliction (vv3, 6).* The sobering rhythm: the people were "awakened to a remembrance of their duty" by the war's affliction (v3), and within two years the remembrance had curdled into pride (v6). Affliction humbles; peace and prosperity puff up; pride brings the next affliction. The cycle is not broken by the system Mosiah built (the reign of judges is functioning, peace reigns) — because the cycle is a disease of the *heart*, which no government can cure. Which is exactly why Alma steps down to treat the heart with the word. The structure was never going to be enough; it never is.
