# Alma 60 — the furious letter: right doctrine, right heart, wrong judgment

**Binding idea:** Moroni writes Pahoran "by the way of condemnation" (60:2), accusing the government of "thoughtless stupor" (60:7) while soldiers die for want of the help that was never sent. The letter is fierce, theologically grounded, and sincere — and built on an assumption that proves false. Its doctrine is profound: the deaths of the righteous are not proof of their wickedness — "the Lord suffereth the righteous to be slain that his justice and judgment may come upon the wicked; therefore ye need not suppose that the righteous are lost because they are slain; but behold, they do enter into the rest of the Lord" (60:13). Its principle is true: "the inward vessel shall be cleansed first, and then shall the outer vessel be cleansed also" (60:23). And Moroni's heart is pure: "I seek not for power, but to pull it down... but for the glory of my God, and the freedom and welfare of my country" (60:36). But his *judgment* of Pahoran — "we know not but what ye are also traitors" (60:18), threatening to march on Zarahemla — is badly mistaken, as chapter 61 will reveal.

**Read:** Alma 60 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Moroni's epistle to Pahoran; the reply comes in ch 61.

## Thoughts

The doctrinal heart of the letter is verses 12-13, and it's one of the most important answers in scripture to "why do the righteous suffer and die?" Moroni anticipates the lazy theology that would read the Nephite dead as deserving their fate: "Do ye suppose that, because so many of your brethren have been killed it is because of their wickedness? I say unto you, if ye have supposed this ye have supposed in vain." Then the doctrine: "the Lord suffereth the righteous to be slain that his justice and judgment may come upon the wicked; therefore ye need not suppose that the righteous are lost because they are slain; but behold, they do enter into the rest of the Lord their God." Two truths sit here, and both matter enormously. First, suffering and death are *not* a reliable index of the sufferer's sin — the same correction Jesus gave about the Galileans Pilate slaughtered and the eighteen the tower of Siloam crushed ("suppose ye that they were sinners above all? I tell you, Nay," Luke 13:1-5), and the whole point of Job. The faithful die too, and their death is not God's verdict against them. Second — and this is the sharp edge Moroni aims at Pahoran — when the righteous die through others' *neglect*, the deaths become a witness against the negligent, not against the dead: "it is to your condemnation." The blood of the slain "shall come upon your heads for vengeance; yea, for known unto God were all their cries" (v10). The dead are at rest; the guilt is on the living who failed them. That's a doctrine that cuts both ways with great mercy and great severity — mercy toward the suffering righteous (you are not lost, you enter God's rest), severity toward those whose inaction let them die.

The principle in verse 23 is the other enduring piece: "the inward vessel shall be cleansed first, and then shall the outer vessel be cleansed also" (Matt 23:26, the cup clean inside before out). Moroni applies it politically — the corruption "which first commenced at our head" (v15), the king-men's bid for power, crippled the defense from within, and no external war can be won while the internal vessel is foul. The order is fixed: inside before outside, the heart of the government before the borders of the country. It's a true principle of every level — a nation, a church, a soul. You cannot win the outward battle while the inward vessel is corrupt; the rot at the center defeats you before the enemy does. (And the king-men analysis is exactly right — Moroni correctly diagnoses that the *internal* division, not Lamanite strength, is the real cause of the losses, vv15-16.)

But here is the thing the chapter forces me to hold honestly: Moroni is *right about the doctrine, right about the king-men, right about his own motives, and wrong about Pahoran*. The letter assumes Pahoran is sitting "in a state of thoughtless stupor" (v7), in "idleness" (v22), possibly "seeking for authority," possibly "traitors to your country" (v18). Moroni even invokes revelation: "the Lord saith unto me: If those whom ye have appointed your governors do not repent... ye shall go up to battle against them" (v33), and threatens to "stir up insurrections" and "smite you with the sword" (vv27, 30). And chapter 61 will reveal that every one of those accusatory assumptions about Pahoran's heart is *false* — Pahoran wasn't idle or treasonous; he had been violently driven from the judgment seat by king-men rebels and was fighting for his life, unable to send what he didn't control. So we have a genuinely great man — fierce in a righteous cause, doctrinally sound, sincerely God-fearing, even claiming divine direction for the general principle — who nonetheless judges another person's heart wrongly because he answered a matter before he heard it (Prov 18:13). The text doesn't condemn Moroni; his zeal flows from love of liberty and grief over the dead, and his demand that the government act *was* warranted. But it lets us watch how even righteous anger, built on an assumption of the worst about someone you can't see, lands on the wrong target. The lesson isn't "Moroni was bad" — it's that being right about the doctrine and pure in motive does not guarantee you're right about the person. Zeal can be holy and still misjudge a man.

And verse 36 is the line that keeps Moroni admirable even in his error, because it reveals what his fierceness is *for*: "I am Moroni, your chief captain. I seek not for power, but to pull it down. I seek not for honor of the world, but for the glory of my God, and the freedom and welfare of my country." This is the inversion of D&C 121:39 ("almost all men, as soon as they get a little authority... will immediately begin to exercise unrighteous dominion"). Moroni is the rare man who, holding the largest army in the land, threatens force *only to destroy power-seeking, not to seize power*. His threat to march on the government is aimed at uprooting tyranny, not establishing his own. That's what makes him the man of whom "if all men were like unto Moroni the powers of hell would be shaken" (48:17) — even when his judgment of Pahoran is wrong, his heart is incorruptible. He fears God, not power; he wants liberty, not a throne. The chapter, then, is a remarkable portrait: a man great enough to threaten insurrection against his own government for the sake of freedom, pure enough to want nothing for himself, doctrinally sound, fiercely loving — and still capable of badly misjudging an innocent man. The greatness and the error are both real, and the next chapter's grace is what redeems the error without erasing the greatness.

## Notable

Yes — one of the great doctrinal chapters on suffering: the righteous slain are not lost and their death is no proof of their wickedness (60:12-13, the Luke 13 correction), and when they die through neglect the guilt falls on the negligent, not the dead. "Cleanse the inward vessel first" (60:23). And the searching human study: Moroni's furious letter is right on doctrine, right on the king-men, and pure in motive ("I seek not for power, but to pull it down," 60:36) — yet badly wrong in judging Pahoran (60:18), the cautionary half of a sequence the next chapter redeems.
