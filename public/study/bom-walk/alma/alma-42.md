# Alma 42 — justice and mercy: how God forgives without ceasing to be just

**Binding idea:** Corianton's last and deepest objection: it seems "injustice that the sinner should be consigned to a state of misery" (42:1) — if punishing sinners is unjust, his sin is harmless. Alma answers with the architecture of the whole plan. The Fall cut man off temporally and spiritually, but the expulsion was *mercy* — it created "a probationary time, a time to repent" rather than letting Adam "live forever, having no space for repentance" (42:4-5). Yet the fallen are "in the grasp of justice... which consigned them forever to be cut off" (42:14), and "the work of justice could not be destroyed; if so, God would cease to be God" (42:13). So "God himself atoneth for the sins of the world... to appease the demands of justice, that God might be a perfect, just God, and a merciful God also" (42:15). The resolution: "mercy claimeth the penitent, and mercy cometh because of the atonement" (42:23) — "do ye suppose that mercy can rob justice? I say unto you, Nay; not one whit" (42:25). And the pastoral close: stop denying God's justice to excuse yourself; "only let your sins trouble you, with that trouble which shall bring you down unto repentance" (42:29).

**Read:** Alma 42 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). The doctrinal climax of the Corianton block (39-42); builds on Amulek's seed at Alma 34:15-16.

## Thoughts

Corianton's objection is more sophisticated than his brothers' issues, and it's the kind of objection that always hides a self-interest. He "tries to suppose that it is injustice that the sinner should be consigned to a state of misery" (v1). On its face it sounds like tenderheartedness — surely a good God wouldn't punish. But Alma sees what it's doing: it's the doctrinal scaffolding under the sin. If God is too just (or too kind) to punish, then there's no real consequence to fear, and the lust with Isabel costs nothing. So Alma has to show that God's justice isn't a flaw to be explained away but a perfection without which "God would cease to be God" (a phrase he uses three times in this chapter, vv13, 22, 25). The objection that punishment is unjust gets turned completely around: it's the *removal* of justice that would unmake God.

The treatment of the Fall (vv2-7) is quietly stunning, because it reads the expulsion from Eden not as mere punishment but as mercy in disguise. The cherubim and flaming sword guarded the tree of life specifically so Adam couldn't "put forth his hand, and take also of the tree of life, and eat and live forever" in his fallen state (v3). Why would living forever be bad? Because it would mean "having no space for repentance" (v5) — frozen permanently in the fallen condition, the plan of salvation "frustrated." So the cutting-off, the very thing that looks like God's harshest act, was the gift that created the probationary space, "a time granted unto man to repent" (v4). Death itself, here, is mercy: it's what keeps the fall from being permanent and gives the soul a bounded mortality in which to turn. This reframes the deepest complaint — "why is there death and fallenness at all?" — as the answer rather than the problem. The exile was the opening of the door, not its slamming.

The logical heart (vv16-22) is the law-punishment-repentance chain, and it's tight. "How could a man repent except he should sin? How could he sin if there was no law? How could there be a law save there was a punishment?" (v17). Repentance presupposes sin; sin presupposes law; law presupposes a punishment affixed. Pull out the punishment and the whole structure collapses — "if there was no law given, if men sinned what could justice do, or mercy either, for they would have no claim upon the creature?" (v21). This is the answer to Corianton's "punishment is unjust": without the punishment there is no law, without the law no sin, without sin no repentance, and without repentance no salvation. The very thing Corianton wants removed (the penalty) is what makes mercy *possible*. You can't have a merciful God in a universe with no justice to be merciful *about*. Mercy needs justice the way forgiveness needs a real debt — cancel the debt's reality and there's nothing left to forgive.

And then the resolution, the doctrine this whole arc was built toward (vv15, 22-25). The fallen are in "the grasp of justice," which by itself "consigned them forever to be cut off" — justice alone is a closed door. Mercy can't simply override it, because that would "destroy the work of justice." So the only way mercy enters without injustice is if the debt gets *paid*: "God himself atoneth for the sins of the world... to appease the demands of justice, that God might be a perfect, just God, and a merciful God also" (v15). God doesn't waive the penalty; he pays it himself. That's the genius of it — justice "exerciseth all his demands" (every demand met, in full, by the atonement) "and also mercy claimeth all which is her own" (the penitent, redeemed by that same payment). Both are completely satisfied; neither is cheated. Which is why "mercy claimeth the penitent, and mercy cometh because of the atonement" (v23) — mercy isn't free-floating divine leniency, it's the specific fruit of a price actually paid. And the famous line lands as the necessary conclusion: "do ye suppose that mercy can rob justice? I say unto you, Nay; not one whit. If so, God would cease to be God" (v25). A mercy that robbed justice would be a God who broke his own law to let his favorites off — exactly the corrupt judge the scriptures everywhere condemn. The cross is what makes God able to forgive *and* remain just. (Amulek planted this in 34:15-16 — "mercy can satisfy the demands of justice, and encircles them in the arms of safety" — and Alma here works out the full mechanism.)

The close is the most tender pastoral turn in the three charges, and it's exactly calibrated to Corianton's specific error. "I desire that ye should let these things trouble you no more, and only let your sins trouble you, with that trouble which shall bring you down unto repentance" (v29). There's a trouble that's destructive (the obsessive worry over justice and resurrection that Corianton was using to avoid the real issue) and a trouble that's redemptive (the godly sorrow over sin that drives toward repentance, 2 Cor 7:10). Alma redirects his son from the first to the second: stop troubling yourself with doctrinal objections, and let the right thing trouble you — your sins. Then v30, the precise diagnosis: "deny the justice of God no more. Do not endeavor to excuse yourself in the least point because of your sins, by denying the justice of God." That's the whole maneuver named — Corianton was attacking God's justice as a way of excusing himself, and Alma calls it exactly what it is. Instead: "let the justice of God, and his mercy, and his long-suffering have full sway in your heart; and let it bring you down to the dust in humility." Not crushed, but humbled — brought low enough to receive the mercy the atonement makes available. And then, beautifully, Alma sends his reclaimed son straight back to work: "go thy way, declare the word with truth and soberness, that thou mayest bring souls unto repentance" (v31). The same vocation Alma found after his own conversion (36:24) is the one he hands the son who strayed — repentance isn't the end of Corianton's ministry; it's the beginning of his real one.

## Notable

Yes — one of the supreme doctrinal chapters in the Book of Mormon: the reconciliation of justice and mercy through the atonement (42:15, 23-25, "mercy cannot rob justice"), the Fall/expulsion-as-mercy (42:2-7), the law-punishment-repentance chain (42:16-22), and the tender pastoral close redirecting Corianton from destructive worry to redemptive sorrow (42:29-31). Climax of the Corianton block; develops Amulek's Alma 34:15-16 seed.
