# Mosiah 15 — God Himself Comes Down: Father and Son, the Seed, and the Trap Closed

*(Abinadi expounds Isaiah 53 — the condescension, Christ as Father and Son, "who is his seed," the Mediator betwixt man and justice, and the resolution of the Isaiah-52 "beautiful feet." The doctrinal peak of the Zeniff record. ~148 BC. Mini-study — full synthesis in spin-off `mos13-16_abinadi-christology`.)*

**Binding idea:** Abinadi quoted Isaiah 53; now he tells the priests what it means. The center is the hardest and richest claim in the discourse: the God who comes down is "**the Father and the Son**." How can one person be both — and how does the whole exposition bend back to close the trap the priest set three chapters ago?

**Read:** "**God himself shall come down among the children of men, and shall redeem his people**" (v1). "Because he dwelleth in flesh he shall be called the **Son of God**, and having subjected the flesh to the will of the Father, being the **Father and the Son** — the Father, because he was conceived by the power of God; and the Son, because of the flesh; thus becoming the Father and Son" (vv2-3). "And they are **one God, yea, the very Eternal Father of heaven and of earth**" (v4). The flesh "becoming subject to the Spirit, or the Son to the Father... suffereth himself to be mocked, and scourged, and cast out, and disowned by his people" (v5); "as Isaiah said, as a sheep before the shearer is dumb, so he opened not his mouth" (v6); "the flesh becoming subject even unto death, **the will of the Son being swallowed up in the will of the Father**" (v7). "Thus God breaketh the bands of death... giving the Son power to make intercession... **standing betwixt them and justice**; having... taken upon himself their iniquity and their transgressions, having redeemed them, and **satisfied the demands of justice**" (vv8-9). "**Who shall declare his generation?** ... when his soul has been made an offering for sin he shall see his **seed**... And who shall be his seed?" (v10). "All those who have hearkened unto their words, and believed that the Lord would redeem his people... **these are his seed, or they are the heirs of the kingdom of God**" (v11); "these are they whose sins he has borne; these are they for whom he has died" (v12); "are not the prophets... they are his seed" (v13). "And these are they who have published peace... and said unto Zion: Thy God reigneth! And O **how beautiful upon the mountains were their feet!**" (vv14-15); "the feet of those that are still publishing peace" (v16), "those who shall hereafter publish peace... forever" (v17). "This is not all... how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that is the **founder of peace, yea, even the Lord**, who has redeemed his people" (v18). "Were it not for the redemption... **all mankind must have perished**" (v19). The resurrection: "the Son reigneth, and hath power over the dead" (v20); "a first resurrection" of the prophets and all who believed (vv21-23); and "those who have died... in their ignorance, not having salvation declared unto them" are restored (v24); "**little children also have eternal life**" (v25). "But behold, and fear, and tremble... for the Lord redeemeth none such that rebel against him and die in their sins... that have **wilfully rebelled**... and would not keep [the commandments]" (v26); "he cannot deny himself; for **he cannot deny justice when it has its claim**" (v27). Then he finishes the Isaiah 52 passage the priest began: "thy watchmen shall lift up their voice... they shall see eye to eye, when the Lord shall bring again Zion" (v29); "the Lord hath made bare his holy arm in the eyes of all the nations; and all the ends of the earth shall see the salvation of our God" (v31).

**Thoughts:**
- *Father and Son — the condescension at full concentration (vv1-5).* This is the Book of Mormon's densest statement of how the one Christ is both Father and Son, and it must be read carefully rather than flattened. Abinadi's claim begins with the condescension stated bare: "**God himself** shall come down" (v1) — not an emissary, but the Lord. Then the two titles, each with its ground: He is **the Son** "because of the flesh" — because he dwells in a body and subjects that flesh to the Father's will (vv2-3). He is **the Father** "because he was conceived by the power of God" — and, more broadly, "the very Eternal Father of heaven and of earth" (v4), the Creator. The standard Restoration reading holds these together: Christ is Father as the divine Creator and as the One conceived by God's power, and Son as the mortal who lived in flesh and submitted. And the *unity* is not metaphysical sleight but moral and volitional — "the flesh becoming subject to the Spirit, or the Son to the Father" (v5), culminating in "**the will of the Son being swallowed up in the will of the Father**" (v7). The oneness is a oneness of *will*: the human will of the man Jesus wholly surrendered to the divine will, so that there is, in the end, one God acting. (This is Gethsemane's "not my will, but thine," Luke 22:42, given doctrinal form — and it is the same surrender Benjamin's natural man makes in miniature, 3:19. The Atonement runs on a will swallowed up.)
- *The Mediator standing betwixt man and justice (vv8-9).* Abinadi expounds Isaiah's "intercession for the transgressors" (14:12) into a courtroom image: the redeemed Christ, "having the bowels of mercy... **standing betwixt them and justice**," having "taken upon himself their iniquity and their transgressions" and "**satisfied the demands of justice**." This is the mediation doctrine at its root — Christ does not abolish justice; he *satisfies* it, placing himself between the sinner and the claim against him. Mercy does not defeat justice; mercy *pays* justice. (Alma 42 will build the whole atonement-theory on this; here it is in one sentence.)
- *Who is his seed? — the Atonement makes a family (vv10-13).* Abinadi takes Isaiah's "he shall see his seed" (53:10) and presses the question on the priests: "**who shall be his seed?**" The answer reframes salvation as *posterity*: "all those who have hearkened... and believed that the Lord would redeem his people... these are his seed, or **they are the heirs of the kingdom of God**" — "these are they whose sins he has borne; these are they for whom he has died" (vv11-12). The suffering servant who pours out his soul unto death *gains a family* — every soul redeemed becomes his child. This is the new-birth thread of 5:7 ("spiritually begotten... his sons and his daughters") now anchored in the suffering-servant prophecy: to be born of Christ (5:7) is precisely to be the seed Isaiah said he would see (53:10). The death is fruitful; the offering produces heirs.
- *The trap closed (vv14-18, 29-31).* The priest in 12:21 quoted Isaiah 52:7 — "how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings" — to trap Abinadi: how can you preach doom if scripture promises good tidings of peace? Abinadi now springs the answer he deferred. The beautiful-feet bringers of good tidings are the **prophets** who published peace (past, v15), those "still publishing peace" (present, v16), those "who shall hereafter publish peace... forever" (future, v17) — and then, climactically, "this is not all": ultimately the beautiful feet are "the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, **that is the founder of peace, yea, even the Lord**" (v18). The good-tidings-bringer the priest cited is *Christ himself*. And the move quietly indicts the priests: the beautiful-feet ones publish peace and believe in the Redeemer — which the priests, who publish lies from gilded seats (11:11), do not. Then Abinadi *finishes the passage* the priest only started, reciting Isaiah 52:8-10 entire (vv29-31) — the watchmen, the eye-to-eye seeing, the redeemed Jerusalem, the bared arm. He does not merely escape the trap; he completes the scripture his accuser left half-quoted, owning the text on his own terms. The patient deferral of ch 12 pays off here: refuse the trap, build the doctrine, then close the loop.
- *Mercy wide, justice exact (vv19-27).* The reach of the redemption is sweeping: without it "all mankind must have perished" (v19); the first resurrection gathers the prophets, all who believed, those who "died... in their ignorance" before Christ (the unevangelized dead, v24), and "little children also have eternal life" (v25). But the door is not infinitely elastic. Those who "**wilfully rebelled**... that have known the commandments of God, and would not keep them" have no part in it (v26) — "he cannot deny justice when it has its claim" (v27). The same calibration as the angel's words to Benjamin (3:11-12): the ignorant are covered, the knowing rebel is not — not because God is stingy with mercy but because mercy cannot deny justice its claim without God denying himself. The willfulness is the line. (And it lands as a direct warning to these priests, who *know* the truth — 12:30 — and rebel anyway.)

**Notable / flag:** **Spin-off RUNNING** (`mos13-16_abinadi-christology`) — the full doctrinal synthesis of the Father-Son passage and the seed lands there. This is the **doctrinal peak of the Zeniff record**. Its center is **Christ as the Father and the Son** (vv2-5): Son by the flesh, Father by divine conception and as Creator, "one God" by the *will of the Son swallowed up in the will of the Father* (v7) — a oneness of will, Gethsemane given doctrinal form. The **Mediator stands betwixt man and justice** and *satisfies* it (vv8-9 — mercy pays justice, not defeats it). The **Atonement makes a family**: Isaiah's "he shall see his seed" (53:10) answered — all who believe, and the prophets, are "his seed... heirs of the kingdom" (vv10-13), grounding the 5:7 new-birth thread in the suffering servant. And the **Isaiah-52 trap is closed** (vv14-18): the beautiful-feet good-tidings-bringer is the prophets and ultimately "the Lord, the founder of peace" — and Abinadi finishes the very passage (52:8-10, vv29-31) the priest left half-quoted, owning the text. Closes with **mercy wide / justice exact**: the first resurrection reaches the ignorant dead and little children (vv24-25), but the *willful knowing rebel* is excluded because God "cannot deny justice when it has its claim" (vv26-27) — a direct warning to the priests who know the truth (12:30) and rebel.
