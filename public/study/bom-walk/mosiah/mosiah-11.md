# Mosiah 11 — King Noah, the Anti-Benjamin: Comfortable Seats for the Speaking of Lies

*(The wicked king — the tax, the priests, the palace, the wine; Abinadi's first prophecy and first escape. ~160–150 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Zeniff handed the kingdom to his son with a blessing. The son is the photographic negative of king Benjamin. What does the record build by setting Noah point-for-point against the good king we just left — and why does Abinadi's prophecy of bondage hinge on a single repeated word?

**Read:** "Noah began to reign... and he **did not walk in the ways of his father**" (v1) — "he did not keep the commandments of God, but he did walk after the desires of his own heart. And he had many wives and concubines" (v2). He laid "a tax of one fifth part of all they possessed" (v3) to "support himself, and his wives and his concubines; and also his priests" (v4), having "put down all the priests that had been consecrated by his father, and consecrated new ones... lifted up in the pride of their hearts" (v5). "Thus did **the people labor exceedingly to support iniquity**" (v6), becoming idolatrous because "**deceived by the vain and flattering words of the king and priests**" (v7). Noah built "elegant and spacious buildings" (v8), a palace and throne (v9), ornamented the temple, and made the high-priests' seats of "pure gold" with "**a breastwork... that they might rest their bodies and their arms upon while they should speak lying and vain words to his people**" (v11), and a high tower (vv12-13). "He placed his heart upon his riches, and he spent his time in **riotous living**" (v14); planted vineyards, "and therefore he became a **wine-bibber**, and also his people" (v15). Lamanite raids came (vv16-17); Noah's armies drove them back, "and... because of this great victory they were **lifted up in the pride of their hearts; they did boast in their own strength**, saying that their fifty could stand against thousands... and did **delight in blood**" (v19). "There was a man among them whose name was **Abinadi**... Wo be unto this people, for I have seen their abominations... and **except they repent** I will visit them in mine anger" (v20); "I will deliver them into the hands of their enemies... brought into **bondage**" (v21); "they shall know that I am the Lord their God, and am a **jealous God**" (v22); "none shall deliver them, except it be the Lord" (v23); "when they shall cry unto me I will be **slow to hear their cries**" (v24-25). "They were wroth with him, and sought to take away his life; but the Lord delivered him" (v26). Noah: "**Who is Abinadi, that I and my people should be judged of him? or who is the Lord**, that shall bring upon my people such great affliction?" (v27) — "I command you to bring Abinadi hither, that I may slay him, for he has said these things that he might **stir up my people to anger**" (v28). "The eyes of the people were blinded; therefore they **hardened their hearts**" (v29).

**Thoughts:**
- *Noah is the anti-Benjamin, point for point.* The record just showed us a righteous succession (Mosiah "did walk in the ways of the Lord," tilled his own earth, took no tax, 6:6-7); now it shows the inverse, and the parallel is deliberate. Benjamin "did **not walk in the ways of his father**" is the first thing said of Noah (v1) — and from there every term inverts. Benjamin took *no* tax and labored with his own hands so as not to burden the people (2:14); Noah taxes "**one fifth part of all**" so the people "**labor exceedingly to support iniquity**" (vv3, 6). Benjamin served his people; Noah's people serve his vice. Benjamin's word was true; Noah's priests sit on gold to speak lies. This is not two random kings — it is the Book of Mormon's matched pair of righteous and wicked monarchy, and the pair has a purpose: Noah is the case study that will *abolish kings*. When Mosiah later dismantles the monarchy (ch 29), his argument is precisely this — "if it were possible that you could have just men to be your kings" all would be well, "but... because all men are not just it is not expedient that ye should have a king." Noah is the "not just" king the whole later reform points back to. One bad king at the top corrupts a nation; that is the lesson the record is laying down here for cashing in eighteen chapters later.
- *Gold seats and a breastwork for the speaking of lies (v11).* The chapter's most precise image. Noah gilds the high-priests' seats "with pure gold" and builds them a breastwork "that they might **rest their bodies and their arms upon while they should speak lying and vain words to his people**." Comfort *engineered for the propagation of falsehood* — the staging of false religion made literal: elevated, gilded, restful chairs from which flattery is delivered in ease. The detail matters because the record will return to this exact set: Abinadi is dragged before these priests on these seats (ch 12), and one of them, a young priest named Alma, will rise from that gilded bench converted. The throne of lies becomes the birthplace of the church. The record stages it carefully.
- *Flattery, not force (v7).* The people "became idolatrous, because they were **deceived by the vain and flattering words** of the king and priests." Noah does not conquer his people's faith; he *flatters* it away. This is the Book of Mormon's standing diagnosis of how the wicked gain a following — the same engine Sherem, Nehor, Korihor, and Amalickiah will all run. Truth convicts and so is hated; flattery soothes and so is welcomed. A people are rarely marched into apostasy; they are eased into it by being told what they want to hear.
- *Pride's boast — becoming the enemy (v19).* After one victory the people "did boast in their own strength, saying that their fifty could stand against thousands." This is the precise error the record condemned *in the Lamanites* one chapter ago — they "depended upon their own strength" (10:11) and lost. Now Noah's Nephites adopt it. They have become the thing they fight: trusting the arm of flesh, delighting "in blood, and the shedding of the blood of their brethren." Wickedness erases the difference between a covenant people and its enemies; the boast is the proof that the line has dissolved.
- *Abinadi's prophecy hinges on "except they repent" (vv20-25).* The whole oracle is conditional, not fated. Five times the door is left open — "except they repent," "except they repent and turn," "except this people repent," "except they repent in sackcloth and ashes." Bondage is the *consequence they are choosing*, not a doom decreed. And the prophecy is exact: it predicts not only bondage but a God "**slow to hear their cries**" (v24) — the delayed deliverance that ch 21 will fulfill to the letter when Limhi's people cry and are not immediately answered. The embedded record's end-first structure pays off here: we have already seen the bondage (ch 7), so we read Abinadi's "except they repent" knowing they will not, and knowing every word will land. The prophecy is true because we have already met its fulfillment.
- *"Who is the Lord?" — Noah's Pharaoh moment (v27).* Confronted with "thus saith the Lord," Noah answers exactly as Pharaoh did: "or **who is the Lord**, that shall bring upon my people such great affliction?" (cf Ex 5:2, "Who is the Lord, that I should obey his voice?"). The wicked king who does not know God, set against the prophet sent in God's name — and, like Pharaoh, Noah will be broken by the God he dismissed. Then the classic inversion (v28): the prophet is reframed as the *troublemaker* — "he has said these things that he might **stir up my people to anger**." The one who names the sin is blamed for the division the sin caused (Ahab to Elijah: "art thou he that troubleth Israel?" 1 Kgs 18:17). It is always easier to kill the messenger than to hear the message.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off (Abinadi's doctrinal core is **13-15**, the flagged candidate). The structural key is **Noah as the anti-Benjamin** — the record pairs them point-for-point (no-tax vs one-fifth tax; own-hands labor vs people "laboring to support iniquity"; true word vs gold seats for lies), and the pair is loaded for later: **Noah is the unjust king whose example abolishes the monarchy in ch 29**. The chapter's sharpest image is the **gilded high-priest seats with a breastwork "that they might rest their bodies and their arms upon while they should speak lying and vain words"** (v11) — the very seats where the priest Alma will be converted next chapter. Plus **flattery as the engine of corruption** (v7), **pride's boast** that makes the Nephites the mirror of the enemy they fight (v19 ↔ 10:11), and **Abinadi's conditional prophecy** — "except they repent" five times; the door left open; the bondage chosen, not fated — with the exact detail (God "slow to hear," v24) that ch 21 fulfills. Noah's "**who is the Lord?**" (v27) is his Pharaoh moment.
