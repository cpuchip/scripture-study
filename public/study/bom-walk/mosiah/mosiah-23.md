# Mosiah 23 — Esteem Not One Flesh Above Another: The Righteous in Bondage

*(Alma's people build Helam; Alma refuses to be king; the Lord chastens the righteous with bondage; Amulon — Noah's wicked priest — is set to rule over them. ~145–121 BC. Mini-study. Header: an account of Alma's people, chs 23-24.)*

**Binding idea:** Alma's people did everything right — fled wickedness, built a holy community, refused a king, loved their neighbors — and were brought into bondage anyway. So this chapter holds two things that need each other: why a righteous man *refuses* a crown, and why God lets the righteous suffer. What is the difference between Limhi's bondage and this one?

**Read:** Warned of the Lord, Alma's people flee eight days into the wilderness to "a very beautiful and pleasant land, a land of pure water" (vv1-4), industrious (v5). The people want Alma for king (v6), "but he said... **it is not expedient that we should have a king; for thus saith the Lord: Ye shall not esteem one flesh above another, or one man shall not think himself above another**" (v7); "if it were possible that ye could always have just men to be your kings it would be well... But remember the iniquity of king Noah... and **I myself was caught in a snare**, and did many things which were abominable... which caused me sore repentance" (vv8-9); "the Lord did hear my cries... and has made me an instrument in his hands" (v10), "in this I do not glory, for I am unworthy to glory of myself" (v11). "Ye have been oppressed by king Noah... and were bound with the bands of iniquity" (v12); "as ye have been delivered by the power of God out of these bonds... I desire that ye should **stand fast in this liberty wherewith ye have been made free, and that ye trust no man to be a king over you**" (v13); "trust no one to be your teacher nor your minister, except he be a man of God" (v14); "**every man should love his neighbor as himself**" (v15). Alma is high priest, founder; "none received authority... except it were by him from God," and "none were consecrated except they were just men" (vv16-17). They prosper, calling the land Helam (vv19-20). "**Nevertheless the Lord seeth fit to chasten his people; yea, he trieth their patience and their faith**" (v21) — "whosoever putteth his trust in him the same shall be lifted up at the last day" (v22); "they were brought into bondage, and none could deliver them but the Lord" (v23). A Lamanite army appears; Alma exhorts, "**remember the Lord your God and he would deliver you**" (v27); they pray, the Lord softens the Lamanites' hearts, and "Alma and his brethren **went forth and delivered themselves up into their hands**" (vv28-29). The lost Lamanite army had found Noah's priests at Amulon (vv30-32); spared "because of their wives" (vv33-34), Amulon's group joined them, found Helam, promised Alma's people life and liberty for the way to Nephi, then "**would not keep their promise; but they set guards**" (vv36-37); and the Lamanite king made Amulon "a king and a ruler over" Alma's people (v39).

**Thoughts:**
- *Esteem not one flesh above another — why a good man refuses the crown (vv7-13).* The people offer Alma exactly what Noah seized: a throne. Alma refuses on a principle, "thus saith the Lord: **Ye shall not esteem one flesh above another**." Kingship by its nature elevates one above equals, and equality before God is the divine order; a crown institutionalizes the lie that one flesh outranks another. Alma concedes the ideal case — "if it were possible that ye could always have just men to be your kings it would be well" — but immediately names why the ideal is unsafe: "remember the iniquity of king Noah." You cannot guarantee a just king, and an unjust one with a crown is catastrophe. This is the exact argument Mosiah will use to abolish the monarchy entirely (ch 29) — Alma states the doctrine; Mosiah enacts the reform. And Alma's refusal carries a weight no theory could: "**I myself was caught in a snare**." He was one of Noah's priests; he watched power corrupt from the inside and was corrupted by it. He refuses the crown not as a man who fears power abstractly but as one who has felt it deform him. The most trustworthy refusal of power comes from someone who knows firsthand what it does.
- *Two liberties, one charge to guard them (vv12-15).* Alma names a *double* bondage they were freed from — "oppressed by king Noah" (political) and "bound with the bands of iniquity" (spiritual) — and a double liberty to keep: "stand fast in this **liberty** wherewith ye have been made free" (Gal 5:1's exact phrase). Freedom from sin and freedom from tyranny are, for Alma, two faces of one liberty, and both are guarded the same way — "trust no man to be a king over you," and "trust no one to be your teacher... except he be a man of God." A free people protects itself against both the tyrant and the false teacher; the safeguard against each is the same vigilance. And the positive core is equality lived: "every man should love his neighbor as himself, that there should be no contention" (v15) — the baptismal covenant of 18:8 made the constitution of the society.
- *The chastening of the righteous (vv21-24) — the chapter's hardest and most important move.* Alma's people are *good*. They fled the wickedness, built a holy city, refused a king, consecrated only just men, loved their neighbors. And the narrator says plainly: "**the Lord seeth fit to chasten his people; yea, he trieth their patience and their faith**," and "they were brought into bondage." This is the deliberate counterpoint to Limhi's people. Limhi's bondage was *consequence* — the prosperity covenant run backward, sin reaping its harvest (ch 7, 21). Alma's bondage is *trial* — refinement of the faithful, not punishment of the guilty. **The same affliction means opposite things depending on the heart it meets.** Bondage is not proof of sin; the righteous suffer too, and their suffering is God "trying their patience and their faith." This dismantles any flat reading of the prosperity covenant: obedience really does tend to prosper (Mosiah 2:22), *and* the obedient are also tried (Job's question answered in narrative). The promise is not "the faithful never suffer" but "whosoever putteth his trust in him the same shall be lifted up at the last day" (v22) — the lifting-up is sure, the timing is not. (And the deliverance, when it comes in ch 24, will be *miraculous* precisely because these people were already humble — they didn't need three failed rebellions to break them, ch 21.)
- *Surrender in faith, not rebellion (vv27-29).* Facing the Lamanite army, Alma's people do the opposite of Limhi's. Where Limhi's people rebelled three times by the sword and were beaten before they humbled themselves (21:6-14), Alma exhorts, "remember the Lord your God and he would deliver you," they pray for softened hearts, and they "delivered themselves up into their hands." The already-humble do not need to exhaust self-deliverance first; they surrender trusting God to deliver in His way and time. It looks like defeat — they hand themselves over to the enemy — but it is the posture that the miraculous deliverance of ch 24 will reward. Faith sometimes looks like laying down the sword and trusting the outcome to God.
- *The wicked set to rule the righteous (vv37-39).* The chapter's bitter irony, and the engine of ch 24's trial. **Amulon** — leader of Noah's wicked priests, the men who abandoned their own families and abducted Lamanite daughters — is made "a king and a ruler over" Alma, the righteous founder of the church. And the Lamanites break the very promise Alma trusted (life and liberty for showing the way) and enslave them anyway. Alma dealt honestly and was betrayed; the man who fled wickedness is placed under the rule of the wickedness he fled. This is the trial sharpened to its cruelest point: not merely bondage, but bondage *to the wicked you escaped*, by the betrayal of a promise you kept in good faith. (And it sets the stage for the deepest deliverance in Mosiah — ch 24, where God lightens the burdens these righteous people bear under Amulon.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. Two doctrinal pillars. First, **"esteem not one flesh above another"** (v7) — Alma refuses the crown on the principle of equality before God, conceding the just-king ideal but naming its fatal risk ("remember the iniquity of king Noah"); this is the doctrine **Mosiah will enact in ch 29** to abolish the monarchy. And Alma's refusal carries unique weight because **he refuses power knowing its corruption firsthand** — "I myself was caught in a snare" as one of Noah's priests (vv9-11). Second, and harder: **the Lord chastens the *righteous***. Alma's people did everything right and were "brought into bondage" as a **trial of patience and faith** (v21), not a punishment — the deliberate counterpoint to Limhi's bondage-as-consequence. *The same affliction means opposite things depending on the heart it meets; bondage is not proof of sin.* The promise is not that the faithful never suffer but that "whosoever putteth his trust in him the same shall be lifted up at the last day" (v22) — the lifting sure, the timing not. The chapter also gives **two bondages / two liberties** (Gal 5:1 — freed from Noah *and* from iniquity), **surrender in faith** (Alma's people deliver themselves up, vs Limhi's failed rebellions), and the bitter setup of ch 24: **Amulon, Noah's wicked priest, made king over righteous Alma** — bondage to the wickedness they fled, by a broken promise. Next: Mosiah 24 (Amulon persecutes Alma's people, forbids prayer on pain of death; God makes their burdens light — they cannot even feel them; the miraculous night deliverance — the *other* mode of the deliverance pair).
