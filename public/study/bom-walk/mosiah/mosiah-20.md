# Mosiah 20 — The Innocent Pay for the Guilty: Gideon Reads the Prophecy and Makes Peace

*(Noah's fugitive priests abduct twenty-four Lamanite daughters; the Lamanites blame Limhi's people and wage war; the captured Lamanite king is spared; Gideon solves the mystery and brokers an unarmed peace. ~145–123 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** A crime committed by a handful of fugitives nearly destroys an innocent people. The chapter turns on who bears the consequence of sin and who has the wisdom to read events rightly. What does Gideon see that prevents a slaughter?

**Read:** The Lamanite daughters gather at Shemlon "to sing, and to dance" (v1). Noah's priests, "**being ashamed to return to the city of Nephi**... durst not return to their wives and their children" (v3); hidden in the wilderness, they watch, and when few daughters are gathered they seize "**twenty and four of the daughters of the Lamanites**" and carry them off (vv4-5). The Lamanites, "found that their daughters had been missing... were angry with the people of Limhi, for they thought it was the people of Limhi" (v6), and sent armies (v7). Limhi, discovering them from the tower, ambushes; his people, "not half so numerous," drive the Lamanites because "they fought for their lives, and for their wives, and for their children; therefore... **like dragons did they fight**" (vv8-11). The wounded Lamanite king is found among the dead, brought to Limhi; the people say "let us slay him" (v13), but Limhi: "**Ye shall not slay him**... What cause have ye to come up to war against my people? Behold, my people have not broken the oath" (v14). The king: "**I have broken the oath because thy people did carry away the daughters of my people**" (v15). Limhi, who "had heard nothing concerning this matter," vows to search and execute the guilty (v16). Then **Gideon**, the king's captain: "I pray thee forbear... do ye not remember the **priests of thy father**... And are not they the ones who have stolen the daughters of the Lamanites?" (vv17-18). "For are not the words of **Abinadi fulfilled**, which he prophesied against us — and all this because we would not hearken unto the words of the Lord?" (v21); "it is better that we should be in bondage than that we should lose our lives" (v22). Limhi tells the king the truth (v23); the king, pacified, leads Limhi's people out "**without arms**," bows, and pleads for them (vv24-25); "when the Lamanites saw the people of Limhi, that they were without arms, they had **compassion** on them and were pacified... and returned with their king in peace" (v26).

**Thoughts:**
- *The priests' compounded crime (vv3-5).* The wickedness keeps deepening. Noah's priests had already abandoned their own wives and children in the flight (ch 19); now, "ashamed to return," they "durst not return to their wives and their children" at all — and instead **abduct twenty-four Lamanite daughters** to take as wives. They forsook their families, then stole others'. The sin escalates from cowardice to predation, and they commit it from hiding ("their secret places," v5), as is their pattern. Wickedness, unrepented, does not stay still; it compounds — abandonment becomes abduction. (The number twenty-four quietly echoes the twenty-four Jaredite plates of ch 8; the record keeps its motifs.)
- *The innocent bear the guilty's sin (vv6-16).* The chapter's moral center: Limhi's people are nearly destroyed for a crime they did not commit. The Lamanites "thought it was the people of Limhi" and waged war on the innocent, while the actual culprits — the fugitive priests — hid safely in the wilderness. This is sin's spillover: the guilty escape accountability and leave the innocent to pay the bill. And Limhi, genuinely ignorant ("had heard nothing concerning this matter"), responds with exact justice — not denial, not retaliation, but "I will search among my people and whosoever has done this thing shall perish." A just man doesn't dismiss an accusation against his people; he investigates it. (The pattern recurs across the record: the few wicked endanger the many, and the innocent suffer the consequence of others' sin — which is, in miniature, the human condition the Atonement answers.)
- *Gideon the wise counselor and prophecy-reader (vv17-22).* Gideon — who once drew his sword on Noah in righteous revolt (ch 19) — is now Limhi's captain and the chapter's voice of wisdom. He does three things a good counselor does. He **solves the mystery**: "are not they [the priests] the ones who have stolen the daughters?" — sparing an innocent search and a wrongful execution. He **de-escalates**: pacify the king with the truth rather than punish your own. And, most strikingly, he **reads the catastrophe theologically**: "are not the words of **Abinadi fulfilled**, which he prophesied against us — and all this because we would not hearken?" Gideon alone names the bondage not as bad luck but as the prophesied consequence of a people who would not turn. He sees the prophetic pattern *while inside* the events — the rarest kind of sight. (Gideon becomes a recurring figure of strength-with-wisdom; Alma 1:8, where Nehor kills him.)
- *"Better to be in bondage than lose our lives" (v22).* Gideon's counsel chooses survival over pride: keep the oath, accept the tribute, stop the bloodshed. It is not heroic defiance; it is the wisdom of knowing when to bend. The bondage is real and grievous (ch 7), but Gideon judges that living under tribute beats dying for honor — and that the path out (which ch 22 will provide) requires the people to be alive to take it. There is a time to fight "like dragons" (v11) and a time to "put a stop to the shedding of so much blood" (v22), and wisdom knows which is which.
- *The unarmed peace (vv24-26).* The resolution is quietly radical. Once the truth is told, the Lamanite king leads Limhi's people out to meet his own army — **unarmed** — bows himself before them, and pleads for them. And the Lamanites, "when they saw the people of Limhi, that they were **without arms**," are moved to "compassion." Disarmament plus truth plus a mediator's intercession ends a war that weapons had only inflamed. The vulnerability of the unarmed disarms the armed. This is a flash-forward to the Anti-Nephi-Lehies' radical pacifism (Alma 24), where the unarmed win precisely by laying down the sword — and a flash of the Lamanite capacity for compassion that the grievance-tradition would later be healed into. Even here, the enemy bows and pleads for his enemies.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The chapter's moral center is **the innocent bearing the guilty's sin** — Noah's fugitive priests abduct twenty-four Lamanite daughters (compounding their earlier abandonment of their *own* families into abduction of others'), and Limhi's innocent people are nearly destroyed for it while the actual culprits hide safely (vv3-16). The hero is **Gideon** — once the man who drew his sword on Noah, now the wise captain who solves the mystery, spares the innocent, and uniquely **reads the whole disaster as Abinadi's prophecy fulfilled** "because we would not hearken" (v21), counseling that "**better... bondage than that we should lose our lives**" (v22). And the resolution is the quietly radical **unarmed peace**: truth told, the Lamanite king leads Limhi's people out *without arms*, bows, and pleads for them, and "when the Lamanites saw... that they were without arms, they had compassion" (v26) — a flash-forward to the Anti-Nephi-Lehies (Alma 24) and a glimpse of the Lamanite compassion the grievance would heal into.
