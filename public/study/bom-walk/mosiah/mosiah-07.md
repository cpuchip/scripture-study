# Mosiah 7 — The Lost Colony Found: Bondage as the Covenant in Reverse

*(The narrative pivots — Ammon's expedition finds king Limhi's people in bondage; Limhi's temple sermon; the unnamed slain prophet [Abinadi]. The hinge into the whole Zeniff record. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Mosiah's reign in Zarahemla is peaceful — so why does the book suddenly leave it to go find a colony lost for a generation? Limhi's answer to his own people's suffering is the chapter's spine: their bondage is not God's failure but the covenant running backward. What does it mean that the same God who delivers also *withholds* deliverance — and how is the door back opened?

**Read:** After three years' peace, Mosiah sends sixteen strong men under Ammon (a descendant of Zarahemla) to learn the fate of those who "went up to dwell in the land of Lehi-Nephi" a generation before (vv1-3; cf Omni 1:27-30). They wander forty days, are captured, and brought before the king, who says: "I am Limhi, the son of Noah, who was the son of Zeniff" (v9). Learning Ammon's errand, Limhi is "exceedingly glad" — "my brethren who were in the land of Zarahemla are yet alive" (v14) — for "**we are in bondage to the Lamanites, and are taxed with a tax which is grievous to be borne**"; "**it is better that we be slaves to the Nephites than to pay tribute to the king of the Lamanites**" (v15). Limhi gathers his people to the temple (v17) and preaches: "lift up your heads and be comforted... the time is at hand... when we shall no longer be in subjection... yet I trust **there remaineth an effectual struggle to be made**" (v18). "Put your trust in God... the God of Abraham, and Isaac, and Jacob; and also, that God who brought the children of Israel out of the land of Egypt... through the Red Sea... and fed them with manna" (v19) — "that same God has brought our fathers out of the land of Jerusalem, and has kept and preserved his people even until now; and... **it is because of our iniquities and abominations that he has brought us into bondage**" (v20). He recounts Zeniff, "over-zealous to inherit the land of his fathers" (v21), deceived into a treaty, the people now paying "one half of all we have or possess... or our lives" (v22). "If this people had not fallen into transgression the Lord would not have suffered that this great evil should come upon them" (v25). "**A prophet of the Lord have they slain... a chosen man of God, who told them of their wickedness... and prophesied of... the coming of Christ**" (v26) — "because he said unto them that **Christ was the God, the Father of all things, and... should take upon him the image of man... and that God should come down among the children of men, and take upon him flesh and blood**" (v27), "they did put him to death" (v28). "The Lord hath said: **I will not succor my people in the day of their transgression; but I will hedge up their ways that they prosper not**" (v29); "if my people shall sow filthiness they shall reap the chaff thereof in the whirlwind" (v30), "the east wind, which bringeth immediate destruction" (v31). "**But if ye will turn to the Lord with full purpose of heart, and put your trust in him, and serve him with all diligence of mind... he will, according to his own will and pleasure, deliver you out of bondage**" (v33).

**Thoughts:**
- *The structural pivot — an embedded record, read end-first.* The narrative leaves Mosiah's peaceful Zarahemla and drops into a separate story whose ending we meet before its beginning. Chapters 7-8 are the *frame* (Ammon finds Limhi); chapters 9-22 are the *back-fill* (Zeniff's own first-person record, then Noah, then Limhi, with Abinadi spliced in at 11-17). This is Mormon's editorial architecture showing itself: he splices a colony's whole rise-and-ruin into the main line. And the effect is dramatic irony — we first see Limhi *in chains*, paying half of everything to stay alive, and only then will we be shown Zeniff's bright optimism setting out "over-zealous to inherit the land of his fathers" (v21). Every hopeful line of the coming Zeniff narrative will be read by a reader who already knows where it ends. The structure itself preaches: zeal uncoupled from wisdom runs straight into bondage.
- *Bondage as the covenant in reverse (vv20, 25, 29-32).* Limhi's theology of suffering is the prosperity covenant (2 Ne 1:20; Omni; "inasmuch as ye keep my commandments ye shall prosper") run backward. He does not say God abandoned them; he says "it is **because of** our iniquities... that he has brought us into bondage" (v20) and "**if** this people had not fallen into transgression the Lord would not have suffered" it (v25). The same mechanism that prospers the obedient afflicts the rebellious — and the Lord's word is stark: "**I will not succor my people in the day of their transgression; but I will hedge up their ways that they prosper not**" (v29). This is the hardest face of the covenant: God's deliverance can be *withheld*, and the withholding is itself the discipline. The sowing/reaping images make it agricultural and inexorable — sow filthiness, reap the whirlwind (Hosea 8:7; Gal 6:7). Sin is not punished by an arbitrary God; it ripens into its own harvest.
- *The exodus as the grammar of hope (v19).* Limhi grounds deliverance-hope not in optimism but in *precedent*: the God of Abraham, Isaac, Jacob — who brought Israel through the Red Sea and fed them manna, and "that same God" who "brought our fathers out of the land of Jerusalem." Bondage-and-deliverance is the master-pattern of God's dealings, and Limhi reads his people's plight through Israel's. If the door closed by sin, the same God who opened Egypt's door can open this one. The covenant runs backward *and* forward: into bondage by transgression, out of bondage by turning. Hope is memory of what God has already done.
- *The door back: turn + an effectual struggle (vv18, 33).* Deliverance is conditional and cooperative. Limhi does not promise rescue-by-waiting; he says "there remaineth an **effectual struggle** to be made" (v18) — they must act — and "if ye will **turn to the Lord with full purpose of heart**... he will, **according to his own will and pleasure**, deliver you" (v33). Three things in tension held together: it requires *their* turning (repentance is the condition), it requires *their* struggle (deliverance is not passive), and it remains on *God's* timetable ("his own will and pleasure"). This is the realistic shape of deliverance — not magic, not works-salvation, but turning + striving + waiting on God. (And it foreshadows ch 22: Limhi's people will be delivered, but by stratagem and effort, not miracle — the contrast with Alma's people, delivered miraculously in ch 24, is one of the book's deliberate teaching pairs.)
- *The unnamed prophet — Abinadi previewed (vv26-28).* Limhi reports, with grief, that "a prophet of the Lord have they slain... who prophesied of... the coming of Christ" — and gives the content that got him killed: "Christ was the God, the Father of all things... should take upon him the image of man... God should come down among the children of men, and take upon him flesh and blood." This is Abinadi's Christology (Mosiah 13-15) in summary — the *condescension* (God taking flesh) joined to the *image-of-God* doctrine (man made in God's image, so God will take man's image, v27). The reader is being set up: this is the man, and this is the doctrine, we will meet in person at ch 11-17. That a people would *kill* a prophet for preaching that God would come down and save them is the chapter's quiet horror — and the proximate cause (v28) of the wrath now visible in their bondage.
