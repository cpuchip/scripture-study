# Mosiah 27 — Born of God: The Persecutor Snatched From the Burning

*(Mosiah forbids persecution; Alma the younger and the sons of Mosiah seek to destroy the church; the angel; Alma struck dumb; the rebirth — "all mankind must be born again." ~100–92 BC. Mini-study. Fuller retelling: Alma 36.)*

**Binding idea:** The unbelieving rising generation (ch 26) now produces its most dangerous son — Alma's own. This is the Book of Mormon's Damascus road, and it turns on three things: a father's prayers, an angel's interruption, and a doctrine Alma will spend the rest of his life preaching — that *everyone* must be born again. Why does the chief persecutor become the chief witness?

**Read:** Mosiah proclaims against persecution, commanding "an **equality among all men**" (v3), "every man should esteem his neighbor as himself, laboring with their own hands" (v4), priests and teachers too (v5). Peace and prosperity return (vv6-7). But "the sons of Mosiah were numbered among the unbelievers; and also one of the sons of Alma... he being called Alma" — "a very wicked and an idolatrous man... a man of many words, and did speak much **flattery**... therefore he led many of the people to do after the manner of his iniquities" (v8), "**stealing away the hearts of the people**" (v9), going about secretly "to destroy the church" (v10). "As they were going about rebelling against God, behold, the **angel of the Lord appeared** unto them... in a cloud... with a voice of thunder, which caused the earth to shake" (v11); they fell (v12). "**Alma, arise and stand forth, for why persecutest thou the church of God?**... This is my church... and **nothing shall overthrow it, save it is the transgression of my people**" (v13). "The Lord hath heard... the prayers of his servant, **Alma, who is thy father; for he has prayed with much faith concerning thee** that thou mightest be brought to the knowledge of the truth" (v14). "Go, and **remember the captivity of thy fathers**... they were in bondage, and he has delivered them" (v16), "seek to destroy the church no more... even if thou wilt of thyself be cast off" (v16). Alma "became **dumb**... and he became weak, even that he could not move his hands" (v19); carried helpless to his father, who rejoiced (vv19-20). The priests fasted and prayed "two days and two nights" (v23); then Alma "stood up and began to speak": "I have repented of my sins, and have been redeemed of the Lord; behold **I am born of the Spirit**" (v24). "All mankind, yea, men and women, all nations... **must be born again**; yea, **born of God, changed from their carnal and fallen state, to a state of righteousness**... becoming his sons and daughters" (v25); "thus they become **new creatures**; and unless they do this, they can in nowise inherit the kingdom of God" (v26); "I was like to be cast off" (v27). "After **wading through much tribulation**, repenting nigh unto death, the Lord in mercy hath seen fit to **snatch me out of an everlasting burning**, and I am born of God" (v28). "My soul hath been redeemed from the gall of bitterness and bonds of iniquity. **I was in the darkest abyss; but now I behold the marvelous light of God.** My soul was racked with eternal torment; but I am snatched, and my soul is pained no more" (v29). "Every knee shall bow, and every tongue confess before him" (v31). Alma and the four sons of Mosiah — Ammon, Aaron, Omner, Himni — travel "zealously striving to repair all the injuries which they had done" (vv34-35); "**how blessed are they! For they did publish peace... and they did declare unto the people that the Lord reigneth**" (v37).

**Thoughts:**
- *The Book of Mormon's Damascus road (vv11-16).* The parallel to Paul is unmistakable and surely intended. The most violent persecutor of the church, in the very act of going about to destroy it, is stopped by a heavenly manifestation — a light/cloud, a voice, the earth shaking — and asked the same question Saul heard: "**why persecutest thou the church of God?**" (cf Acts 9:4, "why persecutest thou me?"). And like Paul, the chief enemy becomes the chief witness — the persecutor turned apostle-equivalent. The record is marking Alma the younger as its Paul: the conversion is total, sudden, undeserved, and produces a lifetime of consecrated service in exact reversal of the harm he did. God does not only reclaim the gently straying; He sometimes strikes down the man hardest set against Him and remakes him into His most effective servant.
- *A father's prayers answered (v14).* The hinge the angel names explicitly: "the Lord hath heard... the prayers of his servant, **Alma, who is thy father**; for he has **prayed with much faith** concerning thee." Alma the younger's conversion is the *answer to his father's persistent, faithful prayer*. The angel comes "that the prayers of his servants might be answered according to their faith." This is the great comfort to every parent praying for a wayward child: God hears, and faithful prayer for another soul is not wasted. But note the careful limit — the angel does not *override* Alma's agency; he gives him the chance to choose, warning that he may yet "of thyself be cast off" (v16). The father's prayer did not *make* the son believe; it brought down the intervention that set the choice squarely before him. Prayer for another opens the door; the other must still walk through it. (And he does — but the agency stays his.)
- *The church falls only by its own transgression (v13).* The angel's aside is doctrine: "This is my church... and **nothing shall overthrow it, save it is the transgression of my people**." The church cannot be destroyed from the outside — not by Alma the younger, not by the unbelievers, not by persecution. The only thing that can overthrow it is the *internal* sin of its own members. This reframes every external threat in the record: the persecutor is never the real danger; the danger is always the dissension and transgression within (ch 26's flattered members). A church that keeps its own covenants is invulnerable; one that doesn't needs no outside enemy.
- *Born again — universal and necessary (vv24-26).* This is Alma's foundational doctrine, the thing he will preach for the rest of his life, delivered the moment he can speak again. "**All mankind**, yea, men and women, all nations, kindreds, tongues and people, **must be born again**; yea, born of God, changed from their carnal and fallen state, to a state of righteousness... becoming his sons and daughters... **new creatures**; and unless they do this, they can in nowise inherit the kingdom of God." Two things are insisted on: the new birth is *universal* (not just for the notoriously wicked — *everyone*, every nation) and *necessary* (without it, "in nowise inherit," John 3:5). This is the new-birth thread of the whole book brought to its clearest statement: Benjamin's people "spiritually begotten" (5:7), Abinadi's "his seed" (15:10-13), Alma's own snatching-from-the-burning — all the same doctrine, now made the universal requirement. No one is born righteous; everyone must be *re*born. The respectable need it as much as the idolater; Alma simply learned it the violent way.
- *The before and after of redemption (vv28-29).* Alma's testimony of his own conversion is the Book of Mormon's most vivid experiential account of redemption, and it is built entirely on contrast: "snatch me out of an everlasting burning"; "redeemed from the gall of bitterness and bonds of iniquity"; "I was in the **darkest abyss; but now I behold the marvelous light of God**"; "My soul was **racked with eternal torment; but** I am snatched, and my soul is **pained no more**." Every clause is a *before/after* — abyss to light, torment to peace, burning to rescue. This is not theology about redemption; it is the felt reality of it, the difference a soul knows when it crosses from death to life. (Alma 36 will expand this into the fuller account — the three days, the memory of his sins, the turn to Christ — but the essential testimony is already here: I was in torment, and now I am not, because I was *snatched*.) And the agent is mercy and rescue, not Alma's own climb: he was *snatched*, redeemed, born — all passive, all done *to* him by a God answering a father's prayer.
- *The persecutors become the publishers of peace (vv32-37).* The conversion is verified by its fruit — the reversal is total. Those who "did go about secretly... to destroy the church" now travel openly "zealously striving to **repair all the injuries which they had done**, confessing all their sins." The wreckers become the rebuilders. And the chapter's closing line deliberately echoes Abinadi: "**how blessed are they! For they did publish peace... and they did declare... that the Lord reigneth**" — the exact "beautiful feet" language of Isaiah 52:7 that Abinadi expounded (15:14-18). The whole arc closes the loop: Abinadi named the publishers of peace, was burned, was preserved by one convert (Alma the elder), whose church survived the rising-generation rebellion, which produced these new converts — who *become* the beautiful-feet publishers Abinadi prophesied. And the four sons of Mosiah, named here (v34), are about to carry that published peace to the very people defined by the eternal-hatred grievance (ch 28, Alma 17-26). Redemption does not just save the persecutor; it conscripts him into the healing of the wound he once widened.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off (the fuller retelling is **Alma 36**, Alma's own first-person account with the three days and the memory of his sins — a strong spin-off candidate when reached). This is the **Book of Mormon's Damascus road**: the chief persecutor (Alma the younger, fruit of ch 26's unbelieving generation), stopped mid-attack by an angel and asked "**why persecutest thou the church of God?**" (Acts 9:4), struck dumb and helpless, and reborn — the chief enemy made the chief witness. The hinge is **a father's prayers answered** (v14 — Alma the elder "prayed with much faith concerning thee") *with agency preserved* (the son must still choose). The angel's aside is doctrine: **the church falls only by its own transgression** (v13), never the outside enemy. Alma's first words on rising are his life's message — **all mankind must be born again** (vv24-26): universal (every nation, not just the wicked) and necessary ("in nowise inherit" without it) — the new-birth thread (5:7, 15:10-13) at its clearest. His testimony is the **before/after of redemption** — "darkest abyss; but now... the marvelous light," "racked with eternal torment; but... pained no more," **snatched** from the burning (all passive — done *to* him). And the **persecutors become publishers of peace** (v37) — "how blessed... they did publish peace... declare the Lord reigneth" = Isaiah 52:7 / Abinadi's 15:14-18 *fulfilled* in these very converts, who (with the four named sons of Mosiah, v34) will carry that peace to the Lamanites of the eternal-hatred grievance. The Abinadi arc closes its loop here.
