# Mosiah 2 — "In the Service of Your God": Why You Can Never Get Ahead of His Giving

*(King Benjamin's temple sermon begins — service, indebtedness, the death of boasting. Mini-study. The sermon runs chs 2-5.)*

**Binding idea:** Benjamin, a king who labored with his own hands, teaches that you can never put God in your debt. Why not — and what does that impossibility do to both boasting and the way you serve your neighbor?

**Read:** The people gather at the temple, families in tents "with the door thereof towards the temple" (v6); Benjamin speaks from a tower, his words written for those who cannot hear (vv7-8). "I have not commanded you to come up hither that ye should fear me... But **I am like as yourselves, subject to all manner of infirmities**" (vv10-11). His reign: "I have not sought gold nor silver" (v12); "I, myself, have **labored with mine own hands** that I might serve you, and that ye should not be laden with taxes" (v14) — not to boast, but "that I can answer a clear conscience before God" (v15). "I do not desire to boast, for **I have only been in the service of God**" (v16). "When ye are in the service of your fellow beings ye are only in the service of your God" (v17). "If I, whom ye call your king, do labor to serve you, then ought not ye to labor to serve one another?" (v18). Then the indebtedness: even rendering "all the thanks... which your whole soul has power to possess" (v20), "**if ye should serve him with all your whole souls yet ye would be unprofitable servants**" (v21) — for God created you, lends you breath "from one moment to another" (v21), and "doth **immediately bless you**; and therefore he hath paid you. And ye are still indebted unto him... forever and ever; therefore, **of what have ye to boast?**" (v24). "Ye cannot say that ye are even as much as the dust of the earth" (v25); "I, even I, whom ye call your king, am no better than ye yourselves are; for I am also of the dust" (v26). He assembles them to "**rid my garments of your blood**" (v28), declares Mosiah king (v30), warns against contention and "the evil spirit" (vv32-33), and closes on the contrast: "**consider on the blessed and happy state of those that keep the commandments of God**... blessed in all things, both temporal and spiritual" (v41).

**Thoughts:**
- *You can never get ahead of God's giving (vv20-25).* Benjamin builds the logic carefully: God created you (you owe him your existence), he lends you the breath to serve him "from one moment to another" (so even the *capacity* to obey is borrowed), and the instant you obey, "he doth immediately bless you" (so he pays before you finish). The arithmetic never closes — every act of service is performed with borrowed breath and rewarded faster than it is rendered. Therefore "ye would be unprofitable servants" not because service is worthless but because the giving infinitely outpaces the repaying. You cannot put God in your debt; the ledger runs one direction forever.
- *Which kills boasting at the root (v24).* "Of what have ye to boast?" The indebtedness is so total there is no ground left to stand on and claim credit. This is the positive resolution of the whole instrument-thread of the walk — the axe that boasts against the hewer (2 Ne 20:15), the made-thing that denies its maker (27:27), the power that is "by his grace... that we may know" (Jacob 4:7). Benjamin states the cure plainly: you cannot boast because *everything you have, including the breath to do good with, is on loan.* "Ye cannot say that ye are even as much as the dust of the earth" — and even the dust "belongeth to him who created you."
- *So you serve God by serving each other (vv17-18).* Here is the practical hinge: since you can never repay God directly, you discharge the debt sideways — "when ye are in the service of your fellow beings ye are only in the service of your God" (Matt 25:40). The God you cannot get even with is served by the neighbor you can help. And Benjamin has modeled it: a king who "labored with mine own hands" so the people "should not be laden with taxes." The service flows down (king to people) and outward (people to one another) precisely because it cannot flow adequately up.
- *The servant-king (vv11-14, 26).* Benjamin refuses to be exalted: "I am like as yourselves, subject to all manner of infirmities"; "I... am no better than ye yourselves are; for I am also of the dust." The greatest king in the Nephite record introduces himself by leveling himself — the inverse of the pride that "wears stiff necks and high heads" (Jacob 2:13). Leadership, in Benjamin, is the office of one who serves and then insists he is no one's superior. (The model of stewardship: authority spent entirely on the welfare of the governed.)
- *The watchman, once more (vv27-28).* "Rid my garments of your blood." Benjamin, like Jacob (1:19), assembles the people so that, having taught them, he "might be found blameless" — the leader's accountability for diligence in teaching, whatever the people then choose. He cannot make them obey; he can make sure he taught them, and go to his grave "in peace."
