# Mosiah 22 — Out the Back Door: Deliverance by Stratagem, and the Records Come Home

*(Gideon's plan — the guards drunk on the tribute-wine — frees Limhi's people; they flee to Zarahemla and become Mosiah's subjects; both records reach Mosiah. The Zeniff record ends. ~121–120 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Limhi's people, humbled the hard way (ch 21), cannot fight free of a more numerous enemy. So how are they delivered — and why does the manner of their escape complete a deliberate contrast the record has been building?

**Read:** Ammon and Limhi "consult with the people how they should deliver themselves," gathering all "that they might have the **voice of the people**" (v1). The only way out is to take "their women and children, and their flocks... and depart into the wilderness," since they cannot "deliver themselves out of bondage by the sword" (v2). **Gideon** stands forth: "thou hast hitherto hearkened unto my words many times" (v3); "if thou hast not found me to be an **unprofitable servant**... I will be thy servant and deliver this people out of bondage" (v4). His plan: "the guards of the Lamanites, by night are **drunken**" (v6); "I will... pay the **last tribute of wine** to the Lamanites, and they will be drunken; and we will pass through the secret pass on the left of their camp when they are drunken and asleep" (v7). The king hearkens (v9); he "sent the tribute of wine... and he also sent **more wine, as a present**... and they did drink freely" (v10). "The people of king Limhi did **depart by night**... with their flocks and their herds... being **led by Ammon**" (v11), taking their gold, silver, and provisions (v12). "After being many days in the wilderness they arrived in the land of Zarahemla, and **joined Mosiah's people, and became his subjects**" (v13). "Mosiah received them with joy; and he also received their **records**, and also the records which had been found by the people of Limhi" (v14). The Lamanites pursued "two days," then "could no longer follow their tracks; therefore they were **lost in the wilderness**" (vv15-16).

**Thoughts:**
- *Deliverance by stratagem — the pair completed (vv2-11).* Limhi's people escape not by miracle but by *wit*: Gideon's plan to get the guards drunk and slip out the back pass with the flocks by night. This completes the contrast the record set up in chapter 21. Limhi's people, who had to be humbled "even to the dust" through three failed rebellions before they would cry to God, are delivered through their own ingenuity and effort — God grants the success, but the means are human cleverness and a long hard march. Alma's people, who were *already* humble, will be delivered by a straight miracle, their burdens made supernaturally light and the Lamanites' eyes closed in sleep (24:19-20). Two captive peoples, two modes of deliverance, each fitted to the heart it answers. The lesson is not that one deliverance is better — both peoples go free — but that God meets a people where they are: the proud-then-humbled work for their freedom; the already-humble receive it. (And it answers Gideon's own earlier word: there "remaineth an effectual struggle to be made," 7:18 — deliverance required their work.)
- *The bondage's own instrument becomes the escape (vv7, 10).* The irony is precise and satisfying. The half-tribute of wine that was a symbol of their servitude becomes the very means of their liberation — they pay "the **last** tribute of wine," and extra "as a present," and the guards' drunkenness (the oppressor's own vice) opens the door. The mechanism of the bondage is turned against the bondage. The captives walk out through the gap that their oppressors' weakness creates. (Drunken guards are a recurring vulnerability the righteous exploit — Alma 55 will do the same; the oppressor's intemperance is the captive's opening.)
- *Gideon completes his arc (vv3-9).* The man first seen drawing his sword on Noah in righteous revolt (19) has become the kingdom's indispensable wisdom: peace-broker (20), prophecy-reader (20), and now deliverer (22). His appeal to the king deliberately echoes Benjamin — "if thou hast not found me to be an **unprofitable servant**" (cf 2:21, "ye are unprofitable servants") — but where Benjamin used the phrase to humble all before God, Gideon offers *profitable* service: "I will be thy servant and deliver this people." Strength yoked to wisdom and put at the service of the people; the opposite of Noah's strength spent on himself. (Gideon's faithfulness will cost him his life — Nehor kills him, Alma 1:8 — but here he is the saving hand.)
- *The records come home (v14).* The quiet narrative payoff of the whole embedded arc. Mosiah "received their records" — the Zeniff record we have been reading (chs 9-22) — "and also the records which had been found by the people of Limhi," the twenty-four Jaredite plates (ch 8). Both records now converge in the hands of the one man with the gift to translate them. Much of the long detour into Limhi's land was, in the providence of the narrative, about getting *these records to this seer* — setting up chapter 28, where Mosiah translates the Jaredite plates into what becomes the Book of Ether. Deliverance freed the people; it also delivered the records.
- *The Zeniff record ends.* Chapters 9-22 — the back-fill that opened with "I, Zeniff" — close here. The whole arc is complete: a good-but-over-zealous founder (9), a wicked son who burned a prophet (11-19), a martyr whose one convert founded the church (17-18), bondage as the prophesied consequence (21), and escape by humbled effort (22). Mormon's end-first structure has paid off entirely — we met Limhi in chains (ch 7) and have now watched the whole road into and out of those chains. From here the main narrative resumes: Alma's people (ch 23-24), then the great reunification in Zarahemla (ch 25).
- *The voice of the people (v1).* A small seed worth marking: even the escape decision is made by gathering the people "that they might have the **voice of the people** concerning the matter." Limhi consults rather than commands. This is the principle Mosiah will institutionalize in chapter 29 when he abolishes the monarchy and establishes judges chosen by the voice of the people — and it appears here, in embryo, in a good king who asks his people before he leads them. Righteous leadership consults; the contrast with Noah (who taxed and commanded) is once more deliberate.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The escape chapter completes the **deliverance pair**: Limhi's people, humbled the hard way (ch 21), free themselves by **stratagem** — Gideon's plan to get the guards drunk on the very **tribute-wine of their bondage** and slip out the back pass by night — while Alma's already-humble people will be freed by straight **miracle** (ch 24). Same God, manner fitted to the heart: the proud-then-humbled work for their freedom; the already-humble receive it. The irony is exact — *the instrument of their servitude becomes the instrument of escape* (vv7, 10). **Gideon completes his arc** (sword-on-Noah → peace-broker → prophecy-reader → deliverer), the faithful strong-and-wise "profitable servant." And the **records come home** (v14) — Mosiah receives both the Zeniff record and the 24 Jaredite plates, converging in the seer who will translate them (→ ch 28 → Ether). The **Zeniff record ends** here (9-22 complete), and the seed of the **voice of the people** (v1) appears, which Mosiah will institutionalize in ch 29. Next: Mosiah 23 (back to Alma's people in the wilderness — they establish a righteous community, refuse to be kings'-men, then fall under Lamanite + Amulonite bondage; Alma's "ye shall not esteem one flesh above another" and the trial of the faithful).
