# Mosiah 29 — Because All Men Are Not Just: The King Who Gave the Kingdom Away

*(Mosiah dissolves the monarchy and institutes the reign of judges chosen by the voice of the people; the political theology of the worst case; Alma the younger made chief judge + high priest; Alma the elder and Mosiah die. The book of Mosiah ends. ~92–91 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** No son will take the throne (ch 28), and Mosiah turns the crisis into the book's masterstroke. The whole Noah arc has been building one argument, and here Mosiah states it and acts on it: design a government for the *worst* ruler you might get, not the best. Why abolish kingship — and what replaces it?

**Read:** The people want Aaron for king, but he and all Mosiah's sons have declined (vv1-3). Mosiah sends a written word: the rightful heir declined; appointing another would risk contention and a later claim to the throne (vv4-9). "I will be your king the remainder of my days; nevertheless, **let us appoint judges, to judge this people according to our law**" (v11). "It is better that a man should be judged of God than of man, for the **judgments of God are always just, but the judgments of man are not always just**" (v12). "If it were possible that you could have **just men to be your kings**... even as my father Benjamin did... then it would be expedient that ye should always have kings" (v13). "But... **because all men are not just it is not expedient that ye should have a king**" (v16). "How much iniquity doth **one wicked king** cause to be committed, yea, and what great destruction! Yea, remember **king Noah**" (vv17-18). "Ye **cannot dethrone an iniquitous king save it be through much contention, and the shedding of much blood**" (v21) — he has friends, guards, armies, tears up righteous laws and enacts wicked ones (vv22-23). "Therefore, **choose you by the voice of this people, judges**" (v25). "It is **not common that the voice of the people desireth anything contrary to that which is right; but it is common for the lesser part of the people to desire that which is not right**; therefore... do your business by the voice of the people" (v26). "And **if the time comes that the voice of the people doth choose iniquity, then is the time that the judgments of God will come upon you**" (v27). Appeals run lower → higher judges, and higher judges are judged by lower judges "according to the voice of the people" (vv28-29). "Have no king; that if these people commit sins... they shall be **answered upon their own heads**" (v30); "the sins of many people have been **caused by the iniquities of their kings**" (v31). "I desire that this land be a **land of liberty, and every man may enjoy his rights and privileges alike**" (v32). The people are convinced (v37); "they **relinquished their desires for a king**... every man expressed a willingness to answer for his own sins" (v38), and "cast in their voices" (v39), "exceedingly rejoiced because of the **liberty**" (v39). They loved Mosiah, "for they did not look upon him as a **tyrant** who was seeking for gain... but he had established peace" (v40). "**Alma was appointed to be the first chief judge, he being also the high priest**" (v42); "thus commenced the **reign of the judges**" (v44). Alma the elder dies at 82 (v45); Mosiah dies at 63, "five hundred and nine years from the time Lehi left Jerusalem" (v46); "thus ended the reign of the kings over the people of Nephi" (v47).

**Thoughts:**
- *Design for the worst case — "because all men are not just" (vv12-16).* This is the book's culminating argument, and it is a sophisticated piece of political theology. Mosiah concedes the *ideal*: a just king who establishes God's laws "even as my father Benjamin did" would be the best government there is (v13) — concentrated righteous power does the most good. But he refuses to build on the ideal, because the ideal cannot be *guaranteed*: "because all men are not just it is not expedient that ye should have a king" (v16). You do not get to choose whether your king is a Benjamin or a Noah, and "how much iniquity doth one wicked king cause... what great destruction!" (v17). So the right design principle is not "what works best with a good ruler" but "what does the least damage with a bad one." A wicked king is catastrophic *and* nearly irremovable — "ye cannot dethrone an iniquitous king save it be through much contention, and the shedding of much blood" (v21). Therefore build a system that does not depend on the ruler's virtue and that can correct a bad ruler without civil war. The whole Zeniff-Noah-Limhi arc (chs 9-22) was the case study that earns this conclusion: Mosiah is not theorizing about tyranny; he is pointing at Noah and saying *never again, by design*. (This is the inverse of how power usually reasons — most rulers build for their own continuation; Mosiah builds against the possibility of his own office being abused.)
- *The voice of the people — and its sober caveat (vv25-27).* The replacement is government "by the voice of this people" — judges chosen and held accountable by common consent. And Mosiah grounds it in a realistic anthropology, neither cynical nor naive: "it is **not common** that the voice of the people desireth anything contrary to that which is right; **but it is common for the lesser part** to desire that which is not right." The majority, most of the time, tends toward the right; the corrupting danger is usually the *minority*. So entrust decisions to the whole rather than to one or a few. But the caveat is the hinge of the whole system and the most sobering line in it: "**if the time comes that the voice of the people doth choose iniquity, then is the time that the judgments of God will come upon you**" (v27). The voice of the people is only as safe as the people are righteous. When the *majority* itself goes corrupt, the very mechanism designed to protect them becomes the channel of their destruction — there is no structural safeguard against a people who collectively choose evil. (This is fulfilled, tragically, across Helaman and 3 Nephi, where the voice of the people *does* choose iniquity and the reign of judges collapses into the destruction Mosiah warned of. The reform is brilliant but not self-securing; it rests, finally, on virtue, not structure.)
- *Accountability moves from the king to the people (vv30-34).* The deepest shift is moral, not merely administrative. Under a king, "the sins of many people have been caused by the iniquities of their kings; therefore their iniquities are answered upon the heads of their kings" (v31) — one man's wickedness corrupts a nation *and* he bears the blame for the corruption he caused. Under judges, "if these people commit sins... they shall be answered upon their own heads" (v30), and "the burden should come upon all the people, that every man might bear his part" (v34). The reform *distributes accountability*: no longer can a people blame their corruption on a wicked king; each person answers for himself. This is the political form of the gospel's own principle — every soul accountable for its own sins (AoF 1:2) — and it dignifies the people by making them responsible. A free people is one that has accepted the burden of its own choices.
- *Checks and balances (vv28-29).* Mosiah builds in self-correction at every level: lower judges are accountable to higher judges, and higher judges are judged by a panel of lower judges "according to the voice of the people." No single judge stands above accountability; the system can correct corruption without bloodshed — the exact thing impossible under a king (v21). It is a strikingly modern design — judicial review, layered accountability, removal-without-revolution — born from the lived experience of a people who watched how hard it was to dethrone a Noah.
- *The king who gave the kingdom away is loved more (v40).* The chapter's quiet center. Mosiah voluntarily dissolves his own dynasty, surrenders his family's power, and ends the monarchy itself — and the people "did wax strong in love towards Mosiah... they did not look upon him as a tyrant who was seeking for gain... he had not exacted riches of them, neither had he delighted in the shedding of blood; but he had established peace." The servant-king pattern (Benjamin tilling his own field, 2:14) reaches its culmination here: a king whose final, greatest act of service is to *give the kingdom back to the people*. Power voluntarily surrendered for the people's good is the highest form of kingship — and it is the photographic negative of Noah, who grasped power for gain and blood. The man who let go of the crown is esteemed "beyond measure"; the man who clutched it was burned by his own people. (And it is the deepest commentary on power in the record: the worthiest holder of power is the one most willing to release it.)
- *The reign of judges begins; the book ends (vv41-47).* Alma the younger becomes "the first chief judge, he being also the high priest" — holding both the civil and the religious office, though the offices remain distinct (the careful separation of chs 25-26 now embodied in one trusted man at the founding moment). The reign of the kings ends; Alma the elder (founder of the church) and Mosiah (the last king) both die; 509 years have passed since Lehi left Jerusalem. The book of Mosiah closes on a hinge of history — the end of monarchy, the death of the founding generation, and the beginning of the experiment in self-government that the book of Alma will immediately put to the test (Nehor's priestcraft, Amlici's bid for kingship, the first challenges to the new order). Mosiah ends not with a settled peace but with a *system handed to a people* — and the open question of whether they will keep their voice righteous.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off — **this is the capstone of the book of Mosiah** and a sophisticated political theology earned by the whole Noah arc. The culminating argument: **design government for the worst case** — a just king is ideal *if* you could guarantee one (Benjamin), but "**because all men are not just it is not expedient that ye should have a king**" (v16); you cannot choose whether you get a Benjamin or a Noah, and a wicked king is catastrophic and nearly irremovable "save... the shedding of much blood" (v21). So Mosiah builds a system that does *not* depend on the ruler's virtue: the **voice of the people** (v25), with its sober caveat — it is only as safe as the people are righteous, for "**if the voice of the people doth choose iniquity, then... the judgments of God will come upon you**" (v27, fulfilled later in Helaman/3 Nephi). The reform **shifts accountability from the king to the people** ("answered upon their own heads," every man bearing his part, v30-34 — the political form of individual accountability) and builds in **checks and balances** (layered, removable judges, vv28-29). The chapter's quiet center: **the king who gave the kingdom away is loved more** (v40) — Mosiah dissolves his own dynasty for the people's good, the servant-king culminated, the negative of Noah. **The book of Mosiah ends** (vv45-47): the reign of judges begins under Alma the younger (chief judge + high priest), the founding generation dies, 509 years from Lehi — a system handed to a people, with the open question of whether they will keep their voice righteous. **Next: ALMA — the longest book (63 chapters), opening with the new order immediately tested (Nehor's priestcraft, ch 1; Amlici's bid for kingship, ch 2).** Spin-off candidates flagged ahead: **Alma 5** (the searching-questions sermon), **Alma 7** (Christ's empathic atonement, completing the Abinadi thread), **Alma 12-13** (the priesthood/redemption discourse), **Alma 32-34** (faith as a seed / the great prayer-and-atonement sermons), **Alma 36** (Alma the younger's fuller conversion account), **Alma 39-42** (the counsel to Corianton / justice + mercy), and the **Lamanite-conversion arc (17-26)** that fulfills the grievance-tradition cure.
