# Mosiah 13 — Untouchable Until the Message Is Out: The Law That Cannot Save

*(Abinadi shielded by divine power, face shining like Moses; the rest of the Decalogue; salvation does not come by the law alone — only the Atonement God himself will make. ~148 BC. Mini-study — Christological synthesis in spin-off `mos13-16_abinadi-christology`.)*

**Binding idea:** Noah orders Abinadi killed mid-sentence, and the order simply fails — no one can touch him. Why does God shield the prophet now and not later? And what is Abinadi's central claim about the law these priests swear saves them?

**Read:** Noah: "**Away with this fellow, and slay him; for... he is mad**" (v1). They reach for him; "**Touch me not, for God shall smite you**... for I have not delivered the message which the Lord sent me to deliver" (v3) — "because I have told you the truth ye are angry with me... because I have spoken the word of God ye have judged me that I am mad" (v4). "**His face shone with exceeding luster, even as Moses' did while in the mount of Sinai**" (v5); he "spake with power and authority from God" (v6). "Ye see that ye have not power to slay me, therefore I finish my message... it cuts you to your hearts because I tell you the truth" (v7). "**I finish my message; and then it matters not whither I go, if it so be that I am saved**" (v9). "**What you do with me, after this, shall be as a type and a shadow of things which are to come**" (v10). He reads "the remainder of the commandments... for I perceive that they are not written in your hearts" (v11) — the rest of the Decalogue (vv12-24). "Have ye taught this people that they should observe to do all these things? Nay; for if ye had, the Lord would not have caused me to come forth" (vv25-26). "Ye have said that salvation cometh by the law of Moses... it is expedient that ye should keep the law of Moses **as yet**; but... **the time shall come when it shall no more be expedient to keep the law of Moses**" (v27). "**Salvation doth not come by the law alone; and were it not for the atonement, which God himself shall make for the sins and iniquities of his people, that they must unavoidably perish, notwithstanding the law of Moses**" (v28). The law was given to a "stiffnecked people, quick to do iniquity, and **slow to remember the Lord**" (v29) — "a law of performances and of ordinances... **to keep them in remembrance of God**" (v30). "**All these things were types of things to come**" (v31). "Did they understand the law? Nay... for they **understood not that there could not any man be saved except it were through the redemption of God**" (v32). Did not Moses and "**all the prophets**" testify "that **God himself should come down among the children of men, and take upon him the form of man**" (vv33-34), "and that he, himself, should be oppressed and afflicted?" (v35).

**Thoughts:**
- *Untouchable until the message is out (vv3-9).* The shield has a precise boundary. "God will not suffer that I shall be destroyed **at this time**" — not because Abinadi is too valuable to lose, but because "I have not delivered the message." His protection is instrumental: God guards the *word's delivery*, not the messenger's life. Once the message is out, the shield lifts — "then it matters not whither I go, if it so be that I am saved" (v9). This is the Shadrach-Meshach-Abednego posture exactly: our God is able to deliver, "but if not," we will not bow (Dan 3:16-18). Abinadi is not promised survival; he is promised *completion*. The mission is immortal until finished; the man is then expendable, and he knows it and accepts it. (The shining face — "even as Moses'," Ex 34:29 — marks that this is the same Sinai authority speaking; the priests are resisting Moses' own God while claiming Moses' law.)
- *His martyrdom as a type (v10).* Abinadi consciously frames his coming death: "what you do with me... shall be as a **type and a shadow of things which are to come**." He is not merely resigned to dying; he reads his own martyrdom as part of a pattern. The footnote points forward to Alma (17:13-19) — the priest who will carry the message out and found a church. And more deeply, a righteous man condemned by a corrupt court for testifying of the coming Christ is itself a figure of Christ's own trial and death. Abinadi knows he is enacting something larger than himself; the prophet about to be burned tells his killers their act has typological weight. His death will mean something he will not live to see.
- *Salvation does not come by the law alone (vv27-28) — the chapter's thesis.* This is the doctrinal hinge of the whole Abinadi confrontation. The priests have staked everything on "salvation cometh by the law of Moses." Abinadi grants the law's *present* validity — "expedient... as yet" — and its *coming* obsolescence — "the time shall come when it shall no more be expedient" (fulfilled when Christ declares the law fulfilled, 3 Ne 15:4-10). But the core correction is that the law, even kept perfectly, cannot save: "were it not for the atonement, which **God himself shall make**... they must unavoidably perish, **notwithstanding the law of Moses**." The law is not the means of salvation; it is provisional scaffolding around the One who is. Strip the Atonement away and the most law-observant people still perish. (Paul's exact argument — Gal 2:16, Heb 10:1; and 2 Ne 25:23, saved "by grace, after all we can do.")
- *The law's real purpose: remembrance for the forgetful (vv29-31).* Why give "a very strict law" at all? Because Israel was "stiffnecked... quick to do iniquity, and **slow to remember the Lord**" — the law of performances was given "to keep them in remembrance of God and their duty." The law is pedagogy, not redemption: a daily discipline to hold a forgetful people's attention on God until the Redeemer comes. And note the diagnosis — "slow to remember" — is the same disease Zeniff named of his own people (9:3) and Benjamin built teachers against (6:3). The whole record keeps circling the one chronic human failure: we forget. The law was God's remedy for forgetting, and "all these things were **types of things to come**" (v31) — every ordinance a finger pointing forward to Christ. The keepers who never saw what it pointed at (v32) had the scaffolding and missed the building.
- *The whole prophetic tradition testifies of the condescending, suffering God (vv33-35).* Abinadi closes this movement by appealing past himself to the entire prophetic witness — Moses and "all the prophets... since the world began" — all testifying "that **God himself should come down among the children of men, and take upon him the form of man**... and that he, himself, should be **oppressed and afflicted**." He is laying the track for what comes next: the God who condescends to flesh and is afflicted is precisely Isaiah's suffering servant, whom Abinadi will quote *in full* in the next chapter. The argument is building deliberately toward Isaiah 53.
