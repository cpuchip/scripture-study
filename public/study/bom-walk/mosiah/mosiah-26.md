# Mosiah 26 — As Often As My People Repent: The Leader Who Asks, and the Forgiveness Without Limit

*(A rising generation of unbelievers; dissent erodes the church; Alma, facing an unprecedented case, inquires of the Lord; the revelation on church discipline, forgiveness, and the sheep who hear. ~120–100 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** A new problem hits the church that has never happened before — believing members led into sin — and Alma does not know what to do. The chapter turns on what a righteous leader does when he doesn't know, and what God reveals when asked: a doctrine of forgiveness without a ceiling.

**Read:** "Many of the **rising generation**... could not understand the words of king Benjamin, being little children at the time... and they did not believe the tradition of their fathers" (v1) — not the resurrection, not the coming of Christ (v2); "because of their unbelief they could not understand the word of God; and their hearts were hardened" (v3); they "would not be baptized" (v4). They "deceive many with their **flattering words**, who were in the church, and did cause them to commit many sins" (v6); the sinners are brought to Alma, the high priest, for "king Mosiah had given Alma the authority over the church" (vv7-8). "Alma did not know concerning them" (v9); "there had not any such thing happened before in the church; therefore **Alma was troubled in his spirit**" (v10). He brings them to the king; Mosiah: "**I judge them not; therefore I deliver them into thy hands to be judged**" (v12). "The spirit of Alma was again troubled; and **he went and inquired of the Lord**... for he feared that he should do wrong" (v13); "after he had **poured out his whole soul** to God, the voice of the Lord came" (v14): "Blessed art thou, Alma... because of thy exceeding **faith in the words alone of my servant Abinadi**" (v15); "**because thou hast inquired of me concerning the transgressor, thou art blessed**" (v19); "I covenant with thee that thou shalt have **eternal life**" (v20). "He that will **hear my voice** shall be my sheep... and him will I also receive" (v21); "whomsoever ye receive shall believe in my name; and **him will I freely forgive**" (v22); "it is I that taketh upon me the **sins of the world**" (v23). Those who "never knew me" — "then shall they know that I am... their Redeemer; **but they would not be redeemed**" (v26); "**I will confess unto them that I never knew them**" (v27). "Whosoever transgresseth against me... if he confess his sins before thee and me, and **repenteth in the sincerity of his heart, him shall ye forgive, and I will forgive him also**" (v29); "**as often as my people repent will I forgive them their trespasses**" (v30); "ye shall also forgive one another... **he that forgiveth not his neighbor's trespasses when he says that he repents, the same hath brought himself under condemnation**" (v31); "whosoever will not repent... shall not be numbered among my people" (v32). Alma "wrote them down" (v33); the repentant were numbered, the unrepentant "**their names were blotted out**" (v36); "they began again to have peace and to prosper" (v37).

**Thoughts:**
- *The unbelieving rising generation (vv1-4).* The first time the Nephite church faces *generational* unbelief — and the cause is poignant: "they could not understand the words of king Benjamin, **being little children at the time** he spake." They missed the formative moment. The great covenant assembly of chapters 2-5 happened before they could grasp it, and now, grown, "they did not believe the tradition of their fathers." The hard truth underneath: a founding spiritual experience cannot be *inherited*. Benjamin's people had the mighty change at the tower; their children, who only heard about it, must come to their own faith — and many won't. The faith of the fathers does not transmit automatically; each generation must believe for itself, and a generation that did not have its own conversion can dwindle into unbelief while standing in the same land where its parents were transformed. (And the engine of the erosion is, again, *flattery*, v6 — the same instrument that corrupted Noah's people, 11:7. The dissenters don't argue the believers out; they flatter them out.)
- *The leader who does not know, and asks (vv9-14).* This is the chapter's model of righteous leadership, and it is built on *not-knowing*. Alma faces something "there had not any such thing happened before" — no precedent, no playbook. And he does the opposite of confident improvisation: he is "troubled," he brings it to the king (who defers it back), and finally "he went and inquired of the Lord... **for he feared that he should do wrong**." He "poured out his whole soul to God" before acting. The high priest, the founder of the church, the man with authority over it, refuses to guess on a matter of eternal weight — he asks. And the Lord's response is striking: among the blessings pronounced on Alma is "**because thou hast inquired of me concerning the transgressor, thou art blessed**" (v19). The asking itself is counted as righteousness. Leadership that knows the limits of its own knowledge and takes the hard question to God, rather than ruling by its own wisdom, is blessed *for the asking*. (The opposite of Noah, who ruled by "the desires of his own heart," 11:2.)
- *Faith in the words alone (vv15-16).* Twice the Lord locates the blessing in faith "in the **words alone**" — Alma believed Abinadi's words *alone*, and the people believed Alma's words *alone*. No sign, no vision, no proof — Alma watched a prophet burn and believed his words, and that bare faith on the word is what the Lord blesses "exceeding[ly]." This is the purest form of faith: receiving the message as true without external evidence, on the word alone. It is also the only kind most believers ever have — and the chapter dignifies it as the faith that founded the whole church.
- *As often as my people repent — forgiveness without a ceiling (vv29-31).* The doctrinal heart, given in three movements. First, God forgives the sincerely repentant: "if he confess... and repenteth in the sincerity of his heart, him shall ye forgive, and **I will forgive him also**" (v29). Second, the measure is *unlimited*: "**as often as my people repent will I forgive them their trespasses against me**" (v30). There is no quota on God's forgiveness; the only condition is real repentance, and it may be claimed again and again. Third, the human obligation mirrors the divine: "ye shall also forgive one another... **he that forgiveth not his neighbor's trespasses when he says that he repents, the same hath brought himself under condemnation**" (v31). The reciprocity is exact and binding (D&C 64:10; the Lord's Prayer; the unforgiving servant of Matt 18): God forgives us without limit, and *therefore* we must forgive others without limit — and to withhold forgiveness from the repentant is to bring condemnation on *ourselves*, not on them. The one who will not forgive a repentant brother becomes the greater debtor.
- *The sheep who hear vs. those who would not be redeemed (vv21-28).* The criterion for the church and for salvation is the same: "he that will **hear my voice** shall be my sheep" (v21; John 10). Receive the hearers into the church; do not receive those who will not hear, "for him I will not receive at the last day" (v28). And the most sobering line: those who "never knew me" will at the judgment finally know He is the Redeemer "but they would not be redeemed" (v26) — they will be told "**I never knew you**" (v27; Matt 7:23). Knowing God is *hearing His voice*; the ones cast out are not those who failed but those who **would not hear** — the same self-authored loss as 16:12 ("the arms of mercy... they would not"). The door is open to every sheep that will hear; it closes only on the deaf-by-choice.
- *Discipline aimed at repentance — names blotted out, but the door open (vv32-37).* The first formal church discipline in the record: the repentant are "numbered among the people," the unrepentant "not numbered... and their names were blotted out" (v36; Ex 32:33). But the whole apparatus is *redemptive*, not punitive — the disciplined are given every chance to confess and be restored, and the standard is "as often as my people repent." Alma writes the revelation down (v33) so that judgment is by *revealed principle*, not the judge's whim or mood. Church discipline, rightly done, is not the church protecting itself from sinners; it is the church holding the door open for the repentant while honestly naming who has, for now, walked out. (And the king's refusal to judge it, v12, keeps it ecclesiastical — the church disciplines its own, the state does not.)
- *Alma's eternal life secured (v20).* "I covenant with thee that thou shalt have eternal life" — the Book of Mormon's first explicit promise of eternal life to a named individual (the making-sure of 2 Pet 1:10). Notably it comes not after a great miracle but after humble inquiry and faithful, unprecedented-problem-bearing service. The man blessed with the surest promise is the one who feared most to "do wrong in the sight of God" and asked.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. Two doctrinal hearts. First, **the leader who does not know and asks**: Alma faces an unprecedented case ("there had not any such thing happened before"), refuses to improvise, "feared that he should do wrong," and "poured out his whole soul" inquiring — and the Lord blesses him *for the asking* (v19, "because thou hast inquired... thou art blessed"). Righteous leadership knows the limits of its own knowledge and takes the hard question to God — the opposite of Noah ruling by "the desires of his own heart." Second, **forgiveness without a ceiling**: "**as often as my people repent will I forgive them**" (v30) — no quota, the only condition real repentance — *and* the binding reciprocity: "he that forgiveth not his neighbor's trespasses when he says that he repents, the same hath brought himself under condemnation" (v31, D&C 64:10). God forgives without limit; therefore we must, or condemn ourselves. The chapter also gives the **uninheritable founding experience** (the rising generation who missed Benjamin's sermon and won't believe the fathers' faith — each generation must convert for itself), **faith in the words alone** (Alma believed Abinadi with no sign, v15), the **sheep who hear** vs. those who "**would not be redeemed**" / "I never knew you" (vv26-27, the self-authored loss of 16:12), **church discipline aimed at repentance** (names blotted out, but the door held open, written down as revealed principle not whim), and **Alma's covenant of eternal life** (v20 — the BoM's first such promise to a named person, given after humble inquiry).
