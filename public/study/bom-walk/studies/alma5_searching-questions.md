# Alma 5 — The Searching-Questions Sermon

**Binding question:** How does Alma use *questions* (rather than declarations) to "bear down in pure testimony" and pull down pride — and what is the "mighty change of heart" he keeps pressing them toward?

---

## I. Before the Sermon: What Alma Saw

The sermon of Alma 5 does not begin in chapter 5. It begins in chapter 4, where Alma is watching his congregation fail.

In the eighth year of the judges, the church at Zarahemla had prospered materially and collapsed spiritually. The record is specific: "the people of the church began to wax proud, because of their exceeding riches, and their fine silks, and their fine-twined linen... for they began to wear very costly apparel" (Alma 4:6). Pride in Alma 4 is not a theological abstraction; it is a garment. It is visible. And it spread until "there were envyings, and strife, and malice, and persecutions, and pride, even to exceed the pride of those who did not belong to the church of God" (Alma 4:9). The Nephite saints had out-sinned the unbelievers. Alma's chapter 5 sermon is addressed to that specific, documented failure.

Alma's response is to resign. He gives up the judgment-seat to Nephihah and returns to the high priesthood, and the text explains the decision in a single sentence: "he might pull down, by the word of God, all the pride and craftiness and all the contentions which were among his people, seeing no way that he might reclaim them save it were in bearing down in pure testimony against them" (Alma 4:19).

Three verbs: *pull down*, *reclaim*, *bearing down in pure testimony*. Alma has considered the options — law, persuasion, administration — and rejected them. Pride cannot be argued down or legislated away; it must be *testified against*. And the testimony, to do that work, must be *pure* — unmixed with flattery, uncut by accommodation. Webster's 1828 definition of *testimony* is "open attestation; profession... witness; evidence; proof of some fact." What follows in chapter 5 is Alma presenting evidence to his congregation's own consciences. The questions are the testimony.

## II. The Ground: A Remembered Deliverance (5:1-13)

The sermon begins not with accusation but with history — and the history opens with questions.

"Have you sufficiently retained in remembrance the captivity of your fathers? Yea, and have you sufficiently retained in remembrance his mercy and long-suffering towards them? And moreover, have ye sufficiently retained in remembrance that he has delivered their souls from hell?" (Alma 5:6).

Three parallel questions, each pressing deeper: from *captivity* (a political fact) to *mercy* (a relational fact) to *souls delivered from hell* (a theological fact). Alma is not telling them what their fathers experienced. He is asking whether they have thought about it enough. The word "sufficiently" is the probe — not "do you know the story?" but "have you let it reach you?"

The story itself Alma then narrates in condensed form: God "changed their hearts; yea, he awakened them out of a deep sleep, and they awoke unto God. Behold, they were in the midst of darkness; nevertheless, their souls were illuminated by the light of the everlasting word; yea, they were encircled about by the bands of death, and the chains of hell, and an everlasting destruction did await them" (Alma 5:7). Sleep to waking, darkness to light, chains to freedom — Alma's language for what happened to his father's generation is the language of resurrection applied to living hearts.

Then he identifies the mechanism. Questions again: "did not my father Alma believe in the words which were delivered by the mouth of Abinadi? And was he not a holy prophet? Did he not speak the words of God, and my father Alma believe them?" (Alma 5:11). Three rhetorical questions, each requiring only "yes" in answer, each tightening the chain: Abinadi spoke → Alma the Elder believed → "according to his faith there was a mighty change wrought in his heart" (Alma 5:12).

This genealogy of the change is structural. Alma is the son of the changed man. He himself was converted violently and completely — "I am born of the Spirit" (Mosiah 27:24), "I am born of God" (Mosiah 27:28). When he stands before Zarahemla and asks whether they have been born of God, he is asking a question he has already answered for himself. He is not theorizing.

The ground the sermon builds on is this: the change is real, it happened, it sustained people through bondage and destruction, it made them faithful until the end, "therefore they were saved" (Alma 5:13). That word *therefore* is the pivot — faithfulness was not an ornament on salvation but its cause. And the past change does not carry anyone forward automatically. Which is why the next section presses: *now*.

## III. The Question-Cascade (5:14-31)

Forty-three distinct questions appear across Alma 5's sixty-two verses. The density is highest in this middle section, and the questions shift in kind: from memorial questions (has the past reached you?) to diagnostic questions (what is your present condition?).

The pivot is verse 14: "have ye spiritually been born of God? Have ye received his image in your countenances? Have ye experienced this mighty change in your hearts?"

Three questions going inward: the event (born of God?), the visible mark (his image in your countenance?), the interior reality (the change in your hearts?). The second question is the sharpest. Webster 1828 on *countenance*: "the appearance or expression of the face... approving or encouraging aspect of face; hence, favor, good will." And under *image*: "an imitation, representation, or similitude of any person," citing Genesis 1:27 — "God created man in his own image." To receive his image in your countenance is to bear his likeness outwardly, to carry his favor visibly. The question is whether looking at the congregation's faces you would see God's character there, or something else.

The cascade that follows forces this question into concrete imaginative scenarios. Alma does not say *you are proud*. He asks: "can you imagine to yourselves that ye hear the voice of the Lord, saying unto you... Come unto me ye blessed, for behold, your works have been the works of righteousness upon the face of the earth?" (Alma 5:16). Then immediately the counter: "Or do ye imagine to yourselves that ye can lie unto the Lord in that day, and say — Lord, our works have been righteous works upon the face of the earth — and that he will save you?" (Alma 5:17). Then the alternative picture: "can ye imagine yourselves brought before the tribunal of God with your souls filled with guilt and remorse, having a remembrance of all your guilt, yea, a perfect remembrance of all your wickedness" (Alma 5:18).

"Imagine." "Imagine." "Imagine." Alma asks his congregation to do sustained interior work, to picture themselves at the bar of God *with the life they are currently living* and see which scenario they inhabit. Then: "can ye look up to God at that day with a pure heart and clean hands? I say unto you, can you look up, having the image of God engraven upon your countenances?" (Alma 5:19). The "look up" posture is the posture of prayer and petition. If they cannot look up in honest imagination now, they will not be able to look up then.

All of this is testimony — not declaration, not argument, but evidence presented to the hearer's own conscience with a question attached. Alma is not convicting them; he is opening the space for the Spirit to convict. Rhetoric would manipulate from outside; this question-cascade hands them the mirror and steps back.

The sharpest question comes in verse 26: "if ye have experienced a change of heart, and if ye have felt to sing the song of redeeming love, I would ask, can ye feel so now?"

"Can ye feel so now?" The question has a tense. It does not ask whether they were once converted. It asks whether the conversion is presently alive — whether the felt joy of redemption is accessible to them today, in this meeting, in this season of pride and scorn. The word *now* is the blade of the question. If they cannot answer yes, the prior change has cooled into a memory rather than a living condition.

The questions then sharpen further in their specificity: "Are ye stripped of pride?" (Alma 5:28). "Is there one among you who is not stripped of envy?" (Alma 5:29). "Is there one among you that doth make a mock of his brother, or that heapeth upon him persecutions?" (Alma 5:30). These are not rhetorical in the sense of expecting no answer. They name specific behaviors visible in the community Alma described in chapter 4 — the scorn, the persecution, the envy — and ask each hearer to locate themselves in relation to those behaviors. The question is the sermon.

## IV. Two Shepherds (5:37-42)

After the question-cascade comes a turn. Alma does not stay in interrogation mode. He moves to declaration — but a declaration with a question embedded.

"O ye workers of iniquity; ye that are puffed up in the vain things of the world, ye that have professed to have known the ways of righteousness nevertheless have gone astray, as sheep having no shepherd, notwithstanding a shepherd hath called after you and is still calling after you, but ye will not hearken unto his voice!" (Alma 5:37).

The charge — *you have gone astray* — lands differently because of the word *professed*. They know better. They have made covenants. They have taken his name. And yet: "the good shepherd doth call you; yea, and in his own name he doth call you, which is the name of Christ; and if ye will not hearken unto the voice of the good shepherd, to the name by which ye are called, behold, ye are not the sheep of the good shepherd" (Alma 5:38).

The logic is inescapable and follows John 10 precisely: the sheep know their shepherd's voice; they follow it; a stranger's voice they will not follow. If you are not following the voice of the good shepherd, you are following a different voice — and there is only one other: "the devil is your shepherd, and ye are of his fold; and now, who can deny this?" (Alma 5:39). The question at the end — "who can deny this?" — is directed at their own self-knowledge. You know which voice you have been heeding. So do I. So does God.

The two-shepherd framework closes a logical trap, but not a cruel one. The shepherd is *still calling* (v.37). The door is not closed. The question is whether they will hear.

## V. The Axe and the Fruit (5:34-36, 52)

Threaded through the final movement of the sermon is arboreal imagery that connects the invitation to urgency.

The invitation first: "Come unto me and ye shall partake of the fruit of the tree of life; yea, ye shall eat and drink of the bread and the waters of life freely; yea, come unto me and bring forth works of righteousness, and ye shall not be hewn down and cast into the fire" (Alma 5:34-35). The grammar of that sentence is worth noticing: come → partake → bring forth works → not be hewn down. The movement is from invitation through fruit to survival. The tree of life is not a destination for the passive; it is the source that produces works.

Then the urgency: "the Spirit saith: Behold, the ax is laid at the root of the tree; therefore every tree that bringeth not forth good fruit shall be hewn down and cast into the fire, yea, a fire which cannot be consumed, even an unquenchable fire" (Alma 5:52). The image echoes John the Baptist's preaching recorded in Matthew and Luke — the axe at the root, not trimming branches but severing the tree entirely. Alma attributes it to "the Spirit" rather than to his own authority. The eschatological edge is not Alma's invention; he is reporting what the Spirit told him to say.

The axe is not wielded yet. The tree is at risk, not fallen. The sermon is the warning, and the warning is the mercy.

---

## VI. "I Speak in the Energy of My Soul" (5:43-48)

In the middle of calling his congregation to account, Alma stops and testifies about himself.

"I would that ye should hear me, for I speak in the energy of my soul; for behold, I have spoken unto you plainly that ye cannot err" (Alma 5:43). Then, a few verses later: "Do ye not suppose that I know of these things myself? Behold, I testify unto you that I do know that these things whereof I have spoken are true. And how do ye suppose that I know of their surety? Behold, I say unto you they are made known unto me by the Holy Spirit of God. Behold, I have fasted and prayed many days that I might know these things of myself" (Alma 5:45-46).

Alma asked these questions of himself before asking them of Zarahemla. He fasted and prayed until he could answer. The "energy of my soul" is not rhetorical intensity; it is the force of a person who has done the interior work he is asking them to do. And the thing he knows — the thing at the center of the "pure testimony" — is this: "I know that Jesus Christ shall come, yea, the Son, the Only Begotten of the Father, full of grace, and mercy, and truth. And behold, it is he that cometh to take away the sins of the world, yea, the sins of every man who steadfastly believeth on his name" (Alma 5:48).

Pride is incompatible with steadfast belief precisely because pride substitutes self-elevation for dependence. The questions Alma has asked are not moral inventory for its own sake; they are designed to expose the gap between the congregation's self-assessment and what steadfast belief actually looks like from the outside.

---

## VII. Command and Invitation (5:57-62)

The sermon closes with two modes, named explicitly.

"All you that are desirous to follow the voice of the good shepherd, come ye out from the wicked, and be ye separate, and touch not their unclean things; and behold, their names shall be blotted out, that the names of the wicked shall not be numbered among the names of the righteous" (Alma 5:57). The separation is communal, not merely internal — names in a book, a visible ecclesial act.

Then the distinction: "I speak by way of command unto you that belong to the church; and unto those who do not belong to the church I speak by way of invitation, saying: Come and be baptized unto repentance, that ye also may be partakers of the fruit of the tree of life" (Alma 5:62).

The same sermon is law for covenant members and gospel for the unbaptized. For those who have already taken Christ's name, Alma speaks with authority: come out, be separate, keep the name you bear. For those who haven't — those listening from the margins of the congregation — the word is pure invitation, the same fruit-of-the-tree image from verse 34. The sermon that began with the scouring question "can ye feel so now?" ends with an open door: come, and be baptized, and partake.

The question is not only historical. Alma asked it of his fathers, then of himself. He now asks it of Zarahemla. The reader is next.

---

## Becoming

The question from verse 26 is addressed to me as directly as it was to the saints at Zarahemla: *Can ye feel so now?*

Not: did I once believe. Not: do I hold the right doctrines. But: is the felt reality of redemption alive in me today? Is there a song I could honestly sing about it?

The practical test Alma offers is not a checklist of commandments but a posture. Can I look up? Can I imagine standing before God with my present life and look up honestly? If the honest answer is no, that is not a verdict — it is a question Alma is handing me to take to the same fasting and prayer he took it to. The door in verse 62 stays open.

One concrete practice: take one of Alma's diagnostic questions — "are ye stripped of pride?" or "can ye feel so now?" — and sit with it not as rhetoric but as genuine self-inquiry for a day. Not to perform conviction but to actually ask. Alma didn't deliver the answer; he opened the space.
