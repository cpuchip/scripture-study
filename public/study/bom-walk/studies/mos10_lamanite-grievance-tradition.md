# The Lamanite Grievance Tradition

**Binding question:** How does a false tradition built on real events read through a wrong interpretation harden into civilization-defining hatred — and what eventually heals it?

---

## I. The Grievance Stated

Zeniff is old. He has spent years watching his people bleed for land that Lamanite kings kept promising and then taking back. He has just lost another war. And here, at the end of Mosiah 10, he pauses to explain why. Not the proximate cause, not King Laman's cunning, but the deep cause: the thing that made the hatred available for cunning to exploit.

His explanation runs seven verses (Mosiah 10:12-18), and it is structured with a precision that rewards slow reading. He does not just report what the Lamanites believe. He reports their belief, and then, in the same breath, gives the true account that their belief has distorted.

"Believing that they were driven out of the land of Jerusalem because of the iniquities of their fathers, and that they were wronged in the wilderness by their brethren, and they were also wronged while crossing the sea; and again, that they were wronged while in the land of their first inheritance, after they had crossed the sea, and all this because that Nephi was more faithful in keeping the commandments of the Lord — therefore he was favored of the Lord, for the Lord heard his prayers and answered them, and he took the lead of their journey in the wilderness." (Mosiah 10:12-13)

Four locations. Four wrongs. One cause repeated each time: Nephi's faithfulness and the Lord's response to it. Zeniff is not just documenting a grievance. He is documenting a grievance and its refutation simultaneously, arranged so you cannot miss the structure.

The culminating charge:

"And again, they were wroth with him because he departed into the wilderness as the Lord had commanded him, and took the records which were engraven on the plates of brass, for they said that he robbed them." (Mosiah 10:16)

He robbed them. The phrase is doing enormous work. The records are what Benjamin will later call the only thing standing between Nephites and the same fate — "we should have been like unto our brethren, the Lamanites, who know nothing concerning these things, or even do not believe them when they are taught them, because of the traditions of their fathers, which are not correct" (Mosiah 1:5). The thing they accused Nephi of stealing is the very thing whose absence made the tradition possible.

Then the summary: "And thus they have taught their children that they should hate them, and that they should murder them, and that they should rob and plunder them, and do all they could to destroy them; therefore they have an eternal hatred towards the children of Nephi." (Mosiah 10:17)

"Eternal hatred." The phrase is not Zeniff's coinage. Jacob — the son of Lehi, who lived through the separation — had already used it within the founding generation: "they had an eternal hatred against us, their brethren. And they sought by the power of their arms to destroy us continually." (Jacob 7:24). What Mosiah 10 presents as the explanation for an ongoing war is a condition already named by someone who watched it begin.

---

## II. The Anatomy: Truth-Events and Lie-Interpretation

The grievance is not invented from nothing. That is what makes it durable.

Laman and Lemuel were driven out of Jerusalem. That is true. They did suffer in the wilderness. That is true — the text says so (1 Nephi 17:20-21: "our women have toiled, being big with child; and they have borne children in the wilderness and suffered all things, save it were death"). Nephi did take the lead in the journey. He did take the records. He did become ruler when the people divided.

Every item in the Mosiah 10 indictment points to something that actually happened.

The lie is not in the events. It is in the interpretation.

The ship crossing is the clearest case. On the sea, Laman and Lemuel "began to make themselves merry, insomuch that they began to dance, and to sing, and to speak with much rudeness, yea, even that they did forget by what power they had been brought thither" (1 Nephi 18:9). Nephi tried to speak to them. They bound him: "they did treat me with much harshness; nevertheless, the Lord did suffer it that he might show forth his power" (1 Nephi 18:11). The storm came. The compass stopped working. Three days, then four, driven back. Swollen wrists. Swollen ankles.

From Laman's side: we bound Nephi, and then there was a storm. We were wronged upon the waters.

From the text: they bound Nephi, and therefore the compass stopped, and therefore the storm. "Nevertheless, the Lord did suffer it." The storm was the consequence of the binding, not an ambient hardship that happened to coincide with it.

The same events. Opposite interpretations. And there is no empirical way to force the choice — you cannot prove to someone that a storm was caused by their rebellion against God rather than by oceanic weather. The interpretation requires exactly what Mosiah 10:14 says they lacked: understanding of "the dealings of the Lord."

This is why the grievance is so structurally stable. It is not a lie about what happened. It is a lie about what it meant — and meaning is not falsifiable by pointing at facts, because the facts are agreed upon. The separation happened. The suffering happened. Nephi took the records and the leadership. The Lamanite tradition accepted all of this and simply assigned it different causes.

The murmuring in 1 Nephi 17 already contains the interpretive seed. Laman and Lemuel looked at the wilderness years and read them as evidence of Lehi's and Nephi's wrongness: "we might have enjoyed our possessions and the land of our inheritance; yea, and we might have been happy" (1 Nephi 17:21). Jerusalem was righteous; the wilderness was deprivation; therefore Lehi and Nephi were wrong to bring them out. That logic survived the separation and became the founding document of a civilization.

---

## III. Transmission: "Thus They Have Taught Their Children"

The mechanism is explicit. Mosiah 10:17 doesn't say "and so the children grew up hating them." It says "thus they have taught their children." Active. Deliberate. Curricular.

Jacob confirms this from the other side: within one generation, "many means were devised to reclaim and restore the Lamanites to the knowledge of the truth; but it all was vain, for they delighted in wars and bloodshed" (Jacob 7:24). The teaching is already complete within the founding generation's children. Reclamation is attempted from the beginning and fails from the beginning. The tradition is not something that hardened gradually over centuries — it arrived fully formed, because it was transmitted on purpose.

4 Nephi later makes the pattern explicit in reverse, when it re-emerges among dissenters after 200 years of unity: "they were taught to hate the children of God, even as the Lamanites were taught to hate the children of Nephi from the beginning" (4 Nephi 1:39). "Taught." The same word. The author is noting that this is not an accident or a gradual drift but a replication of a known structure. Hatred of this depth requires a teacher.

The tradition also amplifies over time through a mechanism Moroni identifies later in the war chapters. Writing to Pahoran about why the Lord's judgments are on the Nephites and not the Lamanites, he distinguishes between the two: "it is the tradition of their fathers that has caused their hatred, yea, and it has been redoubled by those who have dissented from us" (Alma 60:32). The Zoramites, the Amalekites, the Amulonites who join the Lamanites — they bring their full Nephite knowledge plus their chosen rejection of it, and they pour that into a tradition that was already violent. The hatred grows more intense not only because more generations have been taught it, but because people with clearer light add their apostasy to it. The tradition is not static. It compounds.

---

## IV. "They Understood Not the Dealings of the Lord"

Mosiah 10:14 is the theological center of the whole passage: "his brethren were wroth with him because they understood not the dealings of the Lord."

This is Zeniff's explanation for the origin, but it points to something structural. Understanding the "dealings of the Lord" — how God works, what suffering is for, why a covenant people passes through a wilderness before inheriting a land — is not automatic knowledge. It requires the scriptures, the prophetic record, the continuous tradition of meaning-making that the records carry. Benjamin says as much: without the records, the Nephites themselves "would have dwindled in unbelief" and would be in exactly the position of the Lamanites, who "know nothing concerning these things, or even do not believe them when they are taught them, because of the traditions of their fathers, which are not correct" (Mosiah 1:5).

The phrase "do not believe them when they are taught them" is worth sitting with. The tradition does not just produce ignorance. It produces an active resistance to correction. By the time someone tries to teach the Lamanites the true account, the false account has already occupied the cognitive space where the true one would go. The tradition becomes the filter through which all new information is received — and the filter rejects what doesn't fit.

This is why Alma 9:16-17 attributes the Lord's extended mercy to the tradition itself: "it is because of the traditions of their fathers that caused them to remain in their state of ignorance; therefore the Lord will be merciful unto them." The ignorance is inherited, not chosen. The condition that would normally mark apostasy — rejecting the truth — is mitigated by the fact that the truth was never available to them in recognizable form. They were taught to hate. They were not taught to hate knowing they were wrong.

---

## V. Partial Exculpation

This creates a genuine distinction that the text maintains carefully throughout.

Moroni in Alma 60 places the Nephites under greater condemnation than the Lamanites precisely because Nephite iniquity is chosen — "your iniquity is for the cause of your love of glory and the vain things of the world" — while Lamanite hatred is tradition-caused. The Lamanites are not innocent. Their deeds are evil and the consequences are real. But culpability is calibrated to knowledge, and the tradition has systematically denied them the knowledge that would make their choices fully informed.

The anti-Nephi-Lehies understand this about themselves. When they finally receive the full story from Ammon, their response is not "now we see that we were wrong about a few things." It is total: "since it has been all that we could do (as we were the most lost of all mankind) to repent of all our sins and the many murders which we have committed" (Alma 24:11). "The most lost of all mankind" — they have grasped not just that their tradition was incorrect but how deeply it shaped them. They are not excusing their murders. They are naming the depth of what they had to climb out of.

The mercy extends forward: "at some period of time they will be brought to believe in his word, and to know of the incorrectness of the traditions of their fathers; and many of them will be saved" (Alma 9:17). The prophecy is not conditional — it is a promise. The Lord has already determined that this will happen, and has prolonged their existence in the land accordingly. The healing is not just possible. It is coming.

---

## VI. The Healing: Weapons Buried

What heals it is not time. It is not cultural shift or gradual softening. It is the arrival of someone who carries the true account and is willing to tell it to the people who inherited the false one.

The sons of Mosiah spend fourteen years among the Lamanites (Alma 17:4). Before they go, they prepare: "they had searched the scriptures diligently, that they might know the word of God... they had given themselves to much prayer, and fasting; therefore they had the spirit of prophecy, and the spirit of revelation, and when they taught, they taught with power and authority of God" (Alma 17:2-3). The scriptures they have searched are the records. The records the tradition called stolen.

When Ammon finally sits with King Lamoni and explains who he is and what God is, the content of his teaching is precise: "he began at the creation of the world, and also the creation of Adam, and told him all the things concerning the fall of man, and rehearsed and laid before him the records and the holy scriptures of the people, which had been spoken by the prophets, even down to the time that their father, Lehi, left Jerusalem. And he also rehearsed unto them... all the journeyings of their fathers in the wilderness, and all their sufferings with hunger and thirst... And he also rehearsed unto them concerning the rebellions of Laman and Lemuel, and the sons of Ishmael, yea, all their rebellions did he relate unto them" (Alma 18:36-38).

He told them about the rebellions. The part the tradition never included — because the tradition was built by the rebels, who had every reason to omit that. Ammon gives Lamoni the complete story: Creation, Fall, Redemption, AND the full Lehi family history including what Laman and Lemuel actually did. The tradition said Nephi usurped and stole. Ammon says: here is what actually happened, and here are the records that carry that account, the same records your tradition says were stolen.

Samuel the Lamanite, looking back on what the preaching of the Nephites accomplished, gives the sequence: "as many of them as are brought to the knowledge of the truth, and to know of the wicked and abominable traditions of their fathers, and are led to believe the holy scriptures, yea, the prophecies of the holy prophets, which are written, which leadeth them to faith on the Lord, and unto repentance, which faith and repentance bringeth a change of heart unto them" (Helaman 15:7). The tradition must be named — specifically called wicked and abominable — before repentance is possible. You cannot repent of what you believe is righteousness. The first movement is recognizing the tradition as a tradition, and as an incorrect one.

When the anti-Nephi-Lehies bury their weapons, their king names what they are burying: "I thank my God, my beloved people, that our great God has in goodness sent these our brethren, the Nephites, unto us to preach unto us, and to convince us of the traditions of our wicked fathers" (Alma 24:7). The weapons are the physical expression of the tradition — every sword they forged to carry out the inherited directive to murder and plunder the children of Nephi. Burying them is the covenantal act: we are not just stopping the violence; we are testifying, permanently, against the tradition that generated it. "This they did, it being in their view a testimony to God, and also to men, that they never would use weapons again for the shedding of man's blood; and this they did, vouching and covenanting with God, that rather than shed the blood of their brethren they would give up their own lives; and rather than take away from a brother they would give unto him" (Alma 24:18).

"Rather than take away from a brother they would give unto him." The tradition said Nephi took. These people are going to give. The grievance ran in one direction for four centuries. The covenant runs the other way.

---

## Becoming

The structure of the Lamanite grievance — real wounds, wrong interpretation, transmitted as identity — does not require ancient scripture to find a contemporary example. The question is whether I carry any story that was handed to me as truth and that might be a tradition: a reading of real events through a lens I inherited rather than examined. What I call "just the way things are" in some relationship, some group, some history — is that understanding, or is it what I was taught?

The healing didn't happen by the Lamanites eventually figuring it out on their own. It happened when someone arrived who carried the complete story, including the part the tradition had suppressed, and who had the Spirit sufficient to make it receivable. That is what the sons of Mosiah's fourteen years of preparation was for. The question it raises is not "is my tradition false?" but "am I willing to be the person who brings the true account to someone for whom it costs something to receive it?"
