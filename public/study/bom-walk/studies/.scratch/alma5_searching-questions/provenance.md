# Alma 5 — Searching Questions | Source Log

*Working document. Kept as research provenance — traces how observations were reached.*

**Binding question:** How does Alma use *questions* (rather than declarations) to "bear down in pure testimony" and pull down pride — and what is the "mighty change of heart" he keeps pressing them toward?

---

## Outline

1. **The memory of deliverance as the ground (5:1-13)** — Alma roots the appeal in their fathers' deliverance from bondage; the "mighty change" wrought in Alma the Elder's people as the founding fact.
2. **The searching questions (5:14-31)** — the heart of the sermon. The question-cascade and its rhetorical force — Alma does not tell them they are proud; he asks them whether they could stand before God *now*.
3. **The two shepherds / two voices (5:37-42, 57-60)** — the good shepherd calls in his own name; the devil is the other shepherd; sheep must know the voice.
4. **The tree and the fruit (5:34-36, 52, 62)** — bring forth good works / good fruit or be hewn down; the axe at the root.
5. **"I speak by way of command" / the call (5:43-62)** — Alma's authority, the call to repentance and separation, the closing invitation.

## Verified Quotes

### Alma 4 — The problem Alma saw before he spoke

#### Alma 4:6
> "the people of the church began to wax proud, because of their exceeding riches, and their fine silks, and their fine-twined linen, and because of their many flocks and herds, and their gold and their silver, and all manner of precious things, which they had obtained by their industry; and in all these things were they lifted up in the pride of their eyes, for they began to wear very costly apparel."

**Observation:** Pride in Alma 4 is specifically material pride — not spiritual arrogance in the abstract but *visible, wearable pride*. This is what he's addressing in Alma 5:53 when he returns to "costly apparel." The problem is named here before the sermon begins.
**Connects to:** Section 1 (context)

#### Alma 4:9
> "there were envyings, and strife, and malice, and persecutions, and pride, even to exceed the pride of those who did not belong to the church of God."

**Observation:** The Nephite church had out-pridded the unbelievers. This is the staggering context. Alma's questions in chapter 5 are not theoretical — they are diagnostic probes into a community already worse than the world around them.
**Connects to:** Section 1 (context), Section 2 (questions)

#### Alma 4:19 — The "pure testimony" mandate
> "he might pull down, by the word of God, all the pride and craftiness and all the contentions which were among his people, seeing no way that he might reclaim them save it were in bearing down in pure testimony against them."

**Observation:** Three verbs: *pull down* (not argue down), *reclaim* (not punish), *bearing down in pure testimony* (not legislation, not shame, not reason alone). Alma chooses the instrument deliberately. The word "pure" in Webster 1828 = unmixed, unadulterated. The testimony would not be diluted with flattery or accommodation.
**Connects to:** All sections — the mandate framing

### Alma 5:1-13 — The memory of deliverance as ground

#### Alma 5:6
> "have you sufficiently retained in remembrance the captivity of your fathers? Yea, and have you sufficiently retained in remembrance his mercy and long-suffering towards them? And moreover, have ye sufficiently retained in remembrance that he has delivered their souls from hell?"

**Observation:** The sermon *opens* with questions — these are memorial questions, not self-examination questions yet. Alma uses three parallel "have ye retained in remembrance" questions to establish that the fathers' deliverance is the evidentiary ground for everything that follows. The change in the fathers is *proof*, and the proof should be *remembered*.
**Connects to:** Section 1

#### Alma 5:7
> "he changed their hearts; yea, he awakened them out of a deep sleep, and they awoke unto God. Behold, they were in the midst of darkness; nevertheless, their souls were illuminated by the light of the everlasting word; yea, they were encircled about by the bands of death, and the chains of hell, and an everlasting destruction did await them."

**Observation:** The language of the deliverance: sleep/awakening, darkness/light, chains/freedom. This is resurrection imagery applied to *living people's hearts*. The same imagery Alma the Younger uses of his own conversion in Mosiah 27:28-29. Pattern: heart-change = coming alive, coming into light, breaking chains.
**Connects to:** Section 1, cross-reference to Mosiah 27

#### Alma 5:11-12 — The Abinadi chain
> "did not my father Alma believe in the words which were delivered by the mouth of Abinadi? And was he not a holy prophet? Did he not speak the words of God, and my father Alma believe them? And according to his faith there was a mighty change wrought in his heart."

**Observation:** Three rhetorical questions + the answer (v.12). Alma grounds the change not in abstract doctrine but in a specific person hearing a specific prophet. Abinadi spoke → Alma the Elder believed → mighty change. Now Alma the Younger is *replicating that moment* with his own congregation. He is doing to them what Abinadi did to his father.
**Connects to:** Section 1; cross-study mos13-16_abinadi-christology

#### Alma 5:13
> "he preached the word unto your fathers, and a mighty change was also wrought in their hearts, and they humbled themselves and put their trust in the true and living God. And behold, they were faithful until the end; therefore they were saved."

**Observation:** The word "therefore" — faithfulness until the end *is the cause* of salvation, not just a correlate. The change was not a one-time event; it sustained them. This is what Alma will press in v.26: "can ye feel so *now*?" The past change doesn't carry forward automatically.
**Connects to:** Section 2

---

### Alma 5:14 — The pivot question

#### Alma 5:14
> "have ye spiritually been born of God? Have ye received his image in your countenances? Have ye experienced this mighty change in your hearts?"

**Observation:** Three questions, each going deeper: (1) born of God? — the event; (2) his image in your countenance? — the visible mark; (3) mighty change in your hearts? — the interior reality. Webster 1828 on *countenance* def. 3: "approving or encouraging aspect of face; hence, favor, good will." To receive *his image* in your countenance = to bear his likeness visibly, to have his favor manifest in your face. This is the New Testament image — 2 Cor. 3:18, "beholding as in a glass the glory of the Lord, are changed into the same image." Alma is asking whether their faces look like Christ.
**Connects to:** Section 2

---

### Alma 5:15-27 — The question-cascade

#### Alma 5:15
> "Do ye exercise faith in the redemption of him who created you? Do you look forward with an eye of faith, and view this mortal body raised in immortality, and this corruption raised in incorruption, to stand before God to be judged according to the deeds which have been done in the mortal body?"

**Observation:** The judgment scene is invoked as a *test* — can you picture yourself there? The next sequence (vv.16-25) will make that picture visceral and uncomfortable.
**Connects to:** Section 2

#### Alma 5:18-19
> "can ye imagine yourselves brought before the tribunal of God with your souls filled with guilt and remorse, having a remembrance of all your guilt, yea, a perfect remembrance of all your wickedness, yea, a remembrance that ye have set at defiance the commandments of God? I say unto you, can ye look up to God at that day with a pure heart and clean hands? I say unto you, can you look up, having the image of God engraven upon your countenances?"

**Observation:** The word "imagine" + the word "remember" — Alma is asking them to do the work of *imagining themselves at the bar of God, now, with the life they are currently living*. "Perfect remembrance of all your wickedness" — no selective memory, no self-justification. The question is diagnostic. Notice: "look up" — the posture of prayer and plea. Can you look up *there* if you will not humble yourself *here*?
**Connects to:** Section 2

#### Alma 5:26 — The permanent edge
> "if ye have experienced a change of heart, and if ye have felt to sing the song of redeeming love, I would ask, can ye feel so now?"

**Observation:** The sharpest question in the chapter. It does not ask "did you once believe?" It asks whether the belief is *present-tense alive*. "Sing the song of redeeming love" — not doctrine, but joy, the felt response to redemption. Alma is not asking about past conversion. He is asking about present spiritual condition. The "so" is doing enormous work: *can you feel, the way you once felt, right now?*
**Connects to:** Section 2

#### Alma 5:27-28
> "Have ye walked, keeping yourselves blameless before God? Could ye say, if ye were called to die at this time, within yourselves, that ye have been sufficiently humble? That your garments have been cleansed and made white through the blood of Christ... Behold, are ye stripped of pride? I say unto you, if ye are not ye are not prepared to meet God."

**Observation:** The garments/pride questions. Notice the sequence: garments cleansed (interior, spiritual) → stripped of pride (exterior, social). The garment image echoes the temple covenant. "Stripped of pride" — the verb is stark; pride must be *removed* like a garment, not moderated.
**Connects to:** Section 2

### Alma 5:33-42 — Two shepherds, one invitation

#### Alma 5:33-34
> "he sendeth an invitation unto all men, for the arms of mercy are extended towards them, and he saith: Repent, and I will receive you. Yea, he saith: Come unto me and ye shall partake of the fruit of the tree of life; yea, ye shall eat and drink of the bread and the waters of life freely;"

**Observation:** After the scouring questions, the invitation. This pivot is crucial: the questions strip away self-deception, and the invitation is what stands after the stripping. The "arms of mercy extended" image echoes 2 Nephi 26:25: "Come unto me all ye ends of the earth."
**Connects to:** Sections 3 and 5

#### Alma 5:37-38
> "O ye workers of iniquity; ye that are puffed up in the vain things of the world, ye that have professed to have known the ways of righteousness nevertheless have gone astray, as sheep having no shepherd, notwithstanding a shepherd hath called after you and is still calling after you, but ye will not hearken unto his voice! Behold, I say unto you, that the good shepherd doth call you; yea, and in his own name he doth call you, which is the name of Christ; and if ye will not hearken unto the voice of the good shepherd, to the name by which ye are called, behold, ye are not the sheep of the good shepherd."

**Observation:** The key move: they have *professed* to know righteousness but have "gone astray." They are not ignorant wolves; they are *sheep who have drifted*. This is addressed to the church. "As sheep having no shepherd" — but the shepherd is still calling. The name: "his own name... which is the name of Christ." At baptism they took Christ's name. Drifting from the shepherd is drifting from the name they bear.
**Connects to:** Section 3

#### Alma 5:39
> "And now if ye are not the sheep of the good shepherd, of what fold are ye? Behold, I say unto you, that the devil is your shepherd, and ye are of his fold; and now, who can deny this? Behold, I say unto you, whosoever denieth this is a liar and a child of the devil."

**Observation:** Binary logic with no soft middle. There are two shepherds; you follow one of them. The question "who can deny this?" is both rhetorical and genuine — can you, looking at your life, deny which shepherd's voice you have been following?
**Connects to:** Section 3

### Alma 5:43-48 — "I speak in the energy of my soul"

#### Alma 5:43-46
> "I would that ye should hear me, for I speak in the energy of my soul; for behold, I have spoken unto you plainly that ye cannot err, or have spoken according to the commandments of God... Do ye not suppose that I know of these things myself? Behold, I testify unto you that I do know that these things whereof I have spoken are true. And how do ye suppose that I know of their surety? Behold, I say unto you they are made known unto me by the Holy Spirit of God. Behold, I have fasted and prayed many days that I might know these things of myself."

**Observation:** This is the pivot from interrogation to personal witness. Alma plants his own testimony in the middle of the cascade: I asked these questions of *myself*. I fasted and prayed until I knew. The "energy of my soul" — the 1828 dictionary would render *energy* as active force, not mere enthusiasm. Alma is not performing; he is *exerting*.
**Connects to:** Section 5, framing the questions as Alma's own lived answer

#### Alma 5:48
> "I say unto you, that I know that Jesus Christ shall come, yea, the Son, the Only Begotten of the Father, full of grace, and mercy, and truth. And behold, it is he that cometh to take away the sins of the world, yea, the sins of every man who steadfastly believeth on his name."

**Observation:** The theological center of the pure testimony: Christ is coming, Christ takes away sins, for every man who *steadfastly believeth*. The questioning has been pointing here. Pride is incompatible with steadfast belief because pride substitutes self-elevation for dependence on him.
**Connects to:** Section 5

### Alma 5:52-62 — The axe, the separation, the invitation

#### Alma 5:52
> "the Spirit saith: Behold, the ax is laid at the root of the tree; therefore every tree that bringeth not forth good fruit shall be hewn down and cast into the fire, yea, a fire which cannot be consumed, even an unquenchable fire. Behold, and remember, the Holy One hath spoken it."

**Observation:** The tree/axe image is from John the Baptist (Matthew 3:10 / Luke 3:9). Alma draws on it via the Spirit's voice. The urgency is eschatological. "At the root" — not trimming branches but severing from the source.
**Connects to:** Section 4

#### Alma 5:57
> "all you that are desirous to follow the voice of the good shepherd, come ye out from the wicked, and be ye separate, and touch not their unclean things; and behold, their names shall be blotted out, that the names of the wicked shall not be numbered among the names of the righteous"

**Observation:** The ecclesial call — not just personal conversion but communal separation. This echoes Isaiah 52:11 ("depart ye, depart ye, go ye out from thence, touch no unclean thing"). The shepherd-voice leads to *coming out*, not just internal change.
**Connects to:** Section 5

#### Alma 5:62
> "I speak by way of command unto you that belong to the church; and unto those who do not belong to the church I speak by way of invitation, saying: Come and be baptized unto repentance, that ye also may be partakers of the fruit of the tree of life."

**Observation:** Alma distinguishes his mode: command to members (accountability), invitation to non-members (grace). The sermon ends not with condemnation but with the same invitation that opened the fruit-of-the-tree section (v.34). The closing is an open door.
**Connects to:** Section 5

### Cross-References — "Mighty Change" Network

#### Mosiah 5:2 — Benjamin's people
> "the Spirit of the Lord Omnipotent, which has wrought a mighty change in us, or in our hearts, that we have no more disposition to do evil, but to do good continually."

**Observation:** The exact phrase "mighty change in us, or in our hearts" — Alma 5:12-14 quotes this almost verbatim. Benjamin's people experienced it corporately and declared it. Alma is asking whether Zarahemla can make the same declaration.
**Connects to:** Sections 1 and 2

#### Mosiah 27:25-28 — Alma the Younger's own conversion
> "Marvel not that all mankind, yea, men and women, all nations, kindreds, tongues and people, must be born again; yea, born of God, changed from their carnal and fallen state, to a state of righteousness, being redeemed of God, becoming his sons and daughters; And thus they become new creatures... I am born of the Spirit... I am born of God."

**Observation:** These are Alma's own words describing his conversion. When he asks Zarahemla "have ye spiritually been born of God?" in Alma 5:14, he is asking a question he has already answered for himself. He is not theorizing. He is re-asking his own conversion question.
**Connects to:** Section 2

#### Mosiah 24:13-14 — The deliverance itself (what Alma 5:3-9 is summarizing)
> "the voice of the Lord came to them in their afflictions, saying: Lift up your heads and be of good comfort, for I know of the covenant which ye have made unto me; and I will covenant with my people and deliver them out of bondage. And I will also ease the burdens which are put upon your shoulders, that even you cannot feel them upon your backs, even while you are in bondage; and this will I do that ye may stand as witnesses for me hereafter"

**Observation:** The original deliverance was covenant-grounded — God remembered their covenant. The eased burdens were not the removal of difficulty but the strengthening to bear it. *That* community-founding experience is what Alma 5 is pressing them to remember and replicate internally.
**Connects to:** Section 1

#### John 10:3-4, 11 — The good shepherd parallel
> "the sheep hear his voice: and he calleth his own sheep by name, and leadeth them out... And when he putteth forth his own sheep, he goeth before them, and the sheep follow him: for they know his voice... I am the good shepherd: the good shepherd giveth his life for the sheep."

**Observation:** Alma's two-shepherd teaching (5:37-42) draws directly on this. The distinguishing mark in both texts is *voice recognition*. You know which shepherd you follow by which voice you have been heeding. Alma makes explicit what John leaves as parable.
**Connects to:** Section 3

## Webster 1828

### "countenance"
- **Def. 1:** "Appearance or expression of the face; look; aspect; mien."
- **Def. 3:** "Approving or encouraging aspect of face; hence, favor, good will, support; aid; encouragement."
- **Study relevance:** "Have ye received his image in your countenances?" (Alma 5:14) — not merely "do you look like Christ" in a superficial way, but: does his *approval and encouragement* show in your face? Countenance in 1828 carried the sense of *bearing another's favor*. The question is whether God's favor is visibly borne.

### "testimony"
- **Def. 3:** "Open attestation; profession."
- **Def. 4:** "Witness; evidence; proof of some fact."
- **Study relevance:** "Pure testimony" in Alma 4:19 = open attestation, public attestation, evidence offered as proof. Not performance or persuasion — *evidence*. The questions ARE the testimony because they present evidence to the hearer's own conscience.

### "image"
- **Def. 1:** "An imitation, representation, or similitude of any person... a copy; a likeness; an effigy..."
- **Gen. 1:27 cited in the entry itself:** "And God created man in his own image."
- **Study relevance:** Webster's dictionary cites Gen. 1:27 under *image*. When Alma asks "have ye received his image in your countenances?" he is invoking the original creation — man was made bearing God's image; the fall obscured it; the new birth restores it. The mighty change = restoration of the image.

## Gap & Critical Analysis

### Confirmed Strong
- **Questions as testimony (binding question)** — Alma 4:19 uses "pure testimony" as the mandate; Alma 5 executes it almost entirely through questions. The link is explicit. Verified from text.
- **"Mighty change" network** — Alma 5:7, 12, 13, 14, 26 all verified; Mosiah 5:2 and Mosiah 27:24-26 verified as direct parallels. The three-generation chain (Abinadi → Alma Elder → Alma's congregation → Alma the Younger himself) is all in the text.
- **Two-shepherd / voice identity** — Alma 5:37-42 verified verbatim; John 10:3-4, 11 verified. The voice-recognition framework is in both texts.
- **Question count** — 43 distinct questions counted from verified text. The canonical claim "40+ questions" is supported.
- **The sermon opens and closes with an invitation** — v.34 (fruit of tree of life, come freely) and v.62 (come and be baptized). The scouring questions are bracketed by mercy. This is in the text.

### Needs Qualification
- **Matthew 3:10 / Luke 3:9 (axe at the root)** — Alma 5:52 says "the Spirit saith: Behold, the ax is laid at the root of the tree." The Baptist parallel is real but Alma attributes it to the Spirit, not John. The connection is genuine but should be framed as shared typology, not direct borrowing.
- **"Image of God restored by new birth"** — the reading of Alma 5:14 ("his image in your countenances") as restoration of the Gen. 1:27 image-of-God is theologically coherent and supported by Webster's citation of Gen. 1:27 under "image," but the text does not make this explicit. Mark as interpretive inference, confident but not declared.
- **The questions as Alma's own lived self-examination** — Alma 5:45-46 says he "fasted and prayed many days" to know these things. The inference that he asked the same questions of himself before asking them of Zarahemla is a reading, not a stated fact. Strong reading, should be framed as such.

### Acknowledged Gaps
- **No conference talk cross-reference fetched** — the sermon search returned no highly relevant conference talks. This is acceptable; the study is text-primary. The binding question is internal to scripture.
- **Pearl of Great Price not checked** — Moses 1 / Abraham themes of image and likeness weren't needed; the Gen. 1:27 reference came through Webster's dictionary entry for "image" and is sufficient.
- **Alma's own conversion narrative (Mosiah 27)** — fetched and logged. The connection to his personal "I am born of the Spirit" is in the scratch file.

### Tensions to Name
- **Questions as testimony vs. questions as rhetoric** — the sermon could be read as masterful rhetoric (guilt-tripping via Socratic method). The study should name this tension: Alma is not using questions to shame but to *create space for the Spirit to convict*. The distinction matters. Rhetoric manipulates from outside; the Spirit convicts from within. The questions are designed to bypass defenses that argument would trigger.
- **Command vs. invitation** — Alma 5:61-62 distinguishes: "I speak by way of command unto you that belong to the church; and unto those who do not belong to the church I speak by way of invitation." This is a real tension the study must surface, not smooth over. The same sermon is both law and gospel depending on the hearer's covenant status.

### Ring Check
The binding question was: *How does Alma use questions (rather than declarations) to "bear down in pure testimony" and pull down pride — and what is the "mighty change of heart" he keeps pressing them toward?*

The study's gathered sources answer this: questions function as pure testimony by presenting evidence to the hearer's own conscience (the Spirit convicts; the questions create the opening). The "mighty change" is birth of God / restoration of the divine image / the present-tense aliveness of faith ("can ye feel so now?"). The sermon circles back: it opens with memorial questions about the fathers' change and closes with an invitation to enter that same change. The ring is closed.

### Posture Check
Sources gathered include: the problem text (Alma 4), the full sermon (Alma 5), the mandate text (Alma 4:19), three cross-references in the "mighty change" network, the good-shepherd parallel (John 10), and the Webster definitions for the load-bearing words. One tension (questions as rhetoric vs. Spirit-space-creation) is named, not resolved. One gap (axe/John the Baptist) is acknowledged as a secondary connection. No sources cherry-picked to support a predetermined thesis; the question count (43) and the command/invitation distinction (vv.61-62) are both data that complicate the thesis rather than confirm it cleanly.
