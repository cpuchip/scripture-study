# Alma 12–13: The Two Directions of a Fallen Heart

**Binding question:** How do these two chapters move from the captivity of the devil and the Fall (ch. 12) to the holy order of the priesthood, foreordination, and entering into the rest of the Lord (ch. 13) — and what binds the two halves into one argument about how a fallen people is brought back to God?

---

## I. The Narrowing Word

The discourse opens mid-confrontation. Zeezrom has been trying to trap Alma and Amulek with trick questions, and Alma has just told him that his plan belongs to the adversary, that the adversary "exercised his power in thee" (Alma 12:5). Then he broadens: "Now I would that ye should remember that what I say unto thee I say unto all" (v. 5). Zeezrom's private trap is now the city's public problem.

Alma explains what the adversary was doing. He was trying "to bring you into subjection unto him, that he might encircle you about with his chains, that he might chain you down to everlasting destruction, according to the power of his captivity" ([Alma 12:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/12?lang=eng&id=p6#p6)). Note the progression: subjection → encirclement → chaining → destruction. The final state is not a dramatic fall but a completed constriction.

Then comes the passage that defines what that constriction is:

> "It is given unto many to know the mysteries of God; nevertheless they are laid under a strict command that they shall not impart only according to the portion of his word which he doth grant unto the children of men, according to the heed and diligence which they give unto him. And therefore, he that will harden his heart, the same receiveth the lesser portion of the word; and he that will not harden his heart, to him is given the greater portion of the word, until it is given unto him to know the mysteries of God until he know them in full. And they that will harden their hearts, to them is given the lesser portion of the word until they know nothing concerning his mysteries; and then they are taken captive by the devil, and led by his will down to destruction. Now this is what is meant by the chains of hell." ([Alma 12:9–11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/12?lang=eng&id=p9-p11#p9))

The last sentence is an explicit definition, not a metaphor. Alma is not invoking "chains" as colorful language for wickedness. He is saying: this process — harden → receive less word → harden more → receive less still → know nothing — this is what the chains of hell *are*. The captivity is epistemic before it is moral. The devil does not seize people from the outside; he waits for the narrowing to complete. When a person has, by accumulated refusals, reduced their access to the word to nothing, they have handed themselves over. The devil's will then moves them because no other word remains to orient them.

The word "mystery" in 1828 carried more weight than it does today. Webster's 1828 definition includes "a kind of secret religious celebration, to which none were admitted except those who had been initiated by certain preparatory ceremonies." The mysteries of God in v. 9 are not merely strange doctrines; they are initiatory truths given to the prepared. The person who gives heed and diligence is prepared to receive them. The one who hardens is not — and so receives less, which makes them less prepared, which yields less still. It is a spiral, not a single choice.

The word Alma leaves hanging at the end of verse 10 — "until it is given unto him to know the mysteries of God until he know them in full" — is the other direction. Receive more → be more prepared → receive more → know in full. That direction will not be developed until chapter 13. But verse 10 puts it there, quietly, before Alma turns to the Fall.

## II. The Cherubim and the Probationary State

Antionah, a chief ruler, interrupts with a question that looks like a challenge but is actually a gift: "What does the scripture mean, which saith that God placed cherubim and a flaming sword on the east of the garden of Eden, lest our first parents should enter and partake of the fruit of the tree of life, and live forever?" ([Alma 12:21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/12?lang=eng&id=p21#p21)).

Alma's response — "This is the thing which I was about to explain" (v. 22) — signals that Antionah's question is not a detour. It is the pivot Alma needed. The cherubim are the opening to the theology he is building.

The standard reading of the cherubim-and-sword passage treats it as punishment: Adam and Eve are expelled, the way back is blocked. Alma reads it differently.

> "Now we see that Adam did fall by the partaking of the forbidden fruit, according to the word of God; and thus we see, that by his fall, all mankind became a lost and fallen people. And now behold, I say unto you that if it had been possible for Adam to have partaken of the fruit of the tree of life at that time, there would have been no death, and the word would have been void, making God a liar... And now behold, if it were possible that our first parents could have gone forth and partaken of the tree of life they would have been forever miserable, having no preparatory state; and thus the plan of redemption would have been frustrated, and the word of God would have been void, taking none effect." (vv. 22–23, 26)

The cherubim are not God's anger. They are the architecture of mercy. Without them, Adam eats, becomes immortal in a fallen state, no death can interrupt the condition, no space for repentance exists, no resurrection is needed, no plan of redemption has any ground to operate on. The flaming sword is what makes repentance possible. By blocking immortality in sin, it forces a probationary state into existence — "therefore this life became a probationary state; a time to prepare to meet God" ([Alma 12:24](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/12?lang=eng&id=p24#p24)).

"Probationary" in 1828 meant what it still means in law: a period of active evaluation with real stakes. The word has not softened since Joseph Smith's time. The probationary state is not comfortable holding; it is trial with an outcome.

After the Fall, God did not leave the fallen people uninstructed. He sent angels, conversed with men, "made known unto them the plan of redemption, which had been prepared from the foundation of the world; and this he made known unto them according to their faith and repentance and their holy works" (v. 30). Again the pattern from chapter 12's opening: knowledge comes according to faith and repentance. It is the same gradient.

The chapter closes its own argument:

> "But God did call on men, in the name of his Son, (this being the plan of redemption which was laid) saying: If ye will repent, and harden not your hearts, then will I have mercy upon you, through mine Only Begotten Son; Therefore, whosoever repenteth, and hardeneth not his heart, he shall have claim on mercy through mine Only Begotten Son, unto a remission of his sins; and these shall enter into my rest." (vv. 33–34)

"Enter into my rest." The destination appears here, in chapter 12, before chapter 13 introduces the priesthood. Alma has already stated where the not-hardened heart goes. The next chapter will show what God built to get them there.

The phrase "harden not your hearts" appears four times in verses 33–37. It is not rhetoric. It is the pivot of the whole discourse. Chapter 12 began by defining what hardening produces (chains of hell). It ends by naming what not-hardening produces (entering rest). Everything in between — the Fall, the cherubim, the probationary state, the plan of redemption — is the argument for why the condition of the heart determines which of those two destinations a fallen person reaches.

## III. The Holy Order and What Foreordination Actually Says

Chapter 13 opens with a backward gesture: "I would cite your minds forward to the time when the Lord God gave these commandments unto his children" ([Alma 13:1](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/13?lang=eng&id=p1#p1)). "Forward" here means back — toward the original time of appointment. Alma is taking the audience to the moment when the priesthood was established, so they can understand why it exists now.

The priests were ordained after the order of the Son, "in a manner that thereby the people might know in what manner to look forward to his Son for redemption" (v. 2). The manner of the priesthood is itself a teaching. It is a type of Christ's redemptive order — not just an administrative structure but an enacted testimony of how fallen people get back to God.

Then comes the foreordination passage, which requires careful reading:

> "And this is the manner after which they were ordained—being called and prepared from the foundation of the world according to the foreknowledge of God, on account of their exceeding faith and good works; in the first place being left to choose good or evil; therefore they having chosen good, and exercising exceedingly great faith, are called with a holy calling..." (v. 3)

The phrase "according to the foreknowledge of God" can be misread as predestination — God picked them, end of discussion. Verses 4 and 5 close that reading:

> "And thus they have been called to this holy calling on account of their faith, while others would reject the Spirit of God on account of the hardness of their hearts and blindness of their minds, while, if it had not been for this they might have had as great privilege as their brethren. Or in fine, in the first place they were on the same standing with their brethren..." (vv. 4–5)

Three times the text insists on equal standing: the others "might have had as great privilege," they started on "the same standing," the separation came from hardness of heart — not from God's arbitrary assignment. The foreordination tracks choices; it does not preempt them. Noah Webster's 1828 dictionary, defining "foreknowledge," quotes Milton: "If I foreknew, Foreknowledge had no influence on their fault." The Book of Mormon and Milton are answering the same Calvinist question in the same direction.

The calling is pre-mortal — "from the foundation of the world" — but it is grounded in foreknown, freely made choices. God saw what they would choose. He called them accordingly. The calling is real; the freedom is real; neither cancels the other.

The purpose of the calling is stated plainly in verse 6: "ordained unto the high priesthood of the holy order of God, to teach his commandments unto the children of men, that they also might enter into his rest." The priesthood exists for one functional reason — that others might enter rest. The priests are not ordained to administer programs. They are ordained as the means by which God's plan of redemption reaches fallen people in their probationary state.

Verse 7 describes the order as "without beginning of days or end of years, being prepared from eternity to all eternity." The priesthood is as old as the plan of redemption that it serves. They were laid down together, from the foundation of the world.

## IV. Melchizedek: The Pattern Proven

Alma does not leave the doctrine abstract. He offers a case study — the most instructive one available.

> "Yea, humble yourselves even as the people in the days of Melchizedek, who was also a high priest after this same order which I have spoken..." (v. 14)

Melchizedek's people are described in terms that could describe Ammonihah:

> "Now this Melchizedek was a king over the land of Salem; and his people had waxed strong in iniquity and abomination; yea, they had all gone astray; they were full of all manner of wickedness." (v. 17)

That is not a morally neutral population. They had gone entirely the wrong direction — the direction chapter 12 named as leading to captivity. Then:

> "But Melchizedek having exercised mighty faith, and received the office of the high priesthood according to the holy order of God, did preach repentance unto his people. And behold, they did repent; and Melchizedek did establish peace in the land in his days." (v. 18)

The people of Salem were as far gone as Alma's audience appears to be. The mechanism was the same: a high priest after the holy order, preaching repentance. And they repented. The [JST account of Genesis 14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/jst/jst-gen/14?lang=eng) expands this briefly: Melchizedek "obtained peace in Salem" not merely by governance but because the priesthood he held, in its fullest expression, brought people to God. The JST calls him "king of heaven by his people" — the peace was more than political.

Alma's point is precise: "none were greater" (v. 19). Of all the high priests, Melchizedek is the exemplar because his situation was the hardest and his people's transformation was the most complete. If Salem could repent from total wickedness through the holy order, Ammonihah has no grounds to claim the task is impossible.

## V. Enter Into His Rest

The phrase "enter into the rest of the Lord" runs through both chapters like a refrain that is building toward a destination. It appears first in Alma 12:34 — "these shall enter into my rest" — spoken as the promise to those who repent and do not harden their hearts. It appears again in 12:37 — "let us enter into the rest of God." Then in 13:6, 12, 13, 16, 29, and finally 13:30 in its negative form.

This is not a theme loosely scattered through the text. It is the thesis-sentence repeated at structurally significant moments because the whole discourse is moving toward it.

What is the rest? [D&C 84:24](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/84?lang=eng&id=p24#p24), commenting on Israel's failure in the wilderness, defines it: "the Lord in his wrath... swore that they should not enter into his rest while in the wilderness, which rest is the fulness of his glory." The rest is not death, not peace of mind, not freedom from striving. It is the fulness of God's glory — an encounter with God himself. The [Hebrews 3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/3?lang=eng) passage Alma's "first provocation" language (Alma 12:36) echoes reaches the same conclusion: the wilderness generation hardened their hearts, God swore in his wrath "They shall not enter into my rest" (Heb 3:11), and the author of Hebrews frames it as a standing offer still available to the living.

Alma 13:11–12 describes what the arrival looks like:

> "Therefore they were called after this holy order, and were sanctified, and their garments were washed white through the blood of the Lamb. Now they, after being sanctified by the Holy Ghost, having their garments made white, being pure and spotless before God, could not look upon sin save it were with abhorrence; and there were many, exceedingly great many, who were made pure and entered into the rest of the Lord their God."

"Exceedingly great many" — Alma does not present this as the attainment of a handful of extraordinary souls. It is many. The process is demanding, but the outcome is not rare. Called → sanctified by Holy Ghost → garments white through the Atonement → pure and spotless → could not look on sin without abhorrence → entered rest. Each step follows from the one before. The sanctification does not merely clean the surface; it changes what the person is able to tolerate.

The closing of the discourse brings both trajectories together:

> "And now, my brethren, I wish from the inmost part of my heart, yea, with great anxiety even unto pain, that ye would hearken unto my words, and cast off your sins, and not procrastinate the day of your repentance... Having faith on the Lord; having a hope that ye shall receive eternal life; having the love of God always in your hearts, that ye may be lifted up at the last day and enter into his rest." (vv. 27, 29)

Then immediately:

> "And may the Lord grant unto you repentance, that ye may not bring down his wrath upon you, that ye may not be bound down by the chains of hell, that ye may not suffer the second death." (v. 30)

The ring is closed. Chapter 12 opened by naming the chains of hell — the destination of the hardened heart. Chapter 13 closes by invoking the same chains as the alternative to entering rest. Between those two mentions: the plan of redemption, the probationary state the cherubim made possible, the holy order prepared from the foundation of the world to teach the commandments so that fallen people might reach God's rest.

The discourse is one argument. The hardened heart goes to the chains. The heart that chooses not to harden — that receives the word, repents, accepts the ministry of the holy order — goes to rest. That is the whole of it. And Alma speaks the close with "great anxiety even unto pain" because he can see which direction Ammonihah is heading. He knows what the chains look like from the inside. He was once on that road.

---

## Becoming

The chains-of-hell passage in Alma 12:11 is not about dramatic apostasy. It describes a process that begins with ordinary hardening — with choosing, just this once, not to receive. The man who hardens does not wake up one morning knowing nothing. He wakes up knowing slightly less than he did yesterday, and that continues for years, and one day the narrowing is complete and he cannot find the category he used to occupy.

The inverse is also true. The man who gives heed and diligence does not know the mysteries of God by Thursday. He wakes up knowing slightly more than he did, and that continues, and one day the expansion is complete. "Until it is given unto him to know the mysteries of God until he know them in full" (12:10). Both processes are daily. Both are cumulative. Neither announces itself as decisive until it is.

The question Alma 12–13 leaves is not which trajectory is available. It is which one is actually happening — in what direction is the heart currently moving, and how long has it been moving that way?
