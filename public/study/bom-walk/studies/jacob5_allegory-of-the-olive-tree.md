# Jacob 5 — The Allegory of the Olive Tree

**Binding Question:** What does the allegory of the olive tree reveal about the labor and grief of a God who keeps coming back to a decaying vineyard — and why does it end with the servants laboring "for the last time" alongside the master?

[Jacob 5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/jacob/5?lang=eng) is the longest chapter in the Book of Mormon — 77 verses — and it comes from a prophet named Zenos whose words survive only through the brass plates, never recovered in the Bible. Jacob reads it aloud to his people as his answer to the hardest question in his ministry. He has just asked it in [Jacob 4:17](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/jacob/4?lang=eng&id=p17#p17): "how is it possible that these, after having rejected the sure foundation, can ever build upon it?" How does God restore what Israel itself has ruined? The allegory is the answer.

---

## I. What Kind of God Does This

Jacob sets up the allegory over two chapters. In Jacob 4 he describes the Jews — "stiffnecked people" who "despised the words of plainness, and killed the prophets" — and says that because they looked beyond the mark they will "reject the stone upon which they might build." Then he says something remarkable: "I will unfold this mystery unto you" (Jacob 4:18). And he proceeds to read 77 verses of Zenos.

The allegory begins with a one-sentence situation: "For behold, thus saith the Lord, I will liken thee, O house of Israel, like unto a tame olive tree, which a man took and nourished in his vineyard; and it grew, and waxed old, and began to decay" (Jacob 5:3).

Three things in that sentence are load-bearing. "Took" — the tree did not plant itself; it was chosen and placed. "Nourished" — not planted and abandoned; tended. "Began to decay" — the problem is not that God failed. The tree decayed despite the nourishing. The allegory never attributes decay to neglect. Every act of corruption in the chapter happens in the gap between nourishment and response.

The Lord of the vineyard sees the decay and does not walk away. "I will prune it, and dig about it, and nourish it, that perhaps it may shoot forth young and tender branches, and it perish not" (v. 4). The word "perhaps" is not uncertainty about divine power. It is the grammar of hope in the face of a tree with agency. God can prune and dig and nourish; he cannot force the fruit.

---

## II. Four Visits Across Dispensations

The allegory moves in four distinct visits. Each is marked by the same formula — the Lord of the vineyard goes down to labor, a long time passes, then he returns again. Tracking these visits is how the dispensational architecture becomes visible.

**Visit 1 (vv. 4–14):** The master goes forth and sees the decay. He prunes, grafts wild branches into the main tree to strengthen the roots, and hides the natural branches — the covenant Israel — in the nethermost parts of the vineyard. "The Lord of the vineyard went his way, and hid the natural branches of the tame olive tree in the nethermost parts of the vineyard, some in one and some in another, according to his will and pleasure" (v. 14). The word "hid" carries weight. Not scattered in judgment, not abandoned in anger — hidden. Purposefully placed. This is the scattering era: covenant Israel scattered to every corner of the earth, but hidden, not lost.

**Visit 2 (vv. 15–28):** "A long time passed away, and the Lord of the vineyard said unto his servant: Come, let us go down into the vineyard, that we may labor in the vineyard" (v. 15). He checks the main tree — the grafted wild branches have taken hold of the root and brought forth fruit "like unto the natural fruit" (v. 17). Then he visits the four hidden natural-branch locations. Three bring forth much fruit even in poor and rocky ground. One — planted in the best ground — brings forth only partial fruit, some wild. The servant questions the placement in poor soil; the Lord answers without apology: "Counsel me not; I knew that it was a poor spot of ground; wherefore, I said unto thee, I have nourished it this long time, and thou beholdest that it hath brought forth much fruit" (v. 22). The poor spot is not a mistake. The nourishing over time is what matters, not the conditions.

[1 Nephi 15:13](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/15?lang=eng&id=p13#p13) gives the interpretive key for when this second visit falls: "in the latter days, when our seed shall have dwindled in unbelief, yea, for the space of many years, and many generations after the Messiah shall be manifested in body unto the children of men." Nephi's own people — scattered as a natural branch in a poor spot — will eventually receive the gospel through the Gentiles and be grafted back. The second visit therefore maps to the meridian era: Christ's ministry, the early church, a moment when the grafted-in gentile mission is bearing fruit and the scattered Israel is producing in unexpected places.

**Visit 3 (vv. 29–49):** "A long time had passed away" again, and the Lord returns — and everything has gone wrong. "All sorts of fruit did cumber the tree" (v. 30). He tastes every variety: "there is none of it which is good" (v. 32). The natural branches in the nethermost parts have also become corrupt — "the first and the second and also the last; and they had all become corrupt" (v. 39). The servant's diagnosis explains what happened: "Is it not the loftiness of thy vineyard — have not the branches thereof overcome the roots which are good? And because the branches have overcome the roots thereof, behold they grew faster than the strength of the roots, taking strength unto themselves" (v. 48). Prosperity overran covenant. Growth without rootedness produces corruption. This is the pattern of apostasy — not malice but loftiness, branches that grew faster than they could be sustained by the root.

Then: "the Lord of the vineyard wept, and said unto the servant: What could I have done more for my vineyard?" (v. 41). God weeps. Not performs grief — weeps. [Moses 7:28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/pgp/moses/7?lang=eng&id=p28#p28) records the same thing: "the God of heaven looked upon the residue of the people, and he wept." Enoch's question is our question: how can one who is holy and from all eternity to all eternity weep? The answer in Moses is that the grief is precisely proportional to the love. "They are the workmanship of mine own hands, and I gave unto them their knowledge... And unto thy brethren have I said, and also given commandment, that they should love one another, and that they should choose me, their Father; but behold, they are without affection" (Moses 7:32-33). The grief is the God who has done everything, whose beloved have turned elsewhere.

The rhetorical question echoes forward from Isaiah 5:4, preserved in [2 Nephi 15:4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/15?lang=eng&id=p4#p4): "What could have been done more to my vineyard that I have not done in it?" Isaiah's vineyard song is the same question in compressed form, ending in judgment. Zenos takes that question and plays it out over all of history — four dispensations, four returns, everything possible tried. The verse in Jacob 5:47 extends the question into detail: "Have I slackened mine hand, that I have not nourished it? Nay, I have nourished it, and I have digged about it, and I have pruned it, and I have dunged it; and I have stretched forth mine hand almost all the day long." The dunging. The word does not let us romanticize the divine labor. God gets into the manure of history — the most unglamorous, most intimate work of the vineyard. And still the corruption comes.

"I have done all" (v. 49). Not "I have done much." All.

**Visit 4 (vv. 50–76):** The servant's plea turns the chapter. "Spare it a little longer" (v. 50). The Lord yields — "it grieveth me that I should lose the trees of my vineyard" (v. 51) — and announces the plan: the scattered natural branches, still living in the nethermost parts, will be grafted back into the mother tree. The hidden Israel returns to heal the corrupted center. "Wherefore, let us take of the branches of these which I have planted in the nethermost parts of my vineyard, and let us graft them into the tree from whence they came" (v. 52). Scattering, it turns out, was not punishment. It was preservation. The roots preserved in the nethermost parts are now the grafts that can save what the loftiness destroyed.

Then the Lord calls for servants — plural, many: "go to, and call servants, that we may labor diligently with our might in the vineyard, that we may prepare the way" (v. 61). The servant goes and brings other servants: "and they were few" (v. 70). Few servants, one last time, laboring to prepare the way for a worldwide gathering. The instruction to these few is direct: "labor in the vineyard, with your might. For behold, this is the last time that I shall nourish my vineyard; for the end is nigh at hand... and if ye labor with your might with me ye shall have joy in the fruit" (v. 71).

"With me." Not for me. With me.

"The Lord of the vineyard labored also with them" (v. 72). He descends into the last labor the same way he descended into the first — not directing from outside but digging, pruning, dunging alongside. The God who kept coming back to a decaying vineyard also kept descending into the work.

## III. The Logic of the Root

The allegory's horticulture is the allegory's theology. The root is the Abrahamic covenant — the relationship God established with Abraham when he said "in thy seed shall all the kindreds of the earth be blessed" ([1 Nephi 15:18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/15?lang=eng&id=p18#p18)). The root never corrupts. "I know that the roots are good" (Jacob 5:36). Every intervention in the chapter — every grafting, scattering, hiding, restoring — is aimed at preserving the root or reconnecting branches to it.

Webster 1828 defines "graft" this way: "A small shoot or scion of a tree inserted in another tree, the stock of which is to support and nourish it. The two unite and become one tree, but the graft determines the kind of fruit." The graft determines the fruit, not the rootstock. This is why wild olive branches grafted into tame roots can bring forth tame fruit — because the root transforms the branch through the moisture it provides. Paul observed the same in [Romans 11:17-18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/rom/11?lang=eng&id=p17-p18#p17): wild branches grafted in "partakest of the root and fatness of the olive tree... thou bearest not the root, but the root thee." And both Paul and Zenos know this is unusual: grafting wild onto tame is not standard horticulture. It is grace acting against nature.

The problem in visit 3 is the inversion of that logic. The branches grew faster than the root could support them. "The wild branches have grown and have overrun the roots thereof; and because that the wild branches have overcome the roots thereof it hath brought forth much evil fruit" (Jacob 5:37). Prosperity and growth are not inherently bad; branches are supposed to grow. But branches that outgrow their root draw strength from themselves rather than from the covenant source. When the branch becomes its own source, the fruit goes wild.

This is not a metaphor about physical abundance. It is a diagnosis of what apostasy actually is: not dramatic rebellion so much as slow overgrowth. The branch does not decide to corrupt. It grows — in good conditions, even in the best-ground location — and the growth itself, unchecked by the root, becomes the problem.

---

## IV. The Grief That Does Not Give Up

"Grieveth me" appears throughout the chapter — when the tree begins to decay (v. 7), when the master nourishes the weakened roots (v. 11), when he hides the scattered natural branches (v. 13), when universal corruption appears (v. 32), when he surveys the corrupted natural branches (v. 46), when the servant pleads "spare it" (v. 51), when the final pruning begins (v. 66). Grief is not a single moment. It is the sustained emotional register of the Lord's relationship with this vineyard across all four visits.

The question "What could I have done more?" (vv. 41, 49) is not rhetorical in the ordinary sense — meaning, obviously nothing else could have been done. It is rhetorical in the sense that the Lord is not expecting an answer. He has already given the answer by having done everything. Pruning, digging, nourishing, grafting, scattering, hiding, returning again, and again, and again. "I have stretched forth mine hand almost all the day long" (v. 47). "Almost all the day" — not all the day. The end approaches. There is a closing time.

Enoch's question in Moses 7:29 is the question that frames the Jacob 5 weeping: "How is it that thou canst weep, seeing thou art holy, and from all eternity to all eternity?" The Lord's answer is that the grief is not weakness — it is the direct expression of what relationship costs. He made them with his own hands. He gave them knowledge. He gave them agency. He asked to be chosen. They chose otherwise. A God who could not grieve over that would be a God who had not actually given those things.

The grief does not produce withdrawal. It produces return. Each visit is driven by the same impulse that produces the grief — the refusal to give up the vineyard. The Lord says "let us go down" again and again not despite the grief but through it. Grief without return would be despair. Return without grief would be indifference. The allegory holds both, in every visit.

---

## V. "For the Last Time" — The Urgency of the Servants

"For the last time" repeats in the final movement like a drumbeat: "labor with our might this last time" (v. 62), "nourished once again for the last time" (v. 63), "dung them once more, for the last time" (v. 64), "this is the last time that I shall nourish my vineyard" (v. 71), "for this last time have we nourished my vineyard" (v. 75), "for the last time have I nourished my vineyard" (v. 76). Six times in sixteen verses. This is not a stylistic tic. It is the allegory insisting that this labor has a nature the previous labors did not: finality.

[D&C 43:28](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/43?lang=eng&id=p28#p28) takes up exactly this language: "Wherefore, labor ye, labor ye in my vineyard for the last time—for the last time call upon the inhabitants of the earth." The Lord's own revelation to Joseph Smith uses Zenos's phrase, in the same context of last-days urgency. The vineyard language is not metaphor borrowed for illustration; it is the Lord's own framework for what the Restoration is.

The servants are few (v. 70). They labor with their might. And the Lord of the vineyard — who has been laboring since v. 4 — labors with them again in v. 72. The instruction to "begin at the last that they may be first, and that the first may be last" (v. 63) echoes Matthew's kingdom-logic: the order of the gathering is not the order of original covenant. The Gentiles who received the gospel first serve as the vehicle by which the scattered natural branches come back. The first become the instrument; the last become the fruit.

The calibration of the final pruning is striking. "Ye shall not clear away the bad thereof all at once, lest the roots thereof should be too strong for the graft, and the graft thereof shall perish" (v. 65). Even in the last labor, the Lord is careful. He does not sweep away all evil immediately, because the new grafts need time to strengthen before they can withstand the removal of the branches that had been the only support. The gathering has a pace — not because God is slow, but because the newly grafted need time to draw from the root before they can stand without the crutch of the surrounding wild branches.

The end of the labor is not triumphalism. "They became like unto one body; and the fruits were equal" (v. 74). And then: "ye shall have joy with me because of the fruit of my vineyard" (v. 75). The joy is shared. The Lord does not receive the fruit alone; the servants who labored alongside him receive the joy alongside him. This is the covenant kept — not just restored fruit, but shared harvest.

## VI. Zenos and Jacob's Purpose

Nephi had already explained the allegory in outline in [1 Nephi 10:12-14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/10?lang=eng&id=p12-p14#p12) and again in [1 Nephi 15:12-18](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/15?lang=eng&id=p12-p18#p12). He understood that the house of Israel would be "compared like unto an olive tree, whose branches should be broken off and should be scattered upon all the face of the earth" and that "after the Gentiles had received the fulness of the Gospel, the natural branches of the olive tree, or the remnants of the house of Israel, should be grafted in." The allegory was not new to Jacob's family. They were living it.

Jacob's decision to read all 77 verses — to his own people, in a single address — is a pastoral act. His people are themselves scattered natural branches, planted in a nethermost part of the vineyard (the poor-soil locations of vv. 21-24 describe their own condition). They need to know their scattering is not abandonment. They need to know the root is alive. They need to know the Lord keeps coming back.

Jacob's binding question in chapter 4 was: how can Israel, having rejected the Messiah, be restored? The allegory answers it by showing that the rejection does not end the Lord's labor — it changes the strategy. The wild branches come in. The natural branches are hidden, not destroyed. The root holds. And in the last days, the hidden natural branches return to heal what the loftiness ruined. The stone that was rejected becomes the cornerstone — not despite the rejection, but through the long labor that continued after it.

---

## Becoming

The servants in Jacob 5 are few, they are called, and they labor with their might alongside the Lord of the vineyard. The study does not let me sit comfortably as an observer of the allegory. The allegory is an invitation.

The pruning instruction is specific: begin at the last that they may be first. The first-last reversal means my work in gathering belongs in the places and people I would not naturally start with — the poor soil, the mixed-fruit branch, the location where the servant says "how comest thou hither to plant this tree, or this branch of the tree? For behold, it was the poorest spot in all the land of thy vineyard." The Lord's answer is "Counsel me not" (v. 22). I know where the branches are. Go nourish.

The other thing that stays is the grief. The Lord of the vineyard grieves throughout — not once, as a crisis response, but as the sustained weight of a love that keeps returning to what is not working. That pattern is something I want to carry into my own relationships with people I am trying to reach. The labor that doesn't produce fruit is not wasted; the nourishing was real even when the fruit went wild. That may be what faithfulness looks like most of the time — pruning and digging and dunging, hoping, returning, without yet seeing the fruit.

"If ye labor with your might with me ye shall have joy in the fruit" (v. 71). The condition is the labor, not the outcome. The joy is promised at the harvest, not at each grafting. But the Lord promises it — and in v. 75, he delivers it, shared.
