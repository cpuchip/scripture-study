# The Lamanite Conversion and the Grievance Cure

**Binding question:** What broke a multi-generational grievance tradition that hatred, war, and Nephite contempt never could?

*This study builds on [mos10_lamanite-grievance-tradition.md](mos10_lamanite-grievance-tradition.md), which diagnosed the disease. Read that first. The walk notes for [Alma 17](../alma/alma-17.md) and [Alma 18](../alma/alma-18.md) are foundational to this study and not duplicated here.*

---

## I. The Wound and the Method (Alma 17)

The mos10 study traced the Lamanite tradition to its root: real events, wrong interpretation, transmitted as curriculum. "Thus they have taught their children that they should hate them, and that they should murder them" (Mosiah 10:17). Four centuries of this. Attempts to reclaim them beginning with the founding generation — Jacob reports them in Jacob 7:24 — and failing every time. By Alma 17 the text describes the target population as "a wild and a hardened and a ferocious people; a people who delighted in murdering the Nephites" (v14). That phrase comes with a footnote pointing back to Mosiah 10:12, the beginning of the grievance account. The connection is structural: the walk notes on Alma 17 already named it. The sons of Mosiah knew exactly where they were going.

What had they done to prepare? "They had waxed strong in the knowledge of the truth; for they were men of a sound understanding and they had searched the scriptures diligently, that they might know the word of God. But this is not all; they had given themselves to much prayer, and fasting; therefore they had the spirit of prophecy, and the spirit of revelation, and when they taught, they taught with power and authority of God." (vv2–3) The scriptures they searched were the records — the same records the tradition called stolen. They saturated themselves in the true account before going to the people who had inherited the false one.

Their stated objective was equally precise: they prayed "to the knowledge of the baseness of the traditions of their fathers, which were not correct" (v9). Not "bring them to Nephite culture." Not "defeat them." Dislodge a story.

The Lord's instruction for how to do it is the most striking detail in the chapter: "Go forth among the Lamanites, thy brethren, and establish my word; yet ye shall be patient in long-suffering and afflictions, that ye may show forth good examples unto them in me" (v11). Not argument. Not proof. Patience, long-suffering, good example. The grievance had been built partly on the charge that Nephites were cunning, deceptive, domineering. The remedy was to live in plain sight for fourteen years as the opposite of what the tradition said they were.

And Ammon enacts it immediately. Offered the king's daughter — offered elevation, relationship, protection — he answers: "Nay, but I will be thy servant" (v25). The son of the Nephite king becomes a Lamanite king's herdsman. The grievance ran on a story about Nephite power and Nephite theft. This Nephite chooses the lowest rung, voluntarily, and stays there. You cannot easily maintain "they lord over us" about a man watching your flocks.

The walk notes on Alma 17 have the Sebus account in full; what matters for this study is Ammon's stated motive: the joy he felt at seeing the scattered flocks was not bloodlust, but opportunity. "I will show forth my power unto these my fellow-servants, or the power which is in me, in restoring these flocks unto the king, that I may win the hearts of these my fellow-servants, that I may lead them to believe in my words." (v29) The miracle is in service of relationship; relationship is in service of belief. The arms carried in as "a testimony" (v39) open the conversation in chapter 18.

## II. The Hearer's Own Cosmology (Alma 18)

The walk notes on Alma 18 trace the pedagogy carefully. The key moves: starting in Lamoni's frame ("Believest thou that there is a Great Spirit? ... This is God"), the holy guile of securing "I will believe all thy words" before risking the content, the self-disclosure about the Spirit that gives power according to faith and desire.

But the most load-bearing thing Ammon does in that chapter — for this study's thesis — is in verses 36–38: "he began at the creation of the world, and also the creation of Adam, and told him all the things concerning the fall of man, and rehearsed and laid before him the records and the holy scriptures of the people... And he also rehearsed unto them concerning the rebellions of Laman and Lemuel." He told Lamoni the true Lehi-family history, including what Laman and Lemuel actually did. The grievance tradition (Mosiah 10) was built by the rebels' descendants. It had every reason to omit the rebellions. Ammon includes them — not as accusation, but as history, from the records the tradition said were stolen. He is laying the complete story open and letting the king read it.

Lamoni believed, and prayed "O Lord, have mercy... upon me, and my people" (18:41) — and fell.

## III. What Fell to the Earth (Alma 19)

Two days after Lamoni fell, the queen had not buried him. She had heard from servants that Ammon was "a prophet of a holy God" with power to do mighty works in God's name (v4). On that basis alone — the servants' word — she asks Ammon to come. He tells her Lamoni is not dead, but sleeps in God, and will rise on the morrow. She says: "I have had no witness save thy word, and the word of our servants; nevertheless I believe that it shall be according as thou hast said." (v9)

Ammon's response to that is remarkable: "Blessed art thou because of thy exceeding faith; I say unto thee, woman, there has not been such great faith among all the people of the Nephites." (v10) A Lamanite queen, with no personal encounter, no miracle, no scripture — only testimony — believed at a level Ammon had not seen in his own people. The grievance tradition produced, in some of the people it had shaped, a capacity to trust that Nephite culture had apparently dulled.

Lamoni rises, stretches forth his hand, and says: "As sure as thou livest, behold, I have seen my Redeemer; and he shall come forth, and be born of a woman, and he shall redeem all mankind who believe on his name." (v13) The Lamanite king, from the other side of the tradition that said the Nephites were liars, has seen the Christ the Nephites had always testified of. Then he sank again, and the queen, and Ammon, all three to the earth.

Into this scene steps Abish. She had been "converted unto the Lord for many years, on account of a remarkable vision of her father" (v16). She had never made it known. When she saw all three prostrate on the earth, she knew it was the power of God; and "supposing that this opportunity, by making known unto the people what had happened among them... would cause them to believe in the power of God, therefore she ran forth from house to house, making it known unto the people." (v17) Abish is the hidden believer becoming the public witness — a conversion that had sat silent for years, now activated by the moment. She gathers the crowd that will contend, argue, divide, and then be spoken to by a risen king.

When the crowd came together they couldn't agree on what they were seeing. Some said Ammon was the Great Spirit; others that he was a monster sent from the Nephites to torment them (vv25–26). The grievance was still active in those who hadn't been softened — the Nephite in the king's house was evidence of affliction, not of grace. Then Abish, "exceedingly sorrowful, even unto tears" at the contention (v28), touched the queen's hand. The queen rose and cried: "O blessed Jesus, who has saved me from an awful hell! O blessed God, have mercy on this people!" (v29) Not "have mercy on me." On this people. The converted do not individualize. The king rose, began to rebuke the contention, and to teach "the words which he had heard from the mouth of Ammon" (v31). The servants testified that "their hearts had been changed; that they had no more desire to do evil." (v33) A church was established.

## IV. The Over-King Confronted (Alma 20)

The encounter with Lamoni's father on the road to Middoni is the most compressed of the conversion sequences, and the most emotionally charged.

The over-king's first words about Ammon: "Whither art thou going with this Nephite, who is one of the children of a liar?" (v10) When Lamoni explains, the king's response is a summary of the grievance tradition at its most raw: "Lamoni, thou art going to deliver these Nephites, who are sons of a liar. Behold, he robbed our fathers; and now his children are also come amongst us that they may, by their cunning and their lyings, deceive us, that they again may rob us of our property." (v13) The footnote to verse 10 points directly to Mosiah 10:16 — "he robbed them." This is the tradition speaking in real time, undiluted, from the highest authority in the Lamanite world.

The over-king commands Lamoni to slay Ammon. Lamoni refuses. The king draws his sword to slay his own son. Ammon steps between them, subdues the king, holds the sword above him — and then asks not for his own life but for Lamoni's freedom and for his imprisoned brethren.

That is the moment. "When he saw that Ammon had no desire to destroy him, and when he also saw the great love he had for his son Lamoni, he was astonished exceedingly." (v26) Ammon could have killed him. He chose mercy — not because he was weak, but because mercy was all he wanted. The over-king had expected what the tradition promised: cunning, deception, theft, domination. He got something the tradition had no category for. He was "desirous to learn them" (v27), and that desire is what Aaron walks into in chapter 22.

Meanwhile: Ammon finds his imprisoned brothers naked, worn by bonds, having "suffered hunger, thirst, and all kinds of afflictions; nevertheless they were patient in all their sufferings." (v29) The Lord had commanded patience in long-suffering. The brothers had lived it literally — bound with cords, without food — and they were patient. This is not metaphor. It is the method, embodied.

## V. Aaron's Template (Alma 21–22)

Aaron's arc is the parallel track. He went first to the city of Jerusalem — a Lamanite city whose name signals how the tradition had re-rooted in place — and preached to the Amalekites, apostate Nephites who were harder than the Lamanites themselves (21:3). They mocked, argued, and threw him out. He went through Middoni, was imprisoned, was delivered by Lamoni and Ammon, and continued preaching until "they did convince many of their sins, and of the traditions of their fathers, which were not correct." (21:17) The pattern is the same as Ammon's, compressed: identify the tradition as the target, speak truth, endure rejection, continue.

Then Aaron goes to the over-king's palace. The king had already been cracked open by Ammon's mercy on the road; he had been "troubled in mind because of the generosity and the greatness of the words of thy brother Ammon" (22:3). He asks Aaron to teach. Aaron starts the same way Ammon did: "Believest thou that there is a God?" The king: "I know that the Amalekites say that there is a God... if now thou sayest there is a God, behold I will believe." (22:7) He is already poised on the edge of yes. Aaron confirms: there is a God, and he is that Great Spirit who brought your fathers out of Jerusalem.

Then the same teaching template: "Aaron did expound unto him the scriptures from the creation of Adam, laying the fall of man before him, and their carnal state and also the plan of redemption, which was prepared from the foundation of the world, through Christ, for all whosoever would believe on his name." (22:13) Creation, Fall, Redemption. In one stroke, this teaching relocates the central injury. The grievance tradition said the primary wound was Nephi usurping the birthright. Aaron says: the primary wound is the Fall, which every human being inherited, and which a Redeemer is already moving to heal. The in-group/out-group frame — Nephites wronged us, Lamanites are the true heirs — collapses. Not because it's argued down, but because a larger frame has been offered that makes it irrelevant. We are all fallen. We all need a Redeemer. There is no Lamanite exception to the Fall and no Nephite exception to the need for grace.

The over-king asked: "What shall I do that I may have this eternal life of which thou hast spoken? Yea, what shall I do that I may be born of God, having this wicked spirit rooted out of my breast, and receive his Spirit, that I may be filled with joy, that I may not be cast off at the last day? Behold, said he, I will give up all that I possess, yea, I will forsake my kingdom, that I may receive this great joy." (22:15) And then he prayed: "O God, Aaron hath told me that there is a God; and if there is a God, and if thou art God, wilt thou make thyself known unto me, and I will give away all my sins to know thee, and that I may be raised from the dead, and be saved at the last day." (22:18)

"I will give away all my sins to know thee." The give-away-all-my-sins study from this project traces that prayer's weight at length. For this study, the specific detail matters: the over-king is not asking to be absolved of minor guilt. He is the king of a people defined by the tradition. His sins include the murders the tradition authorized and the hatred it cultivated across his entire reign. Giving them away is not a transaction. It is the dismantling of a self built on a false story.

He fell, and rose, and "his whole household were converted unto the Lord." (22:23) He stood before the gathering crowd and pacified them. And then he sent a proclamation throughout all his land that the missionaries should have free access — that his people might be convinced "concerning the wicked traditions of their fathers, and that they might be convinced that they were all brethren, and that they ought not to murder, nor to plunder, nor to steal, nor to commit adultery, nor to commit any manner of wickedness." (23:3) "That they were all brethren." The proclamation names the exact cognitive reorientation the gospel had accomplished: the tradition had said the Nephites were enemies, liars, thieves. The king now says: they are brethren. The over-king's conversion changed the legal and social landscape of the entire Lamanite nation.

## VI. The Name and the Never-Fall (Alma 23)

Thousands were converted. And then the text makes a claim that has to stop you cold: "as many of the Lamanites as believed in their preaching, and were converted unto the Lord, never did fall away." (23:6)

Never. The footnote goes to Alma 27:27, and the firmness is confirmed there. This is not careless phrasing. The author is drawing a distinction, and the next verse explains it: "they did lay down the weapons of their rebellion, that they did not fight against God any more, neither against any of their brethren." (23:7) The conversion was not surface-level. Something structural had changed.

What changed? The tradition had been their identity. To be a Lamanite was to carry the grievance, to teach it to your children, to enact it in war. When the tradition was replaced — not suppressed but actually replaced, by a true account they had received and embodied — the converted had no old identity to drift back into. There was no "Lamanite way of life" they could return to while still being who they had become. The Anti-Nephi-Lehies took a new name precisely because the old one no longer fit: "they were desirous that they might have a name, that thereby they might be distinguished from their brethren... they called their names Anti-Nephi-Lehies; and they were called by this name and were no more called Lamanites." (23:16–17) The renaming is not decorative. It marks a break from the identity-container that had carried the tradition. You cannot go back to a name you've laid down.

The Amalekites and Amulonites were not converted, save only one Amalekite, and not a single Amulonite (23:14). This matters for the thesis. These were Nephite apostates — people who had the records, knew the true account, and chose rejection with full information. The tradition, for them, was a chosen weapon, not an inherited filter. The gospel that softened the Lamanites could not penetrate them. Alma 24:30 explains the principle: "after a people have been once enlightened by the Spirit of God, and have had great knowledge of things pertaining to righteousness, and then have fallen away into sin and transgression, they become more hardened." The grievance cure worked on those who held a false tradition involuntarily. It did not work on those who had chosen apostasy over truth.

## VII. The Deepest Proof (Alma 24)

The Amalekites and Amulonites, finding that the Lamanites who should have been their instrument for destroying the converted were now among the converted, stirred up the remaining Lamanites to war. The Anti-Nephi-Lehies held a council. There was not one among them who would take up arms (24:6). The king addressed them.

His speech opens with a direct naming of what had happened to them: "I thank my God, my beloved people, that our great God has in goodness sent these our brethren, the Nephites, unto us to preach unto us, and to convince us of the traditions of our wicked fathers." (24:7) He calls the missionaries "our brethren." He calls the inherited tradition "wicked." He does not soften either term. Then: "since it has been all that we could do (as we were the most lost of all mankind) to repent of all our sins and the many murders which we have committed, and to get God to take them away from our hearts, for it was all we could do to repent sufficiently before God that he would take away our stain—" (24:11)

"The most lost of all mankind." Not "we were victims of a wrong tradition." They claim both the tradition's culpability and their own. The repentance is not easy or abstract. It has cost them everything they were.

Therefore, the king says, "our swords have become bright, then let us stain our swords no more with the blood of our brethren." (24:12) The swords are an image — cleansed through the blood of the Son of God; now to be kept clean by never being used again. And then the covenantal act: "they took their swords, and all the weapons which were used for the shedding of man's blood, and they did bury them up deep in the earth." (24:17) The covenant's terms: "it being in their view a testimony to God, and also to men, that they never would use weapons again for the shedding of man's blood; and this they did, vouching and covenanting with God, that rather than shed the blood of their brethren they would give up their own lives; and rather than take away from a brother they would give unto him." (24:18)

The tradition said Nephi took. The covenant says: we will give. The grievance ran in one direction for centuries. The covenant reverses it permanently.

The Lamanite army arrived. The Anti-Nephi-Lehies "went out to meet them, and prostrated themselves before them to the earth, and began to call on the name of the Lord; and thus they were in this attitude when the Lamanites began to fall upon them, and began to slay them with the sword." (24:21) A thousand and five died without resistance, praising God even as the swords fell (24:22–23).

Then the text describes what happened in the attackers: "there were many whose hearts had swollen in them for those of their brethren who had fallen under the sword, for they repented of the things which they had done. And it came to pass that they threw down their weapons of war, and they would not take them again, for they were stung for the murders which they had committed." (24:24–25) The converted who died that day converted more through their dying than could have been reached through their fighting. "The people of God were joined that day by more than the number who had been slain." (24:26)

This is the axis of the whole study. You cannot maintain the grievance narrative — "they are bloodthirsty liars who hate us" — about people who lay down and let you kill them while praising God. The Anti-Nephi-Lehies did not argue the tradition down. They did not produce counter-evidence. They refused, physically and covenantally, to be the kind of people the tradition required its enemies to be. And that refusal was more devastating to the tradition than any Nephite army had ever been.

The text is careful here about who could and couldn't be reached: "among those who joined the people of the Lord, there were none who were Amalekites or Amulonites, or who were of the order of Nehor, but they were actual descendants of Laman and Lemuel." (24:29) The apostates watched people die rather than fight and were not moved. The descendants of the original tradition watched the same scene and threw down their weapons in anguish. The difference is not intelligence or sensitivity — it is what Isaiah 6 and the New Testament call hardness of heart: the apostates had chosen it; the Lamanites had inherited it, and it could be broken.

## VIII. The Ripple (Alma 25)

The Lamanites who had fled the scene of the dying Anti-Nephi-Lehies went and destroyed Ammonihah — the city that had burned the converts and driven Alma and Amulek out. That destruction fulfilled Alma's prophecy (16:9). Then they fought the Nephites, were driven back, and in the course of that defeat something happened: "many of them, after having suffered much loss and so many afflictions, began to be stirred up in remembrance of the words which Aaron and his brethren had preached to them in their land; therefore they began to disbelieve the traditions of their fathers, and to believe in the Lord." (25:6)

The word had been planted. Defeat and suffering turned the Lamanites' attention back to what they had heard, and what they had heard was stronger than the tradition when they were honest enough to compare them. More came over and joined the Anti-Nephi-Lehies. They too buried their weapons (25:14).

And the seed of the priests of Noah — the Amulonites who had compounded the tradition with their apostasy, who had burned the converts in Alma 25:5 — were hunted down by the very Lamanites they had incited. Abinadi's prophecy, spoken at the moment they condemned him, was fulfilled: "What ye shall do unto me shall be a type of things to come" (25:10). The ones who burned the prophet were burned. The ripple of the mission extended backward in time, fulfilling prophecy, and forward in space, converting more.

## IX. Ammon's Psalm (Alma 26)

Alma 26 is Ammon's account of the whole mission, delivered to his brothers, and it opens by answering a charge that the text has been building toward since chapter 17. When the sons of Mosiah announced their intention to go to the Lamanites, their Nephite brethren laughed: "Do ye suppose that ye can bring the Lamanites to the knowledge of the truth? Do ye suppose that ye can convince the Lamanites of the incorrectness of the traditions of their fathers, as stiffnecked a people as they are; whose hearts delight in the shedding of blood?" (26:24) The mockers also suggested the alternative: "Let us take up arms against them, that we destroy them and their iniquity out of the land." (26:25)

Ammon's answer is the whole ten-chapter record: "if we had not come up out of the land of Zarahemla, these our dearly beloved brethren, who have so dearly beloved us, would still have been racked with hatred against us, yea, and they would also have been strangers to God." (26:9) The military option the mockers proposed had been tried for centuries. It produced the tradition. The servant option, the patience option, the fourteen-years-living-among-them option — this is what "thousands" brought to the fold of God (26:4).

Aaron rebukes Ammon for boasting. Ammon answers with the cleanest possible articulation of what had powered the whole mission: "I do not boast in my own strength, nor in my own wisdom; but behold, my joy is full, yea, my heart is brim with joy, and I will rejoice in my God. Yea, I know that I am nothing; as to my strength I am weak; therefore I will not boast of myself, but I will boast of my God, for in his strength I can do all things." (26:11–12) The footnote to "in his strength I can do all things" points to Philippians 4:13 — Paul writing from prison about contentment, Ammon writing about missionary labor. The phrase carries in both directions. The posture is the same: not my strength, not my wisdom. The one who went as a servant, who won by losing, is reporting that the servant-frame was not a strategy. It was a theological statement about where power actually lives.

Ammon catalogs what the mission cost them: "we have been cast out, and mocked, and spit upon, and smote upon our cheeks; and we have been stoned, and taken and bound with strong cords, and cast into prison." (26:29) The brethren in Middoni had borne this literally. And then the summary of what the mission demonstrated: "they had rather sacrifice their lives than even to take the life of their enemy; and they have buried their weapons of war deep in the earth, because of their love towards their brethren." (26:32) The converted Lamanites had outrun even the Nephites in love: "has there been so great love in all the land? Behold, I say unto you, Nay, there has not, even among the Nephites." (26:33)

The people whose tradition had made them "the most lost of all mankind" (24:11) became, by the chapter's end, the most loving people on the continent. That is the cure.

## The Answer to the Binding Question

The mos10 study established the disease: a grievance built on real events and wrong interpretation, transmitted deliberately father to child, becoming more virulent as apostates added their full-knowledge rejection to the inherited hatred. It had been tried against by reclamation attempts from the founding generation forward. All of them failed. War never worked — it only fed the tradition more evidence that the Nephites were dangerous. Contempt never worked — it confirmed the tradition's claim that the Nephites thought themselves superior.

What worked was four interlocking things that together undid what the tradition required to survive.

First, the missionaries went as servants, not conquerors. The tradition depended on Nephites behaving in a way consistent with the grievance: superior, deceptive, dominating. A Nephite prince who chose to watch your flocks broke the type. You cannot hate "the Nephites who lord over us" about a man who said "Nay, but I will be thy servant."

Second, they bore patient love for fourteen years. The Lord's command was patience, long-suffering, good example. The missionaries were beaten, imprisoned, driven from houses, mocked. They went back. They suffered "every privation" (26:28). This is not a strategy. It is the structure of love. And love, sustained in the face of hatred without becoming hatred, is deeply disorienting to a tradition that has defined the other as the enemy. The brethren in Middoni lay naked in prison, worn by their bonds, patient — and that patience was the proof that what they said was true.

Third, the gospel reframed the origin question. Ammon, and Aaron after him, taught Creation, Fall, and Redemption — beginning at the beginning. That sequence relocates the deepest wound. The tradition said the primary injury was Nephi's usurpation in the family of Lehi. The gospel said: every person born is already in a deeper wound, the Fall, which has nothing to do with Nephi or Laman or the birthright. The Redeemer addresses that prior wound. Once a Lamanite understood this — really understood it, as the over-king did, weeping on the ground — the grievance shrank to its actual size. What Nephi took (or didn't take) from Laman matters far less than what Christ can give to both of them. The in-group/out-group frame on which the tradition depended could not survive the frame of universal human fallenness and universal divine mercy.

Fourth, and most profoundly: the converted refused to perpetuate the cycle. The tradition required a certain kind of Nephite — cunning, dangerous, worth hating — to sustain itself. The Anti-Nephi-Lehies refused to be a certain kind of Lamanite. They laid down the weapons by which the tradition had expressed and sustained itself. And when the unconverted Lamanites came to kill them, they lay down and died praising God. That act was not legible within the tradition. The tradition had no interpretation for it. The attackers "were stung for the murders which they had committed" (24:25) and threw down their own weapons. You cannot maintain the story "they are our enemies and deserve to die" about people who will literally die for you rather than harm you.

The cure was not argument. It was not superiority. It was not force. It was a group of people who loved the Lamanites enough to become servants among them, to absorb their violence without returning it, to tell them the true story including the parts the tradition had suppressed, and to embody in their own bodies — and in the bodies of the Anti-Nephi-Lehies afterward — the claim that there is a God who loves everyone in the story.

---

## Becoming

The pattern of this mission is not reassuring if you approach it looking for a shortcut. There isn't one. Fourteen years. Prison, bonds, hunger. Houses closed to them. Synagogues they were thrown out of. A city where nobody converted until they were delivered by a converted king who outranked the city's ruler.

But what the pattern is, is instructive about what actually changes people who have been shaped by a false story. It is not argument. It is not proof. It is presence — patient, costly, continued presence — combined with the truth about where the real wound is. The over-king's prayer is the model: "I will give away all my sins to know thee." Not "I will adjust my grievance." Not "I will accept that Nephi was justified." I will give away all my sins. Every one. Including the ones I inherited, including the ones the tradition told me were righteousness, including the hatred I taught my children.

The question it raises for me is not about ancient Lamanites. It's about what stories I received as identity and what it would cost to give them away. And whether I love the people in my life who carry a false story — the one that casts me as the villain — enough to stay servant-present to them for as long as it takes, without needing to win the argument, without needing them to admit I was right. Ammon didn't need Lamoni to say "Nephi was actually correct about everything." He needed Lamoni to say "I have seen my Redeemer." That's a different goal. It's reachable. But not by the methods the mockers proposed.
