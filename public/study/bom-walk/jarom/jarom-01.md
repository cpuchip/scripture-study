# Jarom 1 — "As Though He Already Was": Faith That Collapses Time

*(The whole Book of Jarom — one chapter. The Nephites keep the law, prosper, and look to the Messiah. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Jarom adds little to the record because "what could I write more than my fathers have written?" — yet he leaves one phrase that defines the Nephite faith. How did a pre-Christian people live the law of Moses *Christianly*?

**Read:** Jarom writes "a few words... that our genealogy may be kept" (v1), for "the benefit of our brethren the Lamanites" (v2 — Enos's record-covenant). He withholds his prophesying: "**what could I write more than my fathers have written? For have not they revealed the plan of salvation?**... this sufficeth me" (v2). The people are hard-hearted, "nevertheless, **God is exceedingly merciful unto them, and has not as yet swept them off**" (v3); the non-stiffnecked "have communion with the Holy Spirit" (v4). Two hundred years on, the Nephites "waxed strong... **observed to keep the law of Moses and the sabbath day holy**" (v5), prospered "in gold, and in silver... fine workmanship... buildings, and in machinery" (v8); for "**Inasmuch as ye will keep my commandments ye shall prosper in the land**" (v9). The prophets, priests, and teachers "labored diligently... teaching the law of Moses, and the intent for which it was given; **persuading them to look forward unto the Messiah, and believe in him to come as though he already was**" (v11). "By so doing they kept them from being destroyed... for they did **prick their hearts with the word**, continually stirring them up unto repentance" (v12). After 238 years (vv13), Jarom hands the plates to his son Omni (v15).

**Thoughts:**
- *"Believe in him to come as though he already was" (v11) — the heart of Nephite faith.* This single phrase explains how a people centuries before Bethlehem lived as Christians. They kept the law of Moses, but not as an end — they treated the future Christ as *present*. Faith collapsed the time gap: the Atonement, still hundreds of years off, was held as already accomplished, so they lived now in its light. This is the same forward faith that healed Enos ("faith in Christ, whom thou hast never before heard nor seen," Enos 1:8) and that Jacob named ("we knew of Christ... many hundred years before his coming," Jacob 4:4) — here distilled to its sharpest form. The not-yet Savior was, for their faith, already here.
- *The law kept with its intent (v11).* The teachers taught "the law of Moses, and **the intent for which it was given**." Not the law as ritual for its own sake — the law *understood as pointing to Christ* (the "all things typify Christ," 2 Ne 11:4; the "law... pointing our souls to him," Jacob 4:5). The Nephites at their best did not "look beyond the mark" (Jacob 4:14); they kept the law while seeing through it to the One it was for.
- *Jarom's humility, and a quiet warning (v2).* "What could I write more than my fathers have written? Have not they revealed the plan of salvation?... this sufficeth me." There is real humility here — contentment with the plain plan already given, the opposite of the obscurity-seeking Jacob warned against. But there is also a quiet diminishing: the small-plates record is thinning, each keeper writing less, the prophetic voice growing brief. The plan is enough — but the *record* of new wrestling with it is shrinking. (Omni's book will thin further.) Contentment with "enough" can be faithful (the plan is sufficient) or it can be the start of the "we have enough" drift (2 Ne 28:29). Jarom's is the faithful kind; the line is worth watching as the books shorten.
- *Mercy that has "not as yet" swept them off (v3).* The prosperity is real but conditional, and Jarom names the patience underneath it: God "has not as yet swept them off." The "not yet" is mercy buying time — the same long-suffering hand "stretched out all the day long" (Jacob 6:4). Prosperity is not approval; it is space granted by a patient God.
- *"Prick their hearts with the word" (v12).* The prophets' method is not comfort but pricking — "continually stirring them up unto repentance." The word that keeps a people from destruction is the word that disturbs them, not the word that soothes them (the inverse of the "all is well" lullaby, 2 Ne 28:21). To prick is to keep awake.
