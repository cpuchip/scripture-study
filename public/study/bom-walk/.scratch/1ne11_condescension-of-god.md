### Webster 1828 — "condescension"
Tool: mcp__webster__define

Genuine 1828 definition: "Voluntary descent from rank, dignity or just claims; relinquishment of strict right; submission to inferiors in granting requests or performing acts which strict justice does not require." (The 1828 entry then illustrates: "Raphael, amidst his tenderness, shows such a dignity and condescension in all his behavior, as are suitable to a superior nature.")

(NOTE: The phrasing "voluntary descent from one's rank or dignity in intercourse with an inferior; courtesy toward inferiors" + the standalone "Addison" attribution belong to Webster's 1913 edition, NOT 1828. Corrected to genuine 1828 above.)

**Critical observation:** The 1828 definition carries social hierarchy — a superior graciously stooping. But Nephi's angel uses it differently. The condescension isn't a posture of courtesy; it's an ontological descent. God actually becomes mortal flesh — not just polite to mortals. The word in 1828 was typically about manner; Nephi's use is about nature. This is worth flagging in the study.

**Additional observation on modern definition:** modern meaning = "patronizing attitude toward inferiors." This is almost the opposite of what the text means. In 1828 the word could be positive; today it's nearly always negative. The shift makes the phrase "condescension of God" strange to modern ears — as if God were being patronizing. The text means the opposite: God's willing self-diminishment, not God looking down his nose.

## Gap & Critical Analysis

### Strongest claims — grounded?
1. "The angel interprets the tree immediately after showing the Incarnation." ✓ — vv. 18-22 are consecutive. The interpretive move is explicitly sequential in the text.
2. "The condescension is asked about twice — vv. 16 and 26." ✓ — verbatim in text.
3. "The first condescension question (v. 16) is answered by showing Mary and the infant (vv. 18-20), then the tree interpretation is given (v. 22)." ✓ — the text structure supports this.
4. "The second condescension question (v. 26) is answered by showing the ministry, healing, and cross." ✓ — vv. 27-33 follow.

### Weakest links
- The "two movements" framing (Father's condescension / Son's condescension) is an interpretive tradition, not a direct text claim. The text says "the condescension of God" twice, not "the Father's condescension" and "the Son's condescension." I should flag this as one reading, not the only reading. The text could also be read as: one condescension (the Son's, beginning with the Incarnation), shown in two stages (birth then ministry).
- I have NOT verified the claim that "the two-condescensions reading" comes from Elder McConkie or other standard LDS commentary. I have not read those sources this session. I will present the structural observation without claiming it's the official interpretation.

### Ring check
Binding question: "What is 'the condescension of God' the angel shows Nephi, and how does it interpret the tree of life?"

Does the study answer this? Yes: the condescension is God's voluntary descent into mortal flesh — shown in two stages (birth, then ministry through cross). It interprets the tree because the angel gives that interpretation explicitly: after showing the Incarnation, the angel asks "Knowest thou the meaning of the tree?" and Nephi answers: "it is the love of God." The study can argue that the condescension IS the love of God made visible — the tree is the symbol, the condescension is the reality the symbol represents.

### Posture check
Am I discovering or confirming? Mostly discovering. The strongest finding (that v. 22's interpretation of the tree is structurally bound to the Incarnation shown in vv. 18-20, not free-floating) came from actually reading the text in sequence rather than recalling it. The Webster word-work on "condescension" was genuinely informative. The 1 Ne. 19:10 "yieldeth himself" was a discovery. I'm not forcing a thesis.

One place to watch: I came in with the assumption that there are "two condescensions" (Father and Son). The text doesn't use that language. I'll present the structural observation more carefully: the word "condescension" is used twice, and each time it's followed by a different set of scenes. The text itself makes the two-stage structure; the "Father/Son" labeling is interpretive tradition I've heard but not verified from a source read this session.

### Tensions to name
1. The word "condescension" in the text is never attributed to the Father vs. the Son. It says "the condescension of God" both times. Traditional LDS commentary (which I have NOT directly read this session) sometimes parses this as two condescensions, but the text is more unified — one condescension, shown in the full sweep from Incarnation to Atonement.
2. The love-of-God interpretation in v. 22 is Nephi's own answer — not the angel's. The angel asks, Nephi answers. This is worth noting: Nephi understands the tree before the angel completes the explanation. This suggests he understood it intuitively when he saw the Incarnation.
3. The great-and-spacious building's fall (v. 36) is somewhat disconnected from the condescension theme but part of the same vision. It deserves its own note: the pride of the world fights specifically against the apostles of the Lamb — against the witnesses of the condescension — and it falls. The condescension overcomes pride precisely by going the opposite direction.
