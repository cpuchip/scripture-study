# Ether 1 — the record within the record; the brother of Jared's intercession; the choice land

**Binding idea:** Moroni opens a second, independent witness — the Jaredite record. "I, Moroni, proceed to give an account of those ancient inhabitants who were destroyed by the hand of the Lord upon the face of this north country" (1:1), taken "from the twenty and four plates which were found by the people of Limhi" (1:2). He skips the creation-to-tower portion because it "is had among the Jews" (1:3) and gives "a part of the account... from the tower down until they were destroyed" (1:5). After the genealogy back to Jared (1:6-32), the founding story: at the great tower, when "the Lord confounded the language of the people, and swore in his wrath that they should be scattered" (1:33), Jared sends his brother — "a large and mighty man, and a man highly favored of the Lord" (1:34) — to "cry unto the Lord, that he will not confound us" (1:34), and "the Lord had compassion upon Jared; therefore he did not confound the language" (1:35). The brother of Jared cries again, for their friends (1:36-37), then inquires whether they will be driven out — "who knoweth but the Lord will carry us forth into a land which is choice above all the earth?" (1:38). The Lord answers: "I will go before thee into a land which is choice above all the lands of the earth... a great nation... because this long time ye have cried unto me" (1:42-43).

## Thoughts

The book of Ether is one of the most remarkable things in the Book of Mormon, and the first verses tell us why: it is a *second, entirely independent* witness of the same truths. The Jaredites are not Lehi's people; they are a whole separate civilization that left the Old World at the time of the great tower (Babel, c. 2200 BC by traditional reckoning), more than a millennium and a half before Lehi sailed — and they came to *this same land* and rose and fell here before the Nephites ever arrived. Moroni is abridging the record of Ether, the last Jaredite prophet, which itself was found by Limhi's lost expedition (Mosiah 8, 21) and translated by King Mosiah (Mosiah 28). So we have a record nested three deep: Moroni abridging Ether recording the Jaredites — a witness within a witness within a witness. And the structure of the opening is itself a testimony to the provenance discipline that has run through these closing books: Moroni does not duplicate what is "had among the Jews" (the creation-to-Babel account of Genesis 1-11); he gives only the part the world does not already have, "from the tower down until they were destroyed." Even in abridging, he is careful not to pad the record with what is already available — a scribe who respects both his sources and his reader's time.

That a second civilization, unknown to history, lived and died on the promised land before Lehi reframes the whole Book of Mormon's central covenant. The land "choice above all the lands of the earth" (1:42) is not a one-time promise to one family; it is a recurring covenant, offered to peoples across millennia, always on the same terms. The Jaredites receive exactly the promise Lehi would later receive — a choice land, a great nation, conditioned on faithfulness — and they will demonstrate, centuries before the Nephites, exactly the same rise and fall on the same conditions. Ether is the Book of Mormon's proof that its central pattern is not an accident of one people's history but a *law of the land*: this land is consecrated, it is given to those who serve God, and those who fill it with wickedness are swept off. The Jaredites prove the rule the Nephites would later confirm. Two civilizations, the same covenant, the same outcome — and the same warning to whatever civilization holds the land next.

The founding figure is the brother of Jared, and what he does at the tower establishes the spiritual pattern of the entire book: he *intercedes*. When the Lord confounds the languages and swears the people will be scattered, Jared turns to his brother — "a large and mighty man, and a man highly favored of the Lord" — and says, "Cry unto the Lord, that he will not confound us that we may not understand our words." Notice the relationship: Jared has the idea, but he asks his *brother* to do the praying, because the brother of Jared is the one "highly favored of the Lord," the one whose prayers reach. And the brother of Jared cries, and the result is stated with a word that will recur through this whole chapter: "the Lord *had compassion* upon Jared; therefore he did not confound the language." The language is preserved by prayer. And then the intercession widens, exactly as it did for Enos (Enos 1:9-11) and as the disciples' desire widened in 3 Nephi: the brother of Jared does not stop at saving himself and Jared — "Cry again... that he confound not their language" of *their friends*. He prays for the friends, and "the Lord had compassion upon their friends and their families also, that they were not confounded." True intercession does not stop at the self; it keeps widening outward to include others. The brother of Jared's prayer saved not only his own family's language but his friends' — the circle of mercy enlarges with the heart of the one who prays.

And the word "compassion" is the key to the whole opening (vv35, 37, 40). Three times in a few verses, the Lord's response to the brother of Jared's crying is named as *compassion*. This is the God of Ether — not a distant deity who confounds and scatters with cold sovereignty, but One who "had compassion" the moment His servant cried to Him. The same God who, in His wrath, swore the peoples would be scattered, has compassion on the one who turns to Him in the midst of that judgment. The judgment was real (the languages were confounded, the peoples scattered), but it was never the whole of God; the door to compassion stayed open for anyone who would cry. The brother of Jared discovers what the entire Book of Mormon teaches — that the God of justice is also, and at the same moment, the God of compassion, and that the way to reach the compassion is simply to cry unto Him.

The escalation of the brother of Jared's inquiries (vv38-43) is a model of how to seek guidance. He does not ask only for rescue; he asks, through Jared's prompting, a sequence of increasingly trusting questions: first, will the Lord drive us out? Then, if so, where shall we go? And then the leap of faith — "who knoweth but the Lord will carry us forth into a land which is choice above all the earth? And if it so be, *let us be faithful unto the Lord, that we may receive it for our inheritance*." The questions move from anxiety (will we be driven out?) to hope (might He lead us somewhere choice?) to commitment (let us be faithful, that we may receive it). Faith reaches past the immediate crisis toward the promise, and binds itself to faithfulness in advance of receiving. And the Lord's answer matches the reach of the asking: not merely "you will not be scattered" but "I will go before thee into a land which is choice above all the lands of the earth... a great nation... none greater." The God who had compassion on the prayer for unconfounded language now opens the whole future — a land, a nation, an inheritance.

And the reason the Lord gives for all of it, the last line of the chapter, is worth holding: "thus I will do unto thee *because this long time ye have cried unto me*" (1:43). Not because of their worthiness in the abstract, not because of their strength (though the brother of Jared was mighty), but because they had *cried unto the Lord* over a long time. Persistent prayer is what opened the choice land. The blessing is framed as the Lord's response to a people who would not stop crying unto Him — the same persistence Enos showed, the same the brother of Jared will show again in chapters 2-3 when the barges have no light. The founding lesson of Ether is laid down in its first chapter: cry unto the Lord, keep crying, widen the prayer to include others, reach in faith past the crisis toward the promise — and the God of compassion will go before you into a land choice above all the earth.
