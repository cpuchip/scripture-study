# Ether 7 — the captivity cycle begins; restoration not vengeance; the king who protects the prophets

**Binding idea:** The brother of Jared's warning begins to come true. Orihah reigns in righteousness; his son Kib reigns; but Kib's son Corihor "rebelled against his father... and took him captive, *which brought to pass the saying of the brother of Jared that they would be brought into captivity*" (7:4-5). Kib begets Shule "in his old age, while he was yet in captivity" (7:7); Shule makes steel swords, defeats Corihor, and "restored [the kingdom] unto his father Kib" (7:9) — then, when Corihor repents, "Shule gave him power in his kingdom" (7:13). The cycle repeats with Noah and Cohor (7:15-22). And when prophets come "prophesying that the wickedness and idolatry of the people was bringing a curse upon the land," and the people "revile against the prophets," King Shule "did execute a law... which gave power unto the prophets that they should go whithersoever they would; and by this cause the people were brought unto repentance" (7:23-25).

## Thoughts

The book of Ether now begins to prove the brother of Jared's foresight, and Moroni marks the moment explicitly: when Corihor rebels against his father Kib and "took him captive," it "brought to pass the saying of the brother of Jared that they would be brought into captivity" (7:5). The warning of 6:23 — "surely this thing leadeth into captivity" — is fulfilled in the very next generation but one. A king was anointed; within two generations a son raised an army against his father and imprisoned him. This is the structural danger the brother of Jared saw: concentrate the nation's power in one throne, and the throne becomes the prize men will kill for. The captivity is not incidental to the monarchy; it is the monarchy's natural fruit. And what follows in the rest of Ether is this same cycle turning over and over — rebellion, captivity, restoration, again — exactly as foretold. The prophet who warned did not stop the choice, but his word stands vindicated in the record for every reader who would consider concentrating power in one set of hands.

But within the grim cycle, the chapter holds two bright threads worth marking. The first is that *God preserves the lineage even in captivity*: "Kib dwelt in captivity... nevertheless Kib begat Shule in his old age, while he was yet in captivity" (7:7). The king is imprisoned, the kingdom usurped — and yet, in prison, the line continues, and the son born in captivity (Shule) is the one who will restore the kingdom. God's purposes are not defeated by the captivity; the deliverer is born inside the very imprisonment. The chain of the covenant continues through the dungeon. This is a quiet pattern throughout scripture — the deliverance grows up inside the bondage (Moses born under Pharaoh's death-decree, Joseph rising through the Egyptian prison) — and it offers hope to anyone in a captivity that looks total: the line is not cut; the deliverer may already be growing up in the dark.

The second bright thread is *restoration without vengeance*. When Shule defeats Corihor and could exact retribution, he instead "restored [the kingdom] unto his father Kib" (7:9) — he fought not to seize power for himself but to give it back to the rightful king. And when Corihor — the brother who had imprisoned their father — repents, "Shule gave him power in his kingdom" (7:13). The man who had every right to destroy his treacherous brother instead, upon that brother's repentance, gave him power. This is the rare interruption of the vengeance-cycle: a victor who restores rather than usurps, and who answers a repentant enemy with trust rather than retribution. It is the opposite of what destroyed the Nephites (the vengeance-oath of Mormon 3) and the opposite of what the rest of the Jaredite kings will do. Shule shows that the captivity-cycle *can* be broken, at least for a generation, by a man who refuses to take vengeance.

And the chapter's clearest doctrinal datapoint is Shule's treatment of the prophets (7:23-25). Prophets come "sent from the Lord, prophesying that the wickedness and idolatry of the people was bringing a curse upon the land," and the people "revile against the prophets, and did mock them." Here is the fork that decides a nation's fate, and Shule chooses rightly: rather than joining the mockery or silencing the prophets (as Noah did to Abinadi, as the latter Jaredite kings will do), "king Shule did execute judgment against all those who did revile against the prophets. And he did execute a law throughout all the land, which gave power unto the prophets that they should go whithersoever they would." The righteous king uses the power of the throne to *protect* the prophetic voice — to guarantee the prophets' freedom to preach anywhere, and to restrain those who would silence them. And the result is the thing that saves a people: "by this cause the people were brought unto repentance." When the prophetic voice is protected and free, the people can hear it and repent; when it is silenced (Abinadi burned, Samuel shot at, the prophets cast out), the people ripen in iniquity. Shule's law is a model of how political power ought to relate to the prophetic word: not to control it, not to silence it, but to protect its freedom — because the survival of the nation depends on the people being able to hear the call to repent. "Because the people did repent... the Lord did spare them, and they began to prosper again" (7:26). The protected prophet produces the repentant people produces the spared nation. And Shule himself "remembered the great things that the Lord had done for his fathers in bringing them across the great deep" (7:27) — the same remembering that marked Orihah; the righteous king is the one who remembers what God did for the fathers, and that memory keeps him in righteousness all his days.
