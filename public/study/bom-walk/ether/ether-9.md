# Ether 9 — the combination devours its own; Emer sees the Son of Righteousness; the famine that drives them to repent

**Binding idea:** Akish's secret combination overthrows Omer's kingdom, but "the Lord warned Omer in a dream that he should depart out of the land" (9:3) and he is preserved. The combination then consumes itself: Akish murders Jared his father-in-law, starves his own son to death, and a war between Akish and his sons lasts "unto the destruction of nearly all the people of the kingdom, yea, even all, save it were thirty souls" (9:12) — and Omer is "restored again to the land of his inheritance" (9:13). Under righteous Emer the curse is lifted and the land prospers exceedingly (9:16-19); the choice-land decree is restated — "whoso should possess the land should possess it unto the Lord, or they should be destroyed when they were ripened in iniquity" (9:20) — and Emer "saw the Son of Righteousness, and did rejoice... and died in peace" (9:22). But Heth "embrace[s] the secret plans again of old, to destroy his father" (9:26), slays him, casts out the prophets "into pits" (9:29), and the famine they warned of comes with "poisonous serpents" (9:30-31) — until the people, facing extinction, "began to repent... and cry unto the Lord" (9:34), and He sends rain.

**Read:** Ether 9 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 35 verses. The combination consuming itself, a king who saw Christ, and the famine that broke a hardened people.

## Thoughts

The chapter opens by showing what secret combinations actually *do* to the people who build them, and it is a vivid demonstration of Mormon's principle that "it is by the wicked that the wicked are punished" (Morm 4:5). Akish's combination seizes the kingdom — but then it turns on itself. Akish murders the very king (Jared) who made the deal with him; he imprisons and starves his own son to death out of jealousy; his sons buy the people's loyalty with money and make war on their father; and the war "lasted for the space of many years, yea, unto the destruction of *nearly all the people of the kingdom*, yea, even all, save it were thirty souls" (9:12). A conspiracy built to seize power ended by destroying nearly everyone, including the conspirators. This is the self-consuming nature of evil made plain: the secret combination does not deliver the power and gain it promises; it devours the people who serve it. The thirty survivors are the harvest of Akish's lust for the throne. Evil organized for power is ultimately suicidal — it turns on its own, because the same oath-bound treachery that gains the throne is available to everyone who wants it next.

And against that, the quiet counter-detail: the one man preserved is Omer, "the Lord warned Omer in a dream that he should depart" (9:3), and he obeyed and fled, and so was restored to his land when the conspirators had destroyed themselves. The faithful man who heeds the warning and removes himself from the path of the combination is the one who survives it. He did not fight the combination; he obeyed God's word to depart, and outlasted it. Sometimes the deliverance from organized evil is not confrontation but obedient withdrawal — getting out of the way at God's word, and letting the wicked consume each other.

The middle of the chapter is the recovery under Emer, and two details stand out. First, the choice-land decree is restated at the very *height* of prosperity (9:20): when the land is "exceedingly rich" with fruit, grain, silks, fine linen, gold, silver, and herds, the Lord reminds them that "whoso should possess the land should possess it unto the Lord, or they should be destroyed when they were ripened in iniquity." The reminder comes precisely when prosperity might tempt them to forget — the decree of Ether 2 spoken again at the moment of plenty, because plenty is exactly when a people stops thinking it could ever be swept off. (And the famous detail of the prosperity is the list of animals — "elephants and cureloms and cumoms" (9:19), where Joseph Smith transliterated two unknown Jaredite animal-names rather than guessing at an English equivalent. There is an honesty in that: the translator left untranslated what he did not know, rather than inventing a familiar word. A small instance of the record's own integrity — better to preserve the unknown term than to fabricate certainty.)

The second standout is Emer himself: "he even saw the Son of Righteousness, and did rejoice and glory in his day; and he died in peace" (9:22). Like the brother of Jared, this righteous king *saw the premortal Christ* — "the Son of Righteousness," the title from Malachi 4:2 (3 Ne 25:2). The brother of Jared's vision was unprecedented in *its fullness* (ch 3), but it was not the only Jaredite sighting of Christ; Emer too, generations later, saw the Son of Righteousness and "died in peace" because of it. The thread is worth marking: the God of the Jaredites was Christ, and the righteous among them saw Him. (A datapoint adjacent to the *is-jesus-christ-god* study — another Jaredite who beheld the premortal Christ, named here by the Malachi title "Son of Righteousness.")

The chapter ends in the now-familiar cycle, but with a sharp contrast to chapter 7. Where Shule had *protected* the prophets and the people repented, Heth "embrace[s] the secret plans again of old" (9:26), murders his father with his own sword, and when prophets come crying repentance, "the people believed not... but they cast them out; and some of them they cast into pits and left them to perish" — "according to the commandment of the king, Heth" (9:29). The wicked king silences the prophets; the righteous king protected them. And the consequence is exactly what the prophets warned: "a great dearth upon the land... for there was no rain," and "poisonous serpents... did poison many people" (9:30-31). The famine and serpents are the fulfillment of the very warning the people rejected — the word they cast into pits comes true upon them. And here is the hard mercy of the chapter: the affliction does what the prophets' words could not. "When the people saw that they must perish they began to repent of their iniquities and cry unto the Lord" (9:34). They would not repent at the preaching; they repented at the brink of extinction. And the Lord, the moment "they had humbled themselves *sufficiently*," sent rain and "did show forth his power unto them in preserving them from famine" (9:35). The mercy is real — the moment they turned, He turned — but it is sobering that it took the famine to turn them. The people who would not hear the prophet heard the hunger. This is the recurring tragedy and the recurring mercy: God prefers to reach a people through the prophet's word, but when they silence the prophet, He will reach them through affliction — and even then, the moment they humble themselves, He spares them. The famine was not the end of His mercy; it was the form His mercy had to take when the gentler form was cast into a pit.
