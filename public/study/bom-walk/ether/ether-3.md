# Ether 3 — "I am Jesus Christ. I am the Father and the Son": the brother of Jared within the veil

**Binding idea:** The brother of Jared answers the Lord's question (2:25) by doing the work himself: he molds "sixteen small stones... white and clear, even as transparent glass" (3:1) and carries them up Mount Shelem, asking the Lord only to do the part only God can — "touch these stones, O Lord, with thy finger... that they may shine forth in darkness" (3:4), confessing "we are unworthy before thee; because of the fall our natures have become evil continually" (3:2), yet boldly "I know, O Lord, that thou hast all power" (3:4). The Lord touches the stones, and "the veil was taken from off the eyes of the brother of Jared, and he saw the finger of the Lord; and it was as the finger of a man, like unto flesh and blood" (3:6). Because "never has man come before me with such exceeding faith" (3:9), the Lord shows Himself fully and declares: "I am he who was prepared from the foundation of the world to redeem my people. Behold, I am Jesus Christ. I am the Father and the Son" (3:14) — "this body, which ye now behold, is the body of my spirit; and man have I created after the body of my spirit" (3:16). All this, Moroni says, "that this man might know that he was God" (3:18); for "having this perfect knowledge of God, he could not be kept from within the veil" (3:20).

**Read:** Ether 3 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 28 verses. The greatest vision of faith in the Book of Mormon, and the premortal Christ's declaration of His own divinity.

## Thoughts

The chapter opens with the brother of Jared's answer to the question the Lord left open in chapter 2 — "what will ye that I should do that ye may have light?" — and his answer is the model of how faith and works meet. He does not wait passively for God to solve it; he *goes to work*. He climbs the mountain and molds, out of solid rock, sixteen small stones "white and clear, even as transparent glass." He brings God a finished proposal. But notice precisely *what* he asks God to do: not to make light from nothing, not to solve the whole problem, but to *touch* the stones he has made, to do the one thing only God can do — give them the property of shining. This is the exact division of labor that runs through all faithful action: the brother of Jared does everything within his power (mines the rock, melts it, shapes sixteen clear stones by hand), and asks God only for what is beyond human power (to make them shine in the dark). God would not do for him what he could do for himself — so he did it — and then God did the part that was His alone. Faith is not the abdication of effort; it is effort offered to God for the touch that completes it. The stones are useless until God touches them, but God will not touch stones the man was unwilling to make.

And the *posture* of the brother of Jared's prayer is worth studying as carefully as its content, because it holds two things that usually pull apart: profound humility and astonishing boldness. The humility: "do not be angry with thy servant because of his weakness before thee; for we know that thou art holy... and that we are unworthy before thee; because of the fall our natures have become evil continually" (3:2). He approaches God with no illusion about himself — fallen, weak, unworthy, his nature "evil continually." But in the same breath, the boldness: "I know, O Lord, that thou hast all power, and can do whatsoever thou wilt for the benefit of man... thou canst do this" (3:4-5). He is utterly humble about *himself* and utterly confident about *God*. These are not in tension; they are the two halves of real faith. The man who thinks well of himself will not ask boldly (he thinks he can manage), and the man who thinks poorly of God will not ask boldly either (he doubts God will). Only the one who is honest about his own nothingness *and* certain of God's omnipotence can pray as the brother of Jared prays — abased before God's holiness, fearless before God's power. His weakness is no barrier to his boldness, because his boldness rests on God, not on himself.

Then the veil parts (3:6-8), and the first surprise is not that he sees God but *what* he sees: "he saw the finger of the Lord; and it was as the finger of a man, like unto flesh and blood." He falls down in fear, and explains: "I knew not that the Lord had flesh and blood" (3:8). This is itself a revelation — the brother of Jared did not expect God to have a body in the form of man. He expected, perhaps, formless glory; what he got was a finger like a man's. And the Lord's response unlocks the whole vision: "Because of thy faith thou hast seen that I shall take upon me flesh and blood; and *never has man come before me with such exceeding faith as thou hast*; for were it not so ye could not have seen my finger" (3:9). The faith is named as *unprecedented* — no one in all the generations from Adam had come before the Lord with faith like this. And it earned a sight no one else had been given. When the Lord asks, "Sawest thou more than this?" the brother of Jared's boldness reaches its peak: having seen the finger, he asks to see *everything* — "Lord, show thyself unto me" (3:10). The man who began "we are unworthy" now asks to see God's whole self. That is not contradiction; that is faith doing what faith does — pressing past the partial revelation toward the full one.

And the Lord shows Himself, and declares the words that make this one of the most important Christological chapters in all of scripture (3:14): "Behold, I am he who was prepared from the foundation of the world to redeem my people. Behold, *I am Jesus Christ. I am the Father and the Son*. In me shall all mankind have life... and they shall become my sons and my daughters." This is the *premortal* Christ — centuries before Bethlehem — declaring His own name and nature to the brother of Jared. He names Himself "Jesus Christ" before He has been born under that name. He calls Himself "the Father and the Son" — the title Abinadi expounded (Mosiah 15:2-5): the Son by His flesh, the Father by His being the creator and by the divine investiture, the one Being in whom the Father's will and the Son's are perfectly one. And He claims the life of all mankind: "in me shall all mankind have life... and they shall become my sons and my daughters." (This is a foundational text for the question of whether Jesus is God — the premortal Lord, before His mortal birth, declares Himself God, names Himself Christ, and claims the Father-and-Son identity. It is one of the two passages — with Mosiah 15 and the larger Ether vision — that the answer rests on. Logged prominently for the *is-jesus-christ-god* study: Ether 3:14 is the premortal Christ's own self-declaration of divinity.)

And then the doctrine of the *imago Dei*, stated more directly here than anywhere else (3:15-16): "Seest thou that ye are created after mine own image? Yea, even all men were created in the beginning after mine own image. Behold, this body, which ye now behold, is the body of my spirit; and man have I created after the body of my spirit; and even as I appear unto thee to be in the spirit will I appear unto my people in the flesh." The brother of Jared is seeing Christ's *spirit body* — and that spirit body is in the form of a man, because man is created in the form of God. Genesis 1:26 ("in our image, after our likeness") is given its fullest commentary here: the likeness is real and physical; God's spirit has a body in the form of man, and man's body is patterned after it. And the same form Christ shows now in the spirit, He will show "in the flesh" — the Incarnation foreseen and promised. The vision is at once a revelation of who Christ is (God, in the form of man) and who man is (made in the form of God).

Moroni, narrating, states the purpose plainly (3:17-18): "Jesus showed himself unto this man in the spirit... even as he showed himself unto the Nephites. And he ministered unto him even as he ministered unto the Nephites; and all this, *that this man might know that he was God*." The narrator's own testimony — Jesus is God; the whole vision was given so the brother of Jared would *know* it. (Another foundational *is-jesus-christ-god* datapoint — Moroni the narrator declaring, of Jesus, "that he was God.")

But the verse that names the deepest principle is 19-20, and it is the heart of the chapter's teaching on faith: "because of the knowledge of this man he could not be kept from beholding within the veil; and he saw the finger of Jesus... and he had faith no longer, for he knew, nothing doubting. Wherefore, having this perfect knowledge of God, he could not be kept from within the veil; therefore he saw Jesus." Here is the doctrine of faith perfected into knowledge. The brother of Jared's faith was so complete — "nothing doubting" — that it ceased to be faith and *became knowledge* (the same principle Alma taught in Alma 32:34: when you have a perfect knowledge in that thing, your faith is dormant there). And the consequence is stunning: "having this perfect knowledge of God, *he could not be kept from within the veil*." The veil is not a wall God erects to keep us out; it is a function of our unbelief, and when faith becomes perfect knowledge, *the veil cannot hold*. The brother of Jared did not break through the veil by force or by special permission; he simply believed so completely that there was nothing left for the veil to do. Moroni's later commentary makes it explicit (Ether 12:19-21): it was *because* his faith had become knowledge that he could not be kept out. This reframes the whole spiritual life: the veil thins exactly to the degree that doubt dissolves, and for the one whose knowledge is perfect, the veil is simply not there. The brother of Jared shows what faith looks like at its absolute limit — faith so total it is no longer faith but sight.

The chapter closes with the sealed record and the interpreters (3:21-28). The Lord commands that the vision be written and *sealed up* "until the time cometh that I shall glorify my name in the flesh" — written in a confounded language "that they cannot be read," with "these two stones" (the interpreters, the Urim and Thummim of Mosiah 8:13 and D&C 17) sealed up alongside, so that "these stones shall magnify to the eyes of men these things which ye shall write." This is the origin of the sealed portion of the plates (2 Ne 27) and of the instruments by which Joseph Smith would translate. And the full panoramic vision is given: "he showed unto the brother of Jared all the inhabitants of the earth which had been, and also all that would be... even unto the ends of the earth" (3:25) — the same all-history vision granted to Moses (Moses 1:8), Enoch, and Nephi. And the principle that opened it (3:26): "if he would believe in him that he could show unto him all things—it should be shown unto him; therefore the Lord could not withhold anything from him, for he knew that the Lord could show him all things." The faith that God *can* show all things is the faith that unlocks all things. The brother of Jared saw everything, because he believed without limit that God was able. The chapter is, from first to last, a study in the power of faith that has no doubt left in it — faith that makes stones shine, parts the veil, sees the premortal Christ, and beholds all of history, because it simply will not doubt what God can do.
