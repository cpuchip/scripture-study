# Ether 6 — the storm that drives them home; praise all the day long; "this thing leadeth into captivity"

**Binding idea:** The shining stones are placed "one in each end" of the vessels, and "the Lord caused stones to shine in darkness, to give light unto men, women, and children, that they might not cross the great waters in darkness" (6:2-3). They board, "commending themselves unto the Lord," and "the Lord God caused that there should be a furious wind blow upon the face of the waters, *towards the promised land*" (6:5). Buried again and again "in the depths of the sea" by mountain waves, they cry to the Lord and He brings them back up — "and the wind did never cease to blow towards the promised land while they were upon the waters" (6:8). And the brother of Jared "did thank and praise the Lord all the day long; and when the night came, they did not cease to praise the Lord" (6:9). After 344 days they land, bow down, and "did shed tears of joy before the Lord, because of the multitude of his tender mercies over them" (6:12). Then the founding tension: the people ask for a king, and the brother of Jared warns, "Surely this thing leadeth into captivity" (6:23).

**Read:** Ether 6 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 30 verses. The crossing, the praise, the arrival, and the king who "leadeth into captivity."

## Thoughts

The crossing is where the faith of chapter 3 pays off, and the way it pays off is the heart of the chapter. The brother of Jared made sixteen stones; God touched them; now they are placed in the barges, and "they did give light unto the vessels." The stones the man proposed and God touched now light the way across the deep. This is the whole pattern completed: the unanswered half of the prayer (ch 2), the man's faithful proposal (ch 3), God's touch — and now, on the water, the light that carries them through. Faith's labor, offered to God and touched by Him, becomes the very light that gets a people home.

But the most striking thing in the chapter is *how God answers the crossing* (6:5-8). He does not give them calm seas. He gives them "a furious wind." They are "many times buried in the depths of the sea, because of the mountain waves which broke upon them, and also the great and terrible tempests which were caused by the fierceness of the wind." Buried — not merely tossed, *buried*, submerged under mountain waves, again and again. And yet the same wind that buries them is doing the one thing they need: "the wind did never cease to blow *towards the promised land* while they were upon the waters." This is one of the most important pictures of affliction in all of scripture. The storm that nearly drowns them is the very thing driving them home. God did not calm the wind; He *aimed* it. The furious wind, the mountain waves, the terrible tempests — the affliction itself — was not an obstacle to the journey; it was the means of it. They could not have crossed without the wind, and the wind that propelled them was the same wind that buried them. There is no other way to read it: the suffering was the vehicle. God's answer to their need for passage was not the removal of the storm but the harnessing of it toward the promise. For anyone in a tempest who wonders why God does not calm it, Ether 6 offers the hard and hopeful answer: sometimes the storm is not the enemy of your journey but its engine, and the same wind that buries you is blowing you home.

And against that backdrop — buried in the deep, tossed by mountain waves, in a sealed barge for the better part of a year — the brother of Jared's response is the thing that undoes me: "he did thank and praise the Lord all the day long; and when the night came, they did not cease to praise the Lord" (6:9). Not complaint. Not "why this storm?" Not the murmuring that Laman and Lemuel offered in their storm (1 Ne 18:9). *Praise* — all the day long, and through the night. This is the same man who, three chapters ago, was chastened for *neglecting* prayer for four years (2:14). The transformation is complete: the man who once forgot to call upon the Lord now will not stop praising Him, even buried under the waves. And the reason he can praise in the storm is that he understands what the storm is doing — it is carrying them home. Gratitude in the tempest is not denial of the tempest; it is faith that the tempest is in God's hand and aimed at the promise. The brother of Jared sang in the barge because he trusted the wind. And "they did have light continually, whether it was above the water or under the water" (6:10) — the stones God touched shone even in the depths; the light He gave did not fail when they were submerged. The light held under the waves. So does His presence.

The arrival is rendered with quiet beauty (6:12): "when they had set their feet upon the shores of the promised land they bowed themselves down upon the face of the land, and did humble themselves before the Lord, and did shed tears of joy before the Lord, because of the multitude of his tender mercies over them." Three hundred and forty-four days on the water — nearly a year, echoing Noah's flood — and the first act on solid ground is worship: bowing, humbling, weeping for joy at God's "tender mercies." The journey through the storm ends not in relief or self-congratulation but in tears of gratitude. The people who praised through the tempest now weep with thanksgiving on the shore. (The phrase "tender mercies" is the same Nephi opened his record with, 1 Ne 1:20 — the recurring Book of Mormon name for the personal kindnesses of God to those who trust Him.) And the founding generation is "taught to walk humbly before the Lord; and they were also taught from on high" (6:17) — taught directly by God, the best possible beginning for a people.

But the chapter ends on the seed of the whole Jaredite tragedy (6:19-30). The brother of Jared, grown old, gathers the people to ask what they desire before he dies, and "the people desired of them that they should anoint one of their sons to be a king over them." And the brother of Jared's response is immediate and grieved: "this was grievous unto them. And the brother of Jared said unto them: *Surely this thing leadeth into captivity*." Here is the same anti-monarchy wisdom that Samuel gave Israel (1 Sam 8:10-18) and that King Mosiah would build his entire reform on (Mosiah 29): a king concentrates power in one fallible man, and the people's liberty becomes hostage to his righteousness. The brother of Jared sees, at the very founding, where it leads — and the entire book of Ether is the proof of his foresight: the Jaredite kings will dethrone and imprison and murder one another across generations, the people repeatedly "in captivity," until the whole civilization destroys itself. He saw it coming on day one. But Jared overrules him — "suffer them that they may have a king" — and so the die is cast. There is a sober realism here: the brother of Jared, the great man of faith, could not save his people from the choice they were determined to make. He warned; they wanted a king; Jared yielded; and the warning would be vindicated across the rest of the book. (There is one bright detail: kingship is not forced. Pagag refuses, and "his father would not" constrain him, and "he commanded them that they should constrain no man to be their king," 6:25. Even the king must consent; the office cannot be imposed.) Orihah, the first king, is righteous — he "did walk humbly before the Lord, and did remember how great things the Lord had done for his father, and also taught his people" (6:30). The institution begins well, with a good man who remembers and teaches. But the warning stands over the whole book like a shadow: *this thing leadeth into captivity*. A good first king does not undo the structural danger; it only delays it. The Jaredites will spend the rest of Ether proving the brother of Jared right.

## Notable

Yes — the crossing and the founding warning. **The storm that buries them is the wind that drives them home** (6:5-8, the affliction is the vehicle, not the enemy; God aims the wind rather than calming it); **praise all the day long in the storm — gratitude in the tempest** (6:9-10, the brother of Jared sings buried in the deep; the transformed man who once neglected prayer); **the light God gives holds even under the waves** (6:10); **the journey through the storm ends in worship and tears of joy** (6:12, weeping for the multitude of His tender mercies); **"surely this thing leadeth into captivity" — the anti-monarchy warning** (6:22-23, the warning the whole book vindicates); **kingship cannot be forced** (6:25, "constrain no man to be your king"); and **a good first king delays but does not remove the structural danger** (6:28-30).
