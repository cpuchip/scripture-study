# Ether 5 — Moroni's word to the translator; the three witnesses; "ye shall know that I have authority when ye shall see me"

**Binding idea:** Moroni interrupts the Jaredite narrative to speak directly across fourteen centuries to the future translator: "I have told you the things which I have sealed up; therefore touch them not in order that ye may translate; for that thing is forbidden you, except by and by it shall be wisdom in God" (5:1). He prophesies the witnesses: "ye may be privileged that ye may show the plates unto those who shall assist to bring forth this work; and unto three shall they be shown by the power of God; wherefore they shall know of a surety that these things are true" (5:2-3). The testimony stands fourfold: "the testimony of three, and this work, in the which shall be shown forth the power of God and also his word, of which the Father, and the Son, and the Holy Ghost bear record—and all this shall stand as a testimony against the world at the last day" (5:4). And Moroni stakes his own authority on the judgment: "if I have no authority for these things, judge ye; for ye shall know that I have authority when ye shall see me, and we shall stand before God at the last day. Amen" (5:6).

## Thoughts

This short chapter is one of the most intimate moments in the Book of Mormon, because Moroni stops narrating the Jaredites and turns to speak *directly to the man who would one day translate his words* — Joseph Smith, who would not be born for fourteen hundred years. "Touch them not in order that ye may translate." The pronoun is singular and personal: *ye* who will translate. Moroni knew there would be a translator; he addressed him by his task; he gave him an instruction (leave the sealed portion alone) that Joseph would in fact obey. This is the same astonishing intimacy as Mormon 8:35 ("Jesus Christ hath shown you unto me, and I know your doing") — the writers of this record were shown the future and wrote into it, not as a message in a bottle cast at random but as a letter addressed to known recipients. Moroni is the angel who would one day stand in Joseph Smith's room and deliver these very plates; here, writing them, he speaks to that future encounter as though it were already underway. The boundary between writer and reader, between ancient and modern, is thinner in the Book of Mormon than anywhere else in scripture, because its authors were granted sight of the very people who would receive it.

The heart of the chapter is the law of witnesses (5:2-4). "Unto three shall they be shown by the power of God; wherefore they shall know of a surety that these things are true." This is the prophecy of the Three Witnesses — Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, and Martin Harris — who would be shown the plates by an angel "by the power of God" (D&C 17), and whose testimony stands at the front of every copy of the Book of Mormon. And it rests on the ancient law: "in the mouth of three witnesses shall these things be established" (Deut 19:15; 2 Cor 13:1; Matt 18:16). God does not ask the world to believe on Joseph Smith's word alone; He establishes the truth of the record in the mouth of multiple witnesses, exactly as His law requires. But notice that Moroni names *more* than three witnesses — he names a fourfold (really a sevenfold) testimony: "the testimony of three, and *this work*, in the which shall be shown forth *the power of God* and also *his word*, of which *the Father, and the Son, and the Holy Ghost bear record*." Count them: the three men, the work itself, the power of God shown in it, the word it contains, and the witness of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost. The book does not lean on the three men alone; it carries its own witness (the work itself testifies), it manifests the power of God, it contains His word, and the entire Godhead bears record of it. The three human witnesses are the legal minimum; the divine witnesses are the real foundation. (The threefold divine witness here — Father, Son, Holy Ghost, each bearing record — is the same Godhead Moroni's father confessed as "one God," now seen from the side of their unified testimony: three persons, one record borne.)

And the purpose of all this testimony is sobering: "all this shall stand as a testimony *against the world* at the last day" (5:4). The witnesses are not only for the believer's comfort; they are evidence in the final judgment. A record so abundantly witnessed — three men, the work, the power, the word, the Godhead — leaves the world without excuse. To reject a testimony established by the law of witnesses and sealed by the Godhead is to be judged by that very testimony. But the door is open: "if it so be that they repent and come unto the Father in the name of Jesus, they shall be received into the kingdom of God" (5:5). The testimony that stands *against* the world at the last day is the same testimony that, received, brings a soul *into* the kingdom. The evidence cuts both ways depending on what the reader does with it.

The closing verse is Moroni's boldest personal stake (5:6): "if I have no authority for these things, judge ye; for ye shall know that I have authority when ye shall see me, and we shall stand before God at the last day." This is not the hedging of a man unsure of his ground. Moroni puts his own authority on the table and tells the reader: you will find out whether I had authority when you *meet me* — for we will stand before God together at the judgment. It is a claim that could only be made by someone certain he will be there, certain the reader will be there, and certain the meeting will vindicate him. And for Joseph Smith, the claim was literal in a way it is for no other prophet's reader: Joseph *did* see Moroni, face to face, the angel who gave him the plates. For the rest of us it remains future — but Moroni writes as one who will personally meet every reader of his record at the bar of God and answer for what he wrote. There is a bracing accountability in that. He is not an anonymous ancient voice; he is a man who will look his readers in the eye at the judgment, and he writes knowing it. The whole chapter is the testimony of a man utterly certain of two things: that the record is true, and that he will personally stand behind it before God, with us, at the last day.
