# Ether 13 — the New Jerusalem at the moment of ruin; the prophet in the cavity of a rock; Coriantumr's refused offer

**Binding idea:** Ether prophesies the glory that will rise on this same land even as the Jaredites fall. This land "became a choice land above all other lands... wherefore the Lord would have that all men should serve him who dwell upon the face thereof" (13:2); "it was the place of the New Jerusalem, which should come down out of heaven" (13:3); "a New Jerusalem should be built up upon this land, unto the remnant of the seed of Joseph" (13:6), like "the Jerusalem of old" rebuilt for the house of Israel (13:5, 11) — both peopled by those "whose garments are white through the blood of the Lamb" (13:10-11). But "they esteemed him as naught, and cast him out; and he hid himself in the cavity of a rock by day, and by night he went forth viewing the things which should come upon the people" (13:13), making "the remainder of this record... by night" (13:14). The Lord offers through Ether: "if he would repent, and all his household, the Lord would give unto him his kingdom and spare the people— otherwise they should be destroyed, and all his household save it were himself" (13:20-21). Coriantumr "repented not," and "all the people upon the face of the land were shedding blood, and there was none to restrain them" (13:31).

## Thoughts

The placement of these prophecies is the chapter's first lesson. At the very moment the Jaredite civilization is sliding into its final, total war, Ether's recorded message is not about the destruction — it is about the *glory* that will one day rise on this same land. He prophesies "a New Jerusalem... which should come down out of heaven, and the holy sanctuary of the Lord" (13:3), "built up upon this land, unto the remnant of the seed of Joseph" (13:6); and the old Jerusalem "from whence Lehi should come," after its destruction "built up again, a holy city unto the Lord" for the house of Israel (13:5). Two holy cities — the New Jerusalem on this continent (the seed of Joseph) and the rebuilt Jerusalem of old (gathered Israel) — both peopled by those "whose garments are white through the blood of the Lamb" (13:10-11). The hope is set *deliberately against* the ruin. As one people destroys itself on this land, the prophet sees the consecrated city that will rise here in the latter days. The land's destiny is not the Jaredite catastrophe; the catastrophe is one chapter, and the land's true future is the New Jerusalem. There is a profound consolation in the timing: the darkest hour of the book contains its brightest prophecy. The Jaredites would not see it, but Ether saw it, and recorded it, so that we — the people who would one day hold this land — would know what it is ultimately *for*.

And the type Ether draws is worth pausing on (13:7): "as Joseph brought his father down into the land of Egypt, even so he died there; wherefore, the Lord brought a remnant of the seed of Joseph out of the land of Jerusalem, that he might be merciful unto the seed of Joseph that they should perish not, even as he was merciful unto the father of Joseph that he should perish not." The mercy Joseph showed his father Jacob — bringing him to Egypt to preserve him in the famine — is a *type* of the mercy God showed Joseph's seed, bringing Lehi's family out of Jerusalem to preserve them from the destruction coming on the old land. The pattern of preservation runs through the generations: Joseph saves his father; God saves Joseph's children; and the remnant of Joseph will yet build the New Jerusalem. God's mercy to the seed of Joseph is a chain reaching from Egypt to the latter day, each link a preservation of the line through which the holy city will finally come. The "first who shall be last, and last who shall be first" (13:12) — the great reversal — is the final note: the despised remnant of Joseph, scattered and broken, will be the builders of the city that comes down from heaven.

The image of Ether himself in these verses is unforgettable and deeply resonant with the close of the whole Book of Mormon (13:13-14): "they esteemed him as naught, and cast him out; and he hid himself in the cavity of a rock by day, and by night he went forth viewing the things which should come upon the people. And as he dwelt in the cavity of a rock he made the remainder of this record, viewing the destructions which came upon the people, by night." Here is the last prophet of a dying civilization, cast out, hiding in a rock, coming out under cover of darkness to *watch the destruction and write it down*. He cannot stop it — the people have rejected every word — but he can witness it truthfully and preserve the record. This is exactly Mormon's posture as the "idle witness" (Morm 3:16): when the faithful can no longer save a people, they can still bear witness, recording what happened for those who come after. Ether in the cavity of the rock, watching his world burn by night and writing it on the plates, is the perfect image of the prophet's final office — not to prevent the ruin he was unheard about, but to make sure the truth of it survives. There is a loneliness in it that aches: one man, hidden in a rock, the sole faithful witness of a civilization's self-destruction, writing in the dark. But there is also a quiet faithfulness that is its own kind of heroism — he kept recording to the end, because the record was what could reach the people the warning could not. The whole book of Ether exists because a cast-out prophet hid in a rock and wrote by night.

The final third of the chapter begins the war that will not end until everyone is dead (13:15-31), and the Lord's dealing with Coriantumr is the chapter's last doctrinal weight. Even now, with the secret combinations risen against him and the land descending into total war, the Lord sends Ether with an offer (13:20-21): "if he would repent, and all his household, the Lord would give unto him his kingdom and spare the people." The mercy is extended to the very end — repent, and not only will you be spared, but your *whole people* will be spared. The destruction is never inevitable from God's side; it is always conditional on the refusal to repent. But the alternative is laid out with terrible precision: "otherwise they should be destroyed, and all his household save it were himself. And he should only live to see the fulfilling of the prophecies... another people receiving the land for their inheritance; and Coriantumr should receive a burial by them; and every soul should be destroyed save it were Coriantumr." This is the prophecy of Coriantumr's fate — the lone survivor of a whole civilization, living only long enough to see the next people (the Mulekites/people of Zarahemla, Omni 1:21) inherit the land, and to be buried among them. It is a fate worse than death: to outlive *everyone*, to watch your entire nation destroy itself, and to die a stranger among the people who replaced you. And Coriantumr "repented not, neither his household, neither the people." The offer of salvation — for himself and for everyone — was on the table, and he chose the war instead. The chapter ends with the blood already flowing and "none to restrain them" — the point past which even God's offered mercy cannot reach a people who will not take it. The wars rage between Coriantumr and Shared back and forth across named valleys, and the only thing certain is that it will not stop until the prophecy of utter destruction is fulfilled.
