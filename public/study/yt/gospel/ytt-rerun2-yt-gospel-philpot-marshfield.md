## 3. Alignment with Canon

### A. The “Spell / Enchantment / Divination” Frame
Philpot’s core move is to demystify occult vocabulary by anchoring it to language and deception. He cites **2 Nephi 28:21** (pacifying and lulling), **2 Corinthians 3:14–4:4** (blinded minds), **2 Nephi 27:5** (spirit of deep sleep), and **Isaiah 47:9** (sorceries and enchantments). These passages *do* describe mass deception as a spiritual condition. His Webster 1828 definitions are accurate: an incantation is “the act of using certain formulas of words and ceremonies,” and a charm involves words imagined to possess “occult or unintelligible power.”

However, **the jump from “words can deceive” to “grammar is etymologically a grimoire” is illustrative, not probative.** The shared Latin root (*grammatica*) does not mean that English grammar classes are functionally equivalent to occult initiation. It is a rhetorical flourish that entertains more than it proves. The canonical passages he cites warn against *false doctrine* and *pride*, not against syntax itself.

**Verdict:** The frame is **faithful in direction** (deception is real and language-mediated) but **overstated in pitch** (ordinary education is not automatically sorcery).

### B. Citation Accuracy: 2 Thessalonians
Philpot says at **[17:29]**: “2 Thessalonians 2:1, ‘And for this cause God shall send them strong delusion, and they shall believe a lie.’”

**Verified text:** The quoted words appear in **2 Thessalonians 2:11**, not 2:1. Verse 2:1 reads: “Now we beseech you, brethren, by the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, and by our gathering together unto him.”

**Verdict:** **Sloppy citation.** In a talk about the precision of language and the danger of false meanings, misattributing a verse by ten verses is a notable lapse.

### C. Climate Science / Economics as “Divination”
At **[22:48]** Philpot asks whether climate science is divination. He defines divination via Webster 1828 as “the act of divining or foretelling future events,” then observes that scientists and economists predict the future. He concludes that “our entire society is dedicated to divination.”

**Canonical check:** Scripture condemns divination (Deuteronomy 18:10–14; Acts 19:19) as the attempt to gain hidden knowledge through supernatural or occult means—spiritism, necromancy, reading entrails. The Bible does not condemn forecasting based on observable natural patterns (farmers predict rain; Joseph predicted famine via revelation-assisted interpretation of natural signs). To equate climate modeling with biblical divination is **equivocation**: it trades on the surface similarity “predicts the future” while ignoring the scriptural category, which is about *illegitimate spiritual power*, not empirical modeling.

**Verdict:** **Strained.** The argument risks teaching listeners to distrust all secular expertise rather than to discern spirit from matter.

### D. Youth Teaching: “There Isn’t Anything” in Scripture
Philpot claims at **[26:46]** that D&C 68 is the *only* scripture on the authority to teach youth, and it assigns that authority to parents. He infers that church youth programs are therefore methodologically suspect.

**Verified text:** **D&C 68:25** does place primary responsibility on parents: “inasmuch as parents have children in Zion... that teach them not... the sin be upon the heads of the parents.”

However, the claim that there is **nothing else** is false. **Mosiah 4:14–15** commands parents to teach children. **Alma 5** describes priests teaching the people. **D&C 20** outlines the duties of teachers. More importantly, the Church’s youth organizations operate under **continuing prophetic authority**—the same authority that gave us D&C 68. An argument from silence used to undermine current Church methodology is not a scriptural argument; it is a **rhetorical wedge**.

**Verdict:** **Misleading.** Parental responsibility is real and often neglected, but using it to delegitimize organized youth teaching is an overreach.

### E. “New and Everlasting Covenant” — Only Three References?
Philpot claims there are only three references to the “new and everlasting covenant” in all scripture: D&C 22, 131, and 132.

**Search result:** The phrase or its near-equivalent also appears in **D&C 1:22** (“That mine everlasting covenant might be established”), **D&C 49:9** (“mine everlasting covenant, even that which was from the beginning”), and **Ezekiel 16:60** (“I will establish unto thee an everlasting covenant”).

**Verdict:** **Factually incorrect.** A speaker who styles himself as a precise researcher should not make easily falsifiable quantitative claims.

### F. Genesis 1 as Seven Dispensations
Philpot proposes at **[54:41]** that Genesis 1 is Adam’s prophecy of seven thousand-year periods: Day 1 = Adam/Enoch; Day 2 = Noah; Day 3 = Abraham/Moses; Day 4 = Christ; Day 5 = killing of Christ / rise of the beast; Day 6 = temples/endowment; Day 7 = rest.

**Canonical check:** **D&C 77:7** explicitly links the seven seals to seven thousand-year periods, and Joseph Smith did teach a seven-dispensation framework. However, **nowhere is Genesis 1 identified as Adam’s prophecy of those dispensations.** Philot’s mapping is entirely his own typology. He presents it as self-evident (“It’s so obvious”), but it requires multiple unstated hermeneutical moves: reading “whales” (KJV) as Hebrew *taninim* (serpents/dragons), identifying those dragons with Satan’s kingdom, and assigning exact dispensational boundaries to creation days.

His claim that Genesis 1:21’s Hebrew *taninim* prophesies Satan rising from the sea on the fifth day is **pure allegory**. The Hebrew word is indeed broader than “whales,” but identifying it with the beast of Revelation is not exegesis; it is eisegesis.

**Verdict:** **Speculative, presented as settled.** D&C 107:53 confirms Adam prophesied “whatsoever should befall his posterity,” but the content of that prophecy is not recorded as Genesis 1.

### G. Peter Walking on Water as Second-Coming Parable
Philpot asserts at **[32:02]** that Matthew 14 is “obviously a second coming parable,” with the boat as the Church, the water as the beast/dragon, and Peter as the prophet.

**Canonical check:** The New Testament gives no internal indicator that this narrative is an allegory of the last days. The “boat = Church” metaphor is common in folk exegesis and has some patristic resonance, but **no prophetic discourse in the Restoration canonizes this reading.** Jesus’s immediate concern in the passage is demonstrating his divine identity to the Twelve (“It is I; be not afraid”). To read it primarily as a coded dispensational timeline is to replace the narrative’s Christological center with a structural one.

**Verdict:** **Strained allegory.** Not heresy, but not “obvious” either.

### H. Gender, Priesthood, and the Temple
In the final segment (~**1:05:00**), Philpot argues that women cannot “have priesthood” any more than they can “have manhood” or “fatherhood,” asks “why would she want to be [a priest]?”, and proposes “priestesshood” as the female analogue.

**Canonical check:** It is true that women are not ordained to Aaronic or Melchizedek Priesthood offices. However, recent prophetic teaching has significantly clarified this territory. **Elder Dallin H. Oaks** taught in 2014 (“Keys and Authority of the Priesthood”) and again in 2019 (“Priesthood Authority in the Family and the Church”) that women exercise priesthood authority in their callings and in the temple, and that priesthood keys are exercised by women in Church councils. The temple endowment itself gives women priesthood covenants and authority. Philpot’s framing—“A woman can never be a priest any more than she can be a man”—is **technically true regarding office** but **pastorally and theologically incomplete** in light of current prophetic instruction. His rhetorical question “why would she want to be?” carries a dismissive energy that risks diminishing the dignity of women’s actual priesthood participation.

His temple-as-womb analogy is poetically interesting but **not canonically established**, and his explicit request to pause the camera because “I definitely don't want this on any video” suggests he knows the segment is pastorally volatile.

**Verdict:** **Theologically incomplete and pastorally risky.** It does not align with the most recent prophetic framing of women and priesthood authority.

## 5. Becoming — Personal Application & Stewardship

This evaluation lands on three concrete practices for future study and teaching:

1. **Test etymological claims against canonical usage.** When a speaker (including myself) links a modern word to a biblical warning via root-words, pause. Ask: *Does the Restoration canon use the concept this way, or is the connection purely linguistic?* Log the difference in margin notes. Example: “Enchantment” in scripture warns of pride and spiritual blindness (2 Ne. 28:21), not classroom syntax. Keep the two categories separate.

2. **Audit stewardship boundaries, don’t weaponize them.** D&C 68:25 is a charge to parents, not a cudgel against Church auxiliaries. In my own teaching and family settings, I will ask: *Where am I personally carrying the weight of spiritual instruction this week, and where am I outsourcing it entirely to programs?* Then act on the gap without delegitimizing institutional structures that operate under priesthood keys.

3. **Watch for the “Obviously” Rhetorical Flag.** When I encounter phrases like “It’s so obvious” or “What’s the boat? The Church,” I will recognize this as typological suggestion, not prophetic declaration. I will read it as a devotional hypothesis to be prayerfully tested, not a dispensational timeline to be adopted. If a framework requires me to dismiss secular expertise, distrust current prophetic emphasis, or diminish covenants to make it fit, it fails the fruit test. Close it, return to the canon, and proceed.
