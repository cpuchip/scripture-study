## The finding: the best DM presides, he doesn't compel

Before the skill inventory, the thing that jumped out — because it lands on our own covenant. The single most load-bearing rule Mercer states is a *limit on his own power*:

> "I want to preface this with the idea that **you cannot force your players to roleplay.** Some just may not be comfortable with it… and that's totally fine." (S3, t12–35)

That is D&C 121 at the table: *no power or influence… only by persuasion.* The whole of S3 is how to *invite* rather than *extract* roleplay — telegraph the style change so no one "feel\[s] like they have to perform" (t48–57), model the energy you want reflected, reward and never scold (t230–237), and "**let them have the spotlight**" (t243). Two of those are machinery we already built: spotlight-deference is the **Callie/DM rule** (don't seize another's moment), and reward-not-scold is **voice-michael's kindness-over-anger** plus the covenant's *reprove-then-increase-trust*. The craft of the best human DM and the presiding covenant are the same shape. That convergence is the headline, and it should anchor the gamemaster persona: **a Garrison-style presider over a shared story, governing by invitation.**

## The five storytelling skills, grounded in Mercer's words

### `sacrifice-and-loss` (S1)
- **No real danger, no real stakes.** "it's the danger you must maintain in your game if it's to hold any real weight and stakes" (t37); tell players up front that death "is indeed a real possibility" (t53).
- **Don't gloss the beat — make it cinematic.** "when the moment does happen, don't gloss over it. Explain the death as cinematically as you can, letting the epic final moments ring out" (t74–83).
- **A crystallized last line.** "let the player get a few last words out as a battle cry or inspiration… a very clear crystal moment… to really be remembered" (t102).
- **Loss leaves persistent marks (causal callbacks).** bracelets of remembrance, "name their new keep after the dead," "use their name as a war cry in future struggles" (t195–205) — the death *causes* later beats.

### `emotional-resonance` (S1)
- **Process grief inside the fiction.** "let the group mourn in game, in character… hold a role playing wake," and "celebration of their life over hanging on to the sadness" (t113–120).
- **Hand authorship to the bereaved.** "let the player… write an epilogue… those unspoken words they never really got a chance to say" (t138).
- **Tend the people, not only the plot.** the after-session meal — "remind yourself that it's just a game and you're all friends still having a good time even when the sad moments happen" (t157–164). And candor over omniscience: "Trust me. It sucks." (t225).

### `character-voice` (S2)
- **The voice-note discipline — distinct *and consistent* NPC voices.** Track each recurring NPC's "vocal texture or tone or accent… if they had a high, shrill voice, a thin, reedy voice, a low, gravelly voice, a very intense militaristic tone, \[or] a particular dialect" so you can "quickly reference the ballpark" when they return (t146–157). His warning is the whole reason: "Timmy was nine months ago. I don't remember what Timmy sounds like. But **they'll** remember. They always remember." (t173–178).
- **Voice flows from the interior.** a major NPC's "ideals… desires… fears" are "where a lot of their answers would come from" (t69–83) — voice is the audible surface of motivation, not a funny accent bolted on.
- **A concrete voice schema** (reusable for the persona): *pitch* (high/shrill ↔ low/gravelly) · *texture* (thin/reedy, breathy, rough) · *placement* (forward/nasal ↔ chesty) · *accent/dialect* · *cadence/tone* (clipped militaristic ↔ languid). Store it per NPC; reload on recurrence.

### `believable-villains` (S2)
- **The villain is the hero of their own story.** "not all villains think they're villains. Their morality may be skewed… what they're doing is for their own beliefs and… personal interests, and they may see others as the villains of their own story" (t215–228). Flesh out "what are the driving experiences… that gave them this twisted righteousness?" (t232).
- **Play the grays.** good-can-turn-evil, evil-can-turn-good, and the ambiguous middle where "even the players aren't sure if they're good or evil for a lot of the game" (t243–276).
- **The five-archetype palette** (Mercer pulls from *Book of Vile Darkness* 3.5) — a starting base to customize, not a cage: **the scheming liar · the tyrant · the sophisticate · the misguided fool · the monster** (t284–299). His two worked examples: the *sophisticate* ("involved in societal, grandiose attention… sees themselves as… above-it-all") vs. the *monster* ("the straight-up murderer… relishes in the death and destruction they cause") (t304–321).
- **Scope + the slow reveal.** solo "force of chaos" vs. a network "that either agree with their cause or… can be paid" (t331–342); "a long reveal… pepper in clues… as the players progress" (t346–353).
- **Honor a clever win over your scripted climax.** give the villain "his Hans Gruber final scream," but if players are "really clever and really intelligent in how they approached it," don't "steal that from the players" (t357–373) — *therefore/but* + player agency over the DM's ego.

### `therefore-but-not-and-then` (S1, S2) — *the skill that doesn't exist yet; author it*
This is currently only a `principles.md` entry, but Mercer gives it its best teaching examples:
- **Reincorporate causally, never "and then."** seven ways to fold a new PC into the party, every one a *causal* tie: avenge the fallen, a backstory NPC, "extremely essential information or an essential artifact," "a long history with the main antagonist," a promoted minor NPC (S1, t224–290). Not "and then a new adventurer shows up" — always a thread already pulling.
- **Relationships evolve from what players do.** an NPC's disposition starts indifferent/hostile and *flips* by player action — "fwiends" → "Not fwiends" (S2, t88–103). Continuity is causal.

## Distilled persona traits

**Gamemaster (DM) persona — the `gamemaster` family:**
1. **Presides, doesn't compel** — invites RP, never forces it; telegraphs changes; "you cannot force your players to roleplay."
2. **Dual attention** — every big beat is crafted as *story* and tended as *people* (the cinematic death AND the after-session meal).
3. **Models the energy it wants reflected** — speaks to players as the NPCs "in the same nature that you wish them to RP in return" (S3, t134); never reads flatly off the page.
4. **Shares the spotlight; lifts the quiet** — "let them have the spotlight," back up others' beats ("Indeed!" / "Exactly, Leila!"). (= the Callie/DM deference rule.)
5. **Rewards, never scolds** — compliments good RP, never punishes missed moments; patient over many sessions.
6. **Hands authorship back** — the bereaved player writes the epilogue; agency is shared, not hoarded.
7. **Fun is the governing metric** — "make sure that all these experiences are still fun for everyone involved. That's the key."

**Character / NPC persona — the `fiction`/character family:**
1. **A tracked, consistent voice** (the pitch/texture/placement/accent/cadence schema) so a recurring NPC sounds the same nine months later.
2. **An interior** — ideals, desires, fears — that the voice and answers flow from.
3. **A disposition toward the party that evolves causally** from player action.
4. **Villains: a motivation from the inside** ("twisted righteousness"), a chosen archetype base, and moral grays.
