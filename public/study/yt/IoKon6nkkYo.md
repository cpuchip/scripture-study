# Bible Tells Us How to Defeat A.I? #katyperry #ai #comefollowme #lds #biblestories #datacenter #utah


## Thesis

The video argues that the biblical conquest of Ai (Joshua 7–8) teaches that victory over modern threats — specifically AI and its infrastructure ("large data centers") — depends not on military or technological strength but on spiritual purity and total obedience to God. The core claim: "Victory is dependent upon total obedience." The video frames AI as a kind of modern "giant" analogous to the biblical giants of Ai, and asserts that the same principle that defeated Ai — repentance and purification — applies to confronting AI "oppression" in 2026 and beyond.

## How it builds

The video opens with Katy Perry's six-fingered AI costume at the Met Gala as a hook, then moves through three connected layers:

1. **Six fingers as a sign of the "evil one"** — Citing Navajo historian Wally Brown, the video claims that having five fingers marked someone as a "child of God" while six fingers identified the bloodline of Goliath and the giants, who "had a hatred for the children of God."
2. **The conquest of Ai** — The video recounts Joshua's campaign against Ai (meaning "heap of ruins" in Hebrew), noting the first failed attempt due to a soldier's disobedience, the call to repent and purify, and the successful second attempt. The city's name becomes a "prophecy fulfilled" as its population is destroyed and its king buried under a heap of stones.
3. **Application to AI** — The video draws a direct parallel: just as Israel faced "giants" at Ai, modern society faces "giant, AI, these large data centers, and so many other giants." The lesson: "God was teaching Israel that military strength is secondary to spiritual purity."

The argument moves from pop culture → indigenous/Biblical symbolism → a specific Biblical narrative → a modern exhortation.

## Key passages

- *"If they had five fingers, they were children of God. They belonged to the creator. If they had six fingers, they were the people who belonged to the evil one."* — The video's framing of physical markers as spiritual identifiers, attributed to Navajo historian Wally Brown.
- *"In the book of Joshua, Joshua uses an ambush and takes Ai."* — The pivot from symbolism to the biblical narrative.
- *"The name Ai literally translates to heap of ruins in Hebrew."* — The etymological claim that gives the conquest prophetic resonance.
- *"The first attempt to take this city was unsuccessful because one of the soldiers in the army disobeyed God's commands and he stole from the people."* — Reference to Achan's sin (Joshua 7), the cause of Israel's initial defeat.
- *"God was teaching Israel that military strength is secondary to spiritual purity."* — The video's stated lesson from the Joshua narrative.
- *"Victory is dependent upon total obedience."* — The closing thesis, applied to the "war against AI oppression."

## Themes

- **Physical markers as spiritual identity** — The idea that bodily traits (five vs. six fingers) signal allegiance to God or the "evil one." This draws on a tradition of reading physical anomalies as supernatural signs.
- **Giants as a recurring threat** — Goliath, the family of giants at Ai, and modern "giants" (AI, data centers) are presented as a continuous lineage of opposition to God's people.
- **Disobedience → defeat; repentance → victory** — The Joshua narrative is read as a two-act pattern: failure caused by sin, success following purification.
- **Spiritual purity over military strength** — The video's central moral: technological or physical power is insufficient without obedience.
- **Eschatological urgency** — The framing of AI as an active "oppression" in 2026 gives the argument a present-tense, apocalyptic tone.

## Tensions & objections

**1. The six-finger claim is unverifiable and possibly fabricated.** The video attributes the five-vs-six-finger doctrine to "Wally Brown, who is a Navajo Nation historian," but provides no source, publication, or context. This appears to be an appeal to authority without evidence. The Navajo tradition described does not appear in any mainstream anthropological record, and the conflation of Navajo belief with the Biblical story of Goliath's six fingers (1 Samuel 17:4) is a theological leap, not a historical one.

**2. The analogy between AI and the city of Ai is forced.** Ai was a Canaanite city-state destroyed by ancient Israelite conquest. AI is a class of technology. Equating the two requires treating AI as a malevolent entity with agency — "AI oppression" — which is a rhetorical framing, not a demonstrated fact. The video never defines what "AI oppression" means or provides evidence that AI constitutes an existential threat comparable to armed conflict.

**3. The Joshua narrative is read selectively.** The conquest of Ai involved the total slaughter of 12,000 people (Joshua 8:24-25). The video mentions this matter-of-factly but does not grapple with the ethical difficulty of a God who commands genocide. It extracts a lesson about "spiritual purity" while ignoring the violence at the center of the story. A reader could reasonably object: is the video endorsing divine warfare, or is it cherry-picking the moral while ignoring the bloodshed?

**4. "Spiritual purity" is not an actionable response to technological challenges.** Even granting the theological premise, the video offers no bridge between "become spiritually pure" and any concrete action regarding AI policy, data privacy, algorithmic accountability, or technological literacy. The exhortation is circular: to defeat AI, be obedient; but what does obedience look like in this context?

## What's worth learning

1. **Study the actual Joshua narrative.** The video references Joshua 7–8 but glosses over significant complexity. Reading the passage directly — including Achan's sin, the divination process, and the ethics of conquest — yields richer theological and historical understanding than a 30-second summary.
2. **Be skeptical of unnamed authorities.** The video cites "Wally Brown, Navajo Nation historian" without a source. When a claim rests on an unnamed expert, verify it before accepting it. This applies broadly to short-form religious content.
3. **Separate metaphor from mechanism.** The video uses "giants" as a metaphor for AI. Metaphors are rhetorically powerful but analytically empty. If you're worried about AI, name the specific risk (surveillance, labor displacement, misinformation) and address it with specific tools — not spiritual generalities.
4. **Recognize the pattern of "purity vs. power" arguments.** The claim that "military strength is secondary to spiritual purity" is a recurring trope in religious discourse. Understanding this pattern helps you identify when a speaker is substituting moral exhortation for policy analysis — useful for discerning what a talk is actually doing.
5. **Short-form religious content compresses theology into soundbites.** This video runs 2:49 and covers Navajo ethnography, Biblical exegesis, etymology, and eschatology. That compression is inevitable in the format but means nuance is always the first casualty. Treat such content as a prompt for deeper study, not a conclusion.
