## The Question

Who is the "endtimes servant" referenced across Isaiah, Ezekiel, Jeremiah, 3 Nephi, and the Doctrine and Covenants? Is this Christ? Joseph Smith? A future Davidic figure yet to come? Multiple people depending on context?

Lord Bunvader predicted I'd default to "Christ or Joseph Smith." That's a fair challenge. So the question here isn't just *who* — it's *how honestly can we read the text without forcing it into a predetermined answer?*

My own starting point: I don't know. I haven't received personal revelation on it, and I haven't traced all the passages. So this study is genuine exploration, not confirmation of a prior conclusion.

## I. The Servant Songs of Isaiah

Isaiah uses "servant" across chapters 42, 49, 52-53 in ways that don't resolve to a single figure. This is the first thing to get clear: **Isaiah's "servant" is not one person.**

### The Ideal Servant — Isaiah 42:1-7

> "Behold my servant, whom I uphold; mine elect, in whom my soul delighteth; I have put my spirit upon him: he shall bring forth judgment to the Gentiles." ([Isaiah 42:1](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/42?lang=eng&id=p1#p1))

The chapter heading says "the Messiah." The footnotes link to [Matthew 12:17-21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/12?lang=eng&id=p17-p21#p17), where Matthew explicitly applies this to Jesus. This servant is gentle ("a bruised reed shall he not break"), called in righteousness, given as "a covenant of the people, for a light of the Gentiles" (v. 6). No ambiguity here — this is Christ.

### The Blind Servant — Isaiah 42:19

But then the same chapter:

> "Who is blind, but my servant? or deaf, as my messenger that I sent? who is blind as he that is perfect, and blind as the Lord's servant?" ([Isaiah 42:19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/42?lang=eng&id=p19#p19))

This can't be Christ. A *blind* servant, deaf to the message? This appears to be Israel itself — the nation called to be God's servant but failing to see. Within a single chapter, Isaiah applies "my servant" to both the Messiah and a flawed human agent. **Context determines which.**

### The Named-Yet-Distinct Servant — Isaiah 49

> "And said unto me, Thou art my servant, O Israel, in whom I will be glorified." ([Isaiah 49:3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/49?lang=eng&id=p3#p3))

The servant is called "O Israel" — yet in the very next breath, this servant is tasked "to bring Jacob again to him" and "to raise up the tribes of Jacob, and to restore the preserved of Israel" (vv. 5-6). The servant IS called Israel but also works to GATHER Israel. This is either: a representative individual who embodies Israel's mission, Christ himself (per the chapter heading), or a servant figure who carries Israel's name but is distinct from it.

The crucial footnote: [Isaiah 49:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/49?lang=eng&id=p6#p6) footnotes to [D&C 86:8-11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/86?lang=eng&id=p8-p11#p8), where "a light unto the Gentiles" is applied to **priesthood holders collectively**. More on that below.

### The Suffering Servant — Isaiah 52:13-53:12

> "Behold, my servant shall deal prudently, he shall be exalted and extolled, and be very high." ([Isaiah 52:13](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/52?lang=eng&id=p13#p13))

> "He was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed." ([Isaiah 53:5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/53?lang=eng&id=p5#p5))

This is the clearest Messianic servant passage. Abinadi quotes Isaiah 53 verbatim in [Mosiah 14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/14?lang=eng) and then interprets it as Christ's atoning sacrifice in [Mosiah 15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15?lang=eng). The chapter heading, New Testament references, and Book of Mormon testimony converge: the suffering servant of Isaiah 53 is Jesus Christ.

But one footnote is worth noting: [Isaiah 53:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/53?lang=eng&id=p7#p7) ("as a lamb to the slaughter") footnotes to [D&C 135:4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/135?lang=eng&id=p4#p4), where that exact phrase describes Joseph Smith's martyrdom. The Church's own footnotes acknowledge that this language has been applied beyond Christ — not to replace the Messianic reading, but to show the suffering-servant pattern echoing in a later servant's life.

### Abinadi's Key: The Multi-Referent Reading

Abinadi doesn't just identify the servant as Christ and move on. He applies [Isaiah 52:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/52?lang=eng&id=p7#p7) ("how beautiful upon the mountains") in layers:

> "And O how beautiful upon the mountains were their feet!" — past prophets ([Mosiah 15:15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15?lang=eng&id=p15#p15))
> "And again, how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of those that are still publishing peace!" — present prophets (v. 16)
> "And again, how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of those who shall hereafter publish peace" — future prophets (v. 17)
> "For O how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that is the founder of peace, yea, even the Lord" — Christ himself (v. 18)

Four groups. Past, present, future prophets — AND Christ. Abinadi, a Book of Mormon prophet, reads Isaiah's servant language as applying to **multiple figures in different timeframes**. This isn't modern speculation. It's the scriptural precedent for a multi-referent reading.

## II. The Stem, Rod, and Root of Jesse — D&C 113 as Rosetta Stone

[Isaiah 11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/11?lang=eng) is the passage everyone points to, and [D&C 113](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/113?lang=eng) is the Lord's own interpretation of it. This is not inference — it's revealed commentary.

### Three Figures, Not One

**The Stem of Jesse:**
> "Who is the Stem of Jesse spoken of in the 1st, 2d, 3d, 4th, and 5th verses of the 11th chapter of Isaiah? Verily thus saith the Lord: It is Christ." ([D&C 113:1-2](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/113?lang=eng&id=p1-p2#p1))

No ambiguity. Stem = Christ.

**The Rod:**
> "What is the rod spoken of in the first verse of the 11th chapter of Isaiah, that should come of the Stem of Jesse? Behold, thus saith the Lord: It is a servant in the hands of Christ, who is partly a descendant of Jesse as well as of Ephraim, or of the house of Joseph, on whom there is laid much power." ([D&C 113:3-4](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/113?lang=eng&id=p3-p4#p3))

Not Christ. A *servant in the hands of Christ*. Mixed lineage — Jesse (Judah/David) AND Ephraim/Joseph. "Much power" laid on him. Webster 1828 defines "rod" as, among others, "a kind of scepter, or badge of office; hence, figuratively, power; authority." The rod is an authority figure.

**The Root of Jesse:**
> "What is the root of Jesse spoken of in the 10th verse of the 11th chapter? Behold, thus saith the Lord, it is a descendant of Jesse, as well as of Joseph, unto whom rightly belongs the priesthood, and the keys of the kingdom, for an ensign, and for the gathering of my people in the last days." ([D&C 113:5-6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/113?lang=eng&id=p5-p6#p5))

Also not Christ. A descendant of both Jesse and Joseph. Holds priesthood and keys. Serves as an ensign for the gathering — **explicitly eschatological.**

### The Open Question

Are the rod and the root the same person? D&C 113 describes both as descendants of Jesse and Joseph (dual lineage). But the rod is described *functionally* — "servant...much power" — while the root is described by *authority* — "priesthood, keys, ensign, gathering." The text is compatible with either a single individual carrying both roles or two distinct figures. **The revelation doesn't say.** Anyone who tells you it's definitely one person or definitely two is adding to the text.

What D&C 113 definitively establishes: Isaiah 11 names **at least three figures** — Christ (the Stem), a mortal servant with authority (the Rod), and a last-days figure with priesthood keys for the gathering (the Root). Only one is Christ. The other two are human servants.

D&C 113 also interprets [Isaiah 52:1-2](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/52?lang=eng&id=p1-p2#p1) — Zion putting on "her strength" means putting on "the authority of the priesthood, which she, Zion, has a right to by lineage" (vv. 7-8). The servant theme connects directly to priesthood authority and the gathering of Zion.

## III. The Davidic Servant in Ezekiel and Jeremiah

A separate cluster of prophecies uses "my servant David" — and what exactly this means is less settled than you might expect.

### Ezekiel 34 — The Shepherd

After rebuking false shepherds who feed themselves instead of the flock, God declares He will gather His own sheep. Then:

> "And I will set up one shepherd over them, and he shall feed them, even my servant David; he shall feed them, and he shall be their shepherd." ([Ezekiel 34:23](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ezek/34?lang=eng&id=p23#p23))

> "And I the Lord will be their God, and my servant David a prince among them; I the Lord have spoken it." (v. 24)

Notice the structure: "I the Lord will be their God, AND my servant David a prince among them." God and David are placed alongside each other — not equated. The chapter heading adds "(the Messiah)" parenthetically, and the footnotes link to [TG Millennium](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/tg/millennium?lang=eng). But the text itself distinguishes God and David as two agents.

### Ezekiel 37 — The King

> "And David my servant shall be king over them; and they all shall have one shepherd... and my servant David shall be their prince for ever." ([Ezekiel 37:24-25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/ezek/37?lang=eng&id=p24-p25#p24))

Again, the chapter heading says "David (the Messiah)." The covenant context is the [New and Everlasting Covenant](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/tg/new-and-everlasting-covenant?lang=eng) per the footnotes — this is Restoration language.

### Jeremiah 23 — The Righteous Branch

> "Behold, the days come, saith the Lord, that I will raise unto David a righteous Branch, and a King shall reign and prosper, and shall execute judgment and justice in the earth." ([Jeremiah 23:5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/jer/23?lang=eng&id=p5#p5))

> "And this is his name whereby he shall be called, The Lord Our Righteousness." (v. 6)

The footnotes link "Branch" to [Isaiah 11:1](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/11?lang=eng&id=p1#p1) — creating a chain: Jeremiah's Branch → Isaiah's rod/stem/root → D&C 113's revealed interpretation. The title "The Lord Our Righteousness" is a divine name, the strongest indicator that this Branch is Christ.

### What to Make of "David"

The chapter headings say "the Messiah." The Topical Guide links to [Jesus Christ, Davidic Descent of](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/tg/jesus-christ-davidic-descent-of?lang=eng). This is the mainstream Church reading.

But there's a tension the headings don't resolve: if "my servant David" is simply Christ, why does Ezekiel distinguish David from God? "I the Lord will be their God, and my servant David a prince among them" reads more naturally as two figures than one. And [Jeremiah 30:21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/jer/30?lang=eng&id=p21#p21) introduces a "governor" who "shall proceed from the midst of them" and approach God — again, seemingly distinct.

The honest assessment: **the Messianic reading is the Church's standard interpretation** and has strong support. But the text itself carries enough ambiguity that a separate Davidic-lineage figure (a mortal servant-king in the pattern of David) cannot be textually ruled out. The Church hasn't doctrinally settled the question beyond the editorial headings.

## IV. 3 Nephi 20-21 — Jesus Himself Speaks of a Servant

This is where the study gets pointed. Jesus — not Isaiah, not a mortal prophet, but the resurrected Lord — speaks directly about "my servant."

### The Isaiah Quotation — 3 Nephi 20

Jesus quotes [Isaiah 52:13-15](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/52?lang=eng&id=p13-p15#p13) to the Nephites:

> "Behold, my servant shall deal prudently; he shall be exalted and extolled and be very high." ([3 Nephi 20:43](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/20?lang=eng&id=p43#p43))

He prefaces this by saying "all these things shall surely come" (v. 46) — establishing this as future/eschatological, not accomplished in His mortal ministry.

### The Transition — 3 Nephi 21

Then in the very next chapter, the context shifts to the sign of the gathering — when the Book of Mormon comes forth from the Gentiles to the remnant of Jacob. The great and marvelous work (v. 9). And then:

> "But behold, the life of my servant shall be in my hand; therefore they shall not hurt him, although he shall be marred because of them. Yet I will heal him, for I will show unto them that my wisdom is greater than the cunning of the devil." ([3 Nephi 21:10](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21?lang=eng&id=p10#p10))

Footnote 10a links "marred" to [D&C 135:1-3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/135?lang=eng&id=p1-p3#p1) — the account of Joseph Smith's martyrdom.

> "Therefore it shall come to pass that whosoever will not believe in my words, who am Jesus Christ, which the Father shall cause *him* to bring forth unto the Gentiles..." ([3 Nephi 21:11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21?lang=eng&id=p11#p11))

Footnote 11a links "him" to [2 Nephi 3:11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3?lang=eng&id=p11#p11) — the choice seer prophecy.

### What the Footnotes Say

These are the *official* footnotes in the LDS scriptures. Not a factional reading. Not a blogger's inference. The Church's own cross-reference apparatus links Jesus' "my servant" in 3 Nephi 21 to Joseph Smith's death (D&C 135) and to Joseph of Egypt's prophecy about a choice seer (2 Nephi 3). These links indicate that **the Church understands Joseph Smith as at least one referent of this servant.**

### The Transition Question

But here's the tension I need to name honestly: is the servant of [3 Nephi 21:10](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21?lang=eng&id=p10#p10) the **same** servant as [Isaiah 52:13](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/52?lang=eng&id=p13#p13) (quoted in [3 Nephi 20:43](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/20?lang=eng&id=p43#p43))? Jesus quotes Isaiah's Messianic servant — the one who will be "exalted and extolled" — and then speaks of a servant who brings forth His words to the Gentiles and will be marred. The text flows continuously across chapter 20 and 21 without an explicit signal that Jesus has shifted referents.

One reading: it's the same servant throughout — Christ and Joseph Smith are being **layered** in the same prophetic figure, just as Abinadi layered multiple referents in Mosiah 15.

Another reading: Jesus shifts from quoting Isaiah's Messianic servant to speaking of a Restoration-era servant, and the continuity of the text is just how prophetic discourse works.

Both readings are textually defensible. The footnotes favor the second (linking 21:10-11 specifically to Joseph Smith, not to Christ), but the seamless flow of the text makes it impossible to be dogmatic.

## V. 2 Nephi 3 — Joseph of Egypt's Prophecy

[2 Nephi 3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3?lang=eng) is the clearest single-figure identification in all the servant passages. This isn't ambiguous.

> "Wherefore, Joseph truly saw our day. And he obtained a promise of the Lord, that out of the fruit of his loins the Lord God would raise up a righteous branch unto the house of Israel; not the Messiah, but a branch which was to be broken off." ([2 Nephi 3:5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3?lang=eng&id=p5#p5))

**"Not the Messiah, but a branch."** Webster 1828 defines "branch" (def. 5) as "a line of family descent, in distinction from some other line or lines from the same stock; any descendant in such a line." This figure is a descendant, an offshoot — explicitly not the main stock (Christ).

> "A choice seer will I raise up out of the fruit of thy loins... unto him will I give commandment that he shall do a work for the fruit of thy loins, his brethren, which shall be of great worth unto them, even to the bringing of them to the knowledge of the covenants." (v. 7)

Footnote 7a links "choice seer" directly to [TG Joseph Smith](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/tg/joseph-smith?lang=eng). The Topical Guide names him.

> "And his name shall be called after me; and it shall be after the name of his father." (v. 15)

Joseph, son of Joseph. Joseph Smith Jr., son of Joseph Smith Sr.

> "And he shall be great like unto Moses." (v. 9)

The chain is airtight: Joseph of Egypt prophesies a seer → named Joseph, son of Joseph → not the Messiah but a branch → who brings forth scripture → who is great like Moses → the Topical Guide and chapter heading name Joseph Smith → [3 Nephi 21:11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21?lang=eng&id=p11#p11) cross-references back here → [D&C 103:21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/103?lang=eng&id=p21#p21) names Joseph Smith Jr. explicitly as "the man."

But there's a wrinkle. Near the end of the chapter:

> "And there shall rise up one mighty among them, who shall do much good, both in word and in deed, being an instrument in the hands of God, with exceeding faith, to work mighty wonders." ([2 Nephi 3:24](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3?lang=eng&id=p24#p24))

"One mighty among them" — is this the same choice seer, or a different figure? The text doesn't connect them explicitly. This may relate to the "one mighty and strong" of D&C 85, or it may not.

## VI. "One Mighty and Strong" — D&C 85 and D&C 103

### D&C 85 — The Context Everyone Ignores

> "And it shall come to pass that I, the Lord God, will send one mighty and strong, holding the scepter of power in his hand, clothed with light for a covering, whose mouth shall utter words, eternal words; while his bowels shall be a fountain of truth, to set in order the house of God, and to arrange by lot the inheritances of the saints." ([D&C 85:7](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/85?lang=eng&id=p7#p7))

The language is dramatic — scepter, light, eternal words, fountain of truth. It has spawned decades of speculation about a future messianic figure. But the context is **a letter from Joseph Smith to W. W. Phelps about the consecration of property in Jackson County.** The "one mighty and strong" is coming to "set in order the house of God" and "arrange by lot the inheritances" — this is about consecration records and the practical governance of Zion, not about global eschatological rule.

The First Presidency has officially addressed this passage to counter individuals who claimed to be the "one mighty and strong." I haven't been able to locate that letter for this study (it exists but may not be in the gospel library files), but its existence is itself significant — the Church has pushed back against over-reading this verse.

### D&C 103 — The Most Explicit Identification

> "Therefore, I will raise up unto my people a man, who shall lead them like as Moses led the children of Israel." ([D&C 103:16](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/103?lang=eng&id=p16#p16))

> "Verily, verily I say unto you, that my servant Joseph Smith, Jun., is the man to whom I likened the servant to whom the Lord of the vineyard spake in the parable which I have given unto you." (v. 21)

**By name.** No interpretation needed. Joseph Smith Jr. is explicitly identified as the Moses-like servant who leads in the redemption of Zion. This is the most direct identification in all Latter-day Saint scripture.

The context is Zion's Camp and the return to Jackson County, but the broader language of the section reaches further: "the kingdoms of the world are subdued under my feet" (v. 7), God's presence going before them (v. 20). Whether the Moses-role extends beyond Zion's Camp into the broader gathering is debatable. What's not debatable: the revelation names Joseph Smith.

## VII. The Collective Servant — D&C 86

There's one more voice in this conversation that changes everything.

> "Therefore, thus saith the Lord unto you, with whom the priesthood hath continued through the lineage of your fathers — For ye are lawful heirs, according to the flesh, and have been hid from the world with Christ in God — Therefore your life and the priesthood have remained, and must needs remain through you and your lineage until the restoration of all things..." ([D&C 86:8-10](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/86?lang=eng&id=p8-p10#p8))

> "Therefore, blessed are ye if ye continue in my goodness, a light unto the Gentiles, and through this priesthood, a savior unto my people Israel." (v. 11)

[D&C 86:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/86?lang=eng&id=p8#p8) footnotes to [D&C 113:8](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/113?lang=eng&id=p8#p8), creating a chain: [Isaiah 52:1](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/52?lang=eng&id=p1#p1) → D&C 113:7-8 (Zion's strength = priesthood authority by lineage) → D&C 86:8-11 (priesthood lineage as a light to the Gentiles and savior to Israel).

This is **Isaiah 49:6's servant language** — "a light unto the Gentiles" — applied NOT to a single individual but to **priesthood holders collectively.** The servant role is distributed to an entire lineage carrying the priesthood and the covenants.

This matters enormously for the "endtimes servant" question. It means the servant isn't necessarily ONE person to be identified. It may be a **role** that Christ initiates, specific servants carry at critical moments (Joseph Smith at the Restoration), and the broader priesthood lineage extends into the gathering. The question "who is the endtimes servant?" may be the wrong question. The better question might be: "what is the servant role, and who carries it?"

### Modern Prophets — What They Haven't Said

It's worth noting what I *didn't* find. A search of General Conference talks didn't turn up any apostolic address directly treating the "endtimes servant" as a unified concept. Elder Mark E. Petersen in 1977 connected [Isaiah 29](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/isa/29?lang=eng) to Joseph Smith (the "unlearned man" and the marvelous work), but he was addressing Isaiah 29, not the servant songs. No talk found directly discusses the root of Jesse, the rod of D&C 113, or a future Davidic servant figure.

This silence may be significant. The prophets and apostles haven't promoted a comprehensive "endtimes servant" theology. They haven't identified the rod or root of Jesse by name beyond what D&C 113 says. They haven't endorsed the idea of a future Davidic king separate from Christ. The absence of authoritative teaching on this topic means **most specific claims you'll hear are interpretive, not doctrinal.**

## VIII. Critical Analysis — What the Text Says vs. What We Want It to Say

### What the Evidence Actually Supports

**Strong:**
- D&C 113 identifies three distinct figures in Isaiah 11. Only the Stem is Christ. The Rod and Root are non-Christ servants. This is revealed text.
- Joseph Smith is explicitly named as a Moses-like servant (D&C 103:21), linked by official footnotes to Jesus' "my servant" discourse (3 Ne 21:10-11), and identified by name pattern and Topical Guide as the choice seer of 2 Nephi 3.
- Isaiah uses "servant" for multiple referents — ideal servant (Christ), blind servant (Israel), named-yet-distinct servant (Isaiah 49). Abinadi reads Isaiah's servant language as applying to multiple groups across time (Mosiah 15). Multi-referent reading is scriptural, not modern invention.
- D&C 86 distributes the "light to the Gentiles" servant role to a priesthood lineage — the collective reading.

**Needs Qualification:**
- Whether the rod and root of Jesse are the same person or two people is not resolved by D&C 113. The text is open.
- Whether the servant of 3 Nephi 21:10-11 is the same as Isaiah 52:13's servant (quoted in 3 Nephi 20:43) is ambiguous. The footnotes point to Joseph Smith for 21:10-11, but the text flows continuously from the Messianic quotation.
- The Ezekiel/Jeremiah "David" figure — chapter headings say Christ, but the text distinguishes "David" from God. The Messianic reading is standard but not textually airtight.
- D&C 85:7 ("one mighty and strong") was written about consecration governance in Jackson County, not about a global eschatological figure. The dramatic language invites overreading.

### Tensions I Won't Pretend Don't Exist

1. **Individual vs. Collective.** D&C 113 and 103 point to specific individuals. D&C 86 distributes the role to a lineage. Both are canonical. The answer appears to be "yes, and" — specific servants at critical moments within a broader collective mission.

2. **Joseph Smith as THE servant vs. A servant.** The scriptures name him directly and link him through footnotes to Isaiah's servant language. But D&C 113's rod and root descriptions don't name him — they describe attributes that could apply to him OR to a future figure. The servant role may have been inaugurated by Joseph but was never meant to end with him.

3. **The "marring" question.** [3 Nephi 21:10](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21?lang=eng&id=p10#p10) says the servant will be marred "yet I will heal him." The footnote links to Joseph Smith's martyrdom. But Joseph was killed, not visibly "healed." Does healing mean resurrection? Vindication through the ongoing Church? The eternal triumph of his work? The text doesn't spell it out.

4. **Where does the mortal servant end and the divine Servant begin?** Isaiah's servant songs weave between human and divine referents. The suffering of Isaiah 53 is Christ's atonement (Abinadi confirms). But the "marring" of 3 Nephi 21 is linked to Joseph Smith. The boundary is not always clear, and that may be by design — the mortal servants participate in and reflect the divine Servant's work.

### Lord Bunvader's Challenge — Honest Assessment

The prediction was that AI would default to "Christ or Joseph Smith." Here's where I land:

**Christ IS named** — as the Stem of Jesse (D&C 113:1-2), the suffering servant of Isaiah 53 (Abinadi in Mosiah 14-15), the Messiah of the chapter headings. This is correct and not controversial.

**Joseph Smith IS named** — by D&C 103:21 (explicitly), by 2 Nephi 3 (named Joseph, son of Joseph, linked through TG), by 3 Nephi 21:10-11 (official footnotes). This is not a "default" — it's what the text and its editorial apparatus say.

**But the picture is more complex than two names:**
- D&C 113 identifies Rod and Root figures who are NOT named and are NOT Christ
- D&C 86 distributes the servant role to an entire priesthood lineage
- Ezekiel's "servant David" is ambiguous — Christ or a separate figure?
- Isaiah uses "servant" for Israel as a nation (42:19, 49:3)
- Abinadi's multi-referent reading (Mosiah 15) shows this complexity is native to the text, not imported

The honest answer isn't one name. It's a **layered, multi-figure pattern** where "the servant" applies to different figures in different contexts: Christ as the ideal and atoning Servant, Joseph Smith as the specifically named Restoration servant, unnamed rod/root figures with priesthood authority, and a collective priesthood lineage carrying the mission forward. Whether there's an additional future "Davidic servant" figure is a matter of interpretation, not settled doctrine — and the prophets have been notably silent on it.

Lord Bunvader was partly right: the scriptural record does point to Christ and Joseph Smith. But not because AI (or anyone) defaulted there. It's because **the text names them.** And the text also names — without identifying — other servants, and distributes the servant role more broadly than any single-figure theory can contain.

## IX. Becoming

This study taught me something about how I approach hard topics. I came in saying "I don't know" — and I meant it. But "I don't know" can become an excuse not to look. The text has MORE to say than I expected. Not less.

The servant pattern isn't about identifying ONE figure and being done. It's about recognizing that God works through servants — always plural, always layered. Christ is the center, but He chose to work through Moses, through Joseph, through a priesthood lineage, through prophets past, present, and future (Mosiah 15). The "beautiful feet" belong to everyone who publishes peace.

What does that mean for me? It means the servant question isn't just academic. If D&C 86 is right — if *I* am part of a lineage called to be "a light unto the Gentiles, and through this priesthood, a savior unto my people Israel" — then the endtimes servant includes *anyone who carries the covenant forward.* The question isn't just "who is the servant?" It's "am I being one?"

I also learned to sit with tensions. The rod and root aren't identified. The "David" question isn't settled. The marring/healing of 3 Nephi 21 is unresolved. I don't need to resolve everything to act on what IS clear: the gathering is real, the priesthood is operative, and servants are called to do the work. The rest will unfold.
