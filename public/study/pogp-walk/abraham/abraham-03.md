# Abraham 3 — the order of the stars is a parable of the order of souls

**Binding idea:** God shows Abraham the graded cosmos — Kolob governing down through "one planet above another" to the earth (3:9) — and then makes the move the whole chapter was built for: "**as, also**, if there be two spirits, and one shall be more intelligent than the other… I am the Lord thy God, I am more intelligent than they all" (3:18-19). The astronomy is a teaching method. The hierarchy of stars is a visible parable of the hierarchy of intelligences, and at the top of both stands God — nearest to the throne, greatest of minds. Then the council, shown from the *top* this time: "We will go down… and we will prove them" (3:24-25).

**Read:** By the Urim and Thummim, Abraham sees "the stars, that they were very great, and that one of them was nearest unto the throne of God" — "Kolob… because it is near unto me… I have set this one to govern all those which belong to the same order as that upon which thou standest" (3:2-3). One Kolob-revolution is "a day unto the Lord… one thousand years" of earth-time (3:4). The Lord teaches a graded order: "there shall be the reckoning of the time of one planet above another, until thou come nigh unto Kolob" (3:9), and gives the logical form: "If two things exist, and there be one above the other, there shall be greater things above them; therefore Kolob is the greatest" (3:16). Then He applies it: "**as, also**, if there be two spirits, and one shall be more intelligent than the other, yet these two spirits… have no beginning; they existed before, they shall have no end, they shall exist after, for they are gnolaum, or eternal" (3:18); "there shall be another more intelligent than they; I am the Lord thy God, I am more intelligent than they all… I dwell in the midst of them all" (3:19-21). The Lord shows Abraham "the intelligences that were organized before the world was; and among all these there were many of the noble and great ones" — "Abraham, thou art one of them; thou wast chosen before thou wast born" (3:22-23). Then the council: "there stood one among them that was like unto God, and he said… We will go down… and we will make an earth whereon these may dwell; and we will prove them herewith, to see if they will do all things whatsoever the Lord their God shall command them" (3:24-25); "they who keep their first estate shall be added upon… and they who keep their second estate shall have glory added upon their heads for ever and ever" (3:26). "And the Lord said: Whom shall I send? And one answered like unto the Son of Man: Here am I, send me. And another answered… Here am I, send me. And the Lord said: I will send the first. And the second was angry, and kept not his first estate; and… many followed after him" (3:27-28).

**Thoughts:**
- *The astronomy is the vehicle, not the cargo (3:16-19).* God spends fifteen verses on celestial mechanics — Kolob, governing bodies, reckonings of time, "one above another" — and then says "**as, also**" and pivots the entire apparatus onto *spirits*. The graded cosmos was a parable the whole time. The repeatable logical form — "If two things exist, and there be one above the other, there shall be greater things above them" (3:16) — is applied first to planets (→ Kolob, greatest), then to spirits (→ God, "more intelligent than they all"). The order of the heavens *is* the order of intelligences, rendered visible. God teaches Abraham astrophysics in order to teach him theology: look up at the graded stars, and understand that souls are graded too, and that at the summit of both orders is the Lord, "nearest" and "more intelligent than they all." (This is Euclidean in form — a stated logical rule applied across domains — which our `truth`/Euclid work would recognize: define the relation, then extend it. The cosmos is the diagram; the doctrine of intelligences is the theorem.)
- *Gnolaum — intelligence is the vanishing point in both directions (3:18).* "They have no beginning; they existed before, they shall have no end, they shall exist after, for they are gnolaum, or eternal." The footnote: a transliterated Hebrew word for eternal — *olam* (H5769), whose root sense is "the **vanishing point**… time out of mind (past or future)." That's precisely the doctrine: intelligence runs back past the horizon and forward past the horizon, uncreated and undying (D&C 93:29 — "intelligence… was not created or made, neither indeed can be"). This is the metaphysical floor of the whole Restoration cosmology and the answer our matter-spectrum principle names to "why does anything exist?" — *there was never nothing*; intelligences are gnolaum. The eternality isn't a divine gift to souls; it's their nature.
- *God is supreme, not solitary (3:19, 21).* "I am more intelligent than **they all**… I dwell in the midst of them all." God is the greatest *among* an order of eternal intelligences — supreme by degree, "nearest," "in the midst" — not the lone uncreated mind who made every other from nothing. This is the distinctive Restoration theism, and it's exactly parallel to the astronomy: Kolob isn't the only star, it's the *governing* one, "nearest unto the throne." God is the Kolob of intelligences. His supremacy is real and absolute in *rule* and *wisdom* ("wherein my wisdom excelleth them all," 3:21), but it is supremacy-within-an-order, not creation-of-the-order. (The matter-spectrum's resolution of the classic problems rests here: God works *within* eternal truth and eternal intelligence because He fully comprehends them, not because He invented them.)
- *Foreordination is election by character (3:22-23).* "God saw these souls that they were good… These I will make my rulers… Abraham, thou art one of them; thou wast chosen before thou wast born." The noble and great ones are chosen *because* "they were good" — premortal character is the basis. Foreordination here is not arbitrary predestination; it's God recognizing what souls already *are* and appointing them accordingly (Jer 1:5 in the footnote — "before I formed thee in the belly I knew thee"). Election by recognized goodness, not by fiat.
- *The council, from the top — completing Moses 4 (3:24-28).* Moses 4:1-2 showed this council from *below*: Satan's "send me… give me thine honor," Christ's "thy will be done." Abraham 3:24-28 shows it from *above*, God's vantage: the decision to make an earth and "prove them," the two estates, and "Whom shall I send?" The footnote on "another" (3:27) points straight to Moses 4:1-2 — the editors join the two accounts. The cross-link I flagged at Moses 4 is now closed: **Moses 4 = the rebellion seen from the bottom; Abraham 3 = the choosing seen from the top.** Together they are the full council. And the chilling detail: **both candidates say the identical words** — "Here am I, send me" (3:27). The volunteering looked the same. The difference was entirely in what followed God's choice: Christ is sent; the second, *not* chosen, "was angry, and kept not his first estate." Same offer, opposite hearts — exactly the pronoun-reading of Moses 4 (the words can match while the will diverges). Submission and rebellion sounded alike at the moment of offering; the test was acceptance of not-being-chosen.
- *"We will prove them" names the purpose of mortality (3:25).* The earth exists "to prove them herewith, to see if they will do all things whatsoever the Lord their God shall command them." From God's own mouth in the council: mortality is a *test* (the footnote → TG "Test," "Agency," "Salvation, Plan of"). The second estate is a proving ground, and "they who keep their second estate shall have glory added upon their heads for ever and ever" (3:26). This is the plan-of-salvation's stated rationale at its source — and it answers Moses 1:39 (God's work) with the *mechanism* (the proving).

**Critical analysis / tensions named:**
- *The astronomy is analogical, not a science textbook.* The "greater/slower" of 3:5 is glossed by the footnote as rotation/reckoning of time, not size; the system is about *reckonings of time* per body relative to Kolob, not a modern astrophysical model. Honest to read Abraham 3's astronomy as a *revelatory analogy* (graded order → graded intelligences) rather than a literal cosmography to defend against telescopes. The doctrine (eternal intelligences, God supreme-within-an-order, the council) does not depend on Kolob being locatable.
- *"Intelligences" — the unsettled term.* The chapter uses "intelligences" (3:21-22) and "spirits" (3:18) and "souls" (3:23) somewhat interchangeably, and later Restoration theology debates whether "intelligence" is an eternal individuated self or an eternal substance later organized into spirits. Abraham 3 doesn't settle it (it says intelligences "were organized before the world was," 3:22 — *organized*, not created). Flagging: the eternality is clear (gnolaum); the precise metaphysics of intelligence-vs-spirit is left open by the text and is a live question in our `intelligence` study, not a settled datum.
- *Foreordination ≠ predestination.* The "noble and great" / "chosen before thou wast born" language can be misread as fixing destiny. The chapter guards against it: the very next verses make mortality a *proving* (3:25) with outcomes contingent on keeping one's estate (3:26). Foreordination appoints opportunity; it does not remove the test. Naming the distinction so the election language doesn't swallow the agency the whole council was fought over.
