# Moses 7 — Zion built, and the God who weeps

**Binding idea:** The climax of the Book of Moses. Enoch builds a Zion so unified the Lord takes it up — "of one heart and one mind… no poor among them" (7:18) — and then is shown the opposite: the God of all creation, holy "from all eternity to all eternity," *weeping* over His children (7:28). Enoch's stunned question — "How is it that thou canst weep?" (7:29) — and God's answer are the most radical theology in the Pearl of Great Price: a perfect God is not impassible. He weeps *because* He loves and they refuse to love.

> **Note:** the deep treatments of Enoch's Zion, the gift of charity, and the weeping God already live in our studies **`enoch`** (Walk with Me), **`enoch-charity`** (the architecture of Zion), and **`zion-blueprint`** (Moses 7:18's pillars). This note digests the chapter and records the walk's distinct reading; it links rather than re-deriving.

**Read:** Enoch sees the Lord "face to face" (7:4) and is shown the world for generations. By "so great… faith" he leads God's people; "the earth trembled, and the mountains fled… the rivers of water were turned out of their course" (7:13, the power promised in 6:32-34, fulfilled). "The Lord came and dwelt with his people… and there was no poor among them" — "the Lord called his people Zion, because they were of one heart and one mind, and dwelt in righteousness" (7:16-18); Enoch "built a city… even Zion" (7:19), and "in process of time, was taken up into heaven" — "Behold mine abode forever" (7:21). Then Enoch sees the residue, Satan's chain veiling the earth "with darkness" (7:26) — and "the God of heaven looked upon the residue of the people, and he wept" (7:28). Enoch asks how a God "holy, and from all eternity to all eternity," numberless in His creations, "just… merciful and kind forever," can possibly weep (7:29-31). God answers: "these thy brethren… are the workmanship of mine own hands, and I gave unto them their knowledge… and in the Garden of Eden, gave I unto man his agency; and… I said… that they should love one another, and that they should choose me, their Father; but behold, they are without affection, and they hate their own blood… wherefore should not the heavens weep, seeing these shall suffer?" (7:32-37). He declares His names — "Man of Holiness is my name; Man of Counsel is my name; and Endless and Eternal is my name" (7:35) — and Enoch, catching the grief, weeps until "his heart swelled wide as eternity… and all eternity shook" (7:41). Enoch sees Noah, the flood, and pleads until "the Lord could not withhold," covenanting to "stay the floods" and preserve "a remnant" (7:50-52). He sees "the Son of Man lifted up on the cross" while "all the creations of God mourned; and the earth groaned" (7:55-56), the Restoration, the gathering "as with a flood," and "a New Jerusalem" (7:62) where "we will fall upon their necks, and they shall fall upon our necks, and we will kiss each other" (7:63). "Zion was not, for God received it up into his own bosom; and from thence went forth the saying, Zion is Fled" (7:69).

**Thoughts:**
- *Enoch's question is the classical-theism objection, and God doesn't dodge it (7:29-37).* "How is it that thou canst weep, seeing thou art holy, and from all eternity to all eternity?" — the assumption being that perfection, eternity, and omnipotence preclude being *moved*. The God of the philosophers is impassible: unchanging, untouched, beyond grief. Moses 7 demolishes that. God does not answer "I do not really weep" or "this is only metaphor." He answers with the *reason*: I made them, I gave them knowledge and agency, I commanded them to love, and they refuse — "they are without affection, and they hate their own blood." The tears are the direct cost of love freely given to free beings who won't return it. God weeps not because He is weak but because He is *love* (1 Jn 4:8) facing its own rejection. His omnipotence is stated in the same breath — "I can stretch forth mine hands and hold all the creations which I have made" (7:36) — so the weeping is not a limit on His power; it is the disposition of His power. He could hold every world in His hand and still weep over one hard heart. Power and grief, together, in one God.
- *The grief propagates — God → Enoch → the earth.* The weeping isn't God's alone. Enoch catches it: his "heart swelled wide as eternity… and all eternity shook" (7:41) — the prophet who walked with God for centuries came to feel what God feels (the thesis of `enoch-charity`). Then the *earth* weeps: "Wo, wo is me, the mother of men… When shall I rest, and be cleansed from the filthiness which is gone forth out of me?" (7:48) — the personified creation groaning (Rom 8:22, the footnote-adjacent parallel). And at Calvary, "all the creations of God mourned; and the earth groaned" (7:56). So compassion runs downward through the whole order: God weeps, the seer weeps, the planet weeps. To walk with God is, finally, to be enlarged enough to grieve with Him — charity is catching the divine tears.
- *"The Lord could not withhold" (7:51) — intercession that moves God.* Enoch pleads for Noah's seed "in the name of thine Only Begotten" and "the Lord could not withhold; and he covenanted with Enoch, and sware unto him with an oath." A God who weeps is also a God who can be *entreated* — not manipulated, but genuinely moved by a righteous plea offered in Christ's name. The impassible God cannot be petitioned (nothing reaches Him); the weeping God covenants in response. The two doctrines are one: a God who feels is a God who answers.
- *The embrace is the prodigal's father, cosmic-scale (7:63).* When the two Zions meet, "we will fall upon their necks, and they shall fall upon our necks, and we will kiss each other." That is Luke 15:20 — the father who "fell on his neck, and kissed him" — written into the end of history. The God who wept in 7:28 runs to the reunion in 7:63. The tears and the kiss are the same love at two moments.
- *"Man of Counsel" joins "Man of Holiness" (7:35).* Moses 6:57 gave the Father's name as "Man of Holiness" (whence "Son of Man"); here it expands — "Man of Holiness… Man of Counsel… Endless and Eternal." The God who takes counsel (Abr 4:26, the council) and the God who is holy are the same name. And it sits inside the weeping passage — the Man of *Counsel* is the God who weeps; His deliberative wisdom and His grief are not in tension.
- *Zion is a character, not a place (7:18-19).* "One heart and one mind… no poor among them" — the city is *called* Zion because of what the people *are*, and only then is a city built and named. The order matters (cf. `zion-blueprint`): unity and care come first; the geography is named after the condition, not the reverse. And its 365 years (7:68) — "in process of time" — is the daily-built Zion of our principles (Enoch's people achieved it one day at a time).

**Critical analysis / tensions named — including the hard one:**
- *The "blackness" of Canaan (7:8) and "the seed of Cain were black" (7:22) — flagged, not resolved.* These verses are among the hardest in the Restoration canon. 7:8 says a curse of barrenness and "a blackness came upon all the children of Canaan, that they were despised among all people"; 7:22 says "the seed of Cain were black, and had not place among" the residue. **The chapter's own footnotes on both verses point to 2 Ne 26:33** — "all are alike unto God, black and white, bond and free." So the text that records the "blackness" is editorially cross-referenced to the verse that denies God plays favorites by color. The BoM walk surfaced this exact tension repeatedly (2 Ne 5:21, Alma 3, 3 Ne 2): every *explanation* the canon gives resolves to allegiance/tradition/curse-for-rebellion ("a curse… upon all people that fought against God," 7:15), while the literal "black/blackness" phrasings remain unreconciled with the belief-allegiance framing. **This is bin-4 — for Michael and the Spirit, not the agent.** I record it as a live datapoint for the with-Michael 2 Ne 5:21 study and do not attempt to settle it. (Honesty frame: I note what the text says, note the editorial cross-reference to 2 Ne 26:33, and stop.)
- *Does a weeping God contradict an unchanging God?* The chapter holds both — "from all eternity to all eternity" (7:29) *and* weeping (7:28). The resolution our principles already carry: what is unchanging is God's *character* (love, justice, mercy), and the tears are the *expression* of that unchanging love meeting changing human refusal. God's constancy is the constancy of His love, not the absence of feeling. Flagging the apparent paradox and naming the resolution, not papering over it.
- *The flood as judgment vs. the weeping God.* The same passage has God send "the floods… for my fierce anger is kindled" (7:34) and God weeping over the very people the floods will take (7:37). The text refuses to split them: the God who judges is the God who grieves the judgment ("wherefore should not the heavens weep, seeing these shall suffer?"). Justice and sorrow in one act — the opposite of a wrathful-vs-loving-God dichotomy.
