# Moses 5 — two streams out of Adam's house: the gospel preached, and "murder and get gain"

**Binding idea:** Outside Eden, two things begin in the same family. From Adam: sacrifice that teaches Christ before Christ comes (5:6-8), Eve's declaration that the Fall opened the way to *joy* (5:10-11), and "the Gospel… preached, from the beginning" (5:58). From Cain: the first secret combination — "murder and get gain" (5:31) — the dark root the whole Book of Mormon will trace back here. The chapter is the headwaters of both the covenant and its counterfeit.

**Read:** Driven out, Adam tills the ground and Eve "did labor with him" (5:1); they have sons and daughters and are commanded to "offer the firstlings of their flocks" (5:5). When an angel asks Adam *why* he sacrifices, Adam answers, "I know not, save the Lord commanded me" (5:6), and the angel reveals it: "This thing is a similitude of the sacrifice of the Only Begotten of the Father… thou shalt do all that thou doest in the name of the Son" (5:7-8). The Holy Ghost falls; Adam blesses God: "because of my transgression my eyes are opened, and in this life I shall have joy" (5:10); and Eve, glad, declares: "Were it not for our transgression we never should have had seed, and never should have known good and evil, and the joy of our redemption, and the eternal life which God giveth unto all the obedient" (5:11). They teach their children, but Satan comes — "I am also a son of God… Believe it not" — and many "loved Satan more than God" (5:13). Then Cain: he too "loved Satan more than God" (5:18); his offering is not respected; God warns, "If thou doest well, thou shalt be accepted… sin lieth at the door, and Satan desireth to have thee… And thou shalt rule over him" (5:23). Instead Cain enters Satan's oath — "Swear unto me by thy throat" (5:29) — and proclaims, "Truly I am Mahan, the master of this great secret, that I may murder and get gain" (5:31). He kills Abel and glories: "I am free; surely the flocks of my brother falleth into my hands" (5:33). "Am I my brother's keeper?" (5:34). Cursed and marked, he goes to Nod; his line runs to Lamech, who renews the oath and kills "for the oath's sake" (5:50). "From the days of Cain, there was a secret combination… their works were in the dark" (5:51), and "thus the works of darkness began to prevail" (5:55) — yet the chapter closes on the other stream: "thus the Gospel began to be preached, from the beginning, being declared by holy angels… and by the gift of the Holy Ghost" (5:58-59).

**Thoughts:**
- *"I know not, save the Lord commanded me" (5:6) is the structure of faith, and the reward for it is understanding.* Adam had been sacrificing "many days" before the angel explained why. He obeyed without the reason; the meaning came *after*, as the fruit of obedience. This is the experiential epistemology our `truth`/Alma-32 work names — you act on the word, then you come to know. The ordinance was teaching Christ (a "similitude of the sacrifice of the Only Begotten") *the whole time*, before Adam could have named what it meant. Obedience first, comprehension second — and the comprehension is the gift, not the prerequisite.
- *The first ordinance is already Christ-centered, and so is the first gospel (5:7-9, 58).* Sacrifice doesn't start as a generic appeasement that *later* gets a messianic reading — the angel names it as a similitude of Christ from the start, and commands Adam to do everything "in the name of the Son" and to "repent and call upon God in the name of the Son forevermore." Then the Holy Ghost itself testifies: "I am the Only Begotten of the Father from the beginning" (5:9). The gospel — faith in Christ, repentance, the name of the Son, the Holy Ghost — is *Adam's*, not a later development. This is the gospel-pattern-is-eternal principle in its origin text (cf. `moses-6-gospel-to-adam`, which the next chapter feeds).
- *Eve speaks the most advanced theology in the early chapters (5:11), and it's the felix culpa.* In one sentence she names four goods that the Fall made possible: *seed* (children), *the knowledge of good and evil*, *the joy of our redemption*, and *eternal life*. This is the Restoration's whole reframe of the Fall — not a catastrophe to be merely survived but the doorway to joy (2 Ne 2:25, "Adam fell that men might be, and men are, that they might have joy"). And it is **Eve** who articulates the redemption — the woman who was "beguiled" (4:19) is the first to *understand* what was gained. Her "were it not for our transgression" turns the curse into a hinge. (Adam's joy is personal — "I shall have joy"; Eve's is doctrinal — "the joy of *our* redemption… the eternal life which God giveth unto *all* the obedient." She generalizes it to everyone.)
- *Cain invents the formula the whole Book of Mormon will quote: "murder and get gain" (5:31).* "Truly I am Mahan, the master of this great secret, that I may murder and get gain." This is the founding charter of the secret combination — and the BoM traces it back *here* explicitly (the footnote on 5:25 → Hel 6:27; 5:51 → "Secret Combinations"). Ether 8 names Cain as the source; Helaman 6 says the Gadianton oaths came "from… that same being who… did plot with Cain." So the dark thread I followed all through the BoM walk — Gadianton, the Jaredite combinations, "the Lord worketh not in secret" — has its headwaters in Moses 5:29-31. The mechanism is identical across four thousand years: a secret oath, sworn by death, to murder for gain, "kept… in the dark."
- *Cain calls murder "freedom" — the lie that sin liberates (5:33).* "I am free; surely the flocks of my brother falleth into my hands." He has just bound himself to Satan by oath (5:30) and to murder, and he names it *freedom*. It's the exact inversion of the chapter's true freedom (agency rightly used, 3:17). Sin's signature lie is that the chain is liberty — Cain "gloried in his wickedness" (5:31). Set against Eve's "the joy of our redemption," the contrast is total: real joy comes through the Fall's redemption; Cain's "freedom" is the bondage that follows the oath.
- *The daughters break the secret (5:53).* A quiet, easily-missed reversal: Lamech tells the oath to his wives, "and they rebelled against him, and declared these things abroad." The secret combination's first failure comes from the women who refuse to keep its oath. In a chapter where the men swear by their throats to keep darkness hidden, it's the daughters who drag it into the light. ("And among the daughters of men these things were not spoken" — the combination couldn't fully colonize them.)

**Critical analysis / tensions named:**
- *The "fortunate Fall" without trivializing transgression.* Eve's joy (5:11) and Adam's blessing (5:10) could be read as making the Fall simply *good*. But the same chapter shows its real cost — expulsion, mourning parents (5:27), and the flood of "carnal, sensual, and devilish" that follows (5:13). The Restoration holds both: the Fall was a transgression with genuine consequences *and* the necessary door to joy and seed. Eve isn't celebrating sin; she's recognizing what God's redemption made of it. Flagging the balance, not collapsing it either way.
- *Cain "rejected the greater counsel" (5:25) — accountability proportional to light.* Cain isn't a man who never knew; he heard God directly (5:22-25) and "rejected the greater counsel which was had from God." His becoming "Perdition" (5:24) turns on sin against knowledge, not ignorance — the same Luke 12:48 / Hel 7:24 principle the BoM walk surfaced. Worth naming: the text makes Cain culpable by light received, not by nature.
- *"Master Mahan" — honest about the etymology (5:31 fn).* The footnote itself hedges: "'Mind,' 'destroyer,' and 'great one' are possible meanings of the roots evident in 'Mahan.'" I'll cite it as the source gives it — possible meanings, not a settled translation. (A claim of a definite Hebrew/Egyptian etymology would overreach the footnote.)
