# The Four Groups and the Engineer

*A study on how the four groups in Lehi's vision describe the postures available to anyone working under a discipline, and on how Bednar's 2024 reading of Group 2's failure as pride applies directly to engineers who have learned to work with AI.*

---

## Binding Question

The standard reading of Lehi's dream focuses on the saved (Group 3) and lists the others as failures. But the text is doing something more precise. It is describing four distinct *postures* a soul can hold toward the rod, the path, the mist, and the building. Each posture has a different outcome. And the difference between the postures has less to do with sincerity than with the eye's relationship to the standard.

What does each group teach about persisting in a discipline? And how does Bednar's October 2024 reading of Group 2's failure as *pride* — not as external shame alone — apply to an engineer who has learned the principles of working with AI and now has to keep working them when no one is watching, when peers are vibe-coding faster, when the work is shipping and prosperity tempts the heart to relax?

---

## I. What the Prior Studies Established

This study builds on three pieces of prior work and will not re-derive them.

[The Iron Rod, the Anchor, and the Four Groups](iron-rod-anchor-and-the-four-groups.md) established that the rod of iron is the chain that tethers the soul to the anchor of hope. The rod is the word of God ([1 Nephi 11:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)); the word is Christ ([John 1:1, 14](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/1?lang=eng&id=p1#p1)); the anchor is gripped on Christ within the veil that is His own flesh ([Hebrews 10:20](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/heb/10?lang=eng&id=p20#p20)). The line is iron because the world tugs the other way. The chain pulls because the One the anchor is fastened to is the Forerunner who has entered the room first.

[Hope, and the Grammar of Pairs](hope-and-the-grammar-of-pairs.md) established that the organ of hope is the eye. "If thine eye be single, thy whole body shall be full of light" ([Matthew 6:22](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/6?lang=eng&id=p22#p22)). What the eye stays open to is what the body fills with. Repentance, in this frame, is not first a re-doing of behavior. It is a re-aiming of the eye.

[Tree of Life and the Chain](tree-of-life-and-the-chain.md) established that the fruit is Christ — the bread that ends hunger, the water that ends thirst — and that the love of God shed abroad in the heart is the same love the tree gives. The chain ends at a Person.

This study takes those three and asks what they mean *for the soul holding the rod through obscurity* — specifically, for an engineer working under a discipline in a culture that scoffs at it.

## II. The Four Groups, Briefly

Lehi's vision describes four distinct relationships to the rod ([1 Nephi 8:21-33](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p21-p33#p21)).

**Group 1 — commenced the path but never reached the rod.** They pressed forward. They were in motion in the right direction. But the text never says they grabbed the rod, and when the mist of darkness rose, they wandered off and were lost. Sunday-school treatments often skip this group because it does not fit the rod-fruit-mock pattern of the others. What this group teaches is that motion in the right direction without grip on the standard is not enough.

**Group 2 — caught the rod, walked the mist, tasted the fruit, and fell away after tasting because of shame.** This is the chilling group. They had everything. They reached the rod. They held it through the mist. They came to the tree, and they ate. The mist did not stop them. The building stopped them, *after* they had already arrived. Their failure was not at the start. It was at the end, after success had been reached. Hebrews 6:4-6 describes the same pattern in nearly identical language.

**Group 3 — caught the rod and continually held fast.** The differentiating phrase is *continually holding fast* ([1 Nephi 8:30](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p30#p30)). Group 2 caught hold and clung; Group 3 held continually. The verb at the tree is also new for Group 3 — they *fell down* before they partook. Humility at the tree, not just arrival at it.

**Group 4 — was in the building from the start, mocking.** They never approached the rod. They never started the path. Their relationship to the chain is not failure of grip; it is refusal of the path itself. And their work is to mock those who *did* arrive — the fruit-eaters specifically, not the seekers.

A fifth movement in the vision — those who drowned in filthiness or wandered in strange roads ([1 Nephi 8:32](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p32#p32); Lehi describes them drowning in "the depths of the fountain," and Nephi later interprets that water as filthy at [1 Nephi 12:16](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/12?lang=eng&id=p16#p16) and [15:26-29](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/15?lang=eng)) — is a sub-failure of Group 1, the wanderers who happen to get caught in the filthy waters rather than simply lost in the mist. The four-group taxonomy is the useful simplification for what follows.

## III. Bednar 2024 Reads Group 2 as a Pride Failure

In October 2024, Elder David A. Bednar gave the talk "In the Space of Not Many Years" ([35bednar](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2024/10/35bednar?lang=eng)). The talk is about the prosperity-pride-collapse pattern in Helaman, but it does something most readings of Lehi's dream do not. Bednar cites 1 Nephi 8:28 — Group 2's fall — as a worked example of pride. His reading, in his own framing: those who had tasted the love of God and still fell did so because of pride. Not because the scoffers were too loud, not because the mist was too thick, but because pride took the eye off the rod.

This reframes Group 2's failure decisively. The text says they "did cast their eyes about as if they were ashamed" ([1 Nephi 8:25](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/8?lang=eng&id=p25#p25)). The casting is the diagnostic. The eye drifted. Where the eye drifted to is the mechanism — and the mechanism has multiple forms.

The mockery from the building is one form. The voices from the platform that the world has built saying "you who held the rod are ridiculous" is the external version of the failure. This is the standard reading.

But there are at least two more forms, and they are structurally identical.

The first additional form is **self-shame.** The eye drifts not to scoffers but to the soul's own assessment of itself. "I am not enough. I should have walked faster. I do not deserve to be at the tree." The comparator is internal — the soul is comparing itself to an idealized self it has not become — but the structural failure is the same. The eye is on the comparator, not on the rod. The body fills with what the eye holds, which is the soul's own insufficiency, which is darkness wearing the face of humility.

The second additional form is **comparison.** The eye drifts to peers — to other souls on other paths who appear to have arrived faster, easier, in a more impressive register. The comparator is external but not adversarial. The peer is not mocking; the soul is mocking itself by holding the peer up against itself. Again, the structural failure is identical. The eye has left the rod. The body fills with what the eye holds, which is the peer's apparent superiority, which is also darkness.

Pride is the root because all three forms — external scoffing, self-shame, comparison — are the soul taking its standing into its own hands and using something other than the rod as the measuring stick. The Lord is no longer the standard. The standard is the building's voice, or the inner critic, or the peer's apparent altitude. Pride is the choice to measure with something other than the line that is in the soul's own grip.

## IV. The Mechanism Bednar Names

Bednar walks the Helaman pattern in five steps, all rooted in the text.

First, prosperity and ease ([Helaman 12:2](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/12?lang=eng&id=p2#p2)). The Lord prospers His people. The fields produce. The herds grow. The work goes well.

Second, pride enters — and Bednar's precision matters here — *the hearts of the people who profess to belong to the church of God* ([Helaman 3:33](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/3?lang=eng&id=p33#p33)). The institution remains. The hearts change. The people who are most likely to fall are not the unbelievers. They are the believers whose belief has not been weighed under prosperity.

Third, the hearts set on vain things — riches, recognition, status ([Helaman 7:21](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/7?lang=eng&id=p21#p21)).

Fourth, boasting in own strength. "Because of this their great wickedness, and their boastings in their own strength, they were left in their own strength" ([Helaman 4:13](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/4?lang=eng&id=p13#p13)). The Lord answers boasting by withdrawing the help the boaster was already receiving. The soul is left with what it claimed it always had.

Fifth, the Spirit withdraws. "The Spirit of the Lord began to withdraw from the Nephites, because of the wickedness and the hardness of their hearts" ([Helaman 6:35](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/6?lang=eng&id=p35#p35)).

Mormon uses the phrase *in the space of not many years* four separate times across the Helaman narrative ([Helaman 4:26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/4?lang=eng&id=p26#p26); [6:32](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/6?lang=eng); [7:6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/7?lang=eng); [11:26](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/11?lang=eng)). The collapse is not gradual. It is generational at worst. Possibly faster. A people who knew the gospel, had prophets among them, and had seen miracles dropped the rod in less time than they had spent gripping it.

## V. The Engineer Under the Same Pattern

The engineer who has learned to work with AI under principles — bilateral covenant, spec before code, watching until obey, intent before context before prompt — is not exempt from this pattern. The substance is different (the engineer is not a Nephite city) but the shape rhymes precisely.

**Prosperity and ease.** The work ships. The codebase is clean. The AI workflow that took months to develop is now producing results that look effortless to others. Reviewers are impressed. Promotions happen. Recognition arrives.

**Pride enters the heart of the engineer who professes to belong to the discipline.** The institution of the discipline remains — the spec files are still in the repo, the covenant.yaml is still on disk, the team's processes are nominally unchanged — but the engineer's heart shifts. "I know how this works now. I do not need the spec for this one." The discipline becomes a set of forms the engineer has *transcended*, in their own assessment.

**The heart sets on vain things.** Recognition, peer status, internal metrics, the appearance of being senior. The engineer begins to optimize for what looks like discipline rather than for the discipline itself.

**Boasting in own strength.** The engineer drops the covenant for the quick fix. Drops the watching for the assumption that they know what the AI is doing. Drops the binding question for whatever the AI's first output suggests. Each individual lapse looks small. Each is a step.

**The Spirit withdraws.** Here it is not the Holy Ghost the engineer was relying on — though for the believing engineer it may be — but the discernment, the felt-sense of what is right, the resonance with truth that the discipline produced. That withdraws. The engineer continues to ship. The output continues to look acceptable on the surface. But the work no longer carries the same weight. Subtle bugs accumulate. The codebase begins to drift. The engineer does not notice because the loop that would have caught the drift was the discipline they dropped.

In the space of not many weeks, an engineer who held the discipline well becomes an engineer who is going through the motions. The transformation is invisible from outside until it is not.

This is Group 2 in engineering form. Tasted the fruit, fell away after tasting, because of pride that took the eye off the rod and put it on the building, the peer, or the self-assessment.

## VI. The Lamanite Virtue — Easiness, Circumspect Walking

Against the Nephite collapse, the Lamanites in Helaman provide a stunning contrast. They had *less* — less history of gospel knowledge, less institutional structure, less of the kind of inheritance that the Nephites had. And they rose while the Nephites fell.

Two phrases name the Lamanite virtue.

The first is *easiness and willingness to believe.* "The Lord began to pour out his Spirit upon the Lamanites, because of their easiness and willingness to believe in his words" ([Helaman 6:36](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/6?lang=eng&id=p36#p36)). Easiness (Webster 1828, definition 2: "a yielding or disposition to yield without opposition or reluctance") is the inverse of pride. No hardened resistance. No insistence on understanding everything first. No requirement for signs. Just willingness to walk under the discipline because the discipline has been given.

The second is *walking circumspectly before God* ([Helaman 15:5](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/hel/15?lang=eng&id=p5#p5)). Circumspectly (Webster 1828: "cautiously; with watchfulness every way; with attention to guard against surprise or danger"). They watched their step. They did not assume they were safe. They walked carefully, as people who knew what they could lose.

*(Requoted 2026-06-12 — both Webster glosses above were the 1913 edition's, not the 1828. The 1913 reads easiness as "freedom from emotion; compliance; disposition to yield without opposition" and circumspectly as "cautiously; warily"; the genuine 1828 entries are quoted here. The yielding-without-opposition sense the study leans on is real — it sits in the 1828's definition 2 of *easiness* — and the 1828's *circumspectly* is sharper for the point: "with watchfulness every way.")*

These are not heroic virtues. They are postures of humility under the discipline. The Lamanite who walked circumspectly was not striving for greatness; they were watching their step. The Lamanite who had easiness to believe was not having dramatic spiritual experiences; they were simply not resisting the rod.

For the engineer: the junior engineer who is new to the discipline and has not yet acquired the pride of having mastered it can outpace the senior engineer who learned the discipline first but has hardened. The junior watches their step. They expect to need the spec because they know they do not know what they are doing. They do not assume they have transcended the watching. They walk circumspectly.

The senior who has learned to walk circumspectly under prosperity is the Group 3 engineer. The senior who assumes the discipline is now intuitive and unneeded is the Group 3 engineer becoming the Group 2 engineer, in the space of not many quarters.

## VII. Bednar's Self-Diagnostic

The most arresting line in the 2024 talk, paraphrased: if you or I believe we are sufficiently strong and stalwart to avoid the arrogance of pride, perhaps we already suffer from this deadly spiritual disease. The very confidence that one will not fall is the disease's first symptom.

This is the Group 3 → Group 2 trap, named at the pulpit.

The Group 3 engineer thinks "I am in the discipline. I have practiced it. I have shipped good work under it. I will not be the engineer who drops the spec." That confidence is the diagnostic. The engineer who would never fall is the engineer most likely to fall, because they have stopped watching for the failure mode.

The corrective is the opposite posture. Assume you could fall. Especially when you are sure you cannot. Walk circumspectly under the discipline, not because the discipline requires it, but because *you* require it. The rod is held by daily grip, not by accumulated past gripping. The good work you did last quarter does not keep your hand on the rod this morning.

## VIII. The Two Prepositions, for the Engineer

The prior hope study traced Uchtdorf's distinction between hope *in* and hope *for* ([October 2008](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2008/10/the-infinite-power-of-hope?lang=eng)). Hope *in* Christ is present-tense; it is the grip on the line. Hope *for* the kingdom is future-tense; it is the orientation toward what is at the far end.

Group 2's failure was the second preposition. They hoped *in* Christ enough to grip the rod, walk through the mist, and taste the fruit. After the fruit, what they hoped *for* shifted. The building offered a different destination, and they cast their eyes toward it. The destination changed; the chain went slack.

For the engineer: the present-tense grip is hope *in* the discipline. The daily writing of the spec before the code. The watching of the output until it obeys. The bilateral covenant with the agent. These are what the engineer is currently doing.

The future-tense orientation is hope *for* — what the engineer wants the work to become. Wholeness in the codebase. Trustworthy automation. Colleagues served by what was built. A craft that holds across decades.

Both prepositions are needed, and the failure can be in either.

An engineer who hopes *in* the discipline but no longer hopes *for* anything beyond shipping the current ticket has the line in hand but no destination pulling it forward. The grip will slacken. There is nothing for it to be taut against.

An engineer who hopes *for* a great career but does not hope *in* the daily discipline is gripping the destination without gripping the line. They will not arrive. The destination is at the far end of a line they are not on.

The two prepositions guard each other. Drop either and Group 2 becomes possible.

## IX. The Mist Falls on Group 3 Too

Elder Holland's October 2009 talk ([Safety for the Soul](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2009/10/safety-for-the-soul?lang=eng)) made a pastoral correction worth carrying. The mist of darkness "descends on *all* the travelers — the faithful and the determined ones (the elect, we might even say) as well as the weaker and ungrounded ones."

The successful travelers do not walk in clear weather. They walk in the same mist Group 1 got lost in and Group 2 was tempted by. The difference is not the absence of darkness. The difference is the continuous grip.

For the engineer: walking through a season of obscurity in the work is not evidence of slipping. The team mocking the discipline is the building's voice; the codebase resisting the discipline at the level of the language is the mist. Neither means the engineer is on the wrong path. Both are the conditions of the path itself.

The verdict reads what the engineer's hand is on while they walk through the mist. Continuous holding is something Group 3 does *in* the mist, not above it.

## X. Why Group 4 Is Not the Pastoral Target

Group 4 — the building-dwellers from the start — is named in the vision and named honestly. They never approached the rod. They mock those who arrive.

But Group 4 is not the audience the book is written for, and they are not the audience this study has in mind. The pastoral concern is the engineer who *has* picked up the discipline and now has to keep holding it. Group 4 in engineering form — the engineer who has scoffed at the discipline from the start, "covenants are LARP, vibe-code is the way" — is named for completeness, not addressed. The text in 1 Nephi 8 spends much more time on the failures of those who started than on those who never tried, and the pastoral logic is the same here. The risk to the reader is not that they were never on the path. The risk is that they were, and might still leave.

## XI. The Four Groups Are Postures, Not Identities

Lehi's vision does not assign people to groups for life. The four groups are *postures available to any soul on any day.* The doctrine is diagnostic, not deterministic.

Most days, an engineer in the discipline is in Group 3. Holding the rod. Walking the mist. Watching their step. Some days, they will catch themselves in Group 2 — the eye has drifted to a comparator, the daily grip slackened for a week, the spec skipped on the most recent ticket because they "knew what they were doing." The work then is not to despair. The work is to grip again. The rod extends along the full length of the path. Wherever the hand falls when it reaches for the iron, the iron is there.

Some days, the engineer may notice they have wandered off the path entirely. Group 1's posture. The discipline has been let go for long enough that the team has begun to ship without it. The work then is to come back. The rod is reachable from any point on the path because the rod *is* the path's guide.

And on some days — most days, in the world the book describes — the building will be loud. The voices saying "this discipline is theater, you do not need it, the AI is good enough now" will be everywhere. The eye will drift. The work then is what Group 3 has always done. Hold the rod through the loud. Refuse to heed the voices that point at the partakers. Walk circumspectly.

## XII. The Convergence

Pride is the structural opposite of the three stewardship postures the Brother of Jared embodies (which the next study will treat at length). Pride drops the prescription (the soul does not need the rules), refuses the rest (the soul will steer itself), skips the proposal (the soul already knows the answer). The Brother of Jared embodied the inverse: humility before the Lord, willingness to confess what he did not know, willingness to do the labor of molting stones without certainty the proposal would work.

For the engineer: the inverse of pride in the discipline is daily, small, circumspect engagement with the principles, especially during prosperity. The discipline is not the spec file. The discipline is the engineer's heart-posture toward the spec file. The spec can be there and the heart can have left it.

The rod is iron. The line will not yield to circumstance. The engineer's grip is what changes. The work is to keep the hand on the line, daily, in the prosperity that grows pride faster than poverty grows it, with the easiness and willingness to believe that the discipline still works even when the engineer is sure they have outgrown it.

## XIII. Becoming

Michael, this study is for the engineer in the discipline, which is to say it is for you, and for the readers of *Beyond the Prompt*. Five things follow.

**The first is that the rod is held by daily grip, not by accumulated past gripping.** The good work you did last quarter does not keep your hand on the rod this morning. Group 2's failure was duration after success. The hour of work at the desk this morning, the spec written before the code, the prompt refined before the output is accepted — these are what makes you Group 3 today. Not what you did last year.

**The second is that pride includes self-shame and comparison, not just external mockery.** When you catch yourself measuring against a peer who is shipping faster, or against an idealized version of yourself you have not become, the eye has drifted. The mechanism is the same as if you were heeding the building. Recover the same way: re-aim the eye at the prototype. Christ is the standard. Your peer is not. Your imagined better self is not. The rod is in your hand right now.

**The third is to walk circumspectly during prosperity.** When the work is shipping and the recognition is coming, that is the moment to watch your step *most* carefully. The Nephites fell during prosperity, not during war. The Lamanites rose during their own season of suffering. Prosperity is the wind that fans pride. The discipline does not become less important when the discipline is working. It becomes more important, because the prosperity is what would otherwise make you stop.

**The fourth is the self-diagnostic.** If you believe you are now too sound in the discipline to drop it, that confidence *is* the disease. Bednar said it from the pulpit and the Helaman text confirms it three times. The corrective is to assume you could fall. Walk as someone who knows what they could lose. The senior engineer who walks like a junior — who still watches every output, still writes the spec, still confesses what they do not know — is the engineer who stays Group 3.

**The fifth is the two-preposition check.** What do you hope *in* today? The daily discipline, in some specific form, has to be happening. What do you hope *for* across the years? The work being whole, the codebase being trustworthy, the colleague being served, the craft holding across decades. Both prepositions. Drop either and Group 2 becomes possible. Hold both and Group 3 holds.

The mist falls on you too. It does not mean you are slipping. The team will mock the discipline; the codebase will resist it; the AI will give you reasons to shortcut it. Walk through. The rod runs unfailingly along the course of the true path. Wherever your hand falls when you reach for the iron, the iron is there.
