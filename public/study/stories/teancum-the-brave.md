# The Night the Javelin Didn't Return

**Source:** [Alma 51](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/51?lang=eng), [Alma 52](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/52?lang=eng), [Alma 62](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/alma/62?lang=eng)

---

They had been fighting for years.

Not weeks. Not months. Years of war between the Nephites and the Lamanites — bloodshed, famine, cities taken and retaken. And at the center of it all were two brothers: Amalickiah and Ammoron. They had stolen the Lamanite throne through murder, and they had sworn to drink the blood of the Nephites. Every battle, every death, every village burned — it traced back to them.

Then one night, in the twenty-fifth year of the judges, Teancum ended the first brother.

---

The day had been brutal. Teancum and his men had held the border of Bountiful against Amalickiah's entire army. Every man of Teancum exceeded the Lamanites in strength and skill. They fought from morning until dark, slaying them as the sun went down.

Then both armies pitched tents and slept.

Teancum didn't sleep. He and his servant stole out of camp and walked into the enemy lines. Sleep had overpowered the Lamanites from exhaustion. Teancum moved through the tents, found the king's, and put a javelin to Amalickiah's heart. He caused the death of the king immediately — the king didn't even wake.

Then Teancum returned. He walked back through the enemy camp, alone in the dark, and woke his own men to tell them what he'd done.

One brother down. But the war wasn't over.

---

Ammoron took his place.

Five more years of war. Five years of cities falling and rising, of armies marching and countermarching, of blood and famine. Teancum fought through all of it. He held the line at Mulek. He lured the Lamanites out of their fortifications in a feint retreat, drawing them into an ambush by Moroni's forces. He pushed them back again and again, from city to city, all the way down to the seashore.

Every battle was a stone in the road back to Ammoron.

---

In the thirty-first year, the trap was set.

Moroni had come from the north, Lehi and Teancum were holding the south, and the Lamanite army was encircled in the land of Moroni, trapped between wilderness and sea. Ammoron was there with them. The end was in sight.

They encamped for the night. Both armies were exhausted. The Nephites resolved upon no stratagem. Everyone was done.

Everyone except Teancum.

He was still angry. Five years later, still angry at the man who had taken his brother's place. Still thinking of all the blood — Nephite blood, Lamanite blood — that had been spilled because of these two men and their greed for a stolen throne.

While the rest of the army slept, Teancum went.

He let himself down over the walls of the city, alone, with a cord. He moved from place to place through the Lamanite camp, searching. He found the king. He cast a javelin at him, which pierced him near the heart.

But this time, the king woke. He awakened his servants before he died, and they pursued Teancum and slew him.

---

When Lehi and Moroni learned that Teancum was dead, they were exceedingly sorrowful.

He had been a man who had fought valiantly for his country. A true friend to liberty. He had suffered very many exceedingly sore afflictions.

But he was dead.

---

The next morning, Moroni marched forth against the Lamanites. He slew them with a great slaughter. He drove them out of the land. They fled and did not return at that time.

Peace came to the people of Nephi.

Moroni retired to his own house. Pahoran returned to his judgment seat. Helaman — Teancum's father — took up the ministry again and went forth to preach the word of God with much power.

The war that had consumed a generation ended the morning after Teancum died.

---

Two nights. Two kings. Two javelins.

The first time, he threw the javelin and came back. The second time, he threw the javelin and stayed there.

Five years of war between those two nights. Five years of blood and famine and cities burning, all of it the distance between Amalickiah's death and Ammoron's. Teancum carried that anger across half a decade of battle, and when the moment came — when both armies were tired and sleeping and the war was finally at its end — he still went. Alone. Over the walls. Into the enemy camp.

He didn't come back. But the peace he bought came back for everyone else.

---

*Teancum's name was given to a city after his death. It stood on the northern border, near Desolation, for generations. The people who lived there would have known what the name meant every time they looked at it.*
