**Auftragstaktik** — yes, German. *Auftrag* (mission, charge) + *Taktik*. "Mission command."
It's the Prussian doctrine Boyd built maneuver warfare on: the commander gives the **intent and
the end-state and the boundaries**, and leaves the *how* to the judgment of the person on the
ground. Its opposite is *Befehlstaktik* — detailed-order command, micromanagement. The whole
point is that orientation lives at the edge, where contact happens, so the loop can turn faster
than a centralized one ever could. Which is to say: it's D&C 121 in field-gray. "Persuade, don't
compel; give intent, watch to intent." It's our presiding covenant, and it's Google's "give
agents success criteria, not step-by-step instructions." Three fields, one doctrine — give the
charge, trust the steward, hold to intent.
