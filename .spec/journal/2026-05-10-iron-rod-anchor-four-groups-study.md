# Iron Rod, Anchor, and the Four Groups — Session Journal

## Episode

First substantive study run on the freshly-shipped Claude Code cycle harness. Binding question came from Michael verbatim: is the iron rod the chain that tethers the soul to the anchor of hope, drawing us from here through the veil into the celestial kingdom of God — and what do the four groups in Lehi and Nephi's vision teach about how people relate to hope through the word.

Council moment caught real overlap with three prior studies. `tree-of-life-and-the-chain.md` already established rod=word=Christ. `hope-and-the-grammar-of-pairs.md` already developed the anchor-within-the-veil image and the eye-as-organ-of-hope. `way-truth-life.md` already established veil=Christ's flesh (Heb 10:20). The new study's seam was the *chain itself* — none of the priors treated the rod as the line between soul and anchor.

Phases 1-7 ran in order. The Phase 4 precondition (scratch file MUST contain `## Gap & Critical Analysis` section before drafting) worked as designed — the agent did not skip 3a, and 3a actually surfaced four real qualifications that shaped the draft (chain is the user's metaphor not the scripture's word; the four-group framing is a simplification of a richer multi-group picture; Heb-vs-Ether anchor tension stays surfaced rather than collapsed; Heb 6:4-6 severity needs careful framing).

## Discoveries

1. **Helaman 3:29-30 is the keystone verse.** It states the chain-functional image almost verbatim: "lay hold upon the word of God... lead the man of Christ in a strait and narrow course across that everlasting gulf of misery... and land their souls at the right hand of God in the kingdom of heaven, to go no more out." The verbs *lay hold upon*, *lead across*, *land* are exactly chain-shaped. *Land* is shipping language. The destination ("right hand of God") matches Ether 12:4's anchor destination. The reading the binding question proposed is not inference — Helaman states it.

2. **Hebrews 6 is structurally a Lehi-vision response.** Verses 4-6 describe Group 2 (those who tasted the heavenly gift, fell away, were put to open shame) and verses 18-19 give the anchor (hope as the line that enters within the veil). One chapter contains both halves: the warning to those who let go after tasting AND the line that holds. The pastoral logic is the same as 1 Nephi 8: you who taste must keep holding.

3. **Bednar 2022 already named the cling-vs-hold-fast distinction apostolically.** I had been planning to argue this textually from 1 Ne 8:24 vs. 8:30. Bednar makes it explicit, plus collapses Word=Christ=scriptures=rod into one identity (per John 1 + Article of Faith 8). External warrant strengthens Section IV substantially.

4. **The Heb-vs-Ether anchor tension resolves cleanly when both grammars are kept.** Paul names hope as the device-name (the structural identity once in place). Moroni names hope as the production grammar (how the anchor gets made). Same hope, two angles. The honest move was naming the tension and then resolving it, not papering over it.

5. **Lehi's vision contains more than four groups.** v. 31-32 names multitudes feeling toward the building and many drowned in the depths. Nephi later (15:27-29) clarifies that the water Lehi initially saw as one body is actually two — fountain of living waters AND river of filthiness/hell. Sunday-school four-group framing is a useful simplification of richer text. Named this in the study rather than smoothing over it.

## Surprises

- The fact that Lehi gives the saved group (v. 30) the *fewest* words — "to be short in writing." The dream's narrative attention is mostly given to those who fail. This is a structural feature, not an accident: the warning is what saves.
- Group 1 (path-starters lost in the mist before reaching the rod) is the group most often elided in casual readings. Bringing it forward changed the shape of Section IV.
- The "*not* leaving the principles" JST correction at Heb 6:1 functions as a direct gloss on Group 2's failure: don't drop the foundation while reaching for the higher thing.
