intent: >
  Michael picked up a thread from our ongoing faith/hope/charity arc: the
  meaning of "broken heart and contrite spirit" as a gospel doublet. He
  noticed Amulek's progression in Alma 34 (justice/mercy → faith unto

  saw the same arc in Enos's prayer. Wanted to understand what the words
  actually mean and how this posture brings mercy.

discoveries:
  - title: "Hebrew physicality of shabar and dakah"
    detail: >
      'Broken' = shabar (7665, to burst, crush, destroy). 'Contrite' = dakah
      (1794, to collapse, be pressed down until pulverized). Verified via
      BibleHub/Exa. The words are not gentle metaphors. They describe
      structural collapse and crushing. This physicality changes how we hear
      the command to 'offer a broken heart.'

  - title: "Amulek coined two unique phrases: faith unto repentance, fruit unto repentance"
    detail: >
      Grep-confirmed: both phrases appear ONLY in Alma 34. 'Faith unto
      repentance' (v15-16) and 'fruit unto repentance' (v30). The preposition
      'unto' is directional — faith aims at repentance; fruit demonstrates it.
      The arc is: justice/mercy → faith → cry → charity → fruit.

  - title: "Psalm 34:18's two verbs: nigh vs. saveth"
    detail: >
      The Lord is 'nigh' to the brokenhearted but 'saveth' the contrite.
      Broken heart attracts proximity; contrite spirit receives salvation.
      This nuance surfaced during critical analysis and became a load-bearing
      observation in the draft.

  - title: "Elder Andersen's distinction vs. scriptural fluidity"
    detail: >
      Andersen (Oct 2025): broken heart = godly sorrow (affective); contrite
      spirit = putting yourself in God's hands (volitional). But Psalm 51:17
      reverses the pairing ('broken spirit... broken and contrite heart').
      The distinction is illuminating but not rigid. Both are true at
      different resolutions.

  - title: "The mercy mechanism is architectural, not transactional"
    detail: >
      The study's central argument: the Atonement is always offered (3 Ne 9:14
      'mine arm of mercy is extended'). The broken heart doesn't earn mercy;
      it creates space for mercy to enter. A whole heart is full of self.
      A broken heart has room. This is the answer to the binding question.

  - title: "3 Nephi 12:3 adds 'who come unto me' to Matthew's poor in spirit"
    detail: >
      Matthew 5:3: 'Blessed are the poor in spirit.' 3 Nephi 12:3: 'Blessed
      are the poor in spirit who come unto me.' The blessing is not for
      poverty of spirit in isolation. It is for poverty of spirit that moves
      toward Christ. This parallels 3 Nephi 9:20's 'whoso cometh unto me.'
