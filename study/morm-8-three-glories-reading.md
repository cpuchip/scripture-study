# Reading at Three Altitudes — Mormon 8 Through the Vocabulary of Glory

*A follow-up to [Mormon 8 — The Book That Speaks of Its Own Coming Forth](morm-8-voice-from-the-dust.md). What if D&C 76 gives us not only a map of the next life but a vocabulary for reading this one — and the scriptures themselves?*

## The idea

D&C 76 sorts eternity into degrees of glory: telestial, terrestrial, celestial, with the sons of perdition below them all. We usually read it as a map of where people end up. But the vocabulary also works as a set of **reading altitudes.** The same word can be read at the telestial level, the terrestrial level, or the celestial level, and it means something different at each. "Fine apparel," read low, is rich clothing — status, show. Read high, it is the robe of righteousness, the garment washed white, the vesture of the temple. Same two words. Three elevations.

Run Mormon 8 through that lens — with Webster's 1828 dictionary open, because the early-Restoration words carry meanings our modern ear has flattened — and a striking thing happens. The chapter's whole indictment of the latter-day world turns out to be a *telestial-altitude* reading of words that also have a celestial altitude. And the prophet writing it has the celestial one in view the whole time.

**One honesty marker before we start.** This is a lens we bring to the text, not a code hidden inside it. D&C 76 came in 1832, fourteen centuries after Moroni and three years after this chapter was translated; it describes the kingdoms of glory at the Judgment, and it never claims to be a method for reading scripture. So this is a devotional, typological way of seeing — fruitful, but not the text's literal argument. The discipline that keeps it honest is the dictionary: as we'll see, the double meanings are already sitting in the 1828 English, not only in our wish to find them.

## The vocabulary

D&C 76 fixes the registers with three lights in the sky:

- **Celestial** — "the glory of the sun" ([D&C 76:70](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/76.md)). They "received the testimony of Jesus, and believed on his name and were baptized" (v. 51), were "sealed by the Holy Spirit of promise" (v. 53), are "the church of the Firstborn" (v. 54), "received of his fulness" (v. 56), and "dwell in the presence of God and his Christ forever" (v. 62). Webster 1828: *celestial* = "pertaining to the spiritual heaven; heavenly; divine."
- **Terrestrial** — "the glory of the moon" (v. 78). These are "honorable men of the earth, who were blinded by the craftiness of men" (v. 75); they "receive of the presence of the Son, but not of the fulness of the Father" (v. 77); they are "not valiant in the testimony of Jesus" (v. 79). Webster: *terrestrial* = "earthly; sublunary; mundane" — and *sublunary* literally means *below the moon*.
- **Telestial** — "the glory of the stars" (v. 81). They "received not the gospel of Christ, neither the testimony of Jesus" (v. 82); they are "liars, and sorcerers, and adulterers, and whoremongers" (v. 103); they are "servants of the Most High; but where God and Christ dwell they cannot come, worlds without end" (v. 112).

There is a tell hiding in the dictionary. Look up *celestial* in Webster 1828 and it's there. *Terrestrial* is there. **Telestial is not.** The word does not exist in the 1828 English language; it is a coinage that arrived with this vision. Webster's world had a word for the heavenly and a word for the earthly, but no word for a *third, lower* glory — so the lowest kingdom had to be named into being. The vocabulary of glory was literally not complete until the Restoration finished it.

And below all three sits perdition: those who "denied the Holy Spirit after having received it, and having denied the Only Begotten Son" (v. 35), "the only ones who shall not be redeemed" (v. 38). Hold that pole; Mormon 8 touches it too.

## The method — and why 1828 is the engine

To read a word at three altitudes, you need to recover what the word could hold. That is exactly what Webster 1828 gives, because it was compiled in Joseph Smith's own decade. And the remarkable thing is how often a single entry *already contains* both the low and high readings — the split we're looking for is in the language itself.

Take **fine.** Webster's first definition: "Finished; brought to perfection; refined; free from impurity; excellent." His second: "Aiming at show or effect; loaded with ornament; overdressed or overdecorated; showy." One word, two altitudes — purified-to-perfection on the high end, gaudy-and-showy on the low. So "fine apparel" is genuinely ambiguous in 1828 English: it can mean clothing that shows off, or clothing refined free from impurity. The chapter is about to use it one way. The gospel uses it the other.

## The worked case — "fine apparel"

Mormon 8 hammers apparel. The latter-day churches are "lifted up in the pride of their hearts, unto the wearing of very fine apparel" ([Mormon 8:36](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/morm/8.md)); the people "love money, and your substance, and your fine apparel, and the adorning of your churches, more than ye love the poor and the needy" (v. 37); they "adorn yourselves with that which hath no life" (v. 39). Read at the telestial altitude — Webster's "showy, overdecorated, aiming at effect" — this is exactly what Moroni means. Fine apparel as status. Clothing that has "no life."

Now read the same two words at the celestial altitude, and a whole scriptural wardrobe opens. The righteous are "clothed with purity, yea, even with the robe of righteousness" ([2 Nephi 9:14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/9.md)). "No man can be saved except his garments are washed white; yea, his garments must be purified until they are cleansed from all stain, through the blood" ([Alma 5:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/5.md)). The redeemed are those who "washed their robes, and made them white in the blood of the Lamb" ([Revelation 7:14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rev/7.md)). And in the Kirtland temple dedicatory prayer, the plea is that "our garments may be pure, that we may be clothed upon with robes of righteousness, with palms in our hands, and crowns of glory upon our heads" ([D&C 109:76](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/76.md)). Webster's high definition of *fine* — "refined; free from impurity" — *is* the washed-white robe. To be finely appareled, celestially, is to be cleansed from all stain.

Webster even hands us the bridge. His second definition of *apparel* is "a small ornamental piece of embroidery worn on albs and some other ecclesiastical vestments." In 1828, "apparel" already carried a sacred-vestment sense. The word that Mormon 8 uses to indict the proud is the same word the dictionary uses for temple clothing. The telestial and the celestial readings of "fine apparel" are sitting in the same dictionary entry, an inch apart.

And the chapter's dark counter-image confirms the axis. 2 Nephi 9:14 sets the robe of righteousness against "our uncleanness, and our nakedness." The telestial end of apparel is not actually the gaudy robe — it is nakedness dressed up to look like something. "That which hath no life."

## The rest of the words

The lens, once you have it, runs through the chapter:

- **gain.** Mormon 8 condemns those who "built up churches unto yourselves to get gain" (v. 33) and "build up your secret abominations to get gain" (v. 40). Telestial *gain* is money — Webster quotes "the lust of gain." But Webster's same entry quotes the celestial register too: "Godliness with contentment is great gain" (1 Timothy 6:6) and "What is a man profited, if he shall gain the whole world, and lose his own soul" (Matthew 16:26). The word *gain* contains both the thing the wicked chase and the only thing worth gaining.
- **glory.** D&C 76 itself splits it: "let no man glory in man, but rather let him glory in God" (v. 61). Webster's *glory* runs from "praise, honor, renown" and even "pride; boastfulness; arrogance" up to "the presence of the Divine Being… celestial honor; heaven." Telestial glory is being seen. Celestial glory is the presence of God.
- **churches.** Telestial: institutions "built up to get gain" that say "Come unto me, and for your money you shall be forgiven of your sins" (v. 32). Celestial: "the church of the Firstborn" (D&C 76:54). The terrestrial middle is the honest one to name here — a church with the form of godliness but "not valiant," the moon and not the sun.
- **pollute.** "Why have ye polluted the holy church of God?" (v. 38); "great pollutions upon the face of the earth" (v. 31). Webster's *pollute*: "to render ceremonially unclean; to disqualify or unfit for sacred use." Pollution is precisely the *opposite* of the washed-white garment — it is what unfits a thing for the temple. The apparel axis again, from the other side.
- **the poor and the needy, the sick and the afflicted** (vv. 37, 39). Read telestially, they are passersby to "notice not." Read celestially, they are the standard of judgment — the least of these are where Christ is found. This is the chapter's actual celestial test, and the people fail it.

Notice the honest limit while we're here. *Gain* splits cleanly three ways (money / honor / the soul). *Apparel* and *churches* split most cleanly into two — telestial and celestial — with "terrestrial" as the in-between of respectable-but-empty religion. Not every word makes a tidy threesome, and forcing one would be the lens overriding the text. The reliable axis is the two poles; the terrestrial is the honest middle where it genuinely fits.

## The thesis — a telestial diagnosis by a celestial witness

Here is what the lens reveals about Mormon 8 as a whole. The chapter is a catalog of the telestial: fine apparel, gain, polluted churches, ignored poor, secret combinations. But the man writing it is operating from the celestial altitude. Moroni has "seen them" — the translated disciples — and "they have ministered unto us" (v. 11); he writes with the fulness in view, having been shown our day by Jesus Christ Himself (v. 35). He is reading a telestial world from the elevation of the sun, and grieving at the distance.

The chapter even names both far poles in a single verse. Moroni asks why the people will not "think that greater is the value of an endless happiness than that misery which never dies" (v. 38) — the celestial reward set against the perdition end, in one sentence. And the perdition motion itself surfaces in verse 21: those who "say: We will destroy the work of the Lord." That is the perdition direction — to fight the work after the light has come — the same shape as the anti-Christs of the [last study but one](morm-8-voice-from-the-dust.md), Sherem and Korihor, who denied after they knew. Mormon 8 is not a flat scolding. It is a man at the top of the ladder describing every rung, and pleading with us not to settle on the lowest.

## Think celestial

President Nelson, in October 2023, gave the lens its imperative. "Thinking celestial means being spiritually minded," he taught, quoting Jacob: "to be spiritually-minded is life eternal." He framed it as a way of choosing: "begin with the end in mind… making the celestial kingdom your eternal goal and then carefully considering where each of your decisions while here on earth will place you in the next world." And he named the stakes in the same vocabulary D&C 76 uses: "if we unwisely choose to live telestial laws now, we are choosing to be resurrected with a telestial body."

Nelson was talking about choices, not about reading scripture. But the move is the same one this study has been making, and it extends naturally: to think celestial is to read at the celestial altitude — your wardrobe, your gain, your glory, and the words on the page. Mortality, he said, is "a master class in learning to choose the things of greatest eternal import." Reading is part of that class. When the chapter says "fine apparel" and your eye stops at rich clothing, you have read it telestially. When it lifts to the robe washed white, you have thought celestial.

And the place Nelson points for that schooling is the temple: "There you are shown how to progress toward a celestial life… drawn closer to the Savior." Which closes the apparel loop. The telestial reading of fine apparel is the showy garment; the celestial reading is the robe of righteousness — and the temple is exactly where a person literally puts on clean clothing to be taught how to live at the highest altitude. The wardrobe and the reading meet there.

## What it asks of us

The lens turns outward fast. It is easy to read Mormon 8:36–37 and picture someone else's gaudy church. It is harder to ask the celestial question of my own life: where my substance and attention actually go, against the needy I could notice and don't (v. 37). Reading the chapter at the celestial altitude is not a literary trick; it is the same examination Alma pressed — "can ye look up to God… having your garments cleansed and made white" (the question behind [Alma 5:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/5.md)).

Two commitments fall out of this:

1. **Read upward on purpose.** When a familiar word stops me at its low meaning — *gain*, *glory*, *fine*, *treasure* — pause and ask what it means at the celestial altitude. Webster 1828 is a tool for this, not nostalgia: it preserves the high reading the modern ear has lost.
2. **Dress the inner man before the outer.** The chapter's whole apparel indictment is really about what we love (v. 37). Thinking celestial about apparel means caring more whether the garment is *clean* — washed white, free from stain — than whether it is *fine* in the showy sense.

One connection to carry forward: this is the cousin of the [Abinadi study](https://cpuchip.net/studies/abinadi-how-to-read) on cpuchip.net. There, one verse held many referents across time. Here, one word holds many altitudes of glory. Both say the same thing about scripture — it is not a single-level code to crack but a layered witness that rewards the reader who climbs. The next rung is always there. The question Nelson and Moroni both press is whether we will think high enough to see it.
