# Phase 3: The Spirit World — Where Are They Now?

> *"They were filled with joy and gladness, and were rejoicing together because the day of their deliverance was at hand."*
> — [D&C 138:15](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)

---

## Driving Questions

1. What is paradise? What is spirit prison? Are they separate or aspects of the same place?
2. What happens in the spirit world? Is there progression? Learning? Missionary work?
3. D&C 138 — Joseph F. Smith's vision. What did he actually see? How is missionary work organized there?
4. What does "the same spirit which doth possess your bodies at the time that ye go out of this life" (Alma 34:34) actually mean?
5. **The comfort question:** For someone who is sick and facing death — what does the gospel *actually promise* about what comes next?
6. What about those who weren't baptized? What hope does the spirit world offer?

---

## The Scriptures Speak

### Alma 40: The Space Between

Alma's teaching to Corianton provides the foundational framework. It begins with an honest admission — there are many mysteries "which are kept, that no one knoweth them save God himself" ([Alma 40:3](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/40.md)). But Alma has inquired "diligently of God" and he *does* know some things:

> "Now, concerning the state of the soul between death and the resurrection—Behold, it has been made known unto me by an angel, that the spirits of all men, as soon as they are departed from this mortal body, yea, the spirits of all men, whether they be good or evil, are taken home to that God who gave them life."
> — [Alma 40:11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/40.md)

Every spirit — good or evil — is "taken home to that God who gave them life." The phrase "taken home" is striking. Home. Not "sent away." Not "dispatched." *Home.* Death is a homecoming.

Alma then describes two states:

**Paradise:**
> "The spirits of those who are righteous are received into a state of happiness, which is called paradise, a state of rest, a state of peace, where they shall rest from all their troubles and from all care, and sorrow."
> — [Alma 40:12](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/40.md)

**Webster 1828 — "paradise"**: *"The abode of sanctified souls after death ... A place of bliss; a region of supreme felicity or delight."* Webster directly references Luke 23:43 — Jesus's promise to the thief on the cross: "To day shalt thou be with me in paradise."

**Webster 1828 — "rest"**: In its verb form, *"to cease from action, motion, work, or performance of any kind; to be free from that which harasses or disturbs; be quiet or still; be undisturbed."* Rest from "all their troubles and from all care, and sorrow." Not cessation of existence — cessation of suffering.

Four descriptors pile on each other: happiness, rest, peace, freedom from trouble, care, and sorrow. Whatever paradise is, it is the opposite of mortality's burdens. For someone who is sick and suffering — this is the promise. The pain *stops*.

**Outer darkness:**
> "The spirits of the wicked, yea, who are evil—for behold, they have no part nor portion of the Spirit of the Lord; for behold, they chose evil works rather than good; therefore the spirit of the devil did enter into them, and take possession of their house—and these shall be cast out into outer darkness; there shall be weeping, and wailing, and gnashing of teeth, and this because of their own iniquity, being led captive by the will of the devil."
> — [Alma 40:13](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/40.md)

The contrast is absolute. The righteous rest; the wicked suffer. But note Alma's framing — the wicked suffer "because of their own iniquity, being led captive by the will of the devil." They *chose* evil. The state of darkness is the consequence of choices, not an arbitrary punishment.

Both states are temporary. Both await the resurrection:

> "Thus they remain in this state, as well as the righteous in paradise, until the time of their resurrection."
> — [Alma 40:14](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/40.md)

### Luke 23:43 — "Today Shalt Thou Be with Me in Paradise"

The most intimate reference to the spirit world comes from Christ Himself, on the cross:

> "And he said unto Jesus, Lord, remember me when thou comest into thy kingdom. And Jesus said unto him, Verily I say unto thee, To day shalt thou be with me in paradise."
> — [Luke 23:42-43](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/23.md)

A dying criminal, who had just rebuked his companion and acknowledged his own guilt, turns to Jesus — not in sophistication, but in raw faith: "remember me." And Jesus's response is immediate, personal, specific. Not "someday" but *today*. Not "perhaps" but *verily*. Not "if you qualify" but *with me*.

This is the Savior's own description of where He went when He died. He went to paradise. And He took company with Him.

### 1 Peter 3:18-20 and 4:6 — The Original Teaching

Peter's epistles provide the original New Testament foundation for spirit world theology:

> "For Christ also hath once suffered for sins, the just for the unjust, that he might bring us to God, being put to death in the flesh, but quickened by the Spirit: By which also he went and preached unto the spirits in prison; Which sometime were disobedient, when once the longsuffering of God waited in the days of Noah."
> — [1 Peter 3:18-20](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-pet/3.md)

**Webster 1828 — "quicken"**: *"To make alive; to vivify; to revive or resuscitate, as from death or an inanimate state."* Christ was "put to death in the flesh, but quickened by the Spirit" — His body died but His spirit was made alive, vivified. And in that vivified state, He went and preached.

To whom? "The spirits in prison" — and Peter specifies those who were disobedient in Noah's day. The most wicked generation in scripture. If Christ preached to *them*, there is no one beyond His reach.

Then Peter gives the rationale:

> "For for this cause was the gospel preached also to them that are dead, that they might be judged according to men in the flesh, but live according to God in the spirit."
> — [1 Peter 4:6](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-pet/4.md)

The purpose: that the dead "might be judged according to men in the flesh, but live according to God in the spirit." The standard for judgment is the same whether you hear the gospel in mortality or after death. And the result can be the same: living "according to God in the spirit." This is the theological foundation for the entire work for the dead.

### D&C 138 — The Vision of the Redemption of the Dead

This is the crown jewel of spirit world revelation, and it's extraordinarily detailed. Joseph F. Smith received it on October 3, 1918 — just weeks before his own death. He was pondering the very Peter passages we just read.

**What he saw first — the righteous assembled:**

> "There were gathered together in one place an innumerable company of the spirits of the just, who had been faithful in the testimony of Jesus while they lived in mortality; And who had offered sacrifice in the similitude of the great sacrifice of the Son of God, and had suffered tribulation in their Redeemer's name. All these had departed the mortal life, firm in the hope of a glorious resurrection."
> — [D&C 138:12-14](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)

These are people who lived faithful lives, suffered for their faith, and died with hope. They are *not* passive clouds sitting in ethereal space. They are "gathered together," conversing, rejoicing, *waiting* for something specific — the advent of the Son of God.

> "They were filled with joy and gladness, and were rejoicing together because the day of their deliverance was at hand."
> — [D&C 138:15](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)

**Webster 1828 — "deliverance"**: *"The act of delivering or freeing from restraint, captivity, peril, and the like; rescue."* Even the righteous need *deliverance*. Even paradise is not the final state.

**Christ's arrival — declaring liberty:**

> "The Son of God appeared, declaring liberty to the captives who had been faithful; And there he preached to them the everlasting gospel, the doctrine of the resurrection and the redemption of mankind from the fall, and from individual sins on conditions of repentance."
> — [D&C 138:18-19](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)

**Webster 1828 — "captive"**: *"A prisoner taken by force or stratagem, esp., by an enemy, in war; one kept in bondage or in the power of another."* Even the righteous are "captives" — prisoners of death. Christ declares *liberty* to them.

**The critical revelation — He did NOT go to the wicked personally:**

> "But unto the wicked he did not go, and among the ungodly and the unrepentant who had defiled themselves while in the flesh, his voice was not raised; Neither did the rebellious who rejected the testimonies and the warnings of the ancient prophets behold his presence, nor look upon his face. Where these were, darkness reigned, but among the righteous there was peace."
> — [D&C 138:20-22](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)

This corrects a common assumption. Christ did *not* personally visit the wicked spirits. That raised a question for Joseph F. Smith — how could Peter say Christ preached to spirits in prison if He didn't go to them?

**The answer — organized missionary work:**

> "From among the righteous, he organized his forces and appointed messengers, clothed with power and authority, and commissioned them to go forth and carry the light of the gospel to them that were in darkness, even to all the spirits of men; and thus was the gospel preached to the dead."
> — [D&C 138:30](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)

Christ organized the righteous to teach the wicked. The work of salvation in the spirit world is *organized*, *authorized*, and *empowered*. It's not casual or accidental. These messengers were "clothed with power and authority" — priesthood language. This is structured missionary work.

**What they teach:**

> "These were taught faith in God, repentance from sin, vicarious baptism for the remission of sins, the gift of the Holy Ghost by the laying on of hands, And all other principles of the gospel that were necessary for them to know in order to qualify themselves that they might be judged according to men in the flesh, but live according to God in the spirit."
> — [D&C 138:33-34](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)

The same gospel. The same principles. Including "vicarious baptism" — they're taught about the temple work that must be done for them on earth. The purpose is to "qualify themselves" — there's the "prepare/qualify" language from Phase 2 again.

**Who was there — the great assembly:**

Joseph F. Smith saw Adam, Eve ("our glorious Mother Eve"), Abel, Seth, Noah, Shem, Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, Isaiah, Ezekiel, Daniel, Malachi, Elijah — and "many more, even the prophets who dwelt among the Nephites" ([D&C 138:38-49](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)).

Eve is called "glorious" and appears alongside Adam as a co-leader. Her "faithful daughters who had lived through the ages and worshiped the true and living God" are with her ([D&C 138:39](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)). The spirit world is not a hierarchy of male prophets — it includes the faithful of every kind.

**The bondage of separation:**

> "For the dead had looked upon the long absence of their spirits from their bodies as a bondage."
> — [D&C 138:50](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)

**Webster 1828 — "bondage"**: *"The state of being bound; condition of being under restraint; restraint of personal liberty by compulsion; involuntary servitude."*

This is remarkable. Even the righteous in paradise — those in a state of rest and peace — consider *disembodiment itself* a bondage. The separation of spirit from body is experienced as *restraint*. This connects directly to D&C 93:33-34 from Phase 2: "spirit and element, inseparably connected, receive a fulness of joy; and when separated, man cannot receive a fulness of joy." The spirit world, even at its best, is *incomplete*. The body is not a prison the soul escapes at death — the body is part of what makes fulness possible. Its absence is felt as lack.

**The work continues beyond the first generation:**

> "I beheld that the faithful elders of this dispensation, when they depart from mortal life, continue their labors in the preaching of the gospel of repentance and redemption, through the sacrifice of the Only Begotten Son of God, among those who are in darkness and under the bondage of sin in the great world of the spirits of the dead."
> — [D&C 138:57](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)

The work didn't end with Christ's visit. Faithful elders from our dispensation — Joseph Smith, Hyrum Smith, Brigham Young, and others — continue the work in the spirit world *right now*. The school never closes.

**Redemption is real:**

> "The dead who repent will be redeemed, through obedience to the ordinances of the house of God, And after they have paid the penalty of their transgressions, and are washed clean, shall receive a reward according to their works, for they are heirs of salvation."
> — [D&C 138:58-59](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)

Three requirements: repentance, vicarious ordinances, and paying the penalty. But the end result is clear: "heirs of salvation." The spirit world is not a dead end. It's a place where redemption happens.

### D&C 131:7-8 — All Spirit Is Matter

A short but stunning statement from the Prophet Joseph Smith:

> "There is no such thing as immaterial matter. All spirit is matter, but it is more fine or pure, and can only be discerned by purer eyes; We cannot see it; but when our bodies are purified we shall see that it is all matter."
> — [D&C 131:7-8](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/131.md)

Spirit is not "immaterial" — it's *finer* matter. The spirit world isn't a ghostly, insubstantial realm. It's a *real* place made of *real* (though finer) substance. This connects to the truth study's framework — the spectrum from coarse to fine. Spirits are real beings made of fine matter, with real experiences, in a real environment. The person who dies doesn't become a vague essence. They remain *themselves*, in a body of spirit matter, capable of thought, feeling, communication, and work.

### Ecclesiastes 12:7 — The Spirit Returns

> "Then shall the dust return to the earth as it was: and the spirit shall return unto God who gave it."
> — [Ecclesiastes 12:7](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/eccl/12.md)

The spirit *returns*. It goes back to its origin. Death is not annihilation — it's a return journey. The spirit was *with* God before mortality, and it returns to God's domain after mortality. This was Solomon's ancient witness, centuries before Christ confirmed it on the cross and Peter expanded it.

### D&C 76:73-74 — Afterward Received

In the great Vision of the kingdoms of glory, Joseph and Sidney saw among the terrestrial kingdom:

> "And also they who are the spirits of men kept in prison, whom the Son visited, and preached the gospel unto them, that they might be judged according to men in the flesh; Who received not the testimony of Jesus in the flesh, but afterwards received it."
> — [D&C 76:73-74](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/76.md)

"Afterwards received it." They didn't accept the gospel in life — but they accepted it *after*. And they receive a kingdom of glory. Not the highest — they are not "valiant in the testimony of Jesus" ([D&C 76:79](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/76.md)) — but a glory nonetheless. The spirit world is a place where people *can* receive what they didn't receive in mortality. The door doesn't close at death for everyone.

### D&C 130:2 — That Same Sociality

> "And that same sociality which exists among us here will exist among us there, only it will be coupled with eternal glory, which glory we do not now enjoy."
> — [D&C 130:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/130.md)

President Oaks notes in his talk that this may describe a kingdom of glory rather than the spirit world specifically. But the principle resonates either way: relationships persist. The connections we build in mortality don't dissolve when the body dies. We remain *social* beings in whatever state follows. For a friend facing death — *you won't be alone there*.

### Revisiting Alma 34:34 — "That Same Spirit"

Phase 2 read this verse as a warning about procrastination. Now it becomes directly relevant to Phase 3:

> "That same spirit which doth possess your bodies at the time that ye go out of this life, that same spirit will have power to possess your body in that eternal world."
> — [Alma 34:34](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/34.md)

What does "that same spirit" mean? It's not saying you *can't change* — D&C 138 says the dead who *repent* will be redeemed. It's saying the *disposition* carries forward. The direction you're heading, the spirit you've cultivated, the orientation of your soul — that continues. If you've spent mortality cultivating gratitude, patience, love — those come with you. If you've spent it cultivating bitterness, selfishness, pride — those come too.

This is the school metaphor from Phase 2 extended past graduation. What you learned shapes what you carry into the next stage. But it's not absolute — D&C 138 teaches that even the wicked can repent in the spirit world, though it requires paying "the penalty of their transgressions" ([D&C 138:59](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)).

## Prophetic Witness

### President Dallin H. Oaks — "Trust in the Lord" (October 2019)

[President Oaks](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2019/10/17oaks.md) gives the most careful, authoritative modern treatment of what we know about the spirit world — and what we *don't* know.

His key teaching: **everyone in the spirit world is in some form of bondage.** The righteous experience the bondage of disembodiment — "the long absence of their spirits from their bodies" ([D&C 138:50](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)). The wicked experience an *additional* bondage — the bondage of unrepented sin, described as "prison" ([1 Peter 3:19](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-pet/3.md)).

His framework for the spirit world's purpose: "The work of salvation in the spirit world consists of freeing spirits from what the scriptures frequently describe as 'bondage.'" The gospel is preached to the "ignorant, the unrepentant, and the rebellious so they can be freed from their bondage and go forward to the blessings a loving Heavenly Father has in store for them."

The righteous who have accepted the gospel but lack ordinances have their own kind of bondage — they "await—and perhaps even be allowed to prompt—the performance of their proxy ordinances on earth." Temple work is tied directly to liberating spirits.

And then Oaks' pastoral wisdom: there is much about the spirit world we simply don't know. His counsel is to not teach personal spiritual experiences or near-death accounts as official doctrine, and to trust the Lord with unanswered questions. Two answers for every question about the spirit world:

1. "God loves His children and will surely do what is best for each of us."
2. "Trust in the Lord with all thine heart; and lean not unto thine own understanding" ([Proverbs 3:5-6](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/prov/3.md)).

He also quotes Joseph Smith's teaching that the "spirits of the just are exalted to a greater and more glorious work" in the spirit world — they "are not far from us, and know and understand our thoughts, feelings, and motions."

### Sister Reyna I. Aburto — "The Grave Has No Victory" (April 2021)

[Sister Aburto](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2021/04/42aburto.md) brings the personal comfort dimension that this study's driving question demands. She lost her brother at age nine in an earthquake — devastatingly, suddenly. She spent years asking: "What happened to my brother? Where is he? Where did he go? Will I ever see him again?"

Decades later, the Spirit showed her the meaning of a recurring image she'd had since childhood — her brother knocking at the door, alive: "I am not dead. I am alive." She testified: "I had received a witness that my brother's spirit is not dead; he is alive. He is still progressing in his eternal existence."

She quotes President Nelson: "The only way to take sorrow out of death is to take love out of life." And then: "Death is a necessary component of our eternal existence. No one knows when it will come, but it is essential to God's great plan of happiness."

Her closing testimony reaches directly into the comfort thread:

> "Through the redeeming Atonement and glorious Resurrection of Jesus Christ, broken hearts can be healed, anguish can become peace, and distress can become hope. He can embrace us in His arms of mercy, comforting, empowering, and healing each of us."

## Synthesis: What the Spirit World Teaches Us

### Paradise Is Real — and It's Rest

The scriptural witness is consistent and clear. Paradise is:

- **A state of happiness** ([Alma 40:12](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/40.md))
- **A state of rest** — from troubles, care, sorrow ([Alma 40:12](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/40.md))
- **A state of peace** ([D&C 138:22](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md))
- **A place of joy and gladness** ([D&C 138:15](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md))
- **Where Christ promised to go** — "today shalt thou be with me" ([Luke 23:43](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/23.md))
- **A gathering place** — not isolated, but *together* ([D&C 138:12](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md))
- **An active place** — conversing, rejoicing, working ([D&C 138:15, 57](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md))
- **A real place** — made of spirit matter, which is "more fine or pure" ([D&C 131:7](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/131.md))

For someone who is sick and suffering: the pain ends at death. What follows is rest, peace, joy, gathering with the righteous, and continuing in meaningful work. This is not wishful thinking — it's the word of God through multiple prophets and Christ Himself.

### The Spirit World Is Not the Final Destination

Even paradise has a kind of bondage — the absence of the body. The righteous wait for resurrection, looking upon disembodiment as restraint ([D&C 138:50](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)). This tells us:

1. **The body matters.** D&C 93:33's "spirit and element, inseparably connected, receive a fulness of joy" is felt experientially in the spirit world. Being without a body is *incomplete*. The soul that shouted for joy at the prospect of a body (Job 38:7) still yearns for it even in paradise.

2. **The spirit world is a waystation, not a terminus.** It's the space between death and resurrection — a necessary stage but not the end. Like Phase 2's "probationary state," the spirit world serves a purpose: rest for the righteous, teaching and repentance for the unprepared, and waiting for the resurrection that will make everyone whole again.

3. **Progression continues.** The faithful "continue their labors" ([D&C 138:57](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)). The wicked can repent and be redeemed ([D&C 138:58-59](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)). The school of Phase 2 doesn't close at death — it transitions to a new campus.

### Two Kinds of Bondage, Two Kinds of Liberation

President Oaks' framework is clarifying:

| Bondage | Who | Liberation |
|---------|-----|------------|
| Disembodiment — separation of spirit from body | All spirits, including the righteous | Resurrection |
| Sin — "spirits in prison" | The wicked, unrepentant, or ignorant | Gospel teaching + repentance + vicarious ordinances |

The first bondage is universal — it's inherent in death itself. The second is specific to those who carry unresolved sin. Each has its own liberation mechanism. Both are certain to come through Christ.

### The Comfort Answer

For the friends who are sick — what does the gospel *actually promise*?

1. **Death is a homecoming.** "Taken home to that God who gave them life" ([Alma 40:11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/40.md)). The spirit returns to where it came from ([Ecclesiastes 12:7](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/eccl/12.md)).

2. **The righteous enter rest.** "A state of happiness ... a state of rest, a state of peace, where they shall rest from all their troubles and from all care, and sorrow" ([Alma 40:12](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/40.md)). The pain *stops*.

3. **They are not alone.** An "innumerable company of the spirits of the just" are gathered together ([D&C 138:12](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)). They converse, rejoice, sing praises. Sociality persists ([D&C 130:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/130.md)).

4. **They continue meaningful work.** The faithful don't sit idle — they labor in the work of salvation ([D&C 138:57](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)). Life doesn't end; it *expands*.

5. **They are not far from us.** Joseph Smith taught that the righteous dead "are not far from us, and know and understand our thoughts, feelings, and motions" (quoted by [President Oaks](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2019/10/17oaks.md)).

6. **The separation is temporary.** Resurrection will reunite spirit and body permanently — "bone to his bone, and the sinews and the flesh upon them, the spirit and the body to be united never again to be divided, that they might receive a fulness of joy" ([D&C 138:17](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)).

7. **He consecrates the affliction.** From Phase 2: "he shall consecrate thine afflictions for thy gain" ([2 Nephi 2:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)). The suffering of illness — real and terrible as it is — is consecrated. It's not meaningless, even when it feels that way.

8. **"I am not dead. I am alive."** Sister Aburto's brother's message, carried to her by the Spirit across four decades. The people who die are not extinguished. They live.

### Connections to Existing Studies

- **Truth study**: D&C 131:7-8 says spirit is "more fine or pure" matter — this maps directly onto the truth study's matter spectrum. The spirit world exists at a finer frequency of the same reality we inhabit now.
- **Intelligence study**: The uncreated intelligence (D&C 93:29) clothed in spirit matter continues after death. The core identity persists. "That same spirit" (Alma 34:34) is the agent — the intelligence — and its developed character.
- **Phase 1 (Pre-mortal)**: D&C 138:56 says spirits "received their first lessons in the world of spirits and were prepared to come forth." The spirit world is where we came *from* and where we return to. The pre-mortal world of spirits and the post-mortal spirit world may share more in common than we usually assume.
- **Phase 2 (Mortality)**: Mortality's "school" transitions to continued learning in the spirit world. The faithful teach. The ignorant learn. The rebellious can repent. The school's curriculum doesn't end — it enters a new phase.
- **Way-Truth-Life study**: The veil separating the spirit world from mortality is the same veil the temple represents. Death is a passage through the veil — and on the other side, the keeper of the gate "employeth no servant there" ([2 Nephi 9:41](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/9.md)).
- **Covenants study**: Vicarious ordinances in the temple are literally tied to liberation in the spirit world. When we do temple work, we are freeing captives ([D&C 138:31](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/138.md)). The covenant relationship extends beyond the veil.

## Open Questions for Later Phases

- **Phase 4 (Vicarious Work)**: D&C 138:33 teaches that spirits are taught about "vicarious baptism for the remission of sins." How does the temple work on earth connect mechanically to what's happening in the spirit world? What does D&C 128 add?
- **Phase 5 (Resurrection & Judgment)**: D&C 138:50 — the righteous consider disembodiment a bondage. How does resurrection resolve this? What does "every limb and joint restored to its proper and perfect frame" (Alma 40:23) mean for those who die with broken bodies? This is crucial for the sick friends.
- **Phase 6 (Kingdoms)**: D&C 76:73-74 — those who "received not the testimony of Jesus in the flesh, but afterwards received it" inherit the terrestrial kingdom. What's the relationship between *when* you accept and *where* you end up? Is timing a factor in glory?
- **Unresolved question on Alma 34:34**: If "that same spirit" carries forward, and if the wicked can repent in the spirit world (D&C 138:58), then what does "the night of darkness wherein there can be no labor performed" (Alma 34:33) actually mean? Is Amulek warning about lost *opportunity* (don't waste the advantage of mortality) rather than impossibility (you can never change)? This seems likely, but it deserves more exploration.

### Webster 1828 Definitions
- **paradise**: "The abode of sanctified souls after death … A place of bliss; a region of supreme felicity or delight"
- **bondage**: "The state of being bound; condition of being under restraint; restraint of personal liberty by compulsion; involuntary servitude"
- **captive**: "A prisoner taken by force or stratagem, esp., by an enemy, in war; one kept in bondage or in the power of another"
- **rest**: "To cease from action, motion, work, or performance of any kind; to be free from that which harasses or disturbs"
- **deliverance**: "The act of delivering or freeing from restraint, captivity, peril, and the like; rescue"
- **quicken**: "To make alive; to vivify; to revive or resuscitate, as from death or an inanimate state"
