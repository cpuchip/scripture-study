# Phase 2: The Fall & Mortality as Divine Education

> *"He shall consecrate thine afflictions for thy gain."*
> — [2 Nephi 2:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)

---

## Driving Questions

1. Is mortality a "probation" (testing/proving) or a "school" (education/transformation) — or both?
2. What does Webster 1828 say about "probation"? Does it carry educational connotations we've lost?
3. What does 2 Nephi 2 — Lehi's grand sermon — teach about *why* the Fall was necessary?
4. What is the relationship between agency, opposition, and growth?
5. How does Moses 5-6 (gospel taught to Adam) reframe mortality as designed for learning, not just enduring?

---

## The Scriptures Speak

### Lehi's Grand Theology — 2 Nephi 2

This is the most complete single-chapter theology of the Fall in all of scripture. Lehi builds an interlocking argument that is almost mathematical in its precision:

**Opposition is structurally necessary.**

> "For it must needs be, that there is an opposition in all things. If not so, my firstborn in the wilderness, righteousness could not be brought to pass, neither wickedness, neither holiness nor misery, neither good nor bad. Wherefore, all things must needs be a compound in one; wherefore, if it should be one body it must needs remain as dead, having no life neither death, nor corruption nor incorruption, happiness nor misery, neither sense nor insensibility."
> — [2 Nephi 2:11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)

This isn't just a philosophical observation — Lehi says without opposition, there's no *existence*. A "compound in one" with no differentiation remains "as dead." This connects directly to D&C 93:30 from our Phase 1 finding: "otherwise there is no existence." Both passages argue that agency — and the opposition that gives agency its meaning — aren't optional features. They're structural requirements of reality.

**Things to act vs. things to be acted upon.**

> "And to bring about his eternal purposes in the end of man, after he had created our first parents... it must needs be that there was an opposition; even the forbidden fruit in opposition to the tree of life; the one being sweet and the other bitter. Wherefore, the Lord God gave unto man that he should act for himself. Wherefore, man could not act for himself save it should be that he was enticed by the one or the other."
> — [2 Nephi 2:15-16](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)

Two categories of existence: things that act, and things acted upon. Humans are the first kind. And to *act* requires being *enticed* — drawn toward something. Without options, there is no action, only drift.

**Webster 1828 — "entice"**: *"To draw on, by exciting hope or desire; to allure; to attract."* This isn't coercion — it's attraction. God doesn't force us to choose good; He *entices* us toward it. Satan entices toward evil. We stand between two attractive forces and choose.

**The Fall was necessary — and designed.**

> "And now, behold, if Adam had not transgressed he would not have fallen, but he would have remained in the garden of Eden. And all things which were created must have remained in the same state in which they were after they were created; and they must have remained forever, and had no end. And they would have had no children; wherefore they would have remained in a state of innocence, having no joy, for they knew no misery; doing no good, for they knew no sin."
> — [2 Nephi 2:22-23](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)

Without the Fall: no children, no joy, no moral knowledge. Innocence without capacity. The Garden was not the destination — it was the *starting line*. Remaining in Eden would have been a permanent "compound in one."

And then the verse that makes the whole plan click:

> "Adam fell that men might be; and men are, that they might have joy."
> — [2 Nephi 2:25](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)

Two purposes stacked: the Fall gives existence ("men might be"), and existence gives access to joy ("might have joy"). The Fall isn't a disaster — it's a doorway. And beyond it, the purpose isn't survival or endurance. It's *joy*.

**Freedom is the gift of the Atonement.**

> "And the Messiah cometh in the fulness of time, that he may redeem the children of men from the fall. And because that they are redeemed from the fall they have become free forever, knowing good from evil; to act for themselves and not to be acted upon."
> — [2 Nephi 2:26](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)

The Atonement doesn't just pardon — it *frees*. It moves us from "acted upon" to "acting." Without redemption, the Fall would leave us permanently subject to death and the devil. With redemption, we are free to choose: "liberty and eternal life" or "captivity and death" ([2 Nephi 2:27](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)).

**And the opening frame — consecrated affliction.**

Lehi doesn't begin his theology with an abstraction. He begins with his son Jacob's suffering:

> "Thou art my firstborn in the days of my tribulation in the wilderness. And behold, in thy childhood thou hast suffered afflictions and much sorrow, because of the rudeness of thy brethren. Nevertheless, Jacob, my firstborn in the wilderness, thou knowest the greatness of God; and he shall consecrate thine afflictions for thy gain."
> — [2 Nephi 2:1-2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)

**Webster 1828 — "consecrate"**: *"Consecrated; devoted; dedicated; sacred."* God doesn't just *allow* affliction — He *consecrates* it, makes it sacred, devoted to a purpose. The suffering becomes gain. This is the school metaphor in its most intimate form: the curriculum includes hard things, and those hard things are *holy* when received by faith.

### Alma's Framework: Probation as Preparation

Alma 12 and Alma 42 provide the explicit "probationary state" language. But look at what they actually say:

> "Nevertheless there was a space granted unto man in which he might repent; therefore this life became a probationary state; a time to prepare to meet God; a time to prepare for that endless state which has been spoken of by us, which is after the resurrection of the dead."
> — [Alma 12:24](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/12.md)

Three phrases describing the same thing: probationary state = *time to prepare to meet God* = *time to prepare for the endless state*. "Probation" and "preparation" are used synonymously. Alma doesn't mean probation as punishment or mere testing — he means it as *qualification time*.

Alma 42 makes this even clearer. Alma explicitly calls the probationary state a *preparatory state*:

> "Therefore, as they had become carnal, sensual, and devilish, by nature, this probationary state became a state for them to prepare; it became a preparatory state."
> — [Alma 42:10](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/42.md)

Probationary = preparatory. The words are interchangeable for Alma. And what are they preparing for? To repent and serve God ([Alma 42:4](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/42.md)), which is how they qualify for mercy.

**Webster 1828 — "probation"**: *"Moral trial; the state of man in the present life, in which he has the opportunity of proving his character, and becoming qualified for a happier state."*

This is revelatory. Webster's 1828 definition carries *both* meanings — "proving his character" (testing) AND "becoming qualified" (education/transformation). The word itself, in the language of the Restoration era, was never just about testing. It was about *qualification through trial*. When Alma says "probationary state," his contemporary readers would have heard: "a time for proving your character and becoming qualified for something better."

### Amulek's Urgency — Alma 34

Amulek adds the urgency dimension:

> "For behold, this life is the time for men to prepare to meet God; yea, behold the day of this life is the day for men to perform their labors."
> — [Alma 34:32](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/34.md)

> "I beseech of you that ye do not procrastinate the day of your repentance until the end; for after this day of life, which is given us to prepare for eternity, behold, if we do not improve our time while in this life, then cometh the night of darkness wherein there can be no labor performed."
> — [Alma 34:33](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/34.md)

**Webster 1828 — "prepare"**: *"To fit, adapt, or qualify for a particular purpose or condition; to make ready."* Again the educational connotation: fit, adapt, qualify. We're not just being tested — we're *becoming qualified* for something.

And then the hard verse:

> "Ye cannot say, when ye are brought to that awful crisis, that I will repent, that I will return to my God. Nay, ye cannot say this; for that same spirit which doth possess your bodies at the time that ye go out of this life, that same spirit will have power to possess your body in that eternal world."
> — [Alma 34:34](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/34.md)

This doesn't say people can't change after death (that's Phase 3's question). It says the *disposition* you've cultivated carries forward. The habits, the character, the orientation of your soul — that's what "the same spirit" means. This is the school metaphor again: what you learn (or refuse to learn) shapes what you become.

### Alma 42: Justice, Mercy, and the Great Plan

Alma 42 is Alma's finest theological hour — written to his son Corianton who was troubled by the apparent *injustice* of God's punishment. Alma's answer reframes the entire question:

> "Now, how could a man repent except he should sin? How could he sin if there was no law? How could there be a law save there was a punishment?"
> — [Alma 42:17](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/42.md)

This is a logical chain: punishment → law → sin → repentance. Each step requires the previous one. Without the capacity to fail, there's no capacity to repent. And without repentance, mercy has no object and growth has no mechanism.

> "And thus God bringeth about his great and eternal purposes, which were prepared from the foundation of the world. And thus cometh about the salvation and the redemption of men, and also their destruction and misery."
> — [Alma 42:26](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/42.md)

Same system produces both salvation and destruction. The plan doesn't guarantee one outcome — it creates the *conditions* in which either can happen, depending on choices. That's true education: an environment where genuine learning is possible precisely because genuine failure is also possible.

> "Mercy claimeth the penitent, and mercy cometh because of the atonement."
> — [Alma 42:23](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/42.md)

And the mechanism for recovery from failure is the Atonement. The school has a tutor.

### The D&C 29 Framework: All Things Are Spiritual

D&C 29 frames the necessity of opposition with startling clarity:

> "And it must needs be that the devil should tempt the children of men, or they could not be agents unto themselves; for if they never should have bitter they could not know the sweet."
> — [D&C 29:39](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/29.md)

Without temptation, no agency. Without bitter, no sweet. The existence of evil isn't a flaw in the plan — it's a *requirement* of the plan. Opposition isn't what went wrong; it's what makes the plan work.

> "And thus did I, the Lord God, appoint unto man the days of his probation—that by his natural death he might be raised in immortality unto eternal life, even as many as would believe."
> — [D&C 29:43](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/29.md)

"Appoint unto man the days of his probation." God *designed* probation — it's not punishment for the Fall. It's the curriculum. And the purpose is clear: "raised in immortality unto eternal life." Probation leads somewhere. It's pointed at a destination.

And surrounding this, the stunning declaration that *all* divine law is spiritual:

> "Verily I say unto you that all things unto me are spiritual, and not at any time have I given unto you a law which was temporal; neither any man, nor the children of men... for my commandments are spiritual; they are not natural nor temporal, neither carnal nor sensual."
> — [D&C 29:34-35](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/29.md)

There's no such thing as a purely temporal experience in God's view. Every mortal experience — physical, emotional, relational — is spiritual education. The school of mortality has no secular subjects.

### Moses 5-6: The Gospel Taught from Day One

This is where the "school" metaphor becomes unmistakable. Adam and Eve don't stumble into mortality blind. They are *taught immediately*.

> "And he gave unto them commandments, that they should worship the Lord their God, and should offer the firstlings of their flocks, for an offering unto the Lord. And Adam was obedient unto the commandments of the Lord."
> — [Moses 5:5](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/5.md)

> "And after many days an angel of the Lord appeared unto Adam, saying: Why dost thou offer sacrifices unto the Lord? And Adam said unto him: I know not, save the Lord commanded me. And then the angel spake, saying: This thing is a similitude of the sacrifice of the Only Begotten of the Father."
> — [Moses 5:6-7](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/5.md)

Adam obeys *before understanding*. He offers sacrifice because commanded. Then understanding follows: it's a *similitude* of Christ. This is the pedagogy of mortality — obedience first, comprehension second. Faith precedes understanding. The school teaches through doing.

And then these extraordinary responses:

> "Blessed be the name of God, for because of my transgression my eyes are opened, and in this life I shall have joy, and again in the flesh I shall see God."
> — [Moses 5:10](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/5.md)

> "Were it not for our transgression we never should have had seed, and never should have known good and evil, and the joy of our redemption, and the eternal life which God giveth unto all the obedient."
> — [Moses 5:11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/5.md)

Adam and Eve look back at the Fall — the thing that brought death, pain, labor, sorrow — and they *thank God for it*. Their eyes are opened. They know good and evil. They have children. They know the joy of redemption. They see the Fall not as catastrophe but as *commencement*.

**Webster 1828 — "transgression"**: *"The act of transgressing, or of passing over or beyond any law."* The word literally means "to pass beyond." Adam and Eve passed beyond the boundary of the Garden — and in doing so, passed into the realm of growth, experience, and eventual joy.

Moses 6 then delivers the full gospel to Adam:

> "Inasmuch as thy children are conceived in sin, even so when they begin to grow up, sin conceiveth in their hearts, and they taste the bitter, that they may know to prize the good. And it is given unto them to know good from evil; wherefore they are agents unto themselves."
> — [Moses 6:55-56](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md)

Children taste the bitter *so that* they may prize the good. The bitter isn't incidental — it's pedagogical. And then: "they are agents unto themselves." Agency + opposition + the capacity to taste bitter and prize good = the conditions for moral education.

Then the complete plan is named:

> "And now, behold, I say unto you: This is the plan of salvation unto all men, through the blood of mine Only Begotten, who shall come in the meridian of time."
> — [Moses 6:62](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md)

And just before that, the birth metaphor:

> "By reason of transgression cometh the fall, which fall bringeth death, and inasmuch as ye were born into the world by water, and blood, and the spirit, which I have made, and so became of dust a living soul, even so ye must be born again into the kingdom of heaven, of water, and of the Spirit, and be cleansed by blood, even the blood of mine Only Begotten; that ye might be sanctified from all sin, and enjoy the words of eternal life in this world, and eternal life in the world to come."
> — [Moses 6:59](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md)

Physical birth mirrors spiritual rebirth. Water, blood, spirit — the elements of both birth events. And the promise: "enjoy the words of eternal life *in this world*, and eternal life in the world to come." The school isn't just preparation for later — it offers enjoyment *now*.

### The Natural Man — Mosiah 3:19

King Benjamin's angel delivers the curriculum description:

> "For the natural man is an enemy to God, and has been from the fall of Adam, and will be, forever and ever, unless he yields to the enticings of the Holy Spirit, and putteth off the natural man and becometh a saint through the atonement of Christ the Lord, and becometh as a child, submissive, meek, humble, patient, full of love, willing to submit to all things which the Lord seeth fit to inflict upon him, even as a child doth submit to his father."
> — [Mosiah 3:19](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/3.md)

This is the transformation agenda. Mortality's purpose is to take the "natural man" — the default biological/fallen condition — and through yielding to the Spirit and the Atonement, *become* something else: a saint. Patient. Full of love. Submissive to the Father.

"Willing to submit to all things which the Lord seeth fit to *inflict* upon him." The word "inflict" is harsh. But a father doesn't inflict things on a child out of cruelty — he inflicts discipline, challenge, training. The school has hard lessons. The student's posture is to trust the Teacher.

### D&C 93:33-34 — Spirit and Element

The purpose of a physical body connects directly to mortality's "school":

> "For man is spirit. The elements are eternal, and spirit and element, inseparably connected, receive a fulness of joy; and when separated, man cannot receive a fulness of joy."
> — [D&C 93:33-34](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93.md)

The fulness of joy — the destination Lehi named in 2 Nephi 2:25 — requires *both* spirit and element connected. Mortality isn't just about making choices. It's about learning to be an embodied being — learning to integrate spirit and body into a unified soul. That's why we shouted for joy at the prospect of bodies. That's why resurrection matters. The body isn't a prison; it's half of what makes the fulness possible.

## Prophetic Witness

### Elder David A. Bednar — "We Will Prove Them Herewith" (October 2020)

[Elder Bednar](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2020/10/12bednar.md) explicitly frames mortality as a school with tests:

> "Tests in the school of mortality are a vital element of our eternal progression. Interestingly, however, the word *test* is not found even one time in the scriptural text of the standard works in English. Rather, words such as *prove, examine,* and *try* are used."

He provides the key insight that testing and learning are the same process: "An effective test helps us to compare what we need to know with what we actually know... it also provides a standard against which we can evaluate our learning and development." Tests aren't separate from learning — they *are* the learning mechanism.

His two principles — preparation and pressing forward — mirror the probation/preparation language of Alma exactly. And his example of the grieving missionary father ("As far as the gospel of Jesus Christ is concerned, our family is all in") demonstrates what "proving" looks like in real life: maintaining faith through suffering, not despite it but *through* it.

### President Dallin H. Oaks — "Divine Helps for Mortality" (April 2025)

[President Oaks](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2025/04/47oaks.md) delivers the most beautiful framing of opposition:

> "Essential to God's great plan for the mortal growth of His children was for them to experience 'opposition in all things.' Just as our physical muscles cannot be developed or maintained without straining against the law of gravity, so mortal growth requires us to strain against Satan's temptations and other mortal opposition."

The gravity metaphor is precise: gravity isn't the enemy of muscles — it's the *condition* that makes strength possible. Likewise, opposition isn't the enemy of growth — it's the resistance that makes growth possible. Remove gravity and muscles atrophy. Remove opposition and souls atrophy.

Oaks then catalogs the "divine helps" for mortals: (1) the Light of Christ given to every person, (2) commandments/ordinances/covenants, (3) manifestations of the Holy Ghost, and (4) the gift of the Holy Ghost. The school isn't unsupervised. God provides tutors, textbooks, and a support system.

## Synthesis: The Probation Question Answered

### The False Dichotomy Dissolved

The driving question — is mortality a probation or a school? — is answered decisively by the scriptures and by Webster 1828. It was *never* a dichotomy.

**Webster 1828 "probation"** = "the opportunity of proving his character, **and becoming qualified** for a happier state."

**Alma 42:10** = "this probationary state became a state for them to **prepare**; it became a **preparatory state**."

**Abraham 3:25** (Phase 1, Webster "prove") = "to try **or ascertain** by experiment" + "to **gain experience** of the good or evil of."

**Bednar** = "Tests in the **school** of mortality are a vital element of our eternal progression."

**Oaks** = Opposition is like gravity for muscles — the **mechanism of growth**, not its enemy.

The proving IS the educating. The test IS the curriculum. When you prove metal, you don't just pass/fail — you *refine* it. When you prove a student, the test itself teaches. Mortality is a probation-school: a designed environment where character is both tested and formed through the same experiences.

### The Architecture of Mortality

From the scriptures, here's what mortality is designed to do:

1. **Provide embodiment** — Spirit + element = fulness of joy ([D&C 93:33](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93.md)). The body itself is the first gift of mortality.

2. **Create conditions for agency** — Opposition in all things ([2 Nephi 2:11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)), enticement from both sides ([2 Nephi 2:16](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)), the capacity to act rather than be acted upon.

3. **Teach through experience** — Tasting bitter to prize the good ([Moses 6:55](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md)), consecrated afflictions ([2 Nephi 2:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)), proving/examining/trying.

4. **Qualify for joy** — Adam fell that men might be; men are that they might have joy ([2 Nephi 2:25](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)). Joy is the *purpose*, not just a byproduct.

5. **Transform nature** — Natural man → saint through the Atonement ([Mosiah 3:19](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/3.md)). The goal isn't to endure mortality but to be *changed* by it.

6. **Provide gospel curriculum from day one** — Adam and Eve received commandments, sacrifice, baptism, the Holy Ghost, and the full plan ([Moses 5-6](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/5.md)). The school was never without a teacher.

7. **Offer divine helps** — Light of Christ, commandments, covenants, Holy Ghost ([Oaks, April 2025](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2025/04/47oaks.md)). The test is designed to be passable.

### The Fall Reframed

The Fall isn't a tragedy reversed by the Atonement. It's the *first step* in a designed sequence:

- **Before the Fall**: Innocence without capacity. No children, no joy, no moral knowledge ([2 Nephi 2:22-23](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)).
- **The Fall**: Passage from "acted upon" to capacity to "act for themselves" ([2 Nephi 2:26](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)). Adam and Eve gain eyes that are opened, knowledge of good and evil, children, the joy of redemption ([Moses 5:10-11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/5.md)).
- **Through the Atonement**: Freedom from the permanent consequences of the Fall (death and spiritual death), enabling genuine agency-driven growth ([2 Nephi 2:26](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md); [2 Nephi 9:6-13](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/9.md)).

The sequence is: innocence → fall → redemption → growth → glory. Each step requires the previous one. "All things have been done in the wisdom of him who knoweth all things" ([2 Nephi 2:24](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)).

### Connections to Existing Studies

- **Truth study**: The spectrum from intelligence to element — the Fall is a *descent* on this spectrum that makes ascent possible. D&C 29:34's "all things unto me are spiritual" means every mortal experience traces this spectrum.
- **Truth-Atonement study**: Christ as the mechanism for upward traversal. The Atonement doesn't just forgive — it *transforms* (Mosiah 3:19). Refinement language maps perfectly onto probation-as-qualification.
- **Intelligence study**: D&C 93:29-30's uncreated intelligence needs a body (D&C 93:33-34) to receive a fulness of joy. Mortality bridges the gap between intelligence-as-eternal and soul-as-complete (spirit + element).
- **Agency study**: 2 Nephi 2 is the definitive text on agency. "Things to act and things to be acted upon" — the fundamental partition of existence. Mortality is where we prove which category we choose.
- **Way-Truth-Life study**: The temple enacts this journey. The Garden → Fall → mortal world → veil → presence of God mirrors exactly the passage Lehi describes.
- **Moses 6 study**: We already studied Moses 6's gospel-to-Adam. It's the "course catalog" for mortality's school — the full gospel curriculum delivered at the very beginning.
- **Phase 1 findings**: Mortality is the "second estate" (Abraham 3:26). Those who keep it have "glory added upon their heads for ever and ever." The first estate was already educational (D&C 138:56 — "first lessons in the world of spirits"). Mortality is the *intensive* semester — embodied, veiled, with higher stakes.

## Open Questions for Later Phases

- **Phase 3 (Spirit World)**: Alma 34:34 — "that same spirit which doth possess your bodies" — does this mean growth stops at death? Or does the *direction* carry forward while growth continues? The spirit world question is urgent for the comfort thread.
- **Phase 5 (Resurrection & Judgment)**: D&C 93:33 — if spirit and element "inseparably connected" receive a fulness of joy, what is the spirit world *without* a body? Is it incomplete? And Alma 42:23 — "mercy cometh because of the atonement; and the atonement bringeth to pass the resurrection" — the linked chain continues beyond mortality.
- **The Comfort Thread**: Several pivots here:
  - "He shall consecrate thine afflictions for thy gain" ([2 Nephi 2:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)) — suffering isn't pointless; it's *consecrated*.
  - Moses 5:10-11 — Adam and Eve found joy *because of* the transgression, not despite it. The pattern is: suffering → opened eyes → joy.
  - Oaks' gravity metaphor — opposition makes growth possible. For someone who is sick, the opposition they face is building something they cannot yet see.
  - Mosiah 3:19 — "willing to submit to all things which the Lord seeth fit to inflict upon him, even as a child doth submit to his father." This isn't glib resignation. It's trust.

### Webster 1828 Definitions
- **probation**: "Moral trial; the state of man in the present life, in which he has the opportunity of proving his character, and becoming qualified for a happier state"
- **opposition**: "The act of opposing; an attempt to check, restrain, or defeat; resistance"
- **consecrate**: "Consecrated; devoted; dedicated; sacred"
