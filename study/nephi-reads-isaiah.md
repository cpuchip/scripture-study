# Nephi Reads Isaiah

*A study on Nephi's hermeneutic — what it means to "liken," how he authorizes the move, and where the line runs between likening and rewriting. Sister study to [abinadi-how-to-read.md](abinadi-how-to-read.md).*

---

## Binding Question

Nephi reads Isaiah more aggressively than any other Book of Mormon prophet. He splices entire chapters into his record. He frames them with editorial commentary. And in [2 Nephi 26-27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/26.md), he produces prophetic material that the parent text in [Isaiah 29](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/29.md) does not contain: three witnesses to a sealed book, a dramatized learned-man exchange, a seal-and-hide commandment.

What does Nephi mean when he says "I did liken all scriptures unto us" ([1 Ne. 19:23](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/19.md))? Where does likening end and rewriting begin? And what does his example license, or refuse to license, for the modern reader?

---

## I. The Verb

The first thing to do with a Nephi study is to read his own verb carefully. He uses it twice in his hermeneutic seams, once at the very front of his Isaiah work and once at the very back:

> "I did read unto them that which was written by the prophet Isaiah; for I did liken all scriptures unto us, that it might be for our profit and learning." ([1 Ne. 19:23](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/19.md))
>
> "Now these are the words, and ye may liken them unto you and unto all men." ([2 Ne. 11:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/11.md))

Webster's 1828 gives the verb plainly: "to compare, to represent as resembling or similar." But Nephi's grammar pushes harder than that. The object of *liken* in his usage is not a comparison; it is a people. *Liken the words unto my people.* The text is not held alongside the reader so the reader can spot resemblances. The text is bent toward the reader so the reader stands inside it. Isaiah's "remnant" becomes the Lehite remnant. Isaiah's "branch broken off" becomes Nephi's family. The address moves from the original audience to the present audience without dropping the original.

Jacob picks up the verb when Nephi hands him the pulpit, and he gives it the same shape:

> "And now, the words which I shall read are they which Isaiah spake concerning all the house of Israel; wherefore, they may be likened unto you, for ye are of the house of Israel." ([2 Ne. 6:5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/6.md))

The reasoning is structural, not metaphorical. *Because* the audience is of the house of Israel, the words that Isaiah spoke to the house of Israel apply to the audience. Likening, in Nephi's and Jacob's usage, is the recognition that you are inside the prophet's address rather than outside it.

That is the first hinge. Likening is not creative reading. It is locational reading. It places the reader where the text already pointed.

## II. The Authorization

But Nephi cannot just splice thirteen chapters of someone else's scripture into his own record without explaining why he can. Before he quotes the second large block of Isaiah (what becomes [2 Nephi 12-24](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/12.md), or Isaiah 2-14), he stops and gives his license:

> "And now I, Nephi, write more of the words of Isaiah, for my soul delighteth in his words. For I will liken his words unto my people, and I will send them forth unto all my children, for he verily saw my Redeemer, even as I have seen him.
>
> And my brother, Jacob, also has seen him as I have seen him; wherefore, I will send their words forth unto my children to prove unto them that my words are true. Wherefore, by the words of three, God hath said, I will establish my word." ([2 Ne. 11:2-3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/11.md))

This is the load-bearing claim of Nephi's whole Isaiah project. The witness law from [Deuteronomy 19:15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/deut/19.md) and [2 Corinthians 13:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/2-cor/13.md), "in the mouth of two or three witnesses every word shall be established," is not a pulpit flourish here. It is the structural permission Nephi cites for his hermeneutic. Three prophets have seen the same Person. Their testimonies of that Person therefore establish each other. *Because Isaiah's vision and Nephi's vision land on the same Christ, Nephi can speak in Isaiah's voice without inventing.*

Note what this is not. It is not the claim that any spiritually attuned reader can rewrite scripture. It is the claim that prophets who have received the same vision can corroborate each other's words about it. The witness law authorizes the corroboration; it does not authorize freelance authorship.

This connects directly to the doublet pattern worked out in [hope-and-the-grammar-of-pairs.md](hope-and-the-grammar-of-pairs.md). The gospel keeps pairing things because the witness law makes pairs structural. Two prophets who saw the same Christ form a doublet that establishes a word. Three form a triplet that closes the case. Nephi is invoking that exact logic to license what he is about to do with Isaiah.

## III. Splice and Frame

Having claimed the witness law, Nephi acts on it. After announcing his method in [1 Ne. 19:23-24](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/19.md), he drops in two whole chapters of Isaiah (48 and 49) as [1 Nephi 20-21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/20.md). His brothers ask what it meant, and he expounds in 1 Nephi 22:

> "What meaneth these things which ye have read? Behold, are they to be understood according to things which are spiritual, which shall come to pass according to the spirit and not the flesh?
>
> And I, Nephi, said unto them: Behold they were manifest unto the prophet by the voice of the Spirit; for by the Spirit are all things made known unto the prophets, which shall come upon the children of men according to the flesh." ([1 Ne. 22:1-2](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/22.md))

The pattern is announce, splice, expound. Because it works for two chapters, Nephi runs it at scale: Isaiah 2-14 inserted as 2 Nephi 12-24, with the announcement at chapter 11 and the exposition at chapter 25. He runs it through Jacob in [2 Nephi 6-10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/6.md), where Jacob announces in 6:4-5, quotes Isaiah 49-52 across 6:6 through 8:25, and expounds in 9-10. Nephi has trained his brother to read the same way he reads.

The verb of the third move is worth pausing on. It is *expound*, not *interpret*. Nephi never says he is decoding Isaiah. He says he is making him plain. The text already meant what it meant; the work is to make the meaning land in a Lehite audience that does not know "the manner of the Jews" ([2 Ne. 25:5-6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/25.md)). The splice carries the cargo. The frame docks it.

This is a different operation from what Abinadi does in [Mosiah 14-15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/14.md). Abinadi quotes Isaiah 53 and then expands the typology *within* the text — the Servant is Christ AND his seed AND those who declare his words ([Mosiah 15:10-14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15.md)). The words don't change; the referents multiply. Nephi's splice-and-frame leaves the text alone and adds context around it. Abinadi reads in; Nephi reads around. Both are forms of likening, and both stay disciplined by the original wording.

## IV. The Hermeneutic Key

Thirteen chapters of Isaiah is a lot to drop on a Lehite audience. Nephi knows it. Before moving on, he tells his readers exactly why some of them will understand what he just gave them and others will not:

> "Hearken, O my people, which are of the house of Israel, and give ear unto my words; for because the words of Isaiah are not plain unto you, nevertheless they are plain unto all those that are filled with the spirit of prophecy. But I give unto you a prophecy, according to the spirit which is in me; wherefore I shall prophesy according to the plainness which hath been with me from the time that I came out from Jerusalem with my father; for behold, my soul delighteth in plainness unto my people, that they may learn." ([2 Ne. 25:4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/25.md))

Read that twice. Plainness is not a property of the text. Isaiah's words are not plain unto the people Nephi is addressing. They are plain unto people in a particular spiritual condition — those filled with the spirit of prophecy. The same words, read by two readers, will yield different plainness. The difference is the reader.

Therefore Nephi's diagnosis matches Abinadi's. When Abinadi confronted the priests of Noah, he diagnosed their failure not as a lack of information but as a lack of disposition: "Ye have not applied your hearts to understanding; therefore, ye have not been wise" ([Mosiah 12:27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/12.md)). The priests had memorized the text. They could quote Isaiah 52:7 from the breastwork of their gold seats. But they could not read it, because their hearts were elsewhere. Nephi names the same problem in the positive: those who have the spirit of prophecy will find Isaiah plain, because the spirit of prophecy is the disposition that lets the text deliver itself.

The Bible Dictionary captures the cargo of "spirit of prophecy" tightly: the Holy Ghost's testimony of Jesus Christ ([Rev. 19:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rev/19.md), "the testimony of Jesus is the spirit of prophecy"). Read against Nephi's claim, the logic closes. Isaiah's vision was of Christ; Nephi's vision was of Christ; the spirit of prophecy is the testimony of Christ; *therefore* a reader filled with that spirit reads Isaiah and finds Christ on every page, and finds him plainly. A reader without it reads the same chapters and finds them obscure.

This is the guardrail Nephi names. The spirit of prophecy is what authorizes the reading. Without it, likening becomes projection. With it, likening becomes recognition.

## V. Prophetic Extension

The most aggressive move in Nephi's whole Isaiah project is the one he makes in [2 Nephi 26-27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/26.md). Compare the two chapters side by side and the operation is unmistakable.

[Isaiah 29](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/29.md) has twenty-four verses. It opens with woe to Ariel (the city where David dwelt), passes through the spirit of deep sleep poured on the people, sketches the sealed-book moment in two brief verses (29:11-12), and closes with the marvelous work, the deaf hearing the words of the book, and Jacob's children sanctifying the Holy One. The sealed-book sketch is the smallest unit of the chapter:

> "And the vision of all is become unto you as the words of a book that is sealed, which men deliver to one that is learned, saying, Read this, I pray thee: and he saith, I cannot; for it is sealed: And the book is delivered to him that is not learned, saying, Read this, I pray thee: and he saith, I am not learned." ([Isa. 29:11-12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/29.md))

That is the entire treatment in Isaiah. Two verses. A tight image of inaccessible scripture.

[2 Nephi 27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/27.md) has thirty-five verses. It contains everything Isaiah 29 contains, in roughly the same order, with the marvelous-work passage anchoring the close. But between Isaiah's terse sealed-book sketch and Isaiah's marvelous work, Nephi inserts material that has no parent in Isaiah 29 at all:

> "Wherefore, at that day when the book shall be delivered unto the man of whom I have spoken, the book shall be hid from the eyes of the world, that the eyes of none shall behold it save it be that three witnesses shall behold it, by the power of God, besides him to whom the book shall be delivered; and they shall testify to the truth of the book and the things therein." ([2 Ne. 27:12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/27.md))

Three witnesses. Named here, more than two thousand years before the Three Witnesses to the Book of Mormon would sign their testimony. Then a full dramatized exchange between the unlearned man and the learned man, with the seal-and-hide commandment ([2 Ne. 27:15-22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/27.md)) — what Isaiah handled in two verses, Nephi narrates as a prophetic episode with stage directions.

This is no longer splice and frame. Nephi is not quoting Isaiah here; he is *seeing what Isaiah saw* and reporting parts that Isaiah's record did not preserve. The chapter is presented as Isaiah's vision because Isaiah's vision is what Nephi is inside of — by way of the witness-law claim made back in [2 Ne. 11:2-3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/11.md). Isaiah saw the Redeemer; Nephi saw the Redeemer; the visions overlap; Nephi can extend the report.

The substance of the extension is itself a witness law. Three witnesses to a book ([2 Ne. 27:12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/27.md)). The Lord establishing his word "in the mouth of as many witnesses as seemeth him good" ([2 Ne. 27:14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/27.md)). Nephi authorizes his Isaiah expansion by invoking the witness law, and the content of the expansion is the witness law applied to the latter-day book. The form and the substance match.

## VI. The Boundary

Nephi's hermeneutic, laid out in his own seams, has three operations of increasing weight.

The first is **likening proper**. The reader stands inside Isaiah's address because the reader is structurally inside the address — a remnant of Israel hearing words spoken to the house of Israel. This is what Nephi commends to his children and to "all men" ([2 Ne. 11:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/11.md)). It is available to any reader.

The second is **expounding the splice**. The reader who knows the manner of the Jews can dock Isaiah's words to a new audience by framing them ([2 Ne. 25:1-8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/25.md)). This is what Nephi himself does and what he trained Jacob to do. It is available to teachers, parents, and anyone who has paid the price to understand the original setting well enough to render it plainly elsewhere.

The third is **prophetic extension** — speaking in the voice of the original prophet about the original vision, where the speaker has independently received that vision. This is what Nephi does in 2 Nephi 26-27. It requires having seen what Isaiah saw, certified by the witness law and authorized by canonical office. It is not available to readers as such. It is available to prophets corroborating prophets.

The boundary, then, runs between operations two and three. The modern reader who has been ordained to no apostolic office and granted no Christophany can read Isaiah with the spirit of prophecy and find Christ plain on every page. They can frame Isaiah for a new audience. They can liken Isaiah to themselves and to all men. What they cannot do is extend Isaiah — produce material presented as Isaiah's vision that Isaiah's record did not contain. That move is reserved.

The reservation is not arbitrary. The witness law is what makes corroboration trustworthy. Two readers who claim the same private vision do not establish a word; they establish that they have agreed to claim the same private vision. Two prophets who have each received the vision from the Lord and bear independent record do establish a word. Without the witness law, prophetic extension collapses into authorial license.

This is the way to hold Nephi's example without breaking it. Read with him. Recognize the splices, the frames, the expansions. Notice that he is doing something more aggressive than Abinadi — and notice that he is doing it under explicit authorization that he names twice. Then use his hermeneutic where it is licensed for use: in the likening, in the expounding. Leave the extension to the prophets the Lord raises up to corroborate the prophets we already have.

## VII. What This Changes

Therefore two things change for any serious reader of Isaiah.

**First**, plainness is a spiritual achievement, not a literary one. The complaint that Isaiah is hard is true at the level of vocabulary and reference, and a good study Bible helps. But Nephi's diagnosis cuts deeper. Isaiah is plain to those filled with the spirit of prophecy and obscure to those who are not, and the difference is the reader's testimony of Christ. The way to read Isaiah is not first to learn ancient near-eastern history; it is first to repent toward Christ until the Spirit can rest on the reading. The history helps after that. It cannot replace it.

**Second**, the witness law is doing work in the canon that English-speaking readers tend to miss. Nephi quotes Isaiah at length and explains why he can: Isaiah saw the Redeemer, Nephi saw the Redeemer, Jacob saw the Redeemer, three witnesses establish the word. Christ in 3 Nephi quotes Isaiah at length and explains why we should pay attention: "Great are the words of Isaiah" ([3 Ne. 23:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/23.md)). Joseph Smith reads Isaiah 11 and the angel says "it was about to be fulfilled" ([JS-H 1:40](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/js-h/1.md)). The chain of witnesses runs from Isaiah through Nephi through Christ through Moroni through Joseph, each prophet certifying the prior prophet, each vision overlapping the prior vision. *That* is why Isaiah is in the Book of Mormon. Not as proof-texting. As the establishing of a word by three or more witnesses across millennia, with Christ at the center of the visions every one of them saw.

Read Isaiah after this and the chapters look different. They are not a hard book to crack. They are one prophet's portion of a chorus that has been corroborating itself for two and a half thousand years.

## VIII. Becoming

I am a Sunday School president. I teach scripture every week to people who, like me, were trained more on the manner of the modern world than on the manner of the Jews. The temptation in that calling is to make Isaiah accessible by softening him — by pulling him down into devotional inspiration that anyone can receive without the spirit of prophecy. Nephi's example forbids that move. His path to plainness goes *through* the spirit of prophecy, not around it. If I want my class to find Isaiah plain, the work is not first to simplify Isaiah; the work is first to teach Christ until the spirit of prophecy rests on the room. The simplification follows from the testimony, not the other way around.

There is also a check Nephi puts on me personally. I am not licensed for mode three. When I read Isaiah and a connection lights up (Hebrew root, latter-day fulfillment, doctrinal cross-wire), I can teach it as likening or as expounding. I cannot teach it as Isaiah's vision. The discipline of saying "this is a connection I am drawing" rather than "this is what Isaiah saw" is small in the moment and large over a lifetime. It is the discipline that keeps the chorus a chorus instead of a solo.

And there is a posture Nephi gives me toward my own reading. He says of his own visions and his own writings, repeatedly, "my soul delighteth" — in the words of Isaiah ([2 Ne. 11:2](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/11.md), [25:5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/25.md)), in plainness ([2 Ne. 25:4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/25.md)), in proving Christ ([2 Ne. 11:4, 6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/11.md)), in the covenants ([2 Ne. 11:5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/11.md)). The verb appears eight times across [2 Nephi 11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/11.md) and [25](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/25.md). Delight is the affective signature of the spirit of prophecy at rest in a reader. When my study becomes a chore, when I am grinding through chapters because I should, the spirit of prophecy is somewhere else. The repair is not more grinding. The repair is whatever it takes to get my soul back to delighting in his words.

Liken the words. Expound the splice. Leave the extension to the prophets. And when the soul delights, you are reading the way Nephi read.
