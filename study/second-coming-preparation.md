# What to Do Before He Comes

*A study of prophetic teaching on the Second Coming and family preparation*

**Binding question:** What have prophets and apostles consistently taught about preparing for the Second Coming of Christ — and what does that require of a family right now?

*Related studies: [end-times.md](end-times.md) · [consumption-decreed.md](consumption-decreed.md) · [endtimes-servant.md](endtimes-servant.md) · [zion-in-a-presidency.md](zion-in-a-presidency.md)*

---

## I. The Question Behind the Question

The Apostle Peter wrote that in the last days, scoffers would walk after their own lusts and say: "Where is the promise of his coming?" ([2 Peter 3:3–4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/2-pet/3.md)). That is one category of response to the Second Coming — dismissal. But there is a second, less visible failure mode, named by Jacob in the Book of Mormon: "looking beyond the mark." The Jews couldn't accept Jesus because He didn't fit their image of what the Messiah should look like. The Nephites rejected Samuel's prophecies because it "was not reasonable." Both groups were paying attention. They just missed it.

Elder Christoffel Golden named this in October 2021: the world pursues great knowledge and "much prowess," but this too often "camouflage[s] the unsteady foundation upon which they are built." The call to preparation is not for those who are ignoring the question. It is for those who might be asking the wrong version of it — too focused on catastrophe timelines and not focused enough on the kind of people they are becoming.

Christofferson, speaking at Easter 2026, quoted Peter's deeper question directly: "What manner of persons ought ye to be?" That is the study's binding question in disguise. Not: what will happen? Not: when? But: *who will you be when He comes?*

## II. The Early Drumbeat (1971–1985): Geography and Signs

For the first fifteen years of the modern conference record, the Second Coming was addressed in explicit, structural terms. LeGrand Richards spoke four times between 1971 and 1978 — on "Laying a Foundation for the Millennium," "He Has Sent His Messenger to Prepare the Way," "Prophecy," and "The Second Coming of Christ." His era is the drumbeat: the Restoration itself IS the preparation. Priesthood restored. Keys restored. Israel gathering. Zion being built.

Bruce R. McConkie, speaking in Lima, Peru in 1977 at President Kimball's request, answered the most practical geographic question: *where do we gather?* The early commandment — gather to North America — had been completed. Now, he said, we are in Phase II of a three-phase process:

> "Phase I—From the First Vision… to the secure establishment of the Church in the United States and Canada, a period of about 125 years. Phase II—From the creation of stakes of Zion in overseas areas, beginning in the 1950s, to the second coming of the Son of Man, a period of unknown duration. Phase III—From our Lord's second coming until the kingdom is perfected."

We are in Phase II. Have been since the 1950s. The gathering place for Peruvians is in Peru. The gathering place for Chileans is in Chile. For a family in the American West, it is the ward and stake where you are.

McConkie's definitive statement: "A stake of Zion is a part of Zion. You cannot create a stake of Zion without creating a part of Zion. Zion is the pure in heart." Your local unit is not a waiting room for Zion. It IS Zion, to the degree the people in it are pure in heart. The preparation is not migration. It is transformation.

Ezra Taft Benson, before he became prophet and while serving as Q12 and later prophet, returned again and again to the Book of Mormon as the central preparation tool. In April 1987, as president of the Church, he read the 3 Nephi chapters aloud to himself — the account of the Nephite civilization destroyed just before the Savior's visit to America. The parallel disturbed him:

> "The record of the Nephite history just prior to the Savior's visit reveals many parallels to our own day. … Pride became commonplace. Dishonesty and immorality were widespread. Secret combinations flourished… 'The people began to be distinguished by ranks, according to their riches and their chances for learning.'"

The survivors of that destruction were "the more righteous part." Not all church members. Not all who had been baptized. The *more righteous part*. Benson's message was: read the book, because it was written for our day, and reading it will tell you who survives and why.

## III. The Summit of the Ages (1986–2004): Privilege and Responsibility

Gordon B. Hinckley, at the threshold of the millennium in 1999, didn't itemize signs or survival strategies. He painted in historical perspective:

> "We stand on the summit of the ages, awed by a great and solemn sense of history. This is the last and final dispensation toward which all in the past has pointed."

His preparation call was almost entirely about identity and privilege. Fourteen centuries of darkness. The Reformation. The American founding. And then Joseph Smith in a grove of trees. All of it pointed here, to us, to now. Hinckley's response to that weight: "Given what we have and what we know, we ought to be a better people than we are. We ought to be more Christlike, more forgiving, more helpful and considerate to all around us."

That is not a weak conclusion. It is the prophet's response to standing at the summit: not panic, but conduct. The Restoration was not given to us to produce anxiety. It was given to produce the kind of people who will be ready when He comes.

Hinckley had no doubt He would come: "At some stage in all of this onward rolling, Jesus Christ will appear to reign in splendor upon the earth. No one knows when that will be. Not even the angels in heaven will know of the time of His return. But it will be a welcome day."

## IV. The Most Complete Modern Statement: Oaks 2004

Elder Dallin H. Oaks delivered what is, in the record, the most comprehensive and systematically structured modern treatment of Second Coming preparation. He was then serving in the Quorum of the Twelve. He is now president of the Church. The talk's authority has not diminished with time — if anything, the man's subsequent elevation gives it additional weight.

He organized the field with four "indisputable" facts:

1. The Savior will return personally to reign during a millennium of peace.
2. At His coming, the wicked will be destroyed and the righteous resurrected.
3. No one knows the time.
4. The faithful are taught to study the signs and be prepared.

He then listed the signs — wars and rumors of wars, false prophets, earthquakes, famine, pestilence, iniquity abounding, men's hearts failing — and made an observation that ages well: "the accelerating pattern of natural disasters in the last few decades is ominous." Written in 2004.

But the signs are not the study's point. The parable is.

> "The arithmetic of this parable is chilling. The ten virgins obviously represent members of Christ's Church, for all were invited to the wedding feast and all knew what was required to be admitted when the bridegroom came. But only half were ready when he came."

Half. Oaks does not soften this. He doesn't say "most of us will probably be ready" or "the Lord is merciful." He says the Lord denied the five foolish entrance and said "I know you not." These were church members. They had lamps. They just hadn't filled them with oil.

On gathering, Oaks updated McConkie's 1977 teaching: the current commandment is to gather to stakes in your own homeland — where temples have been built and where the full blessings of eternity are available. These stakes are "for a defense, and for a refuge from the storm, and from wrath when it shall be poured out without mixture upon the whole earth" ([D&C 115:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/115.md)).

On preparation, he made the crucial distinction that has been quoted continuously since:

> "We need to make both temporal and spiritual preparation for the events prophesied at the time of the Second Coming. And the preparation most likely to be neglected is the one less visible and more difficult—the spiritual. A 72-hour kit of temporal supplies may prove valuable for earthly challenges, but, as the foolish virgins learned to their sorrow, a 24-hour kit of spiritual preparation is of greater and more enduring value."

He defined "holy places" — "Stand ye in holy places, and be not moved" ([D&C 87:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/87.md)) — with unusual specificity: "Surely they include the temple and its covenants faithfully kept. Surely they include a home where children are treasured and parents are respected. Surely the holy places include our posts of duty assigned by priesthood authority, including missions and callings faithfully fulfilled."

And then the question that cuts through every other framing:

> "What if the day of His coming were tomorrow? If we knew that we would meet the Lord tomorrow—through our premature death or through His unexpected coming—what would we do today? What confessions would we make? What practices would we discontinue? What accounts would we settle? What forgivenesses would we extend? What testimonies would we bear? If we would do those things then, why not now?"

That question is not rhetorical. It is practical and immediate. Oaks is not asking you to contemplate your mortality in the abstract. He is asking you to identify the specific gap between who you are and who you would be if you knew the time — and then close it today.

## V. Acceleration: Nelson's Era and the Shift in Language (2017–2026)

Something changes in the rhetorical register under Nelson. The signs are still present — he opens his October 2022 talk by acknowledging that "we are accosted daily by an onslaught of sobering news." But the primary frame is not signs. It is *covenants as mechanism*.

> "Each person who makes covenants in baptismal fonts and in temples—and keeps them—has increased access to the power of Jesus Christ. … covenant keepers are entitled to a special kind of rest that comes to them through their covenantal relationship with God."

Nelson frames the difficulty of the last days as fundamentally a problem of spiritual exhaustion — people being overwhelmed and collapsing under the weight of a "sin-saturated, self-centered, and often exhausting world." The preparation is not a bunker but a yoke: "Take my yoke upon you, and learn of me; … and ye shall find rest unto your souls" ([Matthew 11:28–30](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/11.md)).

On the gathering, he is explicit about its scope: "the gathering of Israel is the most important work taking place on earth today. One crucial element of this gathering is preparing a people who are able, ready, and worthy to receive the Lord when He comes again." The Second Coming is the *reason* for the gathering. The gathering is the Second Coming preparation. They are the same work.

His practical call: spend more time in the temple. Understand how the temple teaches you to rise above the fallen world. That is the training ground for the kind of person who can abide His day.

Cook, in 2023, makes this visible at the granular level. Nelson's statement paraphrased through Cook becomes almost startlingly simple: "Anytime you do anything that helps anyone — on either side of the veil — take a step toward making covenants with God and receiving their essential baptismal and temple ordinances, you are helping to gather Israel. It is as simple as that." Any step. Anyone. Either side of the veil.

Eubank carries forward Kimball's 1979 prophecy: women being righteous, articulate, different, distinct, and happy will be a "significant force" in the last-days growth of the Church. Her human-chain story is the picture: dozens of people on a beach, paralyzed because they could only think of traditional solutions, until two people linked arms and created a human chain to the drowning swimmers. Covenant-keeping produces people who see solutions others cannot see.

And Christofferson, in 2026, brings the arc to its current resting point. Peter's question — "What manner of persons ought ye to be?" — is the frame. The answer is character: "Christlike character grows out of a Christlike heart." And the reason it matters for Second Coming preparation: "Adopting the character of Christ is not only a question of our personal preparation but a primary element of preparing the world for the Lord's return and His glorious millennial reign. We tend to underestimate the influence of Christlike individuals in the world, but working one by one has always been Jesus's approach to changing society and establishing His kingdom."

The fifty-year arc has a logic. The Church first had to settle the geographic question — where do we gather? — before anyone could address the how. Once McConkie answered that (stay in your own nation's stake), Oaks could systematize the what: both temporal and spiritual preparation, but above all spiritual, above all the oil that can't be borrowed. That settled, Nelson could name the mechanism: covenants give access to Christ's power, and therefore the person who keeps covenants is not merely prepared for the day but empowered for it. Therefore Christofferson's question is the capstone: not what are you doing, but who are you becoming? Each layer built on the one before.

## VI. What This Means for a Family Right Now

Oaks asked the question that cuts through all the prophecy-watching: if you knew He was coming tomorrow, what would you do today? The preparation implied by that question resolves into five domains.

**Covenants — kept, not just made.** Nelson's central contribution is that covenants give access to power. Making them is the entry. Keeping them is the mechanism. The oil in the ten virgins' lamps is not just baptism. It is daily repentance, scripture, Sabbath, prayer, fast, temple attendance — the whole sustained practice of covenant-keeping over time. Oaks: "We are promisers who do not fulfill and believers who do not perform." The question is not whether you are covenanted but whether the covenant is active in your life.

**The temple — more, not less.** Every talk in this study points toward the temple. McConkie: stakes are built so the full ordinances are available. Oaks: the temple is a holy place. Nelson: spend more time there. Cook: help others get there. The temple is not an event. It is the training environment for becoming the kind of person who can abide the day of His coming. Your family's temple attendance is not optional preparation — it is the preparation.

**Home — the other holy place.** Oaks's definition of holy places is precise: "a home where children are treasured and parents are respected." The home is not incidental to the preparation. It is co-equal with the temple. What happens in your home — the conversations you have, how you treat each other, whether scripture is alive there, whether prayer is real there — is preparation. The wicked Nephites "did not sin ignorantly, for they knew the will of God concerning them" (3 Nephi 6:18). Knowledge is not the problem. Practice is.

**Outward posture — the gathering is active.** Cook and Nelson make clear that individual preparation is not sufficient. The gathering of Israel is the preparation, and the gathering requires outward movement. Your beacon-light example — "kindness, righteousness, happiness, and sincere love for all peoples" — is not optional personal virtue. It is the mechanism by which others find the safe harbor of the covenant. You are not just preparing yourself. You are responsible for helping others prepare. That is missionary work, temple work, family history, ministering, all of it. The same work.

**Character — the long game.** Christofferson's contribution is the one most likely to be missed because it is the least dramatic. But "What manner of persons ought ye to be?" is the right question. Survival is not the goal. The goal is becoming the kind of person who is at home in a world where Christ reigns. The Millennium requires Millennial people — people who have been refined by covenant and practice into charity, humility, patience, honesty, courage. That formation takes decades. It cannot be crammed. Oaks's ten virgins arithmetic stands: half of church members will not be ready. The difference is not in what they were told. It is in what they chose to do — or not do — over a long period of time.

The good news is what Oaks closes with, and it holds: "We do not know when this game will end, and we do not know the final score, but we do know that when the game finally ends, our team wins." Preparation is not performed out of dread. It is performed out of love for the Lord and trust in His design. "We need not fear," Nephi said ([1 Nephi 22:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/22.md)). The righteous need not fear. And the righteous are those doing right, now, whether or not the day seems close.

## Becoming

Oaks's question is the one that cuts: *if you knew He were coming tomorrow, what would you do today?*

Answer it honestly. Not in generalities — specifically. What account would you settle? What practice would you stop? What confession would you make? What testimony would you finally bear to someone who needs to hear it?

The gap between that answer and today is the preparation. And the closing of that gap is not a dramatic act. It is covenant kept today, and tomorrow, and the day after — temple attendance that is consistent rather than occasional, scripture that is habitual rather than inspirational, family history work begun rather than deferred, a sin given a real name before the Lord rather than a euphemism kept.

Benson's contribution was urgent and simple: read 3 Nephi. The survivors were the *more righteous part*. Not perfect. More righteous. The preparation is directional, not absolute. But it is real, and it is daily.

"Behold, I will go before you and be your rearward; and I will be in your midst, and you shall not be confounded. Behold, I am Jesus Christ, and I come quickly." — [D&C 49:27–28](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/49.md)
