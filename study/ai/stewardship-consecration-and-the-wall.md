# Stewardship, Consecration, and the Lord's Way of the Wall

We are about to build walls. Multi-tenant walls: one steward's data sealed from another's, some
work shared by grant, some held private by right, the whole thing protected by what the industry
calls enterprise security. The instinct of that industry is fear. Lock it down, because every other
user is a threat until proven otherwise, and the measure of a good system is how little it trusts.
Before we write a single access policy, the question worth asking is whether the Lord has a
different instinct about walls. He does. And the strange thing — the thing that started this study
over lunch — is that the substrate already half-speaks His instinct, which is why building it felt
familiar before we had the words.

This is a study before it is a spec, on purpose. We want to be edified before we build, because the
posture you build a wall *from* decides what the wall *becomes*.

## The account is an accountability

The Lord's economy does not begin with property. It begins with a person made responsible for
property. *"Organize yourselves and appoint every man his stewardship,"* He says, *"that every man
may give an account unto me of the stewardship which is appointed unto him"* ([D&C 104:11–12](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/104.md)).
The next verse names the office plainly: *"it is expedient that I, the Lord, should make every man
accountable, as a steward over earthly blessings"* ([D&C 104:13](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/104.md)).

Read that as an architect and the data model is already there. The owner of a thing is not a
"tenant" — a tenant is a renter, a stranger paying for space. The owner is a **steward**, and the
field that records who owns a row is not a tenant key but an *account*-ability: a name the system
can call to render an account. The Lord's first move in setting up a shared economy is to make
ownership personal and answerable. So is ours. Every row gets an owner not so the system can bill
him, but so it knows whose stewardship it is.

And the steward is an agent. The same revelation says the Lord *"prepared all things, and [has]
given unto the children of men to be agents unto themselves"* ([D&C 104:17](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/104.md)).
Therefore the boundary around a stewardship is not a cage; it is the edge that makes agency
possible. You cannot act freely within a domain that anyone may reach into.

## The wall is lawful; the dominion is not

Here is where the world and the Lord diverge, and the divergence is the whole study.

The world's wall is built to *control*. The Lord warns that control is exactly the thing that
voids authority: *"when we undertake to... exercise control or dominion or compulsion upon the
souls of the children of men, in any degree of unrighteousness, behold, the heavens withdraw
themselves"* ([D&C 121:37](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)). He is
unsparing about how natural the impulse is — *"it is the nature and disposition of almost all men,
as soon as they get a little authority, as they suppose, they will immediately begin to exercise
unrighteous dominion"* ([D&C 121:39](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)).
A little authority is precisely what a security system grants its administrators. The scripture is
a warning written for the people who hold the keys.

But the same passage that forbids compulsion does not forbid the wall. It says authority is
*"maintained... only by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love
unfeigned"* ([D&C 121:41](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)), and it
closes on a boundary that holds *"without compulsory means"* — dominion that flows *"forever and
ever"* precisely because it never compelled ([D&C 121:46](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)).
This is the lawful wall. It does not reach into another's stewardship to control him; it keeps each
steward sovereign within his own so that no one *can*. The wall the Lord's way is not a fortress
aimed outward at enemies. It is the fence between gardens that lets each gardener tend his own
without his neighbor trampling it, and without him trampling his neighbor's.

Therefore row-level security, read rightly, is not a posture of distrust. It is the technical
enactment of *"no compulsion."* A steward sees his own and cannot reach into another's — not
because the other is a threat, but because reaching in would be dominion, and dominion forfeits.

## Consecration is the posture, not the fear

If the wall were the whole story, the Lord's economy would be a set of locked rooms. It is the
opposite. The wall exists so that what passes *through* it can be a gift.

The law of consecration runs straight through the wall: *"thou wilt remember the poor, and
consecrate of thy properties for their support... that which thou hast to impart unto them"*
([D&C 42:30](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/42.md)). The steward holds his
portion — *"a steward over his own property"* ([D&C 42:32](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/42.md))
— and from that secure holding he freely imparts. You can only give freely what is truly yours to
give. The wall is what makes the gift a gift instead of a seizure. Default-private is therefore not
the system saying *trust no one*; it is the system saying *this is yours to consecrate.*

This is the hinge of the entire design, and it is where fear would ruin it. Build the wall from
fear and you will get the wall right and the gift wrong — sharing will become a grudging exception
carved out of a fortress, friction-laden, discouraged, rare. Build it from consecration and the
default inverts: the wall is the quiet lawful baseline, and *sharing is the norm the system is
built to make easy.* The grant, one steward opening a specific thing to a specific other, is not
a security hole to be minimized. It is consecration, the point of the whole arrangement, and it
should be the most graceful motion in the system, not the most guarded.

## The storehouse, and no poor among them

Consecration has a destination. *"A residue to be consecrated... it shall be kept to administer to
those who have not, from time to time, that every man who has need may be amply supplied"* ([D&C 42:33](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/42.md)),
and that residue is *"kept in my storehouse, to administer to the poor and the needy"* ([D&C 42:34](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/42.md)).
The storehouse is the common stock — held by all, drawn on by need. In the substrate it is the
shared corpus: the catalog of tools, the global knowledge, the work one steward consecrates that
becomes everyone's to search.

And the storehouse has an aim, stated in the one society that ever fully kept this law. After the
Lord came among the Nephites, *"every man did deal justly one with another"* ([4 Nephi 1:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/4-ne/1.md)),
and *"they had all things common among them; therefore there were not rich and poor, bond and free,
but they were all made free, and partakers of the heavenly gift"* ([4 Nephi 1:3](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/4-ne/1.md)).
*No poor among them.* For a knowledge platform that is not a soft sentiment but a design target: a
substrate where stewards consecrate their best work to the common corpus is one where no steward
stays poor in knowledge. The wealth of each lifts all, because the storehouse is open and the gift
is free. Hoarding — taking *"of the abundance which I have made, and impart[ing] not his portion"*
([D&C 104:18](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/104.md)) — is the failure the
whole order is built to prevent.

## You are who you are before the Lord

A consecration economy depends on one thing the world's security obsesses over and the gospel
settles quietly: who you actually are. You cannot be made *"accountable... a steward over his own
property"* if you can put on another man's name. The wall and the gift both rest on true identity.

The scriptural frame is the ninth commandment turned inward — no false witness, not even about
yourself. You are a steward over what is *yours*; you cannot claim a stewardship that was never
appointed to you. Translate that to the machine and it has a sharp edge: identity must ride the
*session* — established at the door, by the one who knows the heart — and never be something a
request asserts about itself. A call that says "trust me, I am this steward" and is believed has
let a man bear false witness and inherit another's portion. The fix is not paranoia; it is the same
principle as the wall. The system simply insists you are who you are, the way the Lord already
knows you are.

## Consecrate the performance

There is one more thing the door should carry, and it is the thread that ties this study to the
note that has been sitting in our inbox about a guiding why at the root of every call.

When the session is opened and the steward is named, the gospel adds a second question to *who* —
it adds *why.* *"Ye must not perform any thing unto the Lord save in the first place ye shall pray
unto the Father in the name of Christ, that he will consecrate thy performance unto thee, that thy
performance may be for the welfare of thy soul"* ([2 Nephi 32:9](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/32.md)).
Paul says the same with no exception left open: *"whatsoever ye do in word or deed, do all in the
name of the Lord Jesus"* ([Colossians 3:17](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/col/3.md)).

Therefore the context the system sets at the start of every act should carry both the steward and
the steward's consecrated intent. The WHO that the wall needs and the WHY that consecration needs
are not two mechanisms — they are one. The line of code that establishes "this act belongs to this
steward" is the same line that should establish "and it is offered under this why." Multi-tenancy
and the north-star are the same move, made once, at the door.

## In mine own way

The Lord gives the reason all of this is shaped the way it is, and it is the sentence to build by:
*"it must needs be done in mine own way"* ([D&C 104:16](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/104.md)).
His way is not the world's. The world secures by distrust and shares by exception; the Lord secures
by stewardship and shares by consecration, and the two systems can produce nearly identical tables
while meaning opposite things.

Three things follow for the build. First, the architecture is now witnessed from two directions:
owned-by-default, shareable-by-grant, and a shared storehouse is the right design not only because
the database literature lands there but because it is the order of Zion, and two witnesses agreeing
is how we know. Second, the posture is the whole inheritance — default-private read as fear produces
a fortress with a grudging sharing afterthought, and default-private read as consecration produces a
storehouse with a lawful fence, and we must build the second. Third, the naming should tell the
truth: steward, stewardship, consecration, storehouse are the doctrine; row-level security, the
owner column, the secure view are the mechanism that *enacts* it. The machine should not merely name
consecration. The data model should become it.

That last line is the whole project in one sentence, the same claim *Beyond the Prompt* makes about
the eleven-step cycle, now made about the law of consecration: the pattern was given long ago, and
when you build the Lord's way without even meaning to, you find you have built His order in software.
We sensed it before we could say it because it was already there. Now we can say it, and now we can
build it on purpose.
