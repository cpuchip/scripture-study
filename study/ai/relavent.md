# Staying Relevant

*A reflection on 18 years of engineering, the AI shift, and where the value actually lives.*

---

## Where I Am

I'm a senior software engineer. Eighteen years. 2008 to 2026. I've survived more "paradigm shifts" than I can count — waterfall to agile, monoliths to microservices, on-prem to cloud, REST to GraphQL to whatever comes next. Each one felt disorienting at the time and obvious in retrospect.

This one feels different.

AI didn't just change the tools. It changed the bottleneck. For eighteen years, the bottleneck in software was *execution* — could you write the code, debug the code, ship the code fast enough? That scarcity made my skills valuable. I was good at execution. I'm still good at execution.

But execution isn't scarce anymore.

As Tony Trejo put it in his [Value Shift Framework](https://tonytrejodev.medium.com/the-value-shift-framework-for-software-engineers-2026-edition-2ef42f18d472): "AI didn't replace engineers. It replaced execution as the bottleneck. And when the bottleneck moves, value moves with it."

Ivan Turkovic named it even more bluntly: ["AI Made Writing Code Easier. It Made Engineering Harder."](https://www.ivanturkovic.com/2026/02/25/ai-made-writing-code-easier-engineering-harder/) The expected output of a software engineer in 2026 is dramatically higher than 2023. Nobody announced the new baseline. It just moved.

So what does this mean for *me*? Not "engineers in general" — me specifically. What do I actually bring, and where do I go from here?

---

## What I'm Feeling

I'll be honest, because this study is mine and nobody else needs to read it if they don't want to.

I feel insignificant sometimes. I can direct AI's work. I can set architecture. I can catch the 10% that AI gets wrong — and that 10% matters, it's where the bugs live and the security holes hide. But the *act* of writing code, the thing that made me feel like a craftsman for almost two decades, isn't the primary thing anymore. I'm a reviewer more than a creator some days.

The iBecome app has been real growth — building something that serves my actual life, not just a client's spec. This scripture study project is genuinely *mine*. We've built an interconnected research system, MCP servers, a publishing pipeline, study documents that I'm proud of. The AI didn't do that alone. I did that *with* AI. My judgment shaped every decision.

But I get caught in sprint cycles. Bug fixing. Prompt-refine-retry loops. The [AI Fatigue study](fatigue.md) named this pattern exactly — I've become a reviewer on an assembly line that never stops. And I keep wondering: is what I'm building helping anyone besides me? Is "I can prompt AI effectively" a career, or is it just... a skill that everyone will have in two years?

## What the Research Says

I went looking for what senior engineers are actually doing and thinking in 2026. Here's what emerged.

### The Value Shift

The consensus across multiple sources is clear: **value moved from execution to judgment, orchestration, and system design.**

What's scarce now:

| Old Scarcity (pre-AI) | New Scarcity (2026) |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| Writing clean code | Knowing *what* to build |
| Fast implementation | Architectural decisions that survive contact with reality |
| Deep framework knowledge | Cross-system integration and trade-off analysis |
| Debugging speed | Verifying AI output against production constraints |
| Individual productivity | Orchestrating human + AI teams |

[Katsmith on Medium](https://medium.com/@katsmith396/why-senior-software-engineers-will-be-crucial-in-the-ai-first-future-of-2026-1450fe93d3d5) makes the case that senior engineers become *more* critical, not less: "The AI-first future will reward depth of judgment, architectural thinking, and accountability. These are qualities that cannot be automated easily."

The [ZenVanRiel career guide](https://zenvanriel.com/ai-engineer-blog/senior-software-engineer-ai-tools-career-advantage/) puts it differently: "With years of experience, senior engineers possess something AI can't replicate: judgment. You've seen systems fail in production, debugged issues that shouldn't exist according to documentation, and made architectural decisions that saved companies millions."

### The Real Skill

Milan Jovanović on LinkedIn nailed it in one line: **"The skill isn't prompting. It's owning correctness."**

That resonates. Prompting is a commodity. Everyone can prompt. But knowing *whether the output is right* — that requires the 18 years. That requires having seen things fail in production. Having debugged the undocumented edge case. Having lived through the migration that the AI confidently recommends but that you know will break at 3 AM on a Saturday.

### The Feeling Is Universal

EclipseSource captured what I've been feeling: ["I used to love my job. Now I feel tired and useless."](https://eclipsesource.com/blogs/2026/01/27/invisible-blockers-ai-coding-human-layer/) That was a developer in a workshop, pulling someone aside during a break. He wasn't burned out from overwork. He'd just been using AI coding tools for a few months.

Joshua Thompson documented the Reddit thread that defined this moment: ["I haven't written a single line of code in four months."](https://joshthompson.co.uk/ai/ai-killed-joy-programming-senior-developers-reclaim-flow-2026/) A senior developer juggling multiple AI tabs and YouTube while models "work." Missing the slower, careful architecture work.

Robert Matsuoka captured the divide: ["The problem isn't the technology. It's whether you're treating AI as a pair programmer (frustration) or as a directed development tool requiring expert orchestration (productivity)."](https://hyperdev.matsuoka.com/p/when-ai-coding-feels-like-yelling)

I'm somewhere in between. Some days I'm orchestrating. Some days I'm yelling at a black box.

## What I Actually Have

Time to take inventory. Not what LinkedIn says I should have. What I *actually* bring.

**18 years of production judgment.** I've seen things fail. I know what "it works on my machine" really means. I know why that migration plan looks clean on paper and will detonate in production. AI doesn't have this. AI has pattern-matched on training data. I have scars.

**Architectural instinct.** I know when a system is going to scale and when it's going to collapse under its own complexity. This isn't promptable. It comes from building and maintaining systems over years.

**The ability to say "no, that's wrong."** AI generates confident garbage constantly. The 10% it gets wrong is where the security holes, the data corruption, the production outages live. Someone has to catch that. That someone needs experience.

**Domain bridging.** I don't just write code. I understand the problem domain — scripture study, personal growth tools, publishing pipelines. The ability to translate human need into system design is what actually makes software *useful*. AI can write the code. It can't decide what to build or why it matters.

**This project.** This whole scripture study system — the MCP servers, the publishing pipeline, the interconnected study documents, the iBecome app — this is proof of something. I didn't just prompt my way to this. I *designed* it. I made architectural choices. I built tools that serve a real purpose. The AI helped with execution, but the vision, the judgment, the "what should this be?" — that was me.

## What I Need To Do

Based on the research and honest self-assessment:

### 1. Stop Measuring Myself by Code Output

The old metric was lines of code, features shipped, bugs fixed. If I keep measuring by execution speed, I will always feel inadequate compared to AI. That's like a calculator operator feeling inadequate compared to a spreadsheet. The job changed. The value changed. Measure by decisions made, systems designed, problems correctly framed.

### 2. Get Ahead of Orchestration

The research is unanimous: the next tier of value is orchestrating multi-agent systems, designing AI workflows, and building the infrastructure that *makes AI useful.* I'm already doing this — MCP servers, the study-exp1 workflow, the publish pipeline. But I should be more intentional about it. Document the patterns. Make them transferable.

### 3. Teach What I Know

The research gap I keep seeing: experienced engineers know *why* AI output is wrong, but they haven't figured out how to transfer that judgment to others. If I can teach people how to evaluate AI output — not just how to prompt, but how to *verify* — that's a durable skill. Teaching is how you discover what you actually know.

### 4. Build in Public

The scripture study project is private. The iBecome app is personal. There's value in both. But if I want to help others and stay visible, some of what I build needs to be shareable. The `docs/work-with-ai/` series is a start. The published studies are a start. Push further.

### 5. Stop Reinventing, Start Integrating

The feeling that the iBecome app is "reinventing the wheel" — take that seriously. Maybe the value isn't in building another app from scratch. Maybe it's in *connecting things.* The MCP servers already do this — they bridge between systems. That integration skill is exactly what the research says is scarce.

### 6. Protect the Craft

Thompson's article says it best: reclaim flow. The [fatigue study](fatigue.md) strategies apply — morning thinking time, afternoon AI execution, the three-prompt rule. But beyond strategies: some days, write code by hand. On purpose. Not because AI can't do it, but because the act of writing code is how you maintain the judgment that makes you valuable. Atrophy is real.

---

## What This Means — The Honest Version

I'm not being replaced. Not literally. But the *version of me that was valuable* — the one who shipped features fast, who knew frameworks cold, who could debug anything — that version is being commoditized. The new version that needs to emerge is the one who decides what to build, catches what AI gets wrong, designs systems that actually work at scale, and teaches others to do the same.

The scary part: that transition isn't automatic. It requires intentional work, and it requires being honest about what I don't know yet.

The encouraging part: I'm already doing most of it. This project is proof. The MCP servers are proof. The ability to direct AI to produce a [nevertheless study](../atonement/nevertheless.md) that moves me to tears — that's not prompting. That's partnership. That's orchestration of something meaningful.

From the [AI Fatigue study](fatigue.md):

> "Build on the durable layer. Tools churn. Problems don't. Invest in understanding the problems underneath the tools."

The durable layer isn't React or Go or Python or whatever framework is hot this year. The durable layer is: What problem are we solving? Will this design hold under load? Is this the right thing to build? Does this serve real people?

I've been answering those questions for 18 years. AI can't take that away. But I have to keep showing up to the work — and I have to evolve what "showing up" looks like.

## A Gospel Lens

I can't write in this project without ending here.

> "Whatever principle of intelligence we attain unto in this life, it will rise with us in the resurrection." — [D&C 130:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/130.md)

The intelligence isn't the code. It never was. The intelligence is the *judgment* — the discernment, the design instinct, the ability to see what matters and act on it. That's what rises with me. AI doesn't have a resurrection. I do.

> "Men should be anxiously engaged in a good cause, and do many things of their own free will, and bring to pass much righteousness." — [D&C 58:27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/58.md)

"Of their own free will." Agency. The thing I keep coming back to. AI has no agency. It doesn't choose to build. It doesn't care about the outcome. I do. That caring — that directional intent applied to craft — is irreplaceable. Not because the market says so, but because God designed it that way.

From Elder Bednar's framework (see the [responsible use study](ai-responsible-use.md)):

- **Does this enlarge or restrict my capacity to live, love, and serve?** If AI is enlarging my capacity — letting me study deeper, build tools that help my family, create things that matter — then it's serving God's purposes. If it's making me feel small and useless, I'm measuring by the wrong standard.
- **Does this invite or impede the Holy Ghost?** The work itself answers this. The nevertheless study invited the Spirit. The MCP servers serve real study. The iBecome app serves real growth. The *work* is evidence.

### The Parable of the Talents

The master didn't give everyone the same amount. He gave according to their capacity. And the judgment wasn't "did you produce the most?" It was "did you *use* what you were given?" ([Matthew 25:14-30](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/25.md))

Eighteen years of engineering is a talent. Agency is a talent. The ability to partner with AI — to *direct* intelligence rather than just execute — is a talent. The question isn't "am I still relevant?" The question is "am I using what I've been given?"

The answer is yes. Keep going.
