# Hope, and the Grammar of Pairs

*A study on hope, the faith-hope-charity triplet, and the spiritual logic of gospel doublets and triplets.*

---

## Binding Question

If faith is the principle of action and charity is seeing as Christ sees, then *what is hope* — and how does the triplet of faith, hope, and charity actually drive a soul toward salvation? And underneath that: the gospel keeps pairing things. Sacrifice and obedience. Laws, rights, and ordinances. Spirit and element. Why? What does a doublet or triplet carry that a single truth alone does not?

---

## I. What Hope Actually Means

Webster's 1828 dictionary gives hope two parts: "a desire of some good, accompanied with an expectation of obtaining it." Wishing without expectation is wistfulness. Expectation without desire is prediction. Hope is the fusion. I want it, *and* I expect it.

That second piece — expectation — is the load-bearing one. The Bible Dictionary's entry on faith opens, "Faith is to hope for things which are not seen, but which are true." Faith and hope are reciprocally defined: faith is the substance of what is hoped for ([Heb. 11:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/heb/11.md)); hope is the orientation of faith toward what is yet unseen ([Alma 32:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/32.md)). Strip away one and the other collapses.

But the BD definition adds a qualifier the modern ear can miss: the things hoped for must be *true*. Gospel hope is not directed at any wished-for thing. It is directed at real things that happen to be unseen. This distinguishes it from wishful thinking and from optimism. Optimism trusts the trend. Hope trusts a Person.

Webster's second sense makes the Person explicit: "One who, or that which, gives hope... The Lord will be the hope of his people" — quoting [Joel 3:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/joel/3.md). Paul says it as flatly as Joel: "Christ, which is our hope" ([1 Tim. 1:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-tim/1.md)). Hope is not a mood. It is an attachment to a Person who has promised something specific.

Mormon names that something specific in the densest passage on hope in scripture:

> "And what is it that ye shall hope for? Behold I say unto you that ye shall have hope through the atonement of Christ and the power of his resurrection, to be raised unto life eternal, and this because of your faith in him according to the promise." — [Moroni 7:41](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md)

The content of hope is the resurrection. The mechanism of hope is the Atonement. The instrument of hope is faith in Christ. Strip out the Christological cargo and the word becomes empty. This is why Peter called the assurance of resurrection a "lively hope" ([1 Pet. 1:3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-pet/1.md)) — a hope that is alive precisely because the One it points toward is alive.

## II. Hope as the Middle Term

Mormon does not present faith, hope, and charity as three parallel virtues. He presents them as a chain whose links depend on each other:

> "How is it that ye can attain unto faith, save ye shall have hope?" ([Moro. 7:40](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md))
>
> "Wherefore, if a man have faith he must needs have hope; for without faith there cannot be any hope." ([Moro. 7:42](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md))

Read those two verses against each other and the picture is reciprocal, not linear. Faith requires hope (you cannot trust what you do not anticipate). Hope requires faith (you cannot anticipate what you do not trust). Neither stands first. They arrive together — and they arrive in a humble heart, because Mormon then specifies that faith and hope are vain unless the person is "meek, and lowly of heart" ([Moro. 7:43](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md)).

Paul gives the same dynamic from the opposite direction. Romans 5 traces hope's *production*:

> "Tribulation worketh patience; And patience, experience; and experience, hope: And hope maketh not ashamed; because the love of God is shed abroad in our hearts by the Holy Ghost given unto us." ([Rom. 5:3-5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rom/5.md))

Tribulation is the soil. Patience is the root. Experience is the trunk. Hope is the branch. And love (the Greek behind charity) is the fruit. Hope is the penultimate term, the place where the chain has just enough vertical lift to open into love. Without hope, charity has no platform. With it, the Spirit pours out love as the next thing.

So hope sits in the middle of the triplet structurally, not arbitrarily. Faith is the trust that initiates. Hope is the orientation that sustains. Charity is the love that completes. Pull hope out and faith has no direction; pull hope out and charity has no soil.

Two more verses widen the picture. Paul, writing about Abraham:

> "That by two immutable things, in which it was impossible for God to lie, we might have a strong consolation, who have fled for refuge to lay hold upon the hope set before us: Which hope we have as an anchor of the soul, both sure and steadfast, and which entereth into that within the veil." ([Heb. 6:18-19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/heb/6.md))

Hope is an anchor. Anchors do their work elsewhere. They hold the ship in this harbor by gripping bottom in another place. Paul's hope-anchor enters *within the veil* — into the Holy of Holies, into the unseen presence of God. This is the geometry of hope: it stabilizes the present by reaching into the future where Christ already is. The hoped-for-thing is not absent; it is *not yet visible from where we are standing.*

Moroni gives the same image in his own words:

> "Whoso believeth in God might with surety hope for a better world, yea, even a place at the right hand of God, which hope cometh of faith, maketh an anchor to the souls of men, which would make them sure and steadfast, always abounding in good works." ([Ether 12:4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/12.md))

Same metaphor, same direction, same fruit. And note the verb chain: hope makes the soul sure and steadfast, which makes it abound in good works. Hope does not paralyze with longing. It activates with confidence.

### The Veil, Made Visible

Joseph's restoration gives that veil a body. The endowment ends with the initiate passing through a veil into a celestial room, a room built to feel like the place hope is anchored to. The temple's exterior reads *Holiness to the Lord, The House of the Lord*, and inside, the cares of Babylon thin out. You sit in the room and feel the Savior nearer than usual. That nearness is a foretaste, close enough to keep the cable taut without violating Paul's rule that "hope that is seen is not hope" ([Rom. 8:24](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rom/8.md)). The temple is hope's anchor made visible. We walk to where the anchor is, stand on the bottom for a few minutes, and walk back out.

Revelation supplies the other end of the cable. John sees two cities at the end of the vision. There is the New Jerusalem coming down from God ([Rev. 21:2](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rev/21.md)), and there is "Mystery, Babylon the Great, the mother of harlots and abominations of the earth" ([Rev. 17:5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rev/17.md)). A voice from heaven calls God's people out of the second toward the first: "Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins" ([Rev. 18:4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rev/18.md)). Hope is the cable strung between those two cities. It enters the veil and grips bottom in the holy one; it pulls the soul out of the harlot one. Lose the anchor and the current carries you toward Babylon by default. Keep it taut and you can be *in* Babylon without belonging to her, the way the temple sits in a city without taking on the city's character.

Notice what the inscription says. Not who built the building. Not what happens inside it. *Whose* it is. *Holiness to the Lord, The House of the Lord*. That is the inscription hope writes on the soul.

## III. The Triplet at Work — Faith, Hope, Charity

Three texts give the triplet in nearly identical wording:

- "And now abideth faith, hope, charity, these three; but the greatest of these is charity." ([1 Cor. 13:13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-cor/13.md))
- "Faith, hope and charity bringeth unto me—the fountain of all righteousness." ([Ether 12:28](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/12.md))
- "Wherefore, there must be faith; and if there must be faith there must also be hope; and if there must be hope there must also be charity." ([Moro. 10:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/10.md))

Paul, Moroni in his father's words, and Mormon. Three writers, separated by oceans and centuries, each giving the same triplet in the same order. This is itself an example of the witness law that section V will treat: "in the mouth of two or three witnesses every word shall be established" ([2 Cor. 13:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/2-cor/13.md)). The triplet is doctrinally established because it has three witnesses.

The order matters. Faith-hope-charity is not arbitrary. Faith trusts the unseen Christ. Hope orients the soul toward what He has promised. Charity is what the soul becomes when that orientation has done its work. The triplet is a single transformation viewed at three stages.

The clearest statement of this is the closing verse of Mormon's sermon on charity:

> "When he shall appear we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is; that we may have this hope; that we may be purified even as he is pure." ([Moro. 7:48](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md))

The grammar is dense. Unpacked: when Christ appears, we shall see Him as He is, AND we shall be like Him. *That seeing is what Mormon calls "this hope."* And the holding of this hope is what purifies us into His likeness.

John gives the identical doctrine in different words:

> "Beloved, now are we the sons of God, and it doth not yet appear what we shall be: but we know that, when he shall appear, we shall be like him; for we shall see him as he is. And every man that hath this hope in him purifieth himself, even as he is pure." ([1 John 3:2-3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-jn/3.md))

And Joseph Smith's school of theology, in *Lectures on Faith* 7, picks up the same passage and builds salvation on it: Christ is the prototype of all saved beings; salvation is being made like Him; "on this hinge turns the door of salvation" (LoF 7:16). Lecture 7 quotes 1 John 3:2-3 directly and asks the question outright: "Why purify himself as he is pure? because, if they do not they cannot be like him."

So Mormon, John, and the Joseph Smith school each independently witness the same doctrine of hope: hope is the present-tense orientation toward the future seeing of Christ, and that orientation is what purifies us into His likeness. Three witnesses. The witness law is satisfied. The doctrine is established.

This is the answer to the binding question — refined, not exactly as posed. Hope is not the present possession of the vision. Paul rules that out: "hope that is seen is not hope: for what a man seeth, why doth he yet hope for?" ([Rom. 8:24-25](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rom/8.md)). Hope is the *anticipation* of the seeing. It is the eye fixed on what will be seen but is not yet visible. And because the eye is fixed there, the soul is shaped by what it is looking at — the way a sunflower is shaped by the sun it has not yet reached.

So the triplet works like this. Faith is the trust that the prototype is real. Hope is the seeing-ahead that fixes the gaze on the prototype. Charity is what the soul becomes when it has been gazing long enough. Faith without hope has nothing to look at. Hope without charity is sentiment that never lands in another's life. Charity without faith has no source. The three are not three options; they are three names for one assimilation, viewed at three stages.

This is also why Moroni warns that losing hope is not neutral:

> "If ye have no hope ye must needs be in despair; and despair cometh because of iniquity." ([Moro. 10:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/10.md))

Despair is not the weakness God gives us to make us humble. Despair is iniquity's fruit. The fight to keep hope is the fight to keep oneself from sin. This is why hope is a moral discipline, not a mood. The mood comes and goes. The discipline of repenting and turning back to the prototype is what keeps the eye fixed.

D&C 4 raises one complication worth naming. There the list is not three but four: "faith, hope, charity *and love*, with an eye single to the glory of God, qualify him for the work" ([D&C 4:5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/4.md)). If charity already means love (per the Greek footnote at 1 Cor. 13:13), why both? The most coherent reading is that charity in 1 Cor. 13 means specifically *the pure love of Christ toward others* (cf. [Moro. 7:47](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md)), while love in D&C 4 is the broader disposition of heart Godward and brotherward together. The triplet expands into a quadruplet without breaking — and the next verse expands further into ten attributes (faith, virtue, knowledge, temperance, patience, brotherly kindness, godliness, charity, humility, diligence). Charity is the only repeat. It is the hinge between the smaller list and the larger one.

Note also the phrase tucked into v.5: "with an eye single to the glory of God." The eye image returns at the moment hope is named, just as it returns in Moro. 7:48 ("we shall see him as he is"). Hope keeps showing up alongside vision because hope *is* the way the soul sees forward.

### The Organ of Hope Is the Eye

Track the eye through the verses that have already arrived in this section: Moroni 7:48 — "*we shall see him as he is*"; 1 John 3:2 — "*we shall see him as he is*"; D&C 4:5 — "*with an eye single* to the glory of God." Three texts, three witnesses, one image. The eye is not incidental to hope; it is the organ through which hope operates.

The Sermon on the Mount states the principle as physiology:

> "The light of the body is the eye: if therefore thine eye be single, thy whole body shall be full of light." ([Matt. 6:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/6.md))

A single eye is not a symbolic eye. It is an eye fixed on one object so steadily that everything coming in is the same kind of light. Whatever the eye is gazing at is what the body is being filled with. This is a claim about how souls work. The body becomes what the eye stays open to.

Joseph's school takes the same image and stretches it forward into glory:

> "And if your eye be single to my glory, your whole bodies shall be filled with light, and there shall be no darkness in you; and that body which is filled with light comprehendeth all things… Therefore, sanctify yourselves that your minds become single to God, and the days will come that you shall see him; for he will unveil his face unto you." ([D&C 88:67-68](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/88.md))

Read v.68 against Hebrews 6:19. Paul's hope-anchor "entereth into that within the veil." D&C 88:68 says the Lord "will unveil his face unto you." Same vocabulary, same direction, same payoff. The veil that the anchor entered is the veil that gets pulled back. Hope reaches in; the Savior reaches out. The single eye is what makes the meeting possible.

*Lectures on Faith* 7 picks up the same passage from John's epistle and builds a theology of salvation on it. The lecture asks where we shall find a saved being to be assimilated into, and answers: Christ is "the prototype or standard of salvation." The lecture then quotes 1 John 3:2-3 directly and presses the question: "Why purify himself as he is pure? because, if they do not they cannot be like him." The conclusion is the line on which the whole hope study turns:

> "He proposed to make them like unto himself; and he was like the Father, the great prototype of all saved beings: And for any portion of the human family to be assimilated into their likeness is to be saved; and to be unlike them is to be destroyed: and on this hinge turns the door of salvation." (LoF 7:16)

The hinge is the assimilation. The way assimilation happens is the eye fixed on the prototype long enough that the body fills with the prototype's light. *Lectures on Faith* states it theologically. Matthew 6 states it physiologically. D&C 88 states it covenantally. Mormon and John state it eschatologically. Five witnesses, one mechanism: where the eye stays, the soul becomes.

This is also why despair is such a serious thing. To despair is to take the eye off the prototype. The body, no longer filling with that light, fills with whatever else is in view. Repentance, in this frame, is not first a re-doing of behavior but a re-aiming of the eye. Behavior follows what the eye is fixed on, the way the body follows the head. The fight to keep hope is the fight to keep the eye where it must stay.

## IV. Other Doublets and Triplets

The faith-hope-charity triplet is not an isolated phenomenon. The gospel arrives in pairs and triads constantly. The deeper question is whether these pairings are pedagogical (a writer's repetition for emphasis) or structural (the truth itself comes bound). Most are structural.

**Sacrifice and obedience.** This pair is canonical, not just rhetorical. The *General Handbook* and *Preach My Gospel* both list the temple covenants in a five-fold sequence: obedience, sacrifice, the gospel of Jesus Christ, chastity, and consecration. Two of those five are obedience and sacrifice in immediate succession. *Lectures on Faith* 6 makes the structural reason explicit: obedience is the daily form of submission, but obedience by itself produces only belief, not the certain knowledge that produces saving faith. Sacrifice is the maximal form. It is what gives the soul actual knowledge that "the course of life which he pursues is according to the will of God" (LoF 6:4). The two laws are not redundant. Obedience is the rhythm; sacrifice is the proof. Together they produce the assurance that lets faith become saving.

**Laws, rights, and ordinances.** This phrasing appears in the temple endowment and in the Restoration vocabulary around priesthood. The triplet describes a complete jurisdiction: *laws* (what God commands), *rights* (what God authorizes humans to do in His name), and *ordinances* (the formal acts that bind heaven and earth together). Remove any one and the system fails. Laws without rights are tyranny. Rights without laws are anarchy. Both without ordinances stay theoretical — they never get embodied in the actual covenant relationship. The triplet covers the three dimensions a covenant needs: directive, authority, and embodiment.

**Spirit and element.** "The elements are eternal, and spirit and element, inseparably connected, receive a fulness of joy" ([D&C 93:33-34](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93.md)). This pairing is metaphysical. It says joy requires both — neither spirit alone nor element alone is enough. The doctrine of the resurrection is the doctrine of this pair refusing to come apart.

**Mercy and justice.** Alma 42 is the canonical treatment. These two are not opposites resolved by compromise; they are two faces of the Atonement that meet without canceling each other. Mercy alone destroys justice. Justice alone destroys mercy. The pair is held together by a third — the suffering of Christ, which is itself the Atonement's logic.

**One heart and one mind.** "The Lord called his people Zion, because they were of one heart and one mind, and dwelt in righteousness" ([Moses 7:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/7.md)). Heart and mind together — affection and conviction. Not just shared belief; shared love. Not just shared love; shared belief. Zion is built on the doublet because either alone produces only a faction or a friendship.

**The Godhead.** The master triplet is the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost. Every other gospel triplet ultimately mirrors this one. The witnessing language follows directly: "the testimony of three witnesses will I send forth of my word" ([D&C 5:15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/5.md)); "in the mouth of three witnesses shall these things be established... of which the Father, and the Son, and the Holy Ghost bear record" ([Ether 5:4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/5.md)). The Godhead is itself the three witnesses that establish God's word.

This is the pattern: when a truth is structural — when it carries weight that one term cannot — the gospel gives it as a pair or a triad. When the truth is unitary, it comes alone. The form of the doctrine is part of the doctrine.

There is also a walking version of the triplet that ties everything together. Nephi gives it as the road from baptism to eternal life:

> "Wherefore, ye must press forward with a steadfastness in Christ, having a perfect brightness of hope, and a love of God and of all men. Wherefore, if ye shall press forward, feasting upon the word of Christ, and endure to the end, behold, thus saith the Father: Ye shall have eternal life." ([2 Ne. 31:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/31.md))

*Steadfastness in Christ* is faith in motion. *Perfect brightness of hope* is vision in motion. *Love of God and of all men* is charity in motion. The triplet is the road. The five-step doctrine of Christ (faith, repentance, baptism, Holy Ghost, endure to the end) is the gate and the destination. The triplet is what carries the soul across the strait and narrow path between them.

## V. Why Pairs and Triads Carry Extra Meaning

The structural reason is older than Christianity. It is in Deuteronomy:

> "One witness shall not rise up against a man for any iniquity, or for any sin, in any sin that he sinneth: at the mouth of two witnesses, or at the mouth of three witnesses, shall the matter be established." ([Deut. 19:15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/deut/19.md))

This was Israel's rule of evidence. A single voice could not establish a fact in court. Two or three could.

Christ took this rule and applied it to His own teaching: "If he will not hear thee, then take with thee one or two more, that in the mouth of two or three witnesses every word may be established" ([Matt. 18:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/18.md)). Paul cited it ([2 Cor. 13:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/2-cor/13.md)). Joseph received it as the standard for Restoration witnesses ([D&C 5:15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/5.md); [Ether 5:4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/5.md)). The Three Witnesses to the Book of Mormon were not chosen for narrative drama. They were chosen because the witness law required at least that many for a doctrine to be established.

So when the gospel pairs truths together — sacrifice and obedience, faith and hope, mercy and justice, spirit and element, one heart and one mind — it is not for emphasis. It is because *one term cannot stand alone as established doctrine.* The pairing is the doctrine's proof of legitimacy. Two or three terms held together establish what one term alone could only assert.

This is also why triplets are stronger than doublets. A doublet meets the minimum (two witnesses). A triplet exceeds it (three witnesses). When Mormon, John, and *Lectures on Faith* all teach the same hope-vision-purification doctrine, the doctrine is not merely supported — it is established at the highest evidentiary standard scripture sets. When Paul, Moroni, and Mormon all give the faith-hope-charity triplet in the same order, the triplet itself is established as doctrine, not just as a teacher's preferred grouping.

There is a second reason, deeper than the legal one. God Himself is a triplet — Father, Son, and Holy Ghost. The pattern of three witnessing as one is not just a courtroom rule we borrow. It is the structure of the Godhead applied to revealed truth. When the gospel arrives in threes, it is taking on the shape of its source. The form mirrors the Form.

This is why a single-verse argument always feels weaker than a multi-verse one, even when the single verse is true. The single verse asserts. The doctrine, properly given, is *established* — by two or three voices that the Spirit has confirmed independently. The grammar of pairs is not decoration. It is the gospel's way of saying: this is true enough that it has come with the evidentiary weight required.

## VI. Becoming

Hope is not a mood. Mormon and Moroni both refused to let it be one. Hope is a discipline maintained against despair, and despair is iniquity's fruit. The discipline is repentance — turning the eye back, again and again, to the prototype.

So the practical question is not "do I feel hopeful?" but "where am I looking?" When the eye fixes on what is unseen but true — Christ as He is, the resurrection, the better world at the right hand of God — the soul is shaped by what it is looking at. When the eye drifts to what is visible and discouraging, the soul is shaped by that instead. Hope as a discipline is the daily work of fixing the gaze.

For someone serving as a Sunday School president — for anyone in a calling — this matters in a specific way. Teaching the triplet without practicing it produces lessons that other people can see through. Faith without hope teaches duty without direction. Hope without charity teaches optimism without love. Charity without faith teaches kindness untethered from the Person who made it possible. The triplet practiced is the triplet taught. There is no shortcut.

The doublet of sacrifice and obedience cuts here too. Obedience is the rhythm of small, daily yeses. Sacrifice is what happens when the yes costs something the soul wanted to keep. Most weeks ask for the rhythm. Some weeks ask for the maximal form — and *Lectures on Faith* says it is precisely those weeks that produce the certain knowledge that lets faith become saving. The pair held together is what produces the assurance no single law alone can produce.

A few questions:

- Where is my eye fixed today — on the prototype, or on the visible discouragement?
- Where is my triplet incomplete? Have I let one of the three atrophy?
- Where in my life am I doing only the rhythm of obedience and avoiding the sacrifice that would actually establish my faith?
- Whose faith am I a witness to? Whose hope am I a second voice for? The witness law works for others too — my voice can be the second or third that establishes someone else's belief.

Hope is the eye that holds the gaze on Christ when He is not yet visible. Faith is the feet that walk toward Him in the dark. Charity is what the soul becomes when it has walked long enough that the resemblance starts to show.
