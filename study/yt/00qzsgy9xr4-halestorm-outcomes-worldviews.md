## Binding Question

The host advances three points and endorses a fourth from his guest Andrew Wilson. Which stand up against the actual scriptures and prophetic teachings of the Restoration, and which are partial truths that overreach when stated as boldly as he states them?

The four claims:
1. **Say the quiet part out loud.** Plain bold speech is overdue.
2. **Apply outcomes to religion.** "The question just isn't is it true, it's what does this produce consistently at scale" ([3:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=00qzsgy9xr4&t=229)).
3. **Worldview shapes interpretation.** Most debates aren't about facts; they're about the framework people use to interpret facts.
4. *(Wilson, host endorses)* **Christians should be in charge.** "You want your ideology to dominate." — "Yes." ([25:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=00qzsgy9xr4&t=1500))

## Summary

The host reviews a Triggernometry interview with Andrew Wilson. He pulls three points from the conversation and adds a fourth from his own framing: the LDS Church is "leading the pack" on measurable family/wellbeing outcomes ([17:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=00qzsgy9xr4&t=1043)), and Saints should be bold about it. He endorses Wilson's stronger claim that Christians should "dominate" because their outcomes are best ([25:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=00qzsgy9xr4&t=1500)), with the caveat that he himself is not "advocating for a monarch."

The video sits at an interesting intersection. Two of its main observations — that fruits matter and that worldview shapes interpretation — are scripturally grounded. The political frame those observations get folded into runs straight into canonical Restoration scripture and loses badly.

---

## In Line

**The fruits standard is canonical.** Christ's teaching that "ye shall know them by their fruits" ([Matt. 7:16-17](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/7.md)) is real, and Moroni gives it precise diagnostic form: "every thing which inviteth to do good, and to persuade to believe in Christ, is sent forth by the power and gift of Christ" ([Moroni 7:16](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md)). Alma's seed experiment in [Alma 32:30-43](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/32.md) is the same logic at the level of personal experiment. The host is on solid ground when he says outcomes are an underused tool in religious discussion.

**Worldview shapes interpretation.** This is true at the level of cognitive science (framing, confirmation bias) and at the level of scripture. Abinadi's diagnosis of the priests of Noah was precisely that they had memorized the text but not applied their hearts to understanding ([Mosiah 12:27](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/12.md)). Nephi names the same problem in the positive: Isaiah is plain unto those filled with the spirit of prophecy ([2 Ne. 25:4](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/25.md)). Plainness is a property of the reader, not the text. The host is reading the same dynamic and applying it to debate.

**Saints can be too timid.** There is a real critique buried in the "say the quiet part out loud" framing. Latter-day Saints often soft-pedal what the Restoration actually claims — angels, gold plates, an open canon, an embodied God, the doctrine of exaltation — out of social discomfort rather than scriptural caution. Abinadi did not soft-pedal. Samuel the Lamanite did not soft-pedal. Joseph did not soft-pedal. The plain account of the First Vision is bracingly plain.

**"Normative parasitism" is a useful term.** The observation that secular ideologies often borrow moral frameworks (family, restraint, dignity of the person) from religious traditions while having no internal generator for them is a serious philosophical claim made by people like Alasdair MacIntyre and Carl Trueman. Worth engaging with seriously rather than dismissing.

## Out of Line

**The dominance endorsement.** This is the heart of the problem. Wilson says: "I think Christians should be in charge of basically everything... they have no objection because they have no moral facts. So who cares?" ([16:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=00qzsgy9xr4&t=968)). At [25:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=00qzsgy9xr4&t=1500): "you want your ideology to dominate." Wilson: "Yes. ... Is that a Christian way of doing things? Yes."

The host endorses this without flagging the canonical contradiction. From the Restoration's own scripture:

> "We have learned by sad experience that it is the nature and disposition of almost all men, as soon as they get a little authority, as they suppose, they will immediately begin to exercise unrighteous dominion. Hence many are called, but few are chosen. No power or influence can or ought to be maintained by virtue of the priesthood, only by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned." — [D&C 121:39-41](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)

> "We claim the privilege of worshiping Almighty God according to the dictates of our own conscience, and allow all men the same privilege, let them worship how, where, or what they may." — [Article of Faith 11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/a-of-f/1.md)

> "The civil magistrate should restrain crime, but never control conscience; should punish guilt, but never suppress the freedom of the soul." — [D&C 134:4](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/134.md)

Three independent canonical witnesses — Liberty Jail, Joseph's own creed, and the church's declaration on government — establish the same word. *Persuasion only. Conscience inviolate. Force is the rejected plan.* Wilson is operating inside Reformed dominionism, where the political establishment of Christianity is a coherent goal. The Restoration is not Reformed. The Restoration's whole founding narrative is religious refugees from Missouri and Illinois who were expelled at gunpoint *by* a culture that thought it should "dominate." For an LDS commentator to endorse the same political move from the other side of the table, without flagging that the canonical scripture explicitly forbids it, is a serious miss.

The deeper point — and this is the one the video does not name — is that **force-based salvation is Lucifer's plan**. "I will redeem all mankind, that one soul shall not be lost... wherefore give me thine honor" ([Moses 4:1](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/4.md)). The War in Heaven was, at its root, an argument about whether to compel righteousness. That war's losing side wanted to dominate.

**Prosperity as proof of doctrine.** The host's central rhetorical move is: LDS members rank well on family/wellbeing metrics, *therefore* LDS doctrine is "better." The same Book of Mormon the host's argument depends on contains Mormon's editorial frame on this exact move, and it cuts the other way:

> "We can see that the Lord in his great infinite goodness doth bless and prosper those who put their trust in him. Yea, and we may see at the very time when he doth prosper his people... yea, then is the time that they do harden their hearts, and do forget the Lord their God, and do trample under their feet the Holy One — yea, and this because of their ease, and their exceedingly great prosperity." — [Helaman 12:1-2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/hel/12.md)

Mormon edited the entire Book of Mormon around the observation that prosperity is exactly when hearts harden. Reading prosperity as proof of righteousness inverts his warning. It is the same theological mistake Job's friends made.

**"It's not even a question. It's better. … Period."** ([24:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=00qzsgy9xr4&t=1482)) Bold testimony of the Restoration is one thing — Joseph in his own words, Wilford Woodruff's manifesto, Russell M. Nelson's clarity on the temple. Bold *comparative* claims on sociological metrics are something else. The first is a prophet's warrant. The second is a marketer's warrant. Confusing the two cheapens the first.

## Missed the Mark

**Sociological outcomes ≠ fruit of the Spirit.** Matthew 7 is about discerning *prophets*; Galatians 5:22-23 lists fruit as "love, joy, peace, longsuffering, gentleness, goodness, faith, meekness, temperance" — interior virtues, not survey data. The host slides from "fruit" (a doctrinal category about discernment and the Spirit) to "outcomes" (a sociological category about marriage rates and longevity) without flagging the move. The slide changes the meaning of the verse he's leaning on. Pew can measure fertility. Pew cannot measure meekness.

**The Substack is unsourced.** "I have the Substack article I mentioned on here before" ([17:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=00qzsgy9xr4&t=1050)) — but he doesn't name it on this video, doesn't link it, doesn't show the methodology. The factual claim is plausible (LDS members do score well on many family stability metrics in real research from Pew, IFS, Wilcox), but the host treats an unnamed Substack as if its conclusions establish doctrine.

**The "intelligence = pattern recognition" claim** ([13:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=00qzsgy9xr4&t=817)) is attributed to "a really smart friend" with no scripture, no prophet, no source. The Abraham 3 doctrine of intelligences is a layered teaching about premortal individual identity, eternal noncreation, and varying capacities. Reducing it to "pattern recognition" is a clever framing, but the host presents it as if it carries doctrinal weight.

**The unflagged Wilson endorsement.** The host disclaims one part of Wilson's view ("I'm not out here advocating for a monarch") but endorses the rest with "we should be bold... let the best ideology win." This leaves the audience without a clear marker of where the host agrees with Wilson and where he doesn't. A more careful evaluation would have said: *Wilson's "Christians should rule" claim is incompatible with D&C 121 and Article of Faith 11; here is what I take from him and here is what I have to leave behind.*

## Missed Opportunities

The video could have used several scriptures that would have either strengthened or properly bounded its argument.

- **[D&C 121:34-46](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)** is the Restoration's full statement on dominion vs. persuasion. It is impossible to talk about "Christians being in charge" in an LDS frame without engaging with this passage. The video does not.
- **[Helaman 12:1-6](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/hel/12.md)** is the pride-cycle warning embedded in Mormon's own editorial voice. It is the in-canon answer to "are good outcomes proof of righteousness?" It says: yes, *and* good outcomes are exactly the moment of greatest spiritual danger.
- **[Alma 30:7-12](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/30.md)** records that the Nephite legal system, under prophetic leadership, *explicitly protected* a man preaching against Christ. This is the LDS scripture's own stance on whether the dominant religion should suppress dissent. It is not silent on the question.
- **[John 6:15](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/6.md)**: when the multitude tried to make Christ king by force, he withdrew. The Savior himself refused political dominance.
- **[Matthew 5:3-12](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/5.md)**: the Beatitudes invert the host's outcome metric. Blessed are the poor, the mourning, the meek, the persecuted. None of these score well on Pew.
- **[3 Ne. 11:29-30](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/11.md)**: "he that hath the spirit of contention is not of me, but is of the devil... this is not my doctrine, to stir up the hearts of men with anger, one against another." The "let the best ideology win" framing is contention-flavored even when it's not openly hostile.

## Overall Assessment

This is a video with a real critique buried under a triumphalist frame that contradicts the scripture it claims to vindicate.

The real critique: *Saints often soft-pedal what the Restoration actually claims, and the doctrine has fruits that deserve to be discussed plainly rather than apologetically.* That critique is true and the host is right to make it.

The triumphalist frame: *Our outcomes are best, therefore our ideology should dominate, and we should say so boldly.* This frame is false on its own terms (Helaman 12 says prosperity is when hearts harden) and dangerous in its application (D&C 121:39 says this is exactly the moment unrighteous dominion gets exercised).

The two are entangled in the video, and a viewer who shares the host's frustration with apologetic timidity may pick up the bold testimony of the Restoration *along with* the political dominionism it gets bundled with. That bundling is what the evaluation has to name.

This is not a video to dismiss. The host is intelligent, the worldview frame is genuinely useful, the call to plainness has scriptural anchor. But it is a video to read with D&C 121, Article of Faith 11, D&C 134, Helaman 12, and the Beatitudes open in another tab. Without those, the bold testimony slides into political ambition, and the LDS scripture that ought to bound that slide gets left out of the conversation entirely.
