**To act for themselves.** Not to be acted upon by a badge system.

## Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

The most robust psychological framework for understanding motivation comes from Edward Deci and Richard Ryan (University of Rochester, 1985–present, with a major update paper in 2020). SDT identifies three innate psychological needs:

### The Three Needs

| Need | Definition | What It Looks Like | What Thwarts It |
|------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Autonomy** | Feeling that your actions come from your own volition and choice | "I'm doing this because *I* want to" | Being told exactly what to do and when. Controlling language. Rewards contingent on compliance. |
| **Competence** | Feeling effective, able to master challenges | "I can do this. I'm getting better." | Tasks too easy (boring) or too hard (overwhelming). No feedback. No recognition of growth. |
| **Relatedness** | Feeling connected to and cared about by others | "We're in this together. I'm known and I belong." | Isolation. Competition. Feeling like a number in a system. |

When all three needs are met, intrinsic motivation flourishes naturally. When any need is thwarted — especially autonomy — motivation decreases, sometimes dramatically.

*(Ryan & Deci, 2000, "Self-Determination Theory and the Facilitation of Intrinsic Motivation, Social Development, and Well-Being"; Ryan & Deci, 2020, "Intrinsic and extrinsic motivation from a self-determination theory perspective," Contemporary Educational Psychology)*

### The Motivation Continuum

SDT doesn't treat motivation as a binary (intrinsic vs. extrinsic). It describes a continuum:

```
Amotivation → External → Introjected → Identified → Integrated → Intrinsic
  (nothing)    (reward/    (guilt,       (I see the   (it's part   (I genuinely
               punishment)  ego, shame)    value)       of who       love this)
                                                        I am)
```

**External regulation:** "I do this because I get a badge / avoid a consequence." The behavior stops when the reward stops.

**Introjected regulation:** "I do this because I'd feel guilty if I didn't." Driven by internalized pressure, not genuine valuing. Common in religious contexts where "I should" replaces "I want to."

**Identified regulation:** "I do this because I understand why it matters." The person sees the value even if the activity itself isn't enjoyable. This is where much of real discipleship lives — service isn't always fun, but I serve because I know it matters.

**Integrated regulation:** "I do this because it's who I am." The value has become part of one's identity. A person of integrity doesn't weigh whether to be honest — it's just what they do.

**Intrinsic motivation:** "I do this because I find it inherently satisfying." The activity itself is the reward.

**The design goal isn't to make everything intrinsically motivating.** Not everything will be. The goal is to help people move *along the continuum* — from "I do this because Dad says so" toward "I do this because it's who I am." That's integration. That's becoming.

### What the Gospel Adds

SDT's continuum maps remarkably well onto the gospel's progression:

| SDT Level | Gospel Parallel |
|-----------|----------------|
| External | Obedience out of fear of punishment (Alma 36:12–14) |
| Introjected | Obedience out of duty / guilt ("I should read my scriptures") |
| Identified | Obedience because I see the blessings (D&C 130:20–21) |
| Integrated | Covenant identity — "I am a disciple, so I act like one" |
| Intrinsic | Charity — the pure love of Christ, where service IS joy (Moroni 7:47) |

The gospel's trajectory is from commandment → covenant → character → charity. The system we design should support that trajectory, not short-circuit it.

## The Overjustification Effect

In 1973, Lepper, Greene, and Nisbett at Stanford conducted a landmark experiment with preschool children. Kids who already enjoyed drawing were divided into three groups:

1. **Expected reward:** Told in advance they'd receive a certificate for drawing.
2. **Unexpected reward:** Given a certificate after drawing, but didn't know about it beforehand.
3. **No reward:** No certificate at all.

Results: The expected reward group later drew **significantly less** on their own than the other groups. The children who had been drawing for fun now treated it as work — something you only do when there's a reward.

*(Lepper, Greene & Nisbett, 1973, "Undermining Children's Intrinsic Interest with Extrinsic Reward," Journal of Personality and Social Psychology)*

### When This Happens

The overjustification effect occurs when:
- The reward is **expected in advance** (not a surprise)
- The reward is **contingent on doing the activity** (if-then: "if you do X, you get Y")
- The activity was **already intrinsically interesting** to the person
- The reward becomes the **perceived reason** for doing the activity

### When It Doesn't Happen

External rewards do NOT undermine motivation when:
- The reward is **unexpected** (you didn't know it was coming)
- The reward is **informational, not controlling** (it communicates competence, not compliance)
- The activity was **not already intrinsically motivating** (rewards can kickstart interest in things the person hasn't tried yet)
- The person has **high autonomy** (they chose to pursue the reward themselves)

### Applied to Title of Liberty

If we say: "Complete 3 service projects to earn the Liberty & Service badge" → we've created an if-then contingency. Service becomes labor for a badge. When the badge is earned, service stops.

If instead we say: "Tell us about a time you served someone this month" → and then we *recognize* the pattern after the fact → the recognition is informational ("we see who you're becoming") rather than controlling ("do this to get that").

### A More Recent Finding

Robinson, Gallus, Lee & Rogers (2019, Harvard) ran a field experiment with 15,329 students on awards and attendance:

- **Pre-announced awards** (prospective) did NOT improve attendance
- **Surprise awards** (retrospective) actually DECREASED subsequent attendance
- After prospective awards were removed, attendance dropped below baseline — a "crowding out" effect
- The awards may have inadvertently signaled: "Perfect attendance is so undesirable that we need to bribe you to do it"

*(Robinson et al., 2019, "The demotivating effect (and unintended message) of awards," Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes)*

## Gamification Research — When Badges Work and When They Don't

### The Mixed Evidence

A meta-analysis of 35 studies (Li, Hew & Du, 2024) found that gamification has an overall small positive effect on intrinsic motivation, but with important caveats:

- Gamification supports **autonomy and relatedness** more than competence
- **Meaningful choices and narrative elements** are the strongest positive factors
- Points and badges alone are the **weakest** form of gamification
- Leaderboards can be **actively harmful** through social comparison anxiety

*(Li, Hew & Du, 2024, "Gamification enhances student intrinsic motivation, perceptions of autonomy and relatedness," Educational Technology Research and Development)*

### When Gamification Works

A field study in an elementary school (Grabner-Hagen & Kingsley, 2023) found that gamification CAN support all three SDT needs when:

- Students have **genuine choice** within the system
- Challenges are **designed as "boss challenges"** (culminating real tasks) rather than badge collection
- The system provides **scaffolding** (not just rewards slapped on top of existing tasks)
- **Relatedness** is built into the structure (teamwork, shared narrative)

*(Grabner-Hagen & Kingsley, 2023, "From badges to boss challenges," Computers and Education Open)*

### The Key Insight: Functional Significance

Fei Gao (2024) introduced a critical SDT concept into gamification design: **functional significance**. The same game element — a badge, a leaderboard, a rank — can be experienced as either:

- **Autonomy-supportive** (informational) — "This badge reflects your growth"
- **Controlling** — "You must earn this badge to advance"

It's not WHAT you use but HOW it's framed. The mechanics don't determine the motivation — the meaning does.

*(Gao, 2024, "Advancing Gamification Research and Practice with Three Underexplored Ideas in Self-Determination Theory," TechTrends)*

### Bardach & Murayama: Beyond the Dichotomy

The most recent synthesis (Bardach & Murayama, 2025) argues we should stop asking "do rewards help or hurt?" and instead recognize that extrinsic rewards can **kickstart** the learning process:

1. Extrinsic reward gets someone to TRY an activity
2. The activity generates its own internal reward (curiosity, competence feelings)
3. The internal reward takes over, and the extrinsic reward becomes unnecessary
4. This is **motivation transformation** — from extrinsic to intrinsic

The risk is getting stuck at step 1 — where the extrinsic reward is always needed and the internal reward never develops.

*(Bardach & Murayama, 2025, "The role of rewards in motivation — Beyond dichotomies," Learning and Instruction)*

## Charlotte Mason — What a Homeschool Mom Already Knows

Charlotte Mason (1842–1923) was a British educator whose philosophy has been adopted by millions of homeschool families. Her framework is profoundly relevant:

### "Education is an Atmosphere, a Discipline, a Life"

1. **Atmosphere** (1/3 of education): The ideas that govern the parents' lives. Children absorb the environment. You cannot curriculum-ize this.
2. **Discipline** (1/3): Habit training. The intentional formation of good habits — attention, obedience, truthfulness — through consistent, patient practice. "Habit is ten natures."
3. **Life** (1/3): Living ideas through living books, narration, nature study. The material itself educates. Not worksheets. Not checklists. Living thoughts from living minds.

### Mason on Prizes and Ranks

Charlotte Mason was **explicit** that education should not use:
- Prizes or competitive ranking
- Artificial motivators
- Grades or scores as incentives

Her reasoning: these replace the child's natural curiosity and the inherent richness of the material with an inferior substitute. The child learns to read *for the grade* instead of *for the feast*.

> "Each of us has in his possession an exceedingly good servant or a very bad master, known as Habit." — Charlotte Mason

### What Mason Would Say About Our System

Mason would likely affirm:
- **The Book of Mormon as a living book** — yes, absolutely. Feed children the real thing, not a curriculum ABOUT the real thing.
- **Habit training** — yes, daily scripture study as a HABIT is exactly right. The habit does the heavy lifting, not the motivation system.
- **Family atmosphere** — yes, the parents' own relationship with scripture matters more than any program.

Mason would likely challenge:
- **59 merit badges** — this is "education from without." It's a system imposed on the child rather than an atmosphere that grows the child.
- **Rank progression** — this introduces competitive ranking and artificial milestones. The child's growth is its own reward.
- **Badge requirements** — these turn living activities (service, prayer, scripture study) into checklist items. The life goes out of them.

### The Mason–SDT Alignment

| Charlotte Mason | SDT Need | Notes |
|-----------------|----------|-------|
| Atmosphere | Relatedness | The family environment creates belonging |
| Discipline (habits) | Competence | Habits build capacity without requiring motivation |
| Life (living ideas) | Autonomy / Intrinsic motivation | The material itself sparks interest; the child responds |

## The BSA: What Actually Worked (and What Didn't)

The Baylor University study "Merit Beyond the Badge" (2010) found that Eagle Scouts, compared to non-Scouts, showed greater:
- Planning and goal-setting skills
- Leadership behaviors
- Close relationships
- Volunteering and charitable giving
- Community engagement

*(Jang, Johnson & Kim, 2010, "Eagle Scouts: Merit Beyond the Badge," Baylor Institute for Studies of Religion)*

### Why It Worked (Through an SDT Lens)

| SDT Need | How BSA Addressed It |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Autonomy** | 130+ merit badges to choose from. Only 13 required for Eagle. The Scout chose his own path. |
| **Competence** | Clear, achievable requirements. 1-on-1 work with a merit badge counselor. Progressively harder ranks. |
| **Relatedness** | The troop. The patrol. Camping together. Shared uniform and identity. Adult mentors. |

BSA's genius was the *balance* between structure and choice. The required core was **small** (13 of 21 for Eagle), and the choice space was **enormous** (130+ merit badges). The Scout felt both directed and free.

### Why It Failed for Some

- Only ~6% of Scouts earned Eagle. The program was demanding.
- Institutional dependency — it required a functioning troop, trained adults, meeting space, camping infrastructure.
- The rank system DID create external motivation patterns. Some kids earned badges mechanically with no real engagement.
- Social pressure from parents pushing reluctant kids.

### The Lesson for Title of Liberty

BSA worked **because of choice**, not because of structure. The structure was the container; choice was the fuel. Our v1 design has 59 badges and a highly prescribed path. BSA had 130+ badges and a broad path. If we want the BSA effect, we need much more choice and much less prescription.

## Synthesis: What This Means for Title of Liberty

### The Real Tension

Michael's wife named two tensions. They're actually the same tension:

1. **Open-ended (Children & Youth) vs. Structured (BSA/Title of Liberty)** → This is about **autonomy**
2. **External motivation (badges/ranks) vs. Internal motivation** → This is about **where on the SDT continuum the system operates**

Both concerns point to the same redesign principle: **the system needs to support autonomy far more than it currently does.**

### Design Principles from the Research

1. **Framework tight, content loose.** The pillars, the rhythm (daily/weekly/monthly), the family council — these are the *structure*. What goes INTO those containers should be chosen by the person, not prescribed by the system.

2. **Recognition, not reward.** Stop pre-announcing what kids will earn. Instead, notice what they're becoming. "I've seen you reading your scriptures every morning without being asked. That's the character of a Scout of Nephi." Recognition after the fact is informational. Reward before the fact is controlling.

3. **Habits over badges.** Charlotte Mason is right: habit training is more powerful than achievement tracking. A child who has the HABIT of daily scripture study doesn't need a badge to motivate it. The habit does the work. Focus the system on forming 5–7 core habits, not earning 59 badges.

4. **The Book of Mormon IS the curriculum.** Don't build a system ABOUT the Book of Mormon. Build a system where the family READS the Book of Mormon together and lets it do the work. The book is alive. Trust it.

5. **Choice is the fuel.** If there ARE badges/quests, make the choice space enormous and the requirements small. "Pick any 3 things you want to grow in this quarter" not "complete these 8 specific requirements."

6. **The parent burden must be near zero.** If the system requires Mom to track progress, review badges, plan challenge nights, and run ceremonies — it will die. The tech should handle tracking. The family council should be conversation, not administration.

7. **Start with atmosphere, not curriculum.** The parents' own Book of Mormon practice is 1/3 of the education (Mason). Before any system launches, the adults should be living it first. The kids will absorb what the parents model.

### The Gospel Test

Any system we build should produce people who match [D&C 58:27](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/58.md):

> "Anxiously engaged in a good cause, and do many things of their own free will, and bring to pass much righteousness"

If our system produces people who wait to be commanded (v. 29) — who serve only when a badge requires it, who read only when a rank demands it — we've built the wrong thing.

The test is simple: **Does this system produce agents, or does it produce the acted-upon?**
