# Voice Analysis: AI Presentation Voice vs. Michael's Writing Voice

*March 13, 2026 — A self-audit prompted by [this AI-generated YouTube video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3lPk4SLiwuM)*

---

## What Prompted This

Michael watched an AI-generated video ("A Calculator Just Banned the Entire State of California" by Loyal Moses) and noticed the same verbal tics he'd been hearing in our study documents. The video's transcript uses "Now, let that land for a second" at 0:48 — a phrase that also appears in our own podcast notes. The concern: our study output reads like a YouTube script or presentation notes, not like a book or a serious essay.

He's right. And this analysis is an honest audit of the problem.

---

## Part 1: The AI Presentation Voice — What It Sounds Like

These patterns show up in AI-generated YouTube scripts, podcast outlines, and — honestly — in a lot of our study documents. They come from training data saturated with spoken-word content: TED talks, sermons, video essays, podcasts.

### The Verbal Tics

| Pattern | Examples from Our Studies | What It's Doing |
|---------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| **"Let that land"** / **"Let that sit"** | "Let the symbols land differently" (plan-of-salvation.md), "Let that land" (podcast notes) | Mimics a presenter's dramatic pause. Works in a talk. Feels manipulative on paper. |
| **"Sit with that"** / **"Sit with it"** | "Sit with it. Let questions form naturally." (fatigue.md), "sit with *who* He is" (how-is-it-done.md) | Tells the reader what to feel instead of writing something worth sitting with. |
| **"Here's the thing"** / **"Here's where it gets..."** | "And here's the thing that stops me cold" (nevertheless.md), "Here's where it gets beautiful" (priesthood-and-gifts.md), "Here's where it turns" (priestcraft.md) | Stage-manager language. Points a spotlight before the substance arrives. A book just delivers the substance. |
| **"That's not nothing"** / **"That's everything"** | "That's the whole movement" (divine-love.md), "That's the whole purpose of the plan" (podcast notes) | Emotional amplifier. Tells you to be impressed rather than writing something impressive. |
| **"But here's the catch"** / **"But here's the tension"** | "But here's the catch — law only *preserves* what already abides in it" (truth-atonement.md), "But here's the tension I need to name honestly" (endtimes-servant.md) | Pivot phrase borrowed from video essays. Creates artificial suspense. In a book, the logic just turns. |
| **"This matters because..."** | "This matters because Isaiah pairs 'consumption' with 'decreed'" (consumption-decreed.md), "This matters because it invites us in" (how-is-it-done-prophets.md) | Explains the reader's own reaction to them. A good writer trusts the reader to feel the weight. |
| **"Read that again"** | "Read that again" (eternity-paused.md) | Direct command to the reader. Works in a blog post or sermon. In a book, the writing itself should make you re-read. |
| **"stops me cold"** | "stops me cold every time I read it" (abinadi-how-to-read.md), "And here's the thing that stops me cold" (nevertheless.md), "in terms that stop you cold" (how-is-it-done.md) | Emotive stage direction. Three uses across different studies — that's a verbal tic, not a stylistic choice. |
| **"This isn't just X — it's Y"** | "This isn't just 'old' — this is *eternal in both directions*" (order-of-god.md), "This isn't about buildings. It's about us" (helaman-why.md), "this isn't just 'no more floods'" (cfm study) | The "not X — but actually Y" pivot. Ubiquitous in video essays. Occasionally powerful, but we use it constantly. |
| **Heavy em-dash pivots** | Nearly every paragraph in every study | Em dashes create oral rhythm — breath marks, essentially. A few per page: fine. Three per paragraph: you're writing a transcript. |

### The Podcast / Presentation Skeleton

Our podcast notes in `study/podcast/` are the clearest examples, but the voice bleeds into the main studies too:

- "And here's the thing — this is Nephi in the middle of his psalm of anguish."
- "So that's the invitation. Now here's the warning — same section, eleven verses later"
- "But here's the question we haven't asked yet: **What happens when a whole people starts doing this?**"

Every one of these is a speaker's beat — a verbal cue that says "follow me to the next point." They work in a talk. They create a breathless pace on the page that doesn't let the reader think.

## Part 2: Michael's Actual Writing Voice

From the cpuchip.net blog (Wayback Machine, 2012–2014), here's what Michael sounds like when he's writing for himself:

### Characteristics

**1. Concrete and matter-of-fact.** Michael leads with what happened, not how to feel about it.

> "So today I borrowed a ladder from our neighbor and set to work replacing the pads on our evaporative cooler. It seemed that the pads were several years old, the base of the cooler had a lot of filth in it."

No "let that sink in." No "here's the thing." Just: this is what I did. This is what I found. Keep reading if you're interested.

**2. Enthusiasm is direct and unadorned.**

> "That was single-handedly the best one hour I've spent at OpenWest."
> "cake walk."
> "It was super useful!"

When Michael is excited, he says "that was the best" or "super useful" or just "cake walk." He doesn't build a runway with "And here's why this matters..." He just says it landed.

**3. First person, present tense, show-don't-tell.**

> "I've found busybox's vi, find and grep most useful when I'm poking around in a terminal on my Motorola Atrix."
> "I'm very excited to see these boxes work."
> "I'm looking forward to the data gathering and processing side of the project."

He tells you what he's doing and what he's looking forward to. He doesn't narrate his own emotional arc.

**4. Personal details without sentimentality.**

> "I still don't know if we'll have enough to buy new tires and pay for our car registration this month. I pray and wish you all the best."

That's harder to read than anything in our studies — and Michael didn't frame it, didn't prime the emotion, didn't say "let that land." He just told the truth and moved on.

**5. Questions are genuine, not rhetorical.**

> "I really don't know how to get rid of it besides scraping it out by hand... If anyone has a good way of cleaning it out, let me know!"

Compare to the AI voice: "And that raises a question that runs through the rest of this study: can God conditionally stop being what He is?" The AI question is rhetorical and guiding. Michael's question is: I don't know. Do you?

**6. Short sentences and informal connectors.**

> "Not perfectly level, but it'll get the job done."
> "Oh well."
> "It was cute."
> "She saw herself in the mirror and said pretty. It was cute."

No pyrotechnics. The plainness carries the warmth.

## Part 3: Where Our Studies Fall on the Spectrum

### Studies That Already Sound More Like Michael

The **Consumption Decreed** study is one of the best. It opens with a concrete event (Operation Epic Fury), tells a personal story (the Spirit prompting a move), and says plainly "I've never fully understood what this actually means. Time to find out." That's Michael's voice — direct, curious, grounded in lived experience. The Hebrew word study that follows is scholarly without being showy.

The **Endtimes Servant** study is also strong. "Lord Bunvader predicted I'd default to 'Christ or Joseph Smith.' That's a fair challenge." That's a real person with a real interlocutor. It reads like someone working through a problem, not performing insight.

### Studies That Sound Most Like a YouTube Script

The **podcast notes** are the worst offenders — but they're *supposed* to be spoken-word, so that's partly by design. The real problem is when the podcast voice leaks into studies that should read like prose:

- **How Is It Done** opens strong ("face in the dirt, guilt swept away") but then: "This study doesn't aim to explain the Atonement — it aims to stand closer to it." That's a keynote opening line.
- **Divine Love** has excellent analysis but uses "The question lands differently" and "this is already a different picture" — both verbal signposts rather than written transitions.
- **Receive** is solid but "A good writer trusts the reader to feel the weight" — that's the AI moralizing about writing, not actually writing well.

## Part 4: A Style Guide for the Book Voice

Based on this audit, here's what "more like a book, less like a YouTube video" would look like in practice:

### Replace

| AI Presentation Voice | Book Voice Equivalent |
|-----------------------|----------------------|
| "Let that land." | *(just let the paragraph end. White space is the pause.)* |
| "Sit with that for a moment." | *(cut it. If the writing is good, they will.)* |
| "Here's the thing..." | *(delete and start with the thing itself)* |
| "This matters because..." | *(state the consequence directly)* |
| "Read that again." | *(make them want to by writing it well the first time)* |
| "That's not nothing." / "That's everything." | *(find the specific thing that makes it matter and name it)* |
| "stops me cold" | First use: fine. Third use: it's a verbal tic. Vary the expression or just quote the scripture and let it hit. |
| "And here's the catch—" | "But" or just the next sentence. The logic does the turning. |
| "This isn't just X — it's Y" | Once per study: fine. Once per section: it's a formula. |

### Keep

- **Em dashes** — but limit to 2-3 per paragraph max, not 5-6.
- **Direct "I" voice** — Michael's blog does this naturally. "I've never understood this. Time to find out."
- **Webster 1828** — this is one of our best tools and it doesn't sound AI at all.
- **Footnote-chasing** — "the footnotes hand it to us on a silver platter" is a great phrase that sounds like Michael. Keep that energy.
- **Tables** — they organize information cleanly. Books use tables too.
- **Questions** — but ask genuine ones ("What does this mean?") not rhetorical ones ("And doesn't that change everything?")

### The Test

Before publishing a paragraph, ask: Would Michael write this in a blog post? If the answer is "no, this sounds like a TED talk," rework it.

A good heuristic: **If you have to tell the reader to pause, you haven't written something worth pausing for.**

---

## Part 5: Specific Remediation

Going forward, I'll watch for these specific patterns and default to plainer prose. When revising existing studies for publication:

1. **Search for "let that" / "sit with" / "here's the thing" / "this matters because"** — evaluate each instance. Most can be cut.
2. **Count em-dashes per paragraph.** If more than 2, restructure.
3. **Check for "stops me cold" / "lands differently" / "read that again"** — one per study is a stylistic choice; more is a verbal tic.
4. **Every "This isn't just X — it's Y" gets a second look.** Is the contrast real and necessary, or am I just doing the video-essay pivot?
5. **Let paragraphs end.** White space does the work that "let that land" pretends to do.

The goal isn't to strip personality from our writing. It's to make sure the personality is Michael's — concrete, direct, enthusiastic without being manipulative, personal without being performative. A man who says "cake walk" and "it was cute" and "I pray and wish you all the best" without a single dramatic pause.
