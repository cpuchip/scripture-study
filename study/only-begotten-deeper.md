# Three Births: Spirit, Flesh, and Covenant

> **Binding question:** The scriptures describe three distinct parent-child relationships — spirit children of heavenly parents, mortal children of earthly parents, and covenant children of Jesus Christ. Does the Restoration actually support this framework? And does any of it diminish what it means that Jesus is the *only* one whose physical father and spirit father are the same Being?

---

## Starting with Your Intuition

You're not missing something. You've traced something real.

The framework you described — intelligence → spirit body → physical body, with three corresponding sets of parents — is scriptural. It has support across the standard works and modern revelation. The confusion that sometimes arises isn't because the doctrine is unclear; it's because Christ is called "Father" in several different senses, and when those usages bleed together, the distinct relationships get blurred.

Let me work through each tier, then trace the distinctions.

---

## Tier One: Spirit Children of Heavenly Parents

The doctrine of premortal spirit creation begins with an established fact:

> "Man was also in the beginning with God. Intelligence, or the light of truth, was not created or made, neither indeed can be." — [D&C 93:29](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93.md)

Intelligences are eternal. They weren't created ex nihilo. But they were *organized* — clothed in spirit bodies, given identity and form. Abraham saw the result:

> "Now the Lord had shown unto me, Abraham, the intelligences that were organized before the world was; and among all these there were many of the noble and great ones; And God saw these souls that they were good, and he stood in the midst of them." — [Abraham 3:22–23](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/3.md)

D&C 93:23 confirms: "Ye were also in the beginning with the Father; that which is Spirit, even the Spirit of truth." We were with the Father before mortality. We are his spirit children.

And the Family Proclamation states it plainly: "Each is a beloved spirit son or daughter of **heavenly parents**, and, as such, each has a divine nature and destiny." — [The Family: A Proclamation to the World](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1995/10/the-family-a-proclamation-to-the-world.md)

Plural: *heavenly parents*. A Father and a Mother.

Heavenly Mother is one of the most carefully held doctrines in the Restoration — present in the hymns since 1845, affirmed in the Proclamation, referenced by presidents of the Church — but not much elaborated. Her name is not revealed. Her specific attributes are not detailed. The silence has been interpreted variously, but the *existence* of a heavenly mother is confirmed. What the Proclamation says is enough: we are spirit sons and daughters of heavenly parents. We came from a Family before we came here.

Paul confirms the title from the New Testament side: "Furthermore we have had fathers of our flesh which corrected us, and we gave them reverence: shall we not much rather be in subjection unto the **Father of spirits**, and live?" — [Hebrews 12:9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/heb/12.md). The distinction is explicit. Fathers of flesh, Father of spirits. Two different categories of paternity.

---

## Tier Two: Mortal Children — And Why It Matters for Christ

The physical body comes from mortal parents. This is ordinary biology with one extraordinary exception.

For every person who has ever lived, the chain is:
- Eternal intelligence → organized into spirit body by heavenly parents → born into mortality through earthly parents → receives a physical body of flesh and bone

For Jesus Christ, the second step is identical: organized as the Firstborn spirit child before the world was ([D&C 93:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93.md)). But the third step is different. His mortal body was not fathered by a mortal man.

Mosiah 15:3 says he was "the Father, because he was conceived by the power of God." Alma 7:10 says Mary "shall be overshadowed and conceive by the power of the Holy Ghost." His physical father was the Eternal Father — the same Being who was also his spirit father.

For everyone else who has ever lived, spirit parent and physical parent are different. For Jesus, they are the same. That's what "Only Begotten" means. He is the only person in the history of all creation whose Heavenly Father fathered him twice — once in the premortal spirit creation, and once in the physical birth.

That dual inheritance is what made the Atonement structurally possible. He could die because he had mortality from Mary. He could rise because he had immortality from the Eternal Father. No one who had only mortal parents could do what he did. No immortal being could have done it either. The Only Begotten — with both — could.

Your three tiers don't diminish that uniqueness. They clarify it. *We* have different parents at each tier. *He* had the same Father at both.

---

## Tier Three: Children of Christ Through Covenant

Here the scriptures are explicit and consistent, and they deserve to be read carefully.

King Benjamin closes his address to his people. They've heard the plan of salvation from an angel, covenanted with God, and had their hearts changed. Then he tells them what the covenant accomplished:

> "And now, because of the covenant which ye have made ye shall be called the children of Christ, his sons, and his daughters; for behold, this day he hath **spiritually begotten** you; for ye say that your hearts are changed through faith on his name; therefore, ye are born of him and have become his sons and his daughters." — [Mosiah 5:7](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/5.md)

Not sons and daughters of God the Father — specifically **children of Christ**, Christ's sons, Christ's daughters. The covenant creates a new parent-child relationship with Jesus himself.

After Adam was baptized, Christ spoke to him:

> "Behold, thou art one in me, a son of God; and thus may all become my sons." — [Moses 6:68](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md)

My sons. Not children of the Father. Christ is claiming the role of father to those who come to him.

The brother of Jared saw Christ in his premortal spirit form. Christ identified himself:

> "Behold, I am he who was prepared from the foundation of the world to redeem my people. Behold, I am Jesus Christ. I am the Father and the Son. In me shall all mankind have life, and that eternally, even they who shall believe on my name; and they shall become **my sons and my daughters**." — [Ether 3:14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/3.md)

And D&C 93:22 connects it to the Church: "All those who are begotten through me are partakers of the glory of the same, and are the church of the Firstborn."

Begotten *through* Christ. Partakers of *his* glory. The Church of *the Firstborn* — taking the name of the one who fathered them into the covenant. Every baptized, covenant-keeping member is, in a scripturally precise sense, a child of Jesus Christ.

---

## "My Father, and Your Father" — The Distinction Jesus Maintains

To your question: does Jesus ever say "our Father"?

Not in his own prayers. Not once.

In John 17 — the longest recorded prayer of Jesus, given hours before Gethsemane — he addresses God repeatedly: "Father," "Holy Father," "O righteous Father." Personal, intimate, direct. Never "our Father."

In Matthew 6:9, Jesus says: "After this manner therefore pray **ye**: Our Father which art in heaven." He is giving his disciples a model for *their* prayer. The "our" belongs to the disciples — their collective address to the Father. Jesus is teaching them how to approach the Father, not joining them in that address. He teaches the prayer; he doesn't recite it as his own.

The clearest statement comes after the resurrection, when Mary finds him in the garden. He says:

> "Touch me not; for I am not yet ascended to my Father: but go to my brethren, and say unto them, I ascend unto **my Father**, and **your Father**; and to my God, and your God." — [John 20:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/20.md)

He doesn't say "our Father." He says "my Father, and your Father" — two separate phrases, explicitly distinguished. Even at the resurrection, post-Atonement, after everything that was accomplished, Jesus still maintains the distinction. His sonship to the Father is different from ours in kind, not just degree.

Why? Because he is the Only Begotten — the one whose physical father is the Eternal Father. Our sonship to the Father is real (we are spirit children), but it is different. We are spirit sons and daughters of the Father. He is the Only Begotten Son — spirit AND physical.

---

## Why Christ Is Called "Father" — The Different Senses

This is the source of the confusion you're identifying. Christ is called "Father" in multiple distinct senses across the scriptures, and they don't mean the same thing.

**Sense 1: Father of the redeemed through covenant.**
This is what Mosiah 5:7, Moses 6:68, and Ether 3:14 describe. When we enter covenant with Christ, he spiritually begets us. He is our Father through covenant adoption.

**Sense 2: Father by divine investiture of authority.**
When Christ speaks and acts, he represents the Father perfectly and completely. D&C 93:4 explains: "The Father because he gave me of his fulness." Christ carries the Father's glory, nature, and authority — so completely that "he that hath seen me hath seen the Father" ([John 14:9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/14.md)). When Christ says "I am the Father" in some contexts, he means: I am the authorized representative; to know me is to know the Father.

**Sense 3: Father of creation.**
"By mine Only Begotten I created these things" ([Moses 2:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/2.md)). The worlds were made through Christ. As creator, he is "father" to all created things.

None of these three senses is the same as God the Father's role as our premortal spirit parent. When the scriptures blur between them, the reader has to track which sense is in play.

D&C 130:22 is the key anchor: "The Father has a body of flesh and bones as tangible as man's; the Son also." They are distinct Beings with distinct bodies. They are united in purpose, in nature, in love — but they are not the same person. The Father is the Father. The Son is the Son. The family structure is real, not metaphorical.

## Romans 8 — Your Reading Is Correct

Your instinct about Romans 8:16–17 is right. Let me trace the whole passage:

v.14: "As many as are led by the Spirit of God, they are the sons of God." — Led by the Spirit → children of the Father.

v.15: "Ye have received the Spirit of adoption, whereby we cry, Abba, Father." — Abba is an Aramaic word of personal intimacy: Father, Daddy. The Spirit enables us to address the Father in this way — as children, not as subjects.

v.16: "The Spirit itself beareth witness with our spirit, that we are the children of God." — The Father's children. The Spirit confirms this.

v.17: "And if children, then heirs; heirs of God, and joint-heirs **with** Christ; if so be that we suffer with him, that we may be also glorified together." — [Romans 8:16–17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rom/8.md)

The structure is: children of God the Father → heirs of the Father → joint-heirs *with* Christ, not joint-heirs *instead of* or *equal to* Christ. We share the Father's inheritance alongside the natural Son. We receive through him what he receives by right.

v.29: "He also did predestinate to be conformed to the image of his Son, that he might be the firstborn among many brethren." The goal is to be shaped into Christ's image — not to become the Only Begotten, but to be conformed to it. He is "firstborn among many brethren" — the elder, the pattern, the one whose image we are being shaped toward.

Paul's whole argument in Romans 8 is exactly what you described: we are spirit children of the Father, and through the covenant (mediated by the Spirit) we are confirmed in that sonship and welcomed back to the Father as heirs. Christ is the means. The Father is the destination.

## What About "His Bride the Church"?

Your intuition about Christ and his Church functioning as a kind of spiritual parental unit has real scriptural resonance — the "bride of Christ" imagery runs through Paul (Ephesians 5:25-27), Revelation (Rev 19:7-9), and the Restoration. The marriage supper of the Lamb is a real covenant image.

But the scriptures that describe the covenant as a parent-child relationship consistently put Christ as the sole father in that relationship. Mosiah 5:7 says *he* spiritually begets us. Moses 6:68 says "thus may all become *my* sons." There's no "co-parent" framing in the covenant-adoption texts.

The closest parallel might be this: D&C 132:19-20 establishes that exalted couples in eternal marriage will have "continuation of the seeds forever and ever" — they become creators and parents in turn. D&C 130:2 says "that same sociality which exists among us here will exist among us there." Our heavenly parents had a heavenly family. Exalted couples will form eternal families.

So the model in eternity isn't "Christ and Church as co-parents" but something more like: through covenant with Christ we are adopted as his children and returned to the Father; through eternal marriage we eventually become parental units ourselves, continuing the pattern of the divine family downward.

The "bride" image describes the *covenant relationship* between Christ and his people — it's more about fidelity, love, and covenant than about literal parenthood. The parenthood comes through the spiritual begetting language, and that belongs to Christ.

## The Framework in Summary

| Relationship | Parents | What We Receive |
|---|---|---|
| Spirit children of heavenly parents | God the Father + Heavenly Mother | Spirit body, divine nature, eternal identity |
| Mortal children of earthly parents | Earthly father + mother | Physical body, mortality, capacity to suffer and die |
| Covenant children of Christ | Jesus Christ | Spiritual rebirth, access to the Father, inheritance |

Three real relationships. Three distinct kinds of parenthood. None cancels the others. Each is required for the plan to work.

And Christ occupies a unique position in the table: the Only Begotten is the one person whose spirit parent (Heavenly Father) and physical parent (Heavenly Father through Mary) are the same. That uniqueness isn't a problem for the framework — it's the hinge that holds it together. He could die because he had mortal parentage. He could rise because he had divine parentage. He could redeem because his unique dual nature gave him the access to both life and death that no one else has ever had.

## The Path Back

There's one more thread worth tracing. The covenant relationship with Christ doesn't just adopt us as his children — it returns us to the Father.

D&C 132:12: "No man shall come unto the Father but by me or by my word, which is my law."

D&C 93:19: "I give unto you these sayings that you may understand and know how to worship, and know what you worship, that you may come unto the Father in my name, and in due time receive of his fulness."

We were spirit children of the Father before this world. We came to mortality. The fall cut off direct access to the Father's presence. The covenant with Christ — which makes us spiritually his children — doesn't replace our identity as spirit children of the Father. It restores the path home.

Moses 1 is the lived version of this. God tells Moses: "thou art in the similitude of mine Only Begotten" ([Moses 1:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/1.md)). Moses holds that identity. Satan comes, tries to redefine him ("Moses, son of man"), and eventually claims the title "Only Begotten" himself. Moses's defense throughout is not a theological argument. It's identity: "I am a son of God, in the similitude of his Only Begotten; and where is thy glory?" He knows who he is. He knows whose he is. He knows the real thing by having seen it.

That's the posture the framework gives us. We are spirit children of God. We have a divine nature and destiny (Family Proclamation). We were there in the beginning with the Father (D&C 93:23). We have covenant access to Christ who has opened the path back (D&C 132:12). And the Only Begotten — the one who made the whole thing possible — is our older brother who went before us ([Romans 8:29](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rom/8.md)), the Firstborn among many brethren.

---

## Becoming

The three-tier framework isn't an abstract theological grid. It's a description of who you are and what kind of help you have.

You are a spirit child of God — with a divine mother and father you knew before this world. That identity predates mortality. It doesn't expire.

You have a physical body — the second gift, the one that makes you capable of suffering and learning and feeling and dying and rising. It's the prerequisite for everything the third tier makes possible.

You have (or can have) a covenant relationship with Christ — who has literally claimed the role of father to those who enter covenant with him. He didn't just die for you. He begot you, in Benjamin's language. You are his.

And the goal of all three is to return to the Father — the one whose image you already bear, whose family you already belong to, who sent his Only Begotten so you could find your way back.

The question worth sitting with isn't whether the framework is correct (it is). It's whether you actually live out of it. Do you treat yourself as a person with divine parentage? Do you treat the covenant as something that genuinely changes your status — not just your behavior? Do you think of the Father as someone you are returning *to*, not just approaching for the first time?

Because if the first tier is real, you were known before this world. If the second is real, you have a body worth honoring. If the third is real, you have a relationship with Christ that is not just transactional — it is familial.

You are his.
