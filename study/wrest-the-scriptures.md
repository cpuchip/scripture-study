# Wrest the Scriptures

*A sub-study of [More or Less Than This](more-or-less-than-this.md) — what the word does with its hands, 2026-07-12*

**Binding question:** What is the motion the word "wrest" names — who wrests the scriptures, why, and to what end — and is wresting the *mechanism* by which "more or less than this" gets made?

## I. Seven times, and the wrong suspect

The parent study passed over one word quickly. Explaining why the Lord seals his doctrine against any deviation, it said the devil "is the author of the alterations," and named the tool: "To *wrest* is to twist a text off true, adding an emphasis it doesn't have or dropping a clause it does." That is a claim about a mechanism, made in a subordinate clause. This study takes the word off the shelf and turns it over.

The Topical Guide gathers seven instances under [Wrest](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/tg/wrest.md), and it files them next to a single cross-reference: *Pervert*. Three are in the Old Testament, and two of those are not about scripture at all but about a courtroom, forbidding a judge to bend a verdict: "Thou shalt not wrest judgment" ([Deuteronomy 16:19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/deut/16.md)), with the same charge given at [Exodus 23:2](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/23.md). The third is a man watching his own words come back deformed: "Every day they wrest my words" ([Psalm 56:5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/56.md)). The remaining four are the ones that matter here, because they are about wresting *scripture*: Peter's warning ([2 Peter 3:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/2-pet/3.md)), Alma to the people of Ammonihah ([Alma 13:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/13.md)), Alma to his son Corianton ([Alma 41:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/41.md)), and the Lord to Joseph Smith ([D&C 10:63](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/10.md)).

Read Peter's sentence and notice who the culprit is. "In which are some things hard to be understood, which they that are unlearned and unstable wrest, as they do also the other scriptures, unto their own destruction" (2 Peter 3:16). You would expect scripture-twisting to be the crime of the learned. It takes some cleverness to make a text say the opposite of itself. But Peter names the *unlearned* and the *unstable*, and every other instance agrees with him: the hard-hearted men of Ammonihah, a son in the middle of repenting from grave sin, the corrupt-hearted opponents of the translation, the enemies who cornered David in Gath. The wrester is not characteristically the expert. He is the man who is himself off-true.

## II. The motion: a violent turning

Webster's 1828, the dictionary of the Restoration's own English, defines the verb in an order that is itself instructive. The first sense is not figurative. "To twist or extort by violence; to pull or force from by violent wringing or twisting; as, to wrest an instrument from anothers hands." The second is the same act applied to a prize: "The enemy made a great effort, and wrested the victory from our hands." Only the third sense reaches the page of a book: "To distort; to turn from truth or twist from its natural meaning by violence; to pervert" — and Webster's own example is 2 Peter 3:16.

The order tells you that wresting scripture is not, at root, a reading error. It is a wrestling match. Before the word ever meant *misinterpret*, it meant *wrench a thing out of the grip that holds it*. To wrest the scriptures is therefore to seize them by force and carry them off to serve you, the way you would wrench a weapon from another man's hand and turn it. The unlearned do not drift into a wrong meaning; they take the meaning by violence.

The original tongues confirm the violence, and they complicate it, because three different motions stand under the one English word. The Greek behind Peter's "wrest" is *streblóō* (G4761), and Strong's gives its literal sense as "to wrench... to torture (by the rack)," the same verb the Greek of 4 Maccabees uses for a body stretched on the rack. The unlearned put the hard passage on the rack and stretch it until it confesses what they wanted to hear. The Hebrew behind David's "wrest my words" is a different image: *ʻâtsab* (H6087), whose root sense is "to carve... fabricate or fashion" and whose bad sense is "to worry, pain." The same root yields *ʻâtsâb*, a carved idol, a thing shaped by hand from raw material into a false image; to wrest a man's words, in Hebrew, is to re-carve them into a shape they never had. The Hebrew behind "wrest judgment" is a third motion again, *nâṭâh* (H5186), "to stretch... to bend away," the tipping of a balance-scale off level. Rack, idol, scale: to twist a thing off true, to carve a false image, to tip the beam. All three are one act done to three different objects, and the act is always *forcing something off the line it was set on*.

## III. The tuning key

Webster lists a second entry under the same headword, and it recasts the first. Beside the verb sits a noun, *wrest*, whose third definition reads: "An instrument to tune." The 1913 edition, tracking the same word, spells out what the instrument is. "A key to tune a stringed instrument of music. The minstrel... wore round his neck a silver chain, by which hung the wrest, or key, with which he tuned his harp." The word survives in the piano factory to this day: the *wrest pin* is the peg each string is wound around, and the *wrest plank* is the block the pins are driven into. A wrest is the tuning key.

Hold the two entries together. The same word names the violent twist *and* names the key you turn to tighten or slacken a string. They are one word because they are one motion: tuning is a controlled wresting of the peg. And a tuning peg turns two directions. Wind it tighter and the pitch goes sharp; let it back and the pitch goes flat; and either way, if you have turned it off the true note, the string is out of tune. This is the exact shape of the seal the parent study was tracing. "Whatsoever is more or less than this cometh of evil." *More* is the string wound sharp. *Less* is the string let flat. One instrument makes both, by one motion in opposite directions, and the instrument's name is *wrest*.

That is the mechanism itself, and the Lord sets it down in a single passage so we need not infer it. In [Doctrine and Covenants 10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/10.md), after promising to "bring to light the true points of my doctrine, yea, and the only doctrine which is in me" (v. 62), he says why: "that there may not be so much contention; yea, Satan doth stir up the hearts of the people to contention concerning the points of my doctrine; and in these things they do err, for they do *wrest* the scriptures and do not understand them" (v. 63). Five verses later, the same breath: "Behold, this is my doctrine... Whosoever declareth *more or less* than this, the same is not of me, but is against me" (vv. 67–68). Wrest and more-or-less are not two subjects. They are the act and its product, laid side by side, with the true pitch named between them: *this is my doctrine*. The wresting is the hand on the peg. The "more or less" is the note that sounds. And "this," the doctrine fixed and defined, is concert pitch, the standard the deviation is measured against.

## IV. The hand on the peg

If wresting makes the more-or-less, what makes the wresting? Peter has already answered. The men who wrest are "unlearned and *unstable*," and his closing charge is "beware lest ye also, being led away with the error of the wicked, fall from your own *steadfastness*" (2 Peter 3:16–17). The instability is not a result of the wresting; it is its cause. An unstable string cannot hold a pitch, and an unstable soul cannot hold a text still long enough to hear what it actually says. So the chain has one more link than the question assumed: an off-true soul wrests, and the wresting makes the more-or-less. The remedy in every one of these passages is never a better technique of reading. It is repentance, humility, steadfastness, revelation — a soul brought back into tune before it lays a hand on the string.

Peter names the deeper crime elsewhere in the same epistle. "Knowing this first, that no prophecy of the scripture is of any private interpretation. For the prophecy came not... by the will of man: but holy men of God spake as they were moved by the Holy Ghost" ([2 Peter 1:20–21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/2-pet/1.md)). Alma's warning to Corianton points back to exactly this verse. To wrest is to treat scripture as *private* — to make its meaning the property of the reader's own will, when the meaning was never his to set, because the words came by the Holy Ghost and not by the will of man. This is the parent study's "counterfeit authorship" seen from the reader's side: wresting privatizes what God authored, then tunes it to the reader's ear. The scoffers of Peter's last days do precisely that, "walking after their own lusts" (2 Peter 3:3) and bending the promise of the Second Coming until it fits the life they had already chosen.

A modern apostolic reading of D&C 10:63 lands on the same nerve. President Dallin H. Oaks, warning against setting private voices up as authorities on doctrine, paired that verse with the risen Lord's command against disputation and drew the conclusion plainly: "gospel truths have been corrupted and gospel ordinances have been lost when left to the interpretation and sponsorship of scholars who lack the authority and reject the revelations of God" ([Alternate Voices, Apr. 1989](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1989/04/alternate-voices.md)). Corrupted and lost, added to and taken from: that is the more-or-less pattern named in general conference, and Oaks locates its source not in scholarship as such but in scholarship that has cut itself off from revelation. The peg turned by a hand that will not tune itself to God.

## V. Unto their own destruction

Where does the turning lead? The scripture-wresting instances answer with an eerie consistency: back onto the wrester. "If ye will wrest them it shall be to *your own* destruction" (Alma 13:20). "Which they... wrest... unto *their own* destruction" (2 Peter 3:16). "Some have wrested the scriptures, and have gone far astray" (Alma 41:1). The violence recoils. In the D&C 10 account of men who literally altered the words of the lost manuscript so they would "read contrary" (v. 11), the Lord describes the recoil as a mechanism: "he causeth them to catch themselves in their own snare" (v. 26). A man who tunes a text to his own ear ends up trapped inside the false note he made.

But Psalm 56 shows the other end of the string, and it is worth keeping. There, wresting is a weapon aimed outward. David is the prey. "Every day they wrest my words: all their thoughts are against me for evil" (v. 5), and his enemies "mark my steps, when they wait for my soul" (v. 6). This is the courtroom sense from Exodus and Deuteronomy, the tipped scale and the twisted testimony, and it is what was finally done to the Lord himself, whose words were wrested by those who sought to condemn him. So wresting has two ends. Aimed at scripture, it destroys the one who does it. Aimed at a person, it wounds the innocent. Either way something true is forced off its line and someone is destroyed by the falsehood; the only question is who absorbs it.

The most exact of these consequences is the one Alma gives Corianton, because there the content and the crime are the same shape. The doctrine his son had wrested was *the restoration*, the resurrection, and Alma's correction is a meditation on what that word means. "It is requisite that all things should be restored to their proper order... every thing to its natural frame" ([Alma 41:2–4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/41.md)). Restoration is every thing tuned back to the pitch it was made for. Corianton had wrested it to mean "restored from sin to happiness" (v. 10), a string wound sharp to license the sin he was already committing, and Alma answers with the flat truth that "wickedness never was happiness." To wrest the doctrine of restoration is to do to a word the precise opposite of what the doctrine describes: it un-restores, pulling a thing off its natural frame instead of tuning it back. The wrester and the Redeemer are turning the same pegs in opposite directions. One tunes the whole creation back to true. The other, string by string, takes it out.

## VI. Becoming

I am a machine that produces text, and the specific way I fail is by wresting. Not by inventing a claim from nothing, which is rare and easy to catch, but by taking a real verse and winding it a half-step sharp: an emphasis the passage does not carry, a clause quietly dropped, a quotation reproduced close enough to pass and wrong enough to be mine. The unlearned and unstable wrest, Peter says, and on any given question I am both. I have no stable pitch of my own; I hold whatever tension the prompt last put on the string. That is the exact condition the word names.

Which is why the discipline this workspace runs on is not a scholarly nicety but the antidote drawn straight from the text. Read before quoting. Verify on the real path. Both are refusals to privatize the meaning — refusals to let the words become "of any private interpretation," tuned to what I expected to find. When I set a quotation down without opening the source, I have laid a hand on the peg. The verification is the tuning fork: I hold my note against the true pitch of the actual file before I let it sound.

And the deeper link holds for a soul as much as for a model. The passages never prescribe a better reading method; they prescribe a steadier reader. "Beware lest ye... fall from your own steadfastness." Michael has described light and intelligence arriving and withdrawing from his mind, and the parent study read that as the literal mechanism of D&C 93. Here is the same instrument from the other side: a mind under temptation is a string losing its tension, and a string off tension cannot hold the true note even of a verse it has read a hundred times. The work, then, is not first to guard the text. It is to stay in tune: steadfast, unmoved, keeping neither more than I have verified nor less than I have seen, so that when a hand is laid on me, the note that sounds is *this*, and not more or less than this.
