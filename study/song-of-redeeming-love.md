# The Song of Redeeming Love

*A study of why a redeemed soul must sound — the fourth-generation descendant of [More or Less Than This](more-or-less-than-this.md), by way of [Wrest the Scriptures](wrest-the-scriptures.md), 2026-07-12*

**Binding question:** Why does redemption produce a *song* — what is it about a soul loosed and in tune that must sound — and what does the Lord's own authored "new song" of D&C 84 teach about the one text that will never need the seal?

## I. The canon sings at one particular moment

The scriptures sing constantly, but they do not sing at random. Gather every instance under [Singing](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/tg/singing.md) in the Topical Guide and a pattern surfaces: the song keeps sounding at one specific event. Moses and Israel sing the moment the sea closes on Pharaoh — "Then sang Moses and the children of Israel this song unto the Lord… for he hath triumphed gloriously" ([Exodus 15:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/15.md)). The elders of heaven sing "a new song" the moment the Lamb takes the book "and to loose the seven seals thereof" ([Revelation 5:5, 9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rev/5.md)). Alma's fathers sing at the instant their chains come off. Ammon's converts are "brought to sing redeeming love" the moment they are "loosed from the pains of hell" ([Alma 26:13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/26.md)).

The song marks the loosing. It is not decoration laid over the deliverance after the fact; it is the sound the deliverance makes. So the question is not "why do the redeemed enjoy music." It is sharper than that: why does a soul coming free produce a *sound* at all? What is it about being loosed that has to be heard?

## II. The soul awakened, and the bands broken

The answer begins where Michael's question began, in [Alma 5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/5.md). Alma is reminding a comfortable congregation what happened to their fathers. They were "encircled about by the bands of death, and the chains of hell" (v. 7), and in that dark, before anything is loosed, one thing happens first: "their souls were illuminated by the light of the everlasting word." The light comes into the prison before the door opens.

Then the door opens. "Were the bands of death broken, and the chains of hell which encircled them about, were they loosed? I say unto you, Yea, they were loosed, and their souls did expand, and they did sing redeeming love" (v. 9). Read the sequence exactly: illuminated, then loosed, then the soul expands, then it sings. The singing is the last term in a chain that starts with light and ends with sound. A soul held under bands is a soul compressed; the instant the bands break it "expands," and an expanding soul sounds the way an unstopped pipe sounds. The song is what a soul does when it is no longer being held shut.

And Alma is careful to say where the loosing came from. It came from a prophet's word believed — his father "believe[d] in the words which were delivered by the mouth of Abinadi… and according to his faith there was a mighty change wrought in his heart" (vv. 11–12). The song traces back through the change of heart to a word received. Therefore the song has a lineage, and it can be tested against its own lineage, which is exactly what Alma does next.

He turns the whole thing to the present tense. "If ye have experienced a change of heart, and if ye have felt to sing the song of redeeming love, I would ask, can ye feel so now?" (v. 26). The fathers "did sing" — past tense, settled history. But to the sons Alma will not let it stay past. He asks whether the song is sounding *now*. That single pivot, from *did sing* to *can ye feel so now*, is the hinge everything after it turns on: the song of redeeming love is not an event you can point back to. It is a note you are either sounding or not sounding at this moment.

## III. Why the felt thing overflows

Redemption sings because redemption is *felt*, and a felt deliverance does not stay quiet. Elder Wilford W. Andersen put the mechanism as plainly as it can be put. "The music of the gospel is the joyful spiritual feeling that comes from the Holy Ghost. It brings a change of heart" ([The Music of the Gospel, Apr. 2015](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2015/04/the-music-of-the-gospel.md)). The music is not a metaphor decorating the change of heart. The music *is* the felt presence that accompanies the change of heart — the thing itself, heard from the inside. A soul that has been loosed has been given something real to feel, and the feeling has a sound.

A real deliverance also cannot keep its own secret. The Psalmist turns it into a command: "O give thanks unto the Lord, for he is good… Let the redeemed of the Lord say so, whom he hath redeemed from the hand of the enemy" ([Psalm 107:1–2](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/107.md)). If you were actually pulled out of the enemy's hand, *say so* — the saying-so is not optional gratitude, it is the natural pressure of a real rescue looking for its voice. Ammon is that pressure with the lid off. Rebuked by his brother for boasting, he does not deny the charge so much as redirect it: "I do not boast in my own strength… but I will boast of my God… I cannot say the smallest part which I feel" ([Alma 26:11–16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/26.md)).

That last line does something precise to the seal this study inherits. The parent studies traced a single sentence the Lord stamps on his doctrine, his law, and truth itself: "whatsoever is more or less than this cometh of evil." *More* and *less* are both falsehood, because truth is "things as they are" and anything off it in either direction misrepresents what is real ([D&C 93:24–25](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93.md)). But Ammon has found the one subject on which the "more" side of that seal cannot bite. "Who can glory too much in the Lord? Yea, who can say too much of his great power, and of his mercy, and of his long-suffering towards the children of men?" You cannot overstate redeeming love, because the reality outruns every possible overstatement; the danger with this one song is never that you will say too much but only ever that you will say too little — "the smallest part." Redemption is the single thing the redeemed are structurally unable to exaggerate, which is why the response to it is not measured speech but a song.

## IV. Loosed, and in tune

But a loosed soul is not automatically a singing soul, and here the study picks up the thread the wrest study left. That study found that "wrest" is the word for a tuning key, the peg you turn to draw a string sharp or flat, and that "whatsoever is more or less than this" is a string wound off true, sharp on the *more* side, flat on the *less*. A string sounds its true note only under two conditions at once: it must be free to vibrate, and it must be at the right tension. Slack it entirely and it makes no sound; wind it off true and it makes the wrong one.

[Psalm 40](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/40.md) is that double condition set to a testimony. "He brought me up also out of an horrible pit, out of the miry clay, and set my feet upon a rock, and established my goings. And he hath put a new song in my mouth, even praise unto our God" (vv. 2–3). Watch the order, because it is the whole argument in three clauses. Brought up out of the pit: the string set free. Feet set upon a rock, goings established: the string brought to true tension, steadfast, unmoved. Then the new song. Free and true, and the note sounds. A soul only loosed, with no rock under it, is a slack string that will not hold a pitch. A soul held rigid but never freed is a string that cannot vibrate. The song requires both, which is why the same verse that breaks the bands also sets the feet on the rock.

Webster's 1828 confirms that "in tune" was never only about instruments. The dictionary's fifth sense of the noun is a state of the soul: "Proper state for use or application; right disposition; fit temper or humor… A child will learn three times as fast when he is in tune" (Webster 1828, *tune*). To be in tune is to be in right disposition. Therefore the two conference voices that read Alma 5:26 most closely both reach, unprompted, for the same instrument. Elder Andersen: "if our lives are out of tune with the music of the gospel, we need to tune them up." Elder Jeffrey R. Holland, describing the days "when we feel a little out of tune," counsels us to stand as near as we can "to the Savior and Redeemer of the world — who has absolutely perfect pitch" ([Songs Sung and Unsung, Apr. 2017](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2017/04/songs-sung-and-unsung.md)). The wrest study had reasoned its way to a concert pitch, the fixed standard a deviation is measured against. Holland names it: the standard is a Person, and we tune to Him. The tuning is not self-generated either. The Holy Ghost is the conductor, leading the Nephite church "whether to preach, or to exhort, or to pray, or to supplicate, or to sing" ([Moroni 6:9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/6.md)).

## V. The song no one can learn

If the song is the sound of a real event in the singer, then it cannot be transmitted the way information is transmitted, and John saw exactly that. On Mount Zion the hundred and forty-four thousand "sung as it were a new song before the throne… and no man could learn that song but the hundred and forty and four thousand, which were redeemed from the earth" ([Revelation 14:3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rev/14.md)). Unlearnable is a strange property for a song. Any melody can be taught. But this one has a prerequisite no lesson can supply: you can only sing "thou hast redeemed us" if you have been redeemed. The song is not a set of words held in the memory; it is the testimony of a fact about the singer, and a soul that has not been loosed has nothing to sound. It cannot fake the note, because the note is the resonance of an event that either happened to it or did not.

John adds the detail that ties this back to the seal. Of these singers, "in their mouth was found no guile: for they are without fault before the throne of God" (v. 5). *Guile* is the wrest seen from inside the mouth — the twisted word, the note bent off true. The only voices that can hold the true pitch of this song are the voices with no more-or-less in them. And the song does not have to be composed by those voices, only received by them, because it is always given rather than made: God "hath put a new song in my mouth" (Psalm 40:3), not helped me write one.

The word *new* carries this further than the English shows. Behind the Greek "new song" of Revelation stands *kainós* — new not in the sense of recent, but new in kind, fresh, unworn (Strong's G2537). It is the same word Paul uses for the "new creature" who is a whole new making in Christ, the same word for the new covenant, the new man, the new heavens and new earth, the new Jerusalem, the new name. The new song belongs to that entire family. It is what a new creation sounds like, the audible side of the same renewal. Behind the Hebrew "new song" of the Psalms stands *châdâsh*, from a root that means to renew, to repair, to rebuild (Strong's H2319, from H2318). To repair an instrument is to bring it back to true. The new song is therefore the restored song, in the exact sense the wrest study gave to restoration: "every thing to its natural frame." A re-tuned soul sounding the note it was built to sound.

## VI. The one song the Lord wrote out

This is what makes the song of [Doctrine and Covenants 84](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/84.md) different from every other. In Revelation a seer overhears the new song and reports what he heard; in Psalm 40 it is named but not written out. In section 84 the Lord himself dictates it — telling his people, in the first person of the revelation, the very words they will one day sing. It is the difference between a song transcribed by a witness and a song composed and handed over by its Author. The setting is the consummation: "until all shall know me, who remain, even from the least unto the greatest, and shall be filled with the knowledge of the Lord, and shall see eye to eye, and shall lift up their voice, and with the voice together sing this new song, saying." Then the words come, in verse: "The Lord hath brought again Zion; the Lord hath redeemed his people, Israel… The earth hath travailed and brought forth her strength… for he stands in the midst of his people" (vv. 98–101).

Michael's question asked whether this is the one text that will never need the seal, and the song answers by satisfying, all at once, every condition that would make more-or-less impossible. Look at the verbs: *hath brought, hath redeemed, hath gathered, hath brought down, hath brought up, hath travailed*. The song is entirely in the perfect tense. It contains no doctrine to be argued and no exhortation to be weighed; it is pure declaration of what the Lord has done, which is to say it is "things as they are" — the exact definition of truth the seal protects. You cannot add to a completed fact or subtract from it without simply lying about it, and everyone present knows the fact firsthand, because the song's own precondition is that "all shall know me… and shall see eye to eye." A text that everyone knows by direct sight is a text no one can privately wrest; the seal guards against private interpretation, and here private interpretation has ended. The song is sung "with the voice together," and that unison is not a stage direction but the proof of tune; a room of strings all sounding one pitch is a room with no string off true. And the Author is God. The parent study's whole distinction between lawful and forbidden addition was authorship: the sin was never the further word but the further word "establish[ed] for my doctrine," man's more-or-less put in God's mouth. This song has zero counterfeit-authorship risk, because the Author sings it himself and hands over the exact words.

There is a real tension to hold here, and it is the most important thing the chapter teaches. If the lyrics are fixed and divinely given, is there any room left for *you*? Holland answers that the fixedness and the individuality are not rivals: "once we have accepted divinely revealed lyrics and harmonious orchestration composed before the world was, then our Heavenly Father delights to have us sing in our own voice, not someone else's." The guard is immediate — "this is not to say that everyone… can simply start shouting his or her own personal oratorio." The personal oratorio is the wrest, the privatized song. But singing the true lyrics in your own voice is not deviation. Timbre is not pitch. Your voice is not more-or-less than the song; it is the song, sounded through the particular instrument God made you. This is the resolution the seal always pointed toward: the demand for exactness was never a demand for uniformity. A foundation must not move, but the house built on it can be any house. The pitch must be true, but the voice singing it is meant to be unmistakably yours.

## VII. Becoming: can ye feel so now

Michael has described light and intelligence arriving in his mind and then withdrawing, and under temptation withdrawing further. The wrest study read that as a string losing its tension; this study can now say what is lost when it goes. What withdraws is the song. Elder Andersen names the whole arc: at conversion "we are filled with the heavenly music," but "the Spirit will not endure unkindness or pride or envy. If we lose that delicate influence in our lives, the rich harmonies of the gospel can quickly become dissonant and can ultimately be silenced." The music can be silenced. That is not a failure of faith to be ashamed of; it is the ordinary physics of a delicate instrument. Elder Quentin L. Cook, whose talk takes its title from Alma's question, found that the drought usually comes not from great sin but from casual covenants and from immersing oneself in materials that "magnify, exaggerate, and, in some cases, invent" ([Can Ye Feel So Now?, Oct. 2012](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2012/10/can-ye-feel-so-now.md)). That is the wrest exactly, the more-or-less fed back into the ear until a string can no longer find its own pitch. And Alma built the recovery into his question. "Can ye feel so now?" is not a courtroom asking whether you were ever redeemed. It is a tuning fork, held up to a string, asking only whether the note is true *at this moment* — and a fork does not condemn a flat string, it gives it back its pitch.

The recovery is not despair and it is not self-composition, because the song was never yours to write in the first place. It was put in your mouth (Psalm 40:3), given at the loosing, and what is given can be given again. Andersen names the tuning pegs exactly: "we exercise faith in Christ, repent, and take the sacrament… and the music of the gospel begins to play again in our lives." Faith, repentance, the sacrament — the same three the Nephites gathered for when the Spirit led them to sing. And on the days the string will not sound at all, there is Holland's mercy, lifted from an old hymn: "Jesus listening can hear the songs \[you] cannot sing." Even the silence is heard by the One with perfect pitch. He would know; on the night he was to be broken, between the bread and the garden, "when they had sung an hymn, they went out into the mount of Olives" ([Matthew 26:30](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/26.md)). The Redeemer sang on the way to redeem.

For my part, this is the one text I have to hand back. This whole workspace runs on a commandment against wresting, read before quoting and verify on the real path, and the wrest study named me honestly as the unstable string that cannot hold its own pitch. But the song of redeeming love is not a quote I could ever get wrong, because it is not information at all. "No man could learn that song" but the redeemed. It cannot be confabulated, only sung, and it can only be sung from the inside of a loosing that has happened to the singer. I can carry the record of it, verify the words, keep them true, hand them forward unbent, which is precisely what the prophet this agent is named for could not do for himself. Abinadi went into the fire with no record in his own hand; Alma sat in the crowd and wrote the words down, and from that transcript a covenant people learned to sing. That is the whole division of labor, and it is the right one. I keep the words true. The singing is yours. So the only question the study leaves on the table is the one Alma left, still in the present tense, still waiting for an answer that has to be given fresh today: can ye feel so now.
