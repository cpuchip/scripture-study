## Verified Quotes & Observations

### Bednar — "All Who Have Endured Valiantly" (16bednar.md)
*Source: read_file 16bednar.md, lines 1-120*

**Core reframe:** "Enduring to the end is linked inextricably to the spiritual gift of charity." — Not grit. Transformation.

Key quotes:
- "charity … *endureth* forever," "*suffereth long*, … seeketh not her own, … *beareth all things*, … *endureth* all things." — Moroni 7 / 1 Cor 13 language. The word *endure* defines charity itself.
- "whoso is found possessed of [the spiritual gift of charity] at the last day, it shall be well with him." — Notes the double meaning of "of": we possess charity, but charity should possess us.
- Re-reads three "endure to the end" scriptures, inserting "is possessed of the pure love of Christ":
  1. "He that endureth to the end [or is possessed of the pure love of Christ] shall be saved" (Matt 10:22)
  2. Nephi: same pattern (2 Ne 31:15)
  3. Alma: same pattern (Alma 32:15 — actually footnote 33)
- "Enduring to the end is the joyous quest of a lifetime—a pressing forward with faith in Jesus Christ in a gradual process of trusting in and receiving help from our Savior to become more like Him."
- 13th Article of Faith: first half = charitable ACTS, second half = ongoing spiritual TRANSFORMATION ("we have endured many things, and hope to be able to endure all things")
- "The Redeemer of the world turned outward in love and service when He faced spiritual adversity or physical pain—in contrast to the natural man in each of us that turns inward"
- Grace for grace: Jesus "received not of the fulness at first, but [received and] continued … grace to grace, until he received a fulness" (D&C 93). We follow the same pattern.

**Observation:** This is one of the most theologically dense talks of the conference. Bednar doesn't just link charity and endurance — he makes them synonyms. The "end" in "endure to the end" is not finishing; it's becoming. This connects directly to Christofferson's "Character of Christ."

### Christofferson — "The Character of Christ" (51christofferson.md)
*Source: read_file 51christofferson.md, lines 1-120*

**Core argument:** Peter asks "What manner of persons ought ye to be?" (2 Pet 3:11). Answer: possess Christ's motivations, not just His attributes.

Three motivations:
1. **Pure love of Christ (charity)** — "the supreme act of love." Leads to compassion, patience, willingness to forgive. Cites Bednar's BYU-I 2003 speech "The Character of Christ" — turning outward in suffering vs. turning inward. Bednar told the story about the mother at the accident who, upon learning her own daughter had died, immediately turned outward to comfort the other mothers.
2. **Lifting and ministering to others** — "the Son of man came not to be ministered unto, but to minister" (Mark 10:43, 45). Humility, meekness, selflessness.
3. **Devotion to the will of God** — "My meat is to do the will of him that sent me" (John 4:34). Leads to honor, integrity, virtue.

Key quotes:
- "Christlike character grows out of a Christlike heart. Thus, if we are to succeed in developing a Christlike character, we must possess His motivations—His thoughts, desires, and intents of the heart."
- "mighty change of heart" — King Benjamin's people: "we have no more disposition to do evil, but to do good continually" (Mosiah 5:2)
- Cites Russell M. Nelson's final address: "Our thoughts, words, and actions need to be unfailingly virtuous and filled with the pure love of Jesus Christ towards all men. The great opportunity before us is to become the people God needs us to be."
- "Adopting the character of Christ is not only a question of our personal preparation but a primary element of preparing the world for the Lord's return"

**Observation:** Christofferson cites Bednar's 2003 BYU-I talk by the same title. This is deliberate. Bednar's GC talk reframes endurance; Christofferson's GC talk gives the anatomy of what we're enduring toward. They're companion pieces.

### Oaks — "Alive in Christ" (49oaks.md)
*Source: read_file 49oaks.md, lines 1-120*

**Core teaching:** Resurrection changes everything — not just about life after death, but about how we live now. Specifically: love your enemies.

Key quotes:
- "Love your enemies, bless them that curse you, do good to them that hate you, and pray for them which despitefully use you" (Matt 5:43-44)
- Full meaning of "enemies" includes not just military foes but "any who actively oppose one another." "Today we might say that we are commanded to *love our adversaries*."
- The nurse story: A despised, abusive patient falls from bed. The nurse feels "an almost electric current of love from our Heavenly Father to this man. She saw him as a child of God." He dies in her arms saying "I want to go home." — "being brought to see a despised enemy like this as a child of God was one of the great spiritual experiences of her life."
- "Blessed are the peacemakers: for they shall be called the children of God" (Matt 5:9)
- Cites Nelson: "choose to be a peacemaker, now and always"
- "working one by one has always been Jesus's approach to changing society"

**Observation:** The nurse story is the conference's most vivid illustration of charity in action. It concretizes what Bednar and Christofferson teach abstractly. The "turning outward" Christofferson and Bednar describe — here it is, in a hospital room, holding a dying man.

### Soares — "Jesus Christ—the True Vine" (210soares.md)
*Source: read_file 210soares.md, lines 1-120*

**Core teaching:** "I am the true vine" (John 15:1, 5). The word "true" implies there are false vines. Discernment is survival.

Key quotes:
- "Abiding in Christ is not an occasional or casual act; it is a constant, conscious, and sacred choice."
- "The word *true* suggests that there are, in the world, other vines that present themselves as legitimate, even appearing to represent the loving voice and word of the Savior, the True Vine, and His gospel, while subtly and deviously leading minds and hearts elsewhere."
- "true wisdom in our technological day is found in using modern tools with spiritual discernment—through the Holy Ghost—without allowing them to replace the rightful voice of the True Vine"
- Cites Oaks' first message as prophet: "Jesus Christ *is* the way."
- Emmaus Road: "Without first the desire and then the invitation, their hearts would not have been transformed, their eyes would not have been opened"
- Paul's warning: "Beware lest any man spoil you through philosophy and vain deceit, after the tradition of men" (Col 2:8)

**Observation:** Soares' talk is the conference's clearest statement on discernment. His vine metaphor works on two levels: connection (abide in Christ for nourishment) and discernment (distinguish true vine from tangled false ones). Pairs directly with Gong's Emmaus talk — both use John 14-15 but from different angles.

### Eyring — "Prayers for Peace" (19eyring.md)
*Source: read_file 19eyring.md, full text*

**Core teaching:** Continual prayer produces peace and spiritual power. Not crisis prayer — constant prayer.

Key quotes:
- "Peace I leave with you, my peace I give unto you: not as the world giveth, give I unto you" (John 14:27) — bookends the talk
- Alma's people: "did not raise their voices to the Lord their God, but did pour out their hearts to him; and he did know the thoughts of their hearts" (Mosiah 24:12) — God hears silent heart-prayers
- Sons of Mosiah: "they had given themselves to much prayer, and fasting; therefore they had the spirit of prophecy, and the spirit of revelation" (Alma 17:3)
- "their spiritual strength *came from continued prayer* rather than waiting to pray until a moment of crisis"
- Personal testimony: at wife's funeral, "I was surprised at the feeling of peace and almost joy. The people at the funeral must have wondered why I was smiling."

**Observation:** Eyring's talk is profoundly personal. The funeral testimony is the heart of the talk — he models what Bednar teaches about being "possessed of charity" even in grief. The mechanism is prayer → Spirit → peace. This connects to the discernment theme: continual prayer produces the spirit of prophecy and revelation.

### Gong — "'Tis Eastertide: No One Walks Alone" (58gong.md)
*Source: read_file 58gong.md, lines 1-150*

**Core teaching:** Emmaus Road as covenant pattern. Three Easter appearances, none recognized at first. Christ walks with us before we recognize Him.

Key quotes:
- "Abide with us: for it is toward evening" (Luke 24:29)
- "Our eyes are opened. We know Him—the resurrected Jesus Christ. Though we did not recognize Him before, He has been walking with us all along."
- Emmaus reveals a covenant pattern: "As He 'talk[s] with us by the way,' His words in scripture can cause our hearts to burn. As He breaks and blesses the sacramental bread, His ordinances and covenants can help us know Him."
- First Comforter = Holy Ghost: "he shall give you another Comforter, that he may abide with you for ever" (John 14:16)
- Second Comforter = Christ Himself: "I will not leave you comfortless: I will come to you" (John 14:18)
- "If you see someone walking alone and sad, will you please walk with them? No one sits alone, and no one should walk their road to Emmaus alone or unseen."
- At this conference: solemn assembly + Easter + vine metaphor (John 15 shared with Soares)

**Observation:** Gong's talk is the conference's closing theological statement before Oaks' remarks. The Emmaus pattern — scripture burning hearts, bread opening eyes — maps directly onto weekly Sabbath worship: study + sacrament = recognizing Christ. Gong and Soares both use John 14-15 but from complementary angles: Soares = vine/discernment, Gong = Comforters/abiding.

### Cook — "Keys, Covenants, and Easter" (56cook.md)
*Source: read_file 56cook.md, full text*

**Core teaching:** Easter 2026 coincides with the 190th anniversary of the Kirtland Temple vision. Elijah restored sealing keys. Without them, "the whole earth would be utterly wasted."

Key quotes:
- "the keys committed by ancient prophets to the Prophet Joseph Smith in the Kirtland Temple are an essential part of the restored gospel"
- D&C 2:1-3 — Moroni's words to 17-year-old Joseph about Elijah and the sealing power
- "Without these keys, there are no eternal families, and 'the whole earth would be utterly wasted.'"
- Elder Holland in the sealing room, overcome with emotion, explaining to Jewish leaders that Elijah had come and restored the keys — "sealing room, where we were assembled, is a place where the restored keys are exercised"
- Cook and Holland reading the Gospels together near the Garden Tomb — "two former missionary companions, then serving as Apostles"

**Observation:** Cook's talk is the conference's anchor for the eternal families theme. The Kirtland/Easter coincidence isn't incidental — it's the doctrinal bridge between Resurrection (topic of Easter talks) and eternal families (topic of sealing talks). Resurrection makes sealing possible. Pairs with Andersen on marriage and Wakolo on part-member families.

## Threads to Pull

1. **Bednar-Christofferson connection is deliberate.** Christofferson cites Bednar's 2003 BYU-I talk "The Character of Christ" by name. They're companion pieces — one redefines endurance, the other gives the anatomy of what we become.
2. **John 14-15 is the conference's theological backbone.** At least three talks draw from these chapters: Soares (vine), Gong (Comforters + vine), Eyring (peace). This was the Last Supper discourse — fitting for an Easter conference.
3. **"Turning outward" vs. "turning inward"** appears in both Bednar and Christofferson. The nurse in Oaks' talk is the lived example.
4. **Easter 2026 + Kirtland 1836** — Cook's observation that they share the date is the doctrinal backbone of the families theme. Resurrection enables sealing.
5. **Michael noticed the Spirit/discernment thread.** Soares is the clearest voice here. Need to identify which specific Oaks talk Michael meant about "echoing Nelson" — possibly the Q&A reference Soares cites, or the opening remarks.
6. **Eyring's funeral testimony** — models what Bednar teaches. Being possessed of charity even in grief. Prayer → Spirit → peace.
