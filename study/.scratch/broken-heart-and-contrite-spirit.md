### Psalm 51:17
> "The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit: a broken and a contrite heart, O God, thou wilt not despise."
> — [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/51.md](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/51.md)
> CONTEXT: David after Bathsheba. v16: "thou desirest not sacrifice; else would I give it: thou delightest not in burnt offering." Then v17: the new sacrifice. The psalm reverses the order from 3 Nephi 9 — David says "broken spirit" then "broken and contrite heart"; Christ says "broken heart and a contrite spirit." The doublet exists in both orders across the canon.

### Psalm 34:18
> "The Lord is nigh unto them that are of a broken heart; and saveth such as be of a contrite spirit."
> — [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/34.md](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/34.md)
> NOTE: This is the only place besides Psalm 51 where both "broken heart" and "contrite spirit" appear in the same verse in the OT. But here they are separate clauses — the Lord is nigh to the brokenhearted; He saves the contrite. In Psalm 51:17 and 3 Nephi 9:20 they are paired as a single offering.

### 2 Nephi 2:7
> "Behold, he offereth himself a sacrifice for sin, to answer the ends of the law, unto all those who have a broken heart and a contrite spirit; and unto none else can the ends of the law be answered."
> — [gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)
> NOTE: "Unto none else" — this is absolute. The Atonement's answering power is conditioned on this posture. Not on wealth, lineage, or ritual perfection. On brokenness.

## Webster 1828 Definitions

### broken
> "5. Subdued; humbled; contrite. The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit. Ps. li. 17."
> "7. Crushed and ruined as by something that destroys hope; blighted."
> CIRCULARITY: Webster defines broken as "contrite" and contrite as "broken down." The words are etymologically braided — they define each other.

### contrite
> "2. Broken down with grief and penitence; deeply sorrowful for sin because it is displeasing to God; humbly and thoroughly penitent."
> NOTE: The grief is not generic sadness. It is sorrow "because it is displeasing to God." The orientation is Godward, not self-focused.

### humble
> "2. Thinking lowly of one's self; claiming little for one's self; not proud, arrogant, or assuming; thinking one's self ill-deserving or unworthy, when judged by the demands of God; lowly; weak; modest."
> NOTE: "When judged by the demands of God" — humility is not social modesty. It is the recognition that God's standard reveals our unworthiness. This is the soil from which brokenness and contrition grow.

## Observations & Quotes

1. **The doublet appears in four canonical locations as a paired offering:** Psalm 51:17 (OT), 3 Nephi 9:20 (BoM), D&C 59:8 (D&C), Moroni 6:2 (BoM). Separately: Psalm 34:18 (broken heart + contrite spirit in parallel clauses), Isaiah 57:15 (contrite and humble), Isaiah 66:2 (poor and contrite), 2 Nephi 2:7 (broken heart and contrite spirit as condition for Atonement), D&C 20:37 (prerequisite for baptism).

2. **What it replaces:** In every case where the doublet appears, it replaces or supersedes burnt offerings / blood sacrifice. 3 Nephi 9:19: "your sacrifices and your burnt offerings shall be done away." Psalm 51:16: "thou desirest not sacrifice... thou delightest not in burnt offering." The new sacrifice is not easier — it is deeper. It costs the self, not a sheep.

3. **"Faith unto repentance" and "fruit unto repentance" are Amulek's unique phrases** (Alma 34 only). The preposition "unto" matters — faith is not separate from repentance; it is directional, aimed at repentance. Fruit is not separate from repentance; it is the produce of repentance. The grammar suggests that repentance is the destination, and both faith and fruit are movements toward it.

4. **The Alma 34 / Enos parallel:** Both move from internal reality (faith/words sinking into heart) → vocal petition (cry) → mercy received (remission/encircled in arms of safety) → outward concern (charity/welfare of others). Broken heart and contrite spirit are the bridge between the internal and the external.

5. **Mercy mechanism:** 2 Nephi 2:7 says the Atonement answers the ends of the law "unto all those who have a broken heart and a contrite spirit; and unto none else." This is not God being stingy. It is God recognizing that only a broken heart has room for what He offers. A whole heart is already full — of itself. A broken heart has space for mercy to enter.

6. **Elder Andersen's distinction:** Contrite spirit = putting yourself in God's hands (volitional). Broken heart = godly sorrow (affective). Together they cover the will and the emotions — the whole soul.

7. **3 Nephi 12:3 adds "who come unto me"** to Matthew's "poor in spirit." The blessing is not for poverty of spirit in isolation. It is for poverty of spirit that moves toward Christ.

### Critical Analysis

**1. Active or passive?**
The command is "offer... a broken heart" (active verb, direct object). But the definitions describe a state of being crushed, subdued, broken down (passive). This is a real tension in the text. We do not manufacture our own brokenness — that would be performance. But we do offer it once it is given. The offering is active; the breaking is providential.

**2. Elder Andersen's distinction vs. scriptural fluidity**
Andersen: broken heart = godly sorrow (affective); contrite spirit = putting yourself in God's hands (volitional). But Psalm 51:17 says "broken spirit" and "broken and contrite heart" — the terms are reversed. Scripture uses the words interchangeably; modern teaching distinguishes them. Both are true at different resolutions. The study should present Andersen's distinction as illuminating but not rigid.

**3. "Unto none else" (2 Nephi 2:7)**
This is the strongest claim in the study. "Unto all those who have a broken heart and a contrite spirit; and unto none else can the ends of the law be answered." This does not mean God withholds mercy from the proud. It means the proud cannot receive what is offered. Mercy is always extended (3 Nephi 9:14: "mine arm of mercy is extended towards you"). But only the broken can feel its touch. The channel is open; the posture determines whether the water enters.

**4. The Enos parallel is inferential**
Enos never says "broken heart." The parallel to Alma 34 is structural, not lexical. The study should present it as a parallel arc, not as explicit textual echo.

**5. Faith "unto" repentance**
The preposition matters. "Faith unto repentance" (Alma 34:15-16) and "fruit unto repentance" (v30) both use "unto" as directional. Faith aims at repentance; fruit demonstrates repentance. But the relationship is not fully explained — is faith the means, or is repentance the outgrowth? The text leaves the grammar suggestive rather than definitive. The study should not over-resolve what the text leaves open.

**6. Countervoice: godly sorrow vs. worldly sorrow**
2 Corinthians 7:10 (not yet read): "godly sorrow worketh repentance to salvation... but the sorrow of the world worketh death." Brokenness can become despair if not oriented toward God. The study should distinguish broken-heartedness from mere emotional devastation. The difference is the direction of the break — toward God or away from Him.

**7. Psalm 34:18's two verbs**
"The Lord is nigh unto them that are of a broken heart; and saveth such as be of a contrite spirit." Proximity (nigh) for the broken; salvation (saveth) for the contrite. The broken heart attracts God's nearness; the contrite spirit receives His saving action. This is a nuance the study should surface.

**8. Ring check: Does this answer the binding question?**
The binding question: What do "broken heart" and "contrite spirit" actually mean — and how does this posture bring about mercy from the Savior?
The sources show: (1) the words mean shattered, pulverized, crushed — physically grounded, not abstract; (2) this posture brings mercy because it is the only posture that can receive what is offered. A whole heart has no room. A broken heart has space for mercy to enter, dwell, and heal. The answer is structural, not transactional.
