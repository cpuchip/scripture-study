## I. Binding question

*What does the Sabbath do, that work alone cannot?*

The question is sharper than the standard "what is the Sabbath about?" framing because it forces the study to identify a *function*, not just describe the practice. The function turned out to be threefold: structural cessation, "saw that they were good" pronouncement, and covenantal sign. All three answer the binding question. The study is organized to develop each in turn.

## III. Tensions surfaced and addressed in the study

Per the project's read-before-quoting and surface-tensions covenant clauses, the study acknowledges three tensions rather than suppressing them. Each is named in § VIII.

1. **The Mosaic death penalty (Exodus 31:14-15).** Severity of the consequence is not the modern enforcement, but the seriousness of the day remains. The study treats this honestly without trying to soften the original text. The pastoral point is that the original severity is a witness to the day's structural status, not a model for modern practice.

2. **The shape-change across dispensations.** Jarom 1:5 (Mosiac shape) vs. 4 Nephi 1:12 (post-Christ shape — "continuing in fasting and prayer, and in meeting together oft") vs. D&C 59 (Restoration shape, sacrament-centered). The study argues the *structural function* (cessation enabling covenant renewal) holds across dispensations even as the form moves from agricultural rest to gathering-and-sacrament.

3. **The rest that *remains* (Hebrews 4:9).** The eschatological dimension of "rest" exceeds the weekly Sabbath. The weekly is a foretaste. The study acknowledges this layering rather than collapsing the weekly Sabbath into the destination — protecting against a triumphalist read of "I keep the Sabbath" as arrival rather than rehearsal.
