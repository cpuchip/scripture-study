### gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/113.md — D&C 113 (FULL SECTION)
**Joseph Smith answering questions about Isaiah 11, March 1838**

> "Who is the Stem of Jesse spoken of in the 1st, 2d, 3d, 4th, and 5th verses of the 11th chapter of Isaiah? Verily thus saith the Lord: It is Christ." (vv. 1-2)

> "What is the rod spoken of in the first verse of the 11th chapter of Isaiah, that should come of the Stem of Jesse? Behold, thus saith the Lord: It is a servant in the hands of Christ, who is partly a descendant of Jesse as well as of Ephraim, or of the house of Joseph, on whom there is laid much power." (vv. 3-4)

> "What is the root of Jesse spoken of in the 10th verse of the 11th chapter? Behold, thus saith the Lord, it is a descendant of Jesse, as well as of Joseph, unto whom rightly belongs the priesthood, and the keys of the kingdom, for an ensign, and for the gathering of my people in the last days." (vv. 5-6)

**Observation:** THREE DISTINCT FIGURES identified by revelation:
1. **Stem of Jesse** = Christ (vv. 1-2)
2. **Rod from the Stem** = "a servant in the hands of Christ" — descendant of Jesse AND Ephraim/Joseph, "much power" laid on him (vv. 3-4)
3. **Root of Jesse** = a descendant of Jesse AND Joseph, holds priesthood + keys, for an ENSIGN and GATHERING in the LAST DAYS (vv. 5-6)

The rod and root are NOT identified as Christ. They are servants who descend from both Jesse (Davidic/Judah lineage) and Joseph/Ephraim. The root holds keys "for the gathering of my people in the last days" — this is explicitly eschatological.

**Key question:** Are the rod and the root the SAME person, or two different people? The descriptions overlap (both are descendants of Jesse and Joseph) but the rod is described functionally ("servant in the hands of Christ, much power") while the root is described by authority ("priesthood, keys, ensign, gathering").

Also D&C 113:7-10: Zion putting on "her strength" = putting on "the authority of the priesthood, which she, Zion, has a right to by lineage." The gathering/priesthood/Zion theme continues.

**Connects to:** Outline §II directly. Also §IV (gathering context).

**Chapter heading: "Isaiah speaks about the Messiah"**

> "Behold my servant, whom I uphold; mine elect, in whom my soul delighteth; I have put my spirit upon him: he shall bring forth judgment to the Gentiles." (v. 1)

Footnote 1a → Matt 12:17-21, TG Servant, TG Witness of the Father. Matthew explicitly applies this to Jesus.

But then verse 19 — a DIFFERENT servant?
> "Who is blind, but my servant? or deaf, as my messenger that I sent? who is blind as he that is perfect, and blind as the Lord's servant?" (v. 19)

**Observation:** Isaiah 42 has TWO servant references that seem to be different figures:

This dual-servant pattern in Isaiah is significant. The same chapter uses "servant" for both Christ and for a flawed human agent. Context determines which.

### gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15.md — Mosiah 15 (FULL CHAPTER)
**Abinadi's interpretation of the Isaiah servant — About 148 B.C.**

Abinadi quotes and interprets Isaiah 53 (which he quoted in Mosiah 14 = Isaiah 53 verbatim):

> "And after all this, after working many mighty miracles among the children of men, he shall be led, yea, even as Isaiah said, as a sheep before the shearer is dumb, so he opened not his mouth." (v. 6)

> "And now I say unto you, who shall declare his generation? Behold, I say unto you, that when his soul has been made an offering for sin he shall see his seed. And now what say ye? And who shall be his seed?" (v. 10)

Abinadi answers: his "seed" are those who heard the prophets and believed the Lord would redeem his people (v. 11). **The prophets themselves are his seed** (v. 13).

Then Abinadi applies Isaiah 52:7 ("how beautiful upon the mountains"):
> "And these are they who have published peace, who have brought good tidings of good, who have published salvation; and said unto Zion: Thy God reigneth!" (v. 14)
> "And O how beautiful upon the mountains were their feet!" (v. 15)
> "And again, how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of those that are still publishing peace!" (v. 16)
> "And again, how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of those who shall hereafter publish peace, yea, from this time henceforth and forever!" (v. 17)
> "And behold, I say unto you, this is not all. For O how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that is the founder of peace, yea, even the Lord, who has redeemed his people" (v. 18)

**OBSERVATION — ABINADI'S LAYERED APPLICATION:**
Abinadi applies "beautiful upon the mountains" to THREE groups:
1. Past prophets who published peace (v. 14-15)
2. Present prophets still publishing peace (v. 16)
3. Future prophets who shall hereafter publish peace (v. 17)
4. AND "the founder of peace, yea, even the Lord" (v. 18)

This is a **multi-referent reading** — the Isaiah passage applies to prophets past/present/future AND to Christ himself. Abinadi doesn't force a single-figure interpretation. The "beautiful feet" belong to everyone who publishes the good news.

This supports the emerging pattern: Isaiah's servant language applies to MULTIPLE figures in different contexts — Christ primarily, but also prophets and servants who carry his message.

**Connects to:** Outline §I (Isaiah 52-53 interpretation), §VII (Book of Mormon prophets as interpreters of Isaiah).

## Webster 1828

### SERVANT (n.)
"One who serves, or does services, voluntarily or on compulsion; a person who is employed by another for menial offices, or for other labor, and is subject to his command; a person who labors or exerts himself for the benefit of another, his master or employer; a subordinate helper."

**Key insight:** The 1828 definition emphasizes subordination ("subject to his command") and labor for another's benefit. This fits perfectly how D&C 113 describes the rod — "a servant in the hands of Christ." The servant is not autonomous; he acts under the Master's direction.

### ROD (n.)
1. "A straight and slender stick; a wand"
2. "An instrument of punishment or correction"
3. **"A kind of scepter, or badge of office; hence, figuratively, power; authority"**

**Key insight:** Definition 3 is illuminating for Isaiah 11:1 — "a rod out of the stem of Jesse." A rod as a scepter/badge of authority resonates with D&C 113:3-4 — the rod is "a servant in the hands of Christ...on whom there is laid much power." The rod carries authority.

### STEM (n.)
1. "The principal body of a tree, shrub, or plant; the main stock"
3. **"The stock of a family; a race or generation of progenitors."**
4. "A branch of a family."

**Key insight:** "Stem of Jesse" = the family stock of Jesse, the progenitor line. D&C 113 identifies this as Christ — the main stock of the Davidic line.

### ROOT (n.)
[1828 only has the verb form "to turn up the earth with the snout" — the noun "root" as origin/source was understood but the 1828 dictionary entry is incomplete.]

Modern sense: "The primary source; origin." In botanical terms, the root supports and nourishes the plant from below. The "root of Jesse" (Isa 11:10) is distinct from the stem — it's the foundation, the anchor, the source of supply. D&C 113:5-6 identifies the root as a last-days figure with keys and priesthood.

### BRANCH (n.)
1. "A shoot or secondary stem growing from the main stem"
5. **"A line of family descent, in distinction from some other line or lines from the same stock; any descendant in such a line."**

**Key insight:** "A Branch shall grow out of his roots" (Isa 11:1) — a descendant growing from the root system. 2 Ne. 3:5 uses "branch" similarly: "a righteous branch...not the Messiah, but a branch which was to be broken off." The Branch is a descendant, an offshoot — explicitly NOT the main stock (stem/Christ).

## Critical Analysis Notes (Phase 3a)

### Confirmed Strong
- **D&C 113 identifies THREE distinct figures in Isaiah 11**, only one of which is Christ. This is revealed text, not inference. The rod and root are described with specific attributes (lineage, power, keys). Confidence: HIGH.
- **3 Ne 21:10-11 speaks of a servant who will be marred.** The Church's footnote apparatus directly links this to D&C 135 (Joseph Smith's martyrdom) and 2 Ne 3:11 (the choice seer). Confidence: HIGH — this isn't a factional reading; it's the official footnotes.
- **2 Ne 3 identifies the choice seer as Joseph, son of Joseph** (v. 15). TG explicitly links to Joseph Smith. Chapter heading names him. Confidence: HIGH.
- **D&C 103:21 explicitly names Joseph Smith** as the Moses-like servant. Confidence: HIGHEST — this is by name.
- **Isaiah uses "servant" for multiple referents.** Isaiah 42 has both an ideal servant (vv. 1-7, Christ) and a blind servant (v. 19). Isaiah 49 names the servant "Israel" (v. 3) but then distinguishes him from Israel (v. 5-6). Mosiah 15 applies "beautiful feet" to prophets past/present/future AND Christ. The text itself supports a multi-referent reading. Confidence: HIGH.
- **D&C 86:8-11 applies "light to the Gentiles" to priesthood holders collectively.** This is the collective/corporate reading — the servant role distributed to a lineage. Confidence: HIGH.

### Needs Qualification
- **Are the rod and root of Jesse the SAME person?** D&C 113 describes them with overlapping traits (both descend from Jesse and Joseph), but the descriptions emphasize different things (rod = power/servant, root = keys/ensign/gathering). I assumed they might be the same but the text doesn't say. **Qualify: the text is compatible with either reading.**
- **3 Ne 21:10-11 → Is this the SAME servant as Isaiah 52:13 (quoted in 3 Ne 20:43)?** Jesus quotes Isaiah 52:13-15 (Messianic servant) and then in ch. 21 speaks of "my servant" who will be marred. The textual flow suggests a shift from quoting Isaiah's Messianic figure to speaking of a Restoration-era servant, but the transition is seamless. **Qualify: the text may or may not intend a transition between two different servants.**
- **"My servant David" in Ezekiel/Jeremiah — the chapter headings add "(the Messiah)" parenthetically.** These parentheticals are editorial, not scriptural. The text itself says "servant David" without specifying whether this is a title for Christ, a literal Davidic king, or a typological figure. **Qualify: the Messianic reading is the mainstream Church interpretation (per headings and TG), but the text itself is more open.**
- **D&C 85:7 ("one mighty and strong") — does this describe a FUTURE figure?** The historical context was about consecration in Jackson County. The First Presidency has addressed claims of people who styled themselves as this figure. **Qualify: present the historical context prominently. Note the First Presidency statement (which I haven't read but know exists). Frame carefully.**

### Tensions to Name
1. **Individual vs. Collective.** D&C 113/103 point to specific individuals. D&C 86 distributes the servant role to a priesthood lineage. Both are in the canon. The answer may be "both/and" rather than "either/or."
2. **Joseph Smith as THE servant vs. A servant.** 2 Ne 3 and D&C 103 identify Joseph Smith directly. But did the servant role end with his death, or does it continue? D&C 113's rod and root descriptions don't name Joseph Smith — they describe attributes. Could they apply to a future figure?
3. **Messianic vs. Human "David."** Ezekiel/Jeremiah's "servant David" — chapter headings say Christ, but the text places "David" alongside God ("I the Lord will be their God, and my servant David a prince"). If David IS Christ, this creates redundancy. If David is a separate figure, the text reads more naturally but conflicts with mainstream interpretation.
4. **Isaiah's dual servants.** Isaiah 42 has a perfect servant (Christ) AND a blind servant (Israel/human). Isaiah 53 is clearly Messianic (Abinadi confirms). But the servant songs create a composite figure where the ideal and the flawed exist in tension. Where does the mortal servant end and the divine Servant begin?
5. **The "marring" question.** 3 Ne 21:10 says the servant will be "marred" but healed. The footnote links to D&C 135 (Joseph's death). But Joseph was killed, not healed. Does "healed" mean resurrection? Vindication? The ongoing success of his work? This needs honest exploration.

### Lord Bunvader's Challenge — Assessment
The prediction was that AI would default to "Christ or Joseph Smith." What does the evidence actually show?
- **Christ IS identified** as the Stem of Jesse (D&C 113:1-2) and as the primary referent of Isaiah 53 (Abinadi in Mosiah 14-15). This is correct and not controversial.
- **Joseph Smith IS explicitly identified** as a servant by D&C 103:21, cross-referenced from 2 Ne 3 and 3 Ne 21:10-11. This is not a "default" — it's what the footnotes and Topical Guide say.
- **BUT the picture is MORE complex** than just "Christ and Joseph Smith": D&C 113 identifies rod and root figures who are NOT named; D&C 86 distributes the servant role collectively; Ezekiel's "David" is ambiguous; Isaiah uses "servant" for Israel as a nation.
- **The honest answer is: the scriptures identify MULTIPLE servants** — Christ as the primary/ideal Servant, Joseph Smith as a specifically named Restoration servant, and a broader priesthood lineage that carries the "light to Gentiles" mission. Whether there's an additional future "Davidic servant" figure is a matter of interpretation, not settled doctrine.

Lord Bunvader's challenge was partly right (the AI does find Christ and Joseph Smith) but framed too simply. The text itself is more layered than any single-identification theory.
