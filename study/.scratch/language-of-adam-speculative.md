### Key: The "mama/papa" phenomenon
Wikipedia (Mama and papa): In linguistics, "mama" and "papa" are considered a special case of false cognates. In many languages, /mama/ and /papa/ mean "mother" and "father." Linguists attribute this to baby babble — bilabial consonants like /m/, /p/, /b/ and the open vowel /a/ are the easiest sounds to produce. Parents hear babies' first sounds and assign meaning.

**But — is this the whole story?** The mainstream explanation is "coincidence via babble." A faith-informed reading could ask: what if babies produce these sounds first because they're the most fundamental? What if the Adamic language was grounded in the simplest, most natural human sounds — /a/, /m/, /p/ — and that's WHY babies reach for them first? Not coincidence, but residue.

## Speculative Threads

### Thread 1: Ahman ↔ Amon/Amen/Aman
- **Adamic:** Ahman = God, "the being which made all things"
- **Egyptian:** Amon/Amun = the hidden god, king of gods; later identified with the sun (Amon-Ra)
- **Hebrew:** Amen (אָמֵן) = from aman (to strengthen, confirm); our closing prayer word
- **Arabic:** Aman = safety, security, trust
- Hugh Nibley connected Ahman to Egyptian Amon
- Ask Gramps article: "Ahman could be construed to be some transliteration of 'I Am'" (Hebrew YHWH/Ehyeh)

**Speculative reading:** If Ahman was the original name for God, fragments appear in Egyptian (Amon), Hebrew (Amen/Aman), and Arabic (Aman) — all Afroasiatic languages. The "ah-man" root might be one of those global fragments hiding across language families.

### Thread 2: "Man" as an Adamic Root
- Joseph Smith's Pure Language sample: Ah-MAN (God), Son Ah-MAN (Christ), Sons Ah-MAN (humans), Anglo-MAN (angels)
- English: man, human
- Sanskrit: manu (the first man, ancestor of humanity)
- German: Mann
- Proto-Indo-European: *man- (man, human)

**Speculative reading:** "Man" appears to be an Adamic root meaning "being" or "intelligent being." God is Ah-MAN (the supreme being). We are Sons of Ah-MAN. IN the language of Adam, "Man of Holiness is his name" — this isn't using "man" to mean "male person," it's using it as a category of being. The fact that "man/manu/Mann" is cross-culturally persistent could be because it's one of the fragments that survived the confounding.

### Thread 3: AQ'WA (water) — a global root
- Proto-World: *aq'wa / *haq'wa = water
- Latin: aqua
- Japanese: aka (red, but also related to flowing/liquid in compounds)
- The root appears in virtually every language family on earth

Not directly tied to a revealed Adamic word, but notable as one of the strongest candidates for a "pre-Babel" word. If the confounding was a mingling (Webster: "to blend so that elements cannot be distinguished"), you'd expect the most fundamental words — water, mother, one, two — to resist the mixing the longest.

### Thread 4: Kokob/Kokaubeam ↔ Semitic star words
- **Adamic:** Kokob = star; Kokaubeam = stars/great lights (Abraham 3:13)
- **Hebrew:** Kochav (כוכב) = star
- **Arabic:** Kawkab (كوكب) = star, planet
- These are Semitic cognates, clearly related

**Key question:** Was Abraham being taught the word in the Adamic language, or in a Semitic language he already knew? The Urim and Thummim was the medium (Abraham 3:1). If these are Adamic, then Hebrew/Arabic "star" is a direct Adamic survival. If Abraham was just hearing Semitic, it's less remarkable.

John Tvedtnes (FAIR, 2005): Kolob may be related to Arabic qalb ("heart, center") — Kolob as "the heart/center" nearest God's throne.

### Thread 5: Deseret — the Jaredite word
- **Adamic/Jaredite:** Deseret = honey bee (Ether 2:3)
- The Jaredites' language was NOT confounded (Ether 1:35)
- This may be one of the purest Adamic words we have
- Egyptian: dšr.t = "the red one" (the red crown of Lower Egypt); also dshr = "red/sacred"
- The bee was a symbol of royalty in ancient Egypt (Lower Egypt's symbol was the bee)
- Nibley and others have noted the bee/Egypt connection

**Speculative:** If the Jaredites carried the pre-Babel language to the Americas, "deseret" is genuinely Adamic. Its phonetic similarity to Egyptian dshr.t (and the shared bee symbolism) could mean Egyptian preserved this Adamic root too. This is one of the strongest cases for an Adamic word surviving in recognizable form.

### Thread 6: BUR (ashes, dust) — creation language
- Proto-World: *bur = ashes, dust
- Hebrew: 'afar (עפר) = dust (as in "dust thou art, and unto dust shalt thou return" — Gen 3:19)
- Not a direct phonetic match, but the concept of dust/ashes as fundamental creation vocabulary is interesting

### Thread 7: Gnolaum — eternal
- **Adamic:** Gnolaum = eternal (Abraham 3:18)
- Doesn't match known Hebrew, Egyptian, or Greek roots for "eternal"
- This may be genuinely Adamic with no surviving cognates in known languages
- Or it may be from an ancient language we haven't identified yet

## Honest Assessment

**What's reasonably solid:**
- The "Sample of Pure Language" exists as a historical document (Joseph Smith Papers)
- Revealed Adamic terms are in scripture (D&C, PGP, BoM)
- Kokob/Kochav/Kawkab is clearly a real cognate family
- The 27 global etymologies are a real (if controversial) scholarly proposal
- "Mama/papa" as cross-cultural is well-documented
- Deseret's Egyptian connections have been noted by serious scholars (Nibley et al.)

**What's speculative:**
- Connecting Ahman → Amon → Amen → a single Adamic root
- "Man" as a universal Adamic root (the word is Indo-European, not clearly global)
- Whether the global etymologies represent a single original language or independent development
- Whether Joseph Smith's revealed words are genuinely Adamic or something else (sacred language, prophetic vocabulary, etc.)

**What's honest about the limits:**
- Most mainstream linguists reject the proto-world hypothesis
- Sound symbolism and babble can explain mama/papa without a common ancestor
- Phonetic similarity doesn't prove genetic relationship (false cognates are real)
- We have maybe 20-30 revealed "Adamic" words total — far too few for systematic comparison
- The whole exercise is pattern-matching, and pattern-matching finds what it looks for
