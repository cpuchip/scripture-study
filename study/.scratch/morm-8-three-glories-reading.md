# Scratch — Reading Mormon 8 at Three Altitudes (D&C 76 as a reading lens)

## Binding question

Can D&C 76's vocabulary of glory — telestial / terrestrial / celestial (and
perdition) — function as a *reading altitude*, so the same scriptural word
resolves differently at each level? When we run Mormon 8's load-bearing words
("fine apparel," "gain," "churches," "pollute," "glory," "the poor") through
those registers with Webster 1828 in hand, what does the chapter reveal — and is
Moroni's indictment of a telestial age in fact a *celestial reading* of it? How
does "thinking celestial" (Nelson, Oct 2023) reframe how we read?

Michael's framing: D&C 76 gives a vocabulary on HOW to read. "fine apparel" →
telestial = rich clothes; "fine apparel" → celestial = temple robes. Apply 1828
heavily. Full study; Nelson's "Think Celestial" talk.

## The honesty frame (write this into the study — it is load-bearing)

This is a **devotional / typological lens we bring**, not a claim that Mormon 8
(c. AD 400, translated 1829) secretly encodes D&C 76 (1832). D&C 76 describes
kingdoms of glory at the Judgment; it does not state a hermeneutic. Using its
vocabulary as a reading-altitude is defensible and fruitful, but it is eisegesis
if asserted as the text's literal intent. The Spirit discerns the fruit
(discernment-rubric). Also: most words split cleanly into TWO poles
(telestial ↔ celestial); "terrestrial" is the harder middle — "a form of
godliness, honorable but not valiant" (D&C 76:75,79) — and shouldn't be forced
onto every word. Don't manufacture a tidy 3-way where the text gives a 2-way.

Cousin to the Abinadi study (one verse / many referents). This is one word /
many altitudes of glory. Arc: Abinadi (how to read — referents) → Mormon 8 (the
chapter) → this (how to read — glories).

## D&C 76 — the three glories (verified)

- **Celestial** (vv. 50–70, 92–96): "received the testimony of Jesus, and
  believed on his name and were baptized" (51); "sealed by the Holy Spirit of
  promise" (53); "the church of the Firstborn" (54); "received of his fulness"
  (56); "they are gods, even the sons of God" (58); "dwell in the presence of
  God and his Christ forever" (62); glory "of the sun" (70, 96). Webster (genuine
  1828): "Heavenly; belonging or relating to heaven… conveys the idea of
  superior excellence, delight, purity."
- **Terrestrial** (vv. 71–80, 91): "honorable men of the earth, who were blinded
  by the craftiness of men" (75); "receive of the presence of the Son, but not
  of the fulness of the Father" (77); "not valiant in the testimony of Jesus"
  (79); glory "of the moon" (78). Webster (genuine 1828): "Pertaining to the
  world, or to the present state; sublunary." (note: *sublunary* = below the
  moon — fits.)
- **Telestial** (vv. 81–112): "received not the gospel of Christ, neither the
  testimony of Jesus" (82); "liars, and sorcerers, and adulterers, and
  whoremongers" (103); "thrust down to hell" (84), redeemed only at the last
  resurrection (85); "servants of the Most High; but where God and Christ dwell
  they cannot come, worlds without end" (112); glory "of the stars" (81).
  **Webster 1828: 'telestial' NOT FOUND — a Joseph Smith coinage.**
- **Perdition** (vv. 30–49): "deny the truth and defy my power" (31); "denied
  the Holy Spirit after having received it… denied the Only Begotten Son… having
  crucified him unto themselves" (35); "the only ones who shall not be redeemed"
  (38); the "second death" (37).
- **The lens-key verse:** v. 61 "let no man **glory** in man, but rather let him
  **glory** in God" — the two glories of "glory," in one verse.

## Webster 1828 — the words carry both altitudes (GENUINE 1828, re-verified via webster MCP 2026-06-13)

> The block below was originally drafted from the mislabeled 1913-as-1828 tool
> and has now been corrected to the genuine 1828 (each word re-pulled this
> session). The 2026-06-09 EDITION AUDIT at the foot of this file documents the
> same corrections — they now agree.

- **apparel:** (1) "Clothing; vesture; garments; dress." (2) "External
  habiliments or decorations; appearance; as, religion appears in the natural
  apparel of simplicity. **Glorious in apparel. Isaiah 63:7**." → The genuine
  1828 bridge is Webster's own scriptural example, "Glorious in apparel"
  (Isaiah 63:1 in the authoritative text; local data shows the OCR'd "63:7"),
  applied to the Messiah — not the 1913-only "albs/ecclesiastical-vestments"
  sense, which was FALSE for 1828.
- **fine (adj.):** celestial poles = (5) "Clear; pure; free from feculence or
  foreign matter; as fine gold or silver" + (6) "Refined" // telestial pole =
  (16) "Showy; splendid; elegant" and the closing line "in general, fine, in
  popular language, expresses whatever is excellent, **showy or magnificent**."
  (The genuine 1828 sense 1 is "Small; thin; slender" — not the 1913 "Finished;
  brought to perfection." No "gain thereof is better than fine gold/Prov 3:14"
  example exists in the genuine entry.)
- **gain (n.):** "Profit; interest; something obtained as an advantage. **But
  what things were gain to me, those I counted loss for Christ. Philippians
  3:7**" [the celestial counter-valuation] // "Unlawful advantage. 2 Corinthians
  12:17-18" [the telestial sense]. The verb entry adds "What is a man profited,
  if he shall gain the whole world, and lose his own soul? **Matthew 16:26**."
  (The 1 Tim 6:6 "godliness with contentment" and "lust of gain/Tennyson"
  quotes were 1913 — NOT in genuine 1828.)
- **transfigure:** "To transform; to change the outward form or appearance.
  **And was transfigured before them. Matthew 17:2**." → Mormon 8:33
  "transfigured the holy word of God" = the telestial inversion: changed the
  form of what was glorious. (The 1913 embellishment "especially to change to
  something exalted and glorious" is dropped.)
- **glory (n.):** telestial senses = (5) "Honor; praise; fame; renown;
  celebrity" + (11) "Pride; boastfulness; arrogance; as vain glory" //
  celestial senses = (1) "Brightness; luster; splendor" (quoting 2 Peter 1:17,
  the Transfiguration) + (6) "The felicity of heaven prepared for the children
  of God; **celestial bliss**" + (7) "In scripture, the divine presence." (The
  1913 "presence of the Divine Being… celestial honor; heaven" and "emanation
  of light… beings of peculiar sanctity" wordings are dropped.)
- **pollute:** (1) "To defile; to make foul or unclean; in a general sense. But
  appropriately, among the Jews, to make unclean or impure, in a legal or
  ceremonial sense, **so as to disqualify a person for sacred services, or to
  render things unfit for sacred uses**. Numbers 18:32." → the anti-temple
  word; opposite of "washed white." (Genuine 1828 cites the verse numbers; it
  does not quote "Neither shall ye pollute the holy things.")
- **celestial:** "Heavenly; belonging or relating to heaven; dwelling in heaven;
  as celestial spirits; celestial joys. Hence the word conveys the idea of
  **superior excellence, delight, purity**." (Not the 1913 "spiritual heaven…
  the heavenly Jerusalem.")
- **terrestrial:** (1) "Pertaining to the earth; existing on the earth; as
  terrestrial animals; **bodies terrestrial. 1 Corinthians 15:40**." (3)
  "Pertaining to the world, or to the present state; **sublunary**." (Genuine
  1828 keeps the 1 Cor 15:40 citation and "sublunary"; "earthly; mundane" was
  1913 phrasing.)

## Mormon 8 words at three altitudes (the core table)

| Word (Mormon 8) | Telestial (stars / earthly) | Terrestrial (moon / honorable-not-valiant) | Celestial (sun / the fulness) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **fine apparel** (8:36–37) | costly, showy clothes; status, the pride of the age | respectable dress; the *form* of religion, looking the part | the robe of righteousness washed white in the blood of the Lamb; temple robes (2 Ne 9:14; Alma 5:21; Rev 7:14; D&C 109:76) |
| **gain / get gain** (8:33,40) | money, riches, profit ("lust of gain") | reputation, honor, the praise of the world (8:38) | the soul; "godliness with contentment is great gain" (1 Tim 6:6); not to "gain the whole world and lose his soul" |
| **glory** (cf. D&C 76:61) | renown, being seen; pride/boasting | honorable standing among men | the presence of God; "glory in God" not in man |
| **churches** (8:28,32,36) | built up to get gain; "for your money you shall be forgiven" | defiled/honorable institutions, the form without the fulness | the church of the Firstborn (D&C 76:54) |
| **pollute / pollutions** (8:31,38) | filth, abominations on the earth | the holy made common, casually | "unfit for sacred use" — the inverse of washed-white temple purity |
| **the poor / needy / sick** (8:37–39) | passersby to ignore; an inconvenience | objects of token, occasional charity | Christ Himself ("inasmuch as ye have done it unto one of the least"); the celestial test of the chapter |

Note the chapter names BOTH poles itself: 8:38 "the value of an **endless
happiness** than that **misery which never dies**" = the celestial pole vs the
perdition pole, in one verse. And 8:21 "We will destroy the work of the Lord" =
the perdition motion (deny/fight after light), echoing last study's Sherem/Korihor.

## The thesis

Mormon 8 is a **telestial-age diagnosis written by a celestial witness.** Moroni
has the celestial markers — he has seen Christ, been ministered to by translated
beings (8:11), holds the fulness — and from that altitude he reads the
latter-day world's apparel/gain/churches/pollutions for exactly what they are at
the telestial level, and grieves. The lens is not imposed; the words already run
at multiple altitudes, and **Webster 1828 preserves the double register inside
the very vocabulary.** "Fine apparel" is the cleanest case: the same two words
mean showy overdecoration (telestial, 8:36–37) and refined-to-purity vesture
(celestial — the washed-white robe). To "think celestial" (Nelson) is to choose
the celestial reading — of a word, of a wardrobe, of a life.

## Gap & Critical Analysis

**Strongest, best-supported:**
- The Webster double-register on fine / gain / glory / pollute / apparel is hard
  textual evidence, not my projection — the 1828 entries literally carry both
  altitudes. This is the study's spine and it is solid.
- The celestial-apparel cluster (robe washed white) is a real, dense scriptural
  motif, footnote- and search-confirmed.
- "telestial" absent from Webster 1828 is verifiably true and theologically
  pointed (the lowest glory needed a new word).

**Where to NOT overclaim (honesty checks):**
- Do NOT say Mormon 8 "encodes" the three glories. Frame as a lens we bring; D&C
  76 is a later revelation about the Judgment, not a stated reading method. Name
  the eisegesis risk plainly and let the Spirit be the discerner.
- The 3-way altitude is clean for some words (gain: money/honor/soul) but forced
  for others. Be honest that the dominant axis is the 2-pole telestial↔celestial,
  with terrestrial as "the form without the fulness / honorable but not valiant"
  — a middle that fits *churches* and *apparel-as-respectability* but not every
  word. Don't fabricate a terrestrial column where the text gives two.
- Nelson's "think celestial" is about life-choices, not a scripture-reading
  method. I am extending his frame to reading; say so (it is an honest
  extension, not his claim).
- "fine apparel = temple robes" — the celestial reading is the robe of
  righteousness / washed-white garment (scripturally dense). Temple robes are
  one concrete instance; keep the broader "robe of righteousness" as the anchor
  so it is not narrowed to a single ordinance claim.

**Missing voices considered:** Jacob 2 (costly apparel — Nephite telestial
wardrobe, could add); Isaiah 61:10 (robe of righteousness — 2 Ne 9:14 already
carries it); Matt 22:11 (the wedding garment — strong celestial-apparel image,
optional). The spine answers the binding question without them; add if room.

**Ring check:** answers the binding question — the lens, the word-table, the
thesis (telestial diagnosis / celestial witness), the apparel case worked end to
end, Nelson + 1828 integrated.

**Posture (discovering vs confirming):** the discovery was that Webster 1828
*itself* holds both altitudes in fine/gain/glory — I came expecting to *impose*
the lens and found the dictionary had already split the words. That's the genuine
find, and it disciplines the eisegesis worry: the double meaning is in the
language, not only in me.
