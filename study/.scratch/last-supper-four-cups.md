**Binding question:** How does the Passover — and specifically its four cups — illuminate what Jesus did at the Last Supper, in Gethsemane, and on the cross?

### gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/19.md — John 19:14, 28-37
> "And it was the preparation of the passover, and about the sixth hour: and he saith unto the Jews, Behold your King!" (v.14)

**Observations:**
- v.14 John dates the crucifixion to "the preparation of the passover" — i.e. the lambs were being slain in the temple as Jesus was condemned. John deliberately frames the death as the Passover sacrifice.
- v.28 "Jesus knowing that all things were now accomplished" — the suffering is ALREADY complete when He says "I thirst." Important sequencing: "I thirst" comes AFTER "all things were now accomplished." So the drink that follows is not the agony itself; the agony is done. The drink is a final, deliberate act.
- v.28 "that the scripture might be fulfilled, saith, I thirst" — He speaks "I thirst" in order to fulfill scripture. It is intentional, not merely physiological.
- v.29 "vinegar" footnote 29a → Psalm 69:21 ("in my thirst they gave me vinegar to drink"). The drink fulfills that psalm.
- v.29 "put it upon hyssop" — HYSSOP. The same plant that in Ex 12:22 applied the lamb's blood to the doorposts now lifts the drink to the Lamb's lips. John alone of the gospels names hyssop here (Matt 27:48 / Mark 15:36 say "a reed"). John, the gospel most concerned with Passover typology, chose the word hyssop. KEY.
- v.30 "It is finished" — footnote 30a → TG Jesus Christ, Atonement through. The Greek is tetelestai. He receives the drink, THEN says it is finished, THEN dies. Sequence: drink → declaration → death.
- v.36 "A bone of him shall not be broken" — footnote 36a → **Ex. 12:46; Num. 9:12** (the Passover lamb law). The gospel-library editors explicitly tie the unbroken bones to the paschal lamb. John names Jesus as the Passover lamb in the manner of His death.
- HONEST NOTE: John 19 does NOT call the vinegar a "cup" and does NOT mention the seder's four cups. The "fourth cup completed on the cross" reading is a typological synthesis, not an explicit NT statement. The text gives: Passover framing + hyssop + "I thirst" + "It is finished." The four-cup overlay is devotional interpretation built on those facts.

## Webster 1828

### hyssop
- **Webster 1828:** "A plant (Hyssopus officinalis). The leaves have an aromatic smell, and a warm, pungent taste."
- **Modern dictionary (notable):** "A plant used for sprinkling of blood or water in purification rituals and at Passover."
- **Study relevance:** Webster's 1828 entry is plain botany — but the modern entry explicitly ties hyssop to blood-sprinkling and Passover. Hyssop's scriptural role is the applicator of blood and the agent of purification (Ex 12:22; Lev 14; Ps 51:7 "purge me with hyssop"). When John alone names hyssop at the cross, he reaches for the Passover plant.

### passover
- **Webster 1828, sense 1:** "A feast of the Jews, instituted to commemorate the sparing of the Hebrews in Egypt, when God, smiting the firstborn of the Egyptians, passed over the houses of the Israelites which were marked with the blood of a lamb."
- **Webster 1828, sense 2:** "The sacrifice offered at the feast of the passover; the paschal lamb. Ex. xii."
- **Study relevance:** Webster's sense 2 — "passover" IS the lamb — is exactly Paul's usage in 1 Cor 5:7, "Christ our passover." The word names both the feast and the victim. Christ is the Passover in Webster's second sense.

### gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/116.md — Psalm 116:12-13 (Read-verified)
> "What shall I render unto the Lord for all his benefits toward me? I will take the cup of salvation, and call upon the name of the Lord." (v.12-13)

**Observations:**
- Psalm 116 is part of the Hallel (Psalms 113-118), the songs sung at Passover. v.13 "I will take the cup of salvation" is the verse Jewish tradition associates with the fourth/final cup of the seder. Note: the psalm begins "The sorrows of death compassed me, and the pains of hell gat hold upon me" (v.3) — a Hallel psalm sung at the very meal Jesus kept, and it speaks of death's sorrows and a cup of salvation. v.15: "Precious in the sight of the Lord is the death of his saints." Whatever hymn they sang in Matt 26:30, it carried this language about death and a saving cup.
- HONEST FRAMING: The Bible does not number the cups. Exodus does not mention cups at the first Passover at all (it mentions the lamb, blood, hyssop, unleavened bread, bitter herbs). The four-cup structure is rabbinic — codified in the Mishnah (Pesachim 10), compiled c. 200 AD. The Mishnah postdates Christ. So we cannot assert Jesus followed a fixed four-cup liturgy. What we CAN say from the NT text: Luke records two distinct cups at the supper (22:17, 22:20), which shows multiple cups were in use; and the meal ended with a hymn (the Hallel). The four-named-cups framing is a later, beautiful tradition layered onto these facts.

### Phase 3a — Critical analysis (stress test)
1. STRONGEST CLAIM CHECK — "Jesus stopped before the fourth cup." The NT text says: He took a cup and said "I will not drink henceforth of this fruit of the vine, until..." (Matt 26:29). It does NOT say "this was the third of four cups" or "He skipped the fourth." The claim that He stopped specifically before the *fourth* cup depends entirely on the rabbinic four-cup numbering. HONEST VERDICT: The text supports "He declared a deferral — a cup He would not drink until the kingdom." It does NOT, by itself, support the specific numbering. The four-cup reading is a lens that makes the deferral vivid; it is not stated doctrine. The study must say this plainly.
2. WEAKEST LINK — "The vinegar on the cross IS the deferred fourth cup." This is the most beautiful and the most fragile claim. John 19 never calls the vinegar a cup. "I will not drink... until that day when I drink it new in my Father's kingdom" (Matt 26:29) points to a FUTURE kingdom event (D&C 27 confirms — a yet-future sacrament), NOT to the cross hours later. So the typology "fourth cup finished on the cross" actually sits in TENSION with Jesus' own stated timeline. Must name this tension, not paper over it. The honest reading: the vinegar is better understood as fulfilling Psalm 69:21 and as the bitter cup's last drop than as the seder's fourth cup of rejoicing.
3. MISSING VOICES — All five standard works? OT (Exodus, Psalms), NT (Matt, Mark, Luke, John, 1 Cor), D&C (19, 27 ref) — yes. Book of Mormon: Mosiah 3:7 appears in the footnote chain (bleeding at every pore) — should include it. PGP: not essential here. Modern prophets: McConkie, Holland, Oaks, Nelson (via manual) — adequate.
4. FRAMING — Must distinguish: (a) NT-explicit facts [Last Supper was Passover; Christ is the Passover lamb; bread+cup instituted; "I will not drink henceforth"; hyssop at the cross; "It is finished"]; (b) ancient-tradition [four cups, Hallel, seder structure — real but rabbinic/post-Mishnaic in codified form]; (c) devotional typology [the fourth-cup-on-the-cross synthesis — beautiful, edifying, NOT doctrine]. The study's Section 6 must lay these three tiers out.
5. RING CHECK — Binding question: "How does the Passover and its four cups illuminate what Jesus did at the Last Supper, in Gethsemane, and on the cross?" The text led to: the Passover illuminates ENORMOUSLY (lamb, blood, hyssop, deliverance, memorial→sacrament). The *four cups specifically* illuminate LESS than the popular framing claims — they give a lens, not a proof. The honest answer reframes: "You asked about the four cups; the text gives you the Lamb, the cup, and the hyssop with certainty, and the four-cup numbering as a devotional lens that is true to the spirit even where it outruns the letter."
6. THE BITTER-CUP / FOURTH-CUP ANSWER (binding question core): Where is the bitter cup finished? D&C 19:19 — "I partook and finished my preparations" — past tense, the whole work. McConkie: the agonies of Gethsemane "recurred" on the cross; the Atonement "took place in Gethsemane and at Golgotha." Holland: anguish "began in the Garden of Gethsemane," and "It is finished" on the cross is when "the uttermost farthing had been paid." VERDICT: The bitter cup is not finished IN Gethsemane and then a separate thing happens on the cross. It is one cup, drunk in Gethsemane and drained to the dregs on the cross. "It is finished" (John 19:30) is the moment it is finished. D&C 19:19's "finished" is footnote-linked to John 19:30 by the gospel-library editors themselves. The cup begins in the garden and ends on the cross.
7. POSTURE CHECK — Did I read to confirm? The video's thesis (Jesus completes the fourth cup on the cross) is emotionally compelling. I gathered sources that both support the Passover typology AND complicate the specific four-cup claim (Matt 26:29's future-kingdom timeline; the Mishnaic dating; John never calling the vinegar a cup). Section 6 holds the tension. This is discovery, not confirmation.

OUTLINE ADJUSTMENT: Section 6 should not be a dry caveat appendix — it should be the section where the study is most honest AND most worshipful: the type is true even where the numbering is loose, because the Lamb is real.
