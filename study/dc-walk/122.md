# D&C 122 — ★★ all these things shall give thee experience

**Setting:** **Liberty Jail, ~March 22, 1839** — the same epistle as §121, the next breath. Where §121 answered the lament with the doctrine of power, §122 turns directly to the suffering man and tells him what his suffering is *for*. It is the most personal passage in the section: the Lord names Joseph's own children, the drawn sword, the six-year-old clinging to his garment — and then reframes the entire catalog of horrors as *curriculum*.

**Binding idea:** **"Know thou, my son, that all these things shall give thee experience, and shall be for thy good. The Son of Man hath descended below them all. Art thou greater than he?" (122:7-8).** Every affliction — to the very jaws of hell — is *experience*, and is *for thy good*; and the warrant is not a promise of rescue but the fact that Christ went *lower* ("descended below them all"). The §121 setting (the deepest pit) made this implicit; §122 makes it the explicit law.

**Read:**
- The reach and the rage: "The ends of the earth shall inquire after thy name, and fools shall have thee in derision, and hell shall rage against thee; While the pure in heart, and the wise, and the noble, and the virtuous, shall seek counsel, and authority, and blessings constantly from under thy hand" (122:1-2). "And thy people shall never be turned against thee by the testimony of traitors" (122:3). "And although their influence shall cast thee into trouble, and into bars and walls, thou shalt be had in honor; and but for a small moment and thy voice shall be more terrible in the midst of thine enemies than the fierce lion, because of thy righteousness; and thy God shall stand by thee forever and ever" (122:4).
- The catalog (the worst named): "If thou art called to pass through tribulation; if thou art in perils among false brethren; if thou art in perils among robbers; if thou art in perils by land or by sea; If thou art accused with all manner of false accusations; if thine enemies fall upon thee; if they tear thee from the society of thy father and mother and brethren and sisters; and if with a drawn sword thine enemies tear thee from the bosom of thy wife, and of thine offspring, and thine elder son, although but six years of age, shall cling to thy garments, and shall say, My father, my father, why can't you stay with us? O, my father, what are the men going to do with you? and if then he shall be thrust from thee by the sword, and thou be dragged to prison, and thine enemies prowl around thee like wolves for the blood of the lamb" (122:5-6).
- The turn: "And if thou shouldst be cast into the pit, or into the hands of murderers, and the sentence of death passed upon thee… and above all, if the very jaws of hell shall gape open the mouth wide after thee, know thou, my son, that all these things shall give thee experience, and shall be for thy good" (122:7).
- The ground: "The Son of Man hath descended below them all. Art thou greater than he?" (122:8).
- The charge: "Therefore, hold on thy way, and the priesthood shall remain with thee; for their bounds are set, they cannot pass. Thy days are known, and thy years shall not be numbered less; therefore, fear not what man can do, for God shall be with you forever and ever" (122:9).

**Thoughts:**

- *★★ Affliction is the curriculum — "experience… for thy good" (122:7).* This is the canon's most direct statement that suffering *teaches* and *profits*. The Lord does not promise to remove the perils — He lists them at their absolute worst (the pit, the murderers, the death sentence, "the very jaws of hell") and then says: "**all these things shall give thee experience, and shall be for thy good**." (Two claims fused. First, suffering yields *experience* — it is not waste but *instruction* [the §105:6 "learn obedience by the things which they suffer," Heb 5:8 even-of-Christ; 2 Ne 2:11 opposition-in-all-things; the affliction *forms* what no comfort could]. Second, it is *for thy good* — the §90:24/Rom 8:28 "all things work together for good" given its hardest case [not *some* things, "all these things"; not pleasant things, the *worst* things]. For the project's whole theology of trial: the scatter, the degradation, the hard correction are not the interruption of the work but the *making* of the worker — the §121 lesson on presiding learned *through* the §122 affliction. The one who will hold power only by persuasion [§121:41] is forged by having been wrongly compelled [§122:5-6]; the curriculum and the office are the same school.)

- *★★ "The Son of Man hath descended below them all. Art thou greater than he?" (122:8).* The warrant for the whole reframe is a single Christological fact and a single question. The reason affliction can be "for thy good" is that Christ has already gone *lower* than any of it — "descended below them all" (the condescension, §88:6 "he that ascended… is the same also that descended below all things"; §76:107; Eph 4:9-10). (The question "Art thou greater than he?" is not a rebuke but a *re-anchoring*: if the sinless Son endured *more*, then no suffering of yours places you outside the path — it places you *on* it, beside Him. The matter-spectrum's condescension [the §88 Light of Christ who "descended below all things" precisely so He could be "in and through all things"] given its *pastoral* edge: the God who is in all things got there by going *under* all things. For the one suffering: the floor of your pit is not the bottom — He is below it; therefore the pit is survivable, and shared. This is the deepest comfort in the section — not "you will be rescued" but "you are not alone, and you are not the greatest sufferer; the One who was is *with* you, 122:9.")

- *★ The suffering is bounded — "thy days are known" (122:4,9).* Twice the comfort is *measure*, not removal: "but for a **small moment**" (122:4, picking up 121:7's "small moment") and "thy days are known, and **thy years shall not be numbered less**" (122:9). (The §121:7 "small moment" given its floor: the affliction has an *appointed end* [the enemies' "bounds are set, they cannot pass," 122:9], and — startlingly — the suffering will *not shorten his life by a day* ["thy years shall not be numbered less"]. The fear "what man can do" is answered not by denying man's malice but by *bounding* it: man can afflict, but cannot subtract from the days God has appointed. For the project's "fear not" thread [§3:7/§30:11/§98:14 — fear-of-man cured by Presence]: the cure is not the absence of danger but the *sovereignty of God over the danger's bounds*; "fear not what man can do, for God shall be with you.")
