# D&C 25 — an elect lady

**Setting:** **Harmony, Pennsylvania, July 1830** (with §24, §26) — "the will of the Lord to **Emma Smith**, the Prophet's wife." Joseph and Emma were under the same persecution; Emma had borne the loss of her first child, copied translation manuscript by hand, and stood with Joseph through the 116-pages crisis. This is her revelation — the only one in the D&C addressed to a woman by name — and it both calls her personally and, at the end, speaks to everyone.

**Binding idea:** "Thou art an elect lady, whom I have called" (25:3). Emma is given a real calling — to comfort, to write, to expound scripture, to choose the Church's hymns — and a hard discipline: "murmur not because of the things which thou hast not seen, for they are withheld from thee… which is wisdom in me in a time to come" (25:4). And then the universalizing turn: "this is my voice unto all" (25:16). What was said to Emma is said to everyone.

**Read:** "Hearken unto the voice of the Lord your God… Emma Smith, my daughter; for verily I say unto you, all those who receive my gospel are sons and daughters in my kingdom" (25:1). "Thy sins are forgiven thee, and thou art an elect lady, whom I have called" (25:3). "Murmur not because of the things which thou hast not seen, for they are withheld from thee and from the world, which is wisdom in me in a time to come" (25:4). "The office of thy calling shall be for a comfort unto my servant, Joseph Smith, Jun., thy husband, in his afflictions, with consoling words, in the spirit of meekness" (25:5). "Thou shalt be ordained under his hand to expound scriptures, and to exhort the church, according as it shall be given thee by my Spirit" (25:7). "Thy time shall be given to writing, and to learning much" (25:8). "Thou shalt lay aside the things of this world, and seek for the things of a better" (25:10). "It shall be given thee… to make a selection of sacred hymns… to be had in my church. For my soul delighteth in the song of the heart; yea, the song of the righteous is a prayer unto me" (25:11-12). "Lift up thy heart and rejoice, and cleave unto the covenants which thou hast made" (25:13). "Let thy soul delight in thy husband, and the glory which shall come upon him" (25:14). "This is my voice unto all. Amen" (25:16).

**Thoughts:**
- *"All those who receive my gospel are sons and daughters in my kingdom" (25:1).* The revelation to a woman *opens* by establishing equal standing: sons *and daughters* in the kingdom. Before Emma's specific calling, the Lord sets the floor — she stands in the kingdom by the same gospel as any son. The calling that follows is not a lesser woman's-version; it's expounding scripture, exhorting the church, shaping its worship. (And the title "elect lady," 25:3 — the footnote: "one chosen or set apart"; 2 John 1 addresses an "elect lady" — places Emma in a New Testament line of chosen women. Later, as first president of the Relief Society in Nauvoo, Emma cited this verse: she *was* the elect lady, and the Relief Society the fulfillment.)
- *★ "Murmur not… for they are withheld… wisdom in me in a time to come" (25:4) — the discipline of the withheld.* This is the section's hardest and most enduring teaching, and it threads straight to the reserved-knowledge thread of the walk (§10:37, the temple-reserved figures, Michael's period-language reflection). Emma is told there are things she has *not seen*, withheld from her *and from the world*, and the command is *don't murmur about it* — trust that the withholding is "wisdom in me in a time to come." Not everything is given now; some knowledge is *reserved*, and the spiritual discipline is to hold the not-knowing without resentment. (This is the believer's posture toward the temple-reserved, the not-yet-revealed, the figures-8-through-21: faith includes *trusting what is withheld*, not just believing what is shown. The murmur is the temptation; the meekness — "continue in the spirit of meekness," 25:14 — is the answer.)
- *★ "The song of the righteous is a prayer unto me" (25:12) — and Emma's hymnbook.* Emma is commissioned to "make a selection of sacred hymns… to be had in my church" (25:11) — and she did: the 1835 hymnal, the Church's first, was Emma's. The doctrine under the assignment is lovely: "my soul delighteth in the song of the heart; yea, the song of the righteous is a prayer unto me, and it shall be answered with a blessing." Singing is not ornament to worship; it *is* prayer — God receives a hymn the way He receives a petition, and answers it with blessing. (This dignifies the whole of sacred music: every congregation singing is *praying*, and the prayer is heard. Emma's stewardship was to curate the Church's prayers-in-song.)
- *The calling is genuinely Emma's, and genuinely relational (25:5-8,14).* Part of her office is to *comfort Joseph* "in his afflictions, with consoling words" (25:5) — a marriage-shaped calling, not a diminishment but a recognition that her steadying of the prophet is *kingdom work*. And it runs both ways: "let thy soul delight in thy husband, and the glory which shall come upon him" (25:14). The calling weaves her personal gifts (writing, expounding, music) with her relational place (comfort, delight). (It refuses the false choice between a woman's own calling and her marriage — Emma is given *both*, as one stewardship.)
- *★ "This is my voice unto all" (25:16) — the personal made universal.* The revelation closes by detonating its own specificity: everything said to Emma — the equal standing, the discipline of the withheld, the spirit of meekness, the song as prayer, "cleave unto the covenants thou hast made" — "is my voice unto **all**." Emma's revelation is *everyone's*. The Lord uses one woman's named, particular calling to teach the whole Church. (This is the §15-16 move inverted: there, the *same* words to two people taught universality by repetition; here, *particular* words to one person are declared universal by fiat. Either way, the personal and the universal collapse into each other — what God says to one, rightly heard, is for all.)
