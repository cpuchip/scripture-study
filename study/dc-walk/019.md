# D&C 19 — "I, God, have suffered these things for all"

**Setting:** **Manchester, New York, likely summer 1829** — "a commandment of God and not of man, to **Martin Harris**, given by him who is Eternal" (Joseph's introduction). Martin had pledged his farm to secure the $3,000 to print the Book of Mormon and was wavering. This is the Lord's answer — and it is the most extraordinary thing: before He asks Martin to pay, He tells Martin what *He* paid. The deepest first-person account of the Atonement in all of scripture sits inside a revelation about a printing debt.

**Binding idea:** Two things are joined that look unrelated. First, the Lord redefines "eternal punishment" — it is not endless *duration* but *God's* punishment ("Endless is my name," 19:10-12), lifting the Calvinist terror of infinite hell. Then He shows the suffering that lets anyone escape it: "I, God, have suffered these things for all, that they might not suffer if they would repent" (19:16). And only *then*: Martin, pay the printer. Having heard what Christ bore, the farm seems a small thing.

**Read:** "I am Alpha and Omega, Christ the Lord… the Redeemer of the world" (19:1), "retaining all power, even to the destroying of Satan" (19:3). "Every man must repent or suffer, for I, God, am endless" (19:4). The redefinition: "it is not written that there shall be no end to this torment, but it is written *endless torment*… *eternal damnation*… more express than other scriptures, that it might work upon the hearts" (19:6-7). "I am endless, and the punishment which is given from my hand is endless punishment, for Endless is my name. Wherefore — Eternal punishment is God's punishment. Endless punishment is God's punishment" (19:10-12). Then: "repent, lest I smite you by the rod of my mouth… and your sufferings be sore — how sore you know not, how exquisite you know not, yea, how hard to bear you know not" (19:15). **"For behold, I, God, have suffered these things for all, that they might not suffer if they would repent; but if they would not repent they must suffer even as I; which suffering caused myself, even God, the greatest of all, to tremble because of pain, and to bleed at every pore, and to suffer both body and spirit — and would that I might not drink the bitter cup, and shrink — nevertheless, glory be to the Father, and I partook and finished my preparations unto the children of men"** (19:16-19). "Even in the least degree you have tasted at the time I withdrew my Spirit" (19:20). "Learn of me, and listen to my words; walk in the meekness of my Spirit, and you shall have peace in me" (19:23). Then to Martin: "covet not thine own property, but impart it freely to the printing of the Book of Mormon" (19:26); "pay the debt thou hast contracted with the printer. Release thyself from bondage" (19:35); "Pray always, and I will pour out my Spirit upon you… even more than if you should obtain treasures of earth" (19:38). "Canst thou read this without rejoicing and lifting up thy heart for gladness?" (19:39).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "Eternal/Endless punishment is God's punishment" — the terror is dissolved (19:6-12).* This is one of the great mercies in the canon. The 1820s religious world was haunted by *infinite hell* — the idea that "eternal punishment" means torment without end for all the damned. The Lord overturns it not by softening the words but by *parsing* them: "Endless" and "Eternal" are His *names* (Moses 1:3, "Endless and Eternal is my name"). So "endless punishment" means "*God's* punishment" — punishment that is His, of His kind, administered by Endless — *not necessarily of infinite duration.* The scary words were written "more express… that it might work upon the hearts" — chosen for their *weight*, to move people to repent, not to specify a sentence length. The doctrine that drove people to despair is revealed as a *rhetorical mercy*: God used the heaviest possible words to wake us, while reserving the actual duration to His justice (which §76 will show is graded, not uniform). The fear was real; the infinite-duration reading was a misreading. (This is a *hermeneutical* revelation — the Lord teaching how to read His own earlier words. It rhymes with §1:24 [period-language] and §10:37 [hold-your-peace]: the words mean what God means by them, not what dread assumes.)
- *★ The only first-person Gethsemane (19:18) — and what "exquisite" means.* The Gospels describe Gethsemane from outside (Luke 22:44, the bloody sweat). Here Christ describes it *from inside*, in His own voice: the suffering "caused myself, even God, the greatest of all, to tremble because of pain, and to bleed at every pore, and to suffer both body and spirit." And the wish is left *in the text*: "would that I might not drink the bitter cup, and shrink" — the recoil is not edited out; the God who went through with it first *wanted not to.* Then the turn on a single word — "**nevertheless**, glory be to the Father, and I partook and finished." The whole Atonement pivots on a *nevertheless* (the walk's recurring hinge-word, here at its highest stakes). · And the word "exquisite" (19:15, "how exquisite you know not"): Webster 1828 traces it to *exquiro*, "to seek out" — its root sense is "sought out, searched for with care," and it means "in the highest degree… very sensibly felt." The suffering was not blunt or general; it was *exquisite* — precisely, searchingly felt, each pain found out and registered in the highest degree. Not a wall of pain but a *fully-perceived* one. (This is the matter-spectrum's Christology at its limit: a God refined enough to feel *everything*, "body and spirit," with the most exquisite sensibility — the capacity that made Him the Creator [§14:9] is the capacity that let Him suffer for all.)
- *The withdrawal of the Spirit is a taste of it (19:20).* "Even in the least degree you have tasted at the time I withdrew my Spirit." The Lord tells Martin he already knows a *sliver* of the suffering — the desolation he felt when the Spirit withdrew (after the lost pages, §3/§10:2 "your mind became darkened"). So the suffering Christ bore is, at its core, the *absence of God* — and every soul that has felt the Spirit lift has tasted the least degree of it. Hell is not primarily fire; it is the withdrawal of the Light. (This reframes the whole punishment passage: what Christ spared us is *the loss of God*, which is why "Endless" — God Himself — is the name of the punishment. To be punished is to be without Endless; to be saved is "peace in me," 19:23.)
- *The juxtaposition IS the sermon: Christ's cost, then Martin's farm (19:16-19 → 19:26-35).* The structure is the argument. The Lord describes bleeding at every pore for all mankind — and then turns to Martin and says, *pay the printer; sell part of your land; release thyself from bondage.* After 19:18, the farm is unanswerable. How do you clutch a mortgage in front of a God who "partook and finished" the bitter cup? The Atonement is not abstract here; it is deployed as the reason Martin can let go of his property. *He gave everything for you; impart a portion of thy lands.* (And note the dignity in it — "release thyself from bondage" [19:35]: the debt is the bondage; obeying isn't loss, it's *freedom*. The command to give is framed as the Lord setting Martin free, not taking from him.)
- *"Canst thou read this without rejoicing?" (19:39).* The section that contains the most harrowing words in the D&C *ends in joy.* After the trembling and the bleeding and the bitter cup, the Lord asks Martin — and every reader — "can you read this without rejoicing and lifting up thy heart for gladness?" The right response to the Atonement is not guilt but *gladness*. The suffering was endured precisely so we *wouldn't* have to; to grasp that and stay heavy-hearted misses it. The cost was infinite; the result is meant to be joy. (This is the §6:20 "arms of my love" at the scale of the whole Atonement — the knowing-the-cost is meant to land as *embrace*, not dread.)
