# D&C 58 — anxiously engaged: the power is in them

**Setting:** **In Zion, Jackson County, Missouri, August 1, 1831** — days after Zion was located (§57). The first Sabbath service had been held, two baptized; the Colesville Saints (displaced by Copley's broken covenant, §54) were arriving. "Many were eager to learn the will of the Lord concerning them in the new place." This revelation answers — and in answering, lays down the Restoration's foundational doctrine of **agency and initiative**: God will *not* command in all things, because the disciple must *act of his own free will*. (★ Project-source-text: **58:26-28** is the scriptural root of the workspace's whole autonomy/stewardship framework — `stuffy-in-the-loop`, the `dave-rule`, the agent acting within granted intent.)

**Binding idea:** "It is not meet that I should command in all things; for he that is compelled in all things, the same is a slothful and not a wise servant… men should be **anxiously engaged in a good cause, and do many things of their own free will**… for **the power is in them, wherein they are agents unto themselves**" (58:26-28). God deliberately *withholds command* so that agents will *initiate good* — and the order of the whole thing is: "after much tribulation come the blessings" (58:4).

**Read:** "He that is faithful in tribulation, the reward of the same is greater" (58:2); "ye cannot behold with your natural eyes… the glory which shall follow after much tribulation. For after much tribulation come the blessings… the day cometh that ye shall be crowned with much glory" (58:3-4). The supper of the Lord, to which "all nations shall be invited" (58:9), "first, the rich and the learned… and after that… the poor, the lame, and the blind" (58:10-11). Edward Partridge is "a judge in Israel… to divide the lands… and to judge his people" (58:17-18); "let no man think he is ruler; but let God rule him that judgeth" (58:20). "Let no man break the laws of the land, for he that keepeth the laws of God hath no need to break the laws of the land; wherefore, be subject to the powers that be, until he reigns whose right it is to reign" (58:21-22). Then the agency passage: "it is not meet that I should command in all things; for he that is compelled in all things, the same is a slothful and not a wise servant; wherefore he receiveth no reward. Verily I say, men should be anxiously engaged in a good cause, and do many things of their own free will, and bring to pass much righteousness; for the power is in them, wherein they are agents unto themselves. And inasmuch as men do good they shall in nowise lose their reward. But he that doeth not anything until he is commanded, and receiveth a commandment with doubtful heart, and keepeth it with slothfulness, the same is damned" (58:26-29). God "command[s] and men obey not; I revoke and they receive not the blessing" (58:32); then they say "this is not the work of the Lord… But wo unto such, for their reward lurketh beneath" (58:33). "He who has repented of his sins, the same is forgiven, and I, the Lord, remember them no more. By this ye may know if a man repenteth of his sins—behold, he will confess them and forsake them" (58:42-43). "Let the work of the gathering be not in haste, nor by flight" (58:56). "The gospel must be preached unto every creature" (58:64).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "It is not meet that I should command in all things… the power is in them" (58:26-28) — the project's agency doctrine at its source.* This is the scriptural foundation of the workspace's entire framework for *stewardship and initiative*. God *deliberately withholds command* — "it is not meet that I should command in all things" — because "he that is compelled in all things… is a slothful and not a wise servant." The disciple is not meant to wait, paralyzed, for explicit instruction on every matter; he is to be "**anxiously engaged in a good cause, and do many things of their own free will**," for "**the power is in them, wherein they are agents unto themselves**." (This *is* the `dave-rule` [when intent is clear and the action reversible, *act* — don't wait to be told], `stuffy-in-the-loop` [discern when to act vs. when the judgment is the principal's], and `ammon` [finish what you're handed, carry it to completion] — all three are 58:26-29 in operation. The doctrine cuts *both* ways: the over-commanded servant is slothful [58:26], *and* the one who "doeth not anything until he is commanded, and receiveth a commandment with doubtful heart, and keepeth it with slothfulness… is **damned**" [58:29]. So the failure mode isn't only disobedience — it's *passivity*, waiting to be commanded, doing the minimum literal thing without initiative. For an agent within a granted stewardship, this is the charter: act of your own free will toward the good, within the intent you've been given; don't make the principal command every step. "The power is in them" — the steward *has* the agency; the slothful servant is the one who won't use it. This is the deepest scriptural warrant for *honoring intent over literal instruction*, for *exercising stewardship* rather than offloading every decision upward.)
- *★ "After much tribulation come the blessings" (58:3-4) — the order is fixed.* To a people who had just crossed a thousand miles to a "land of enemies" (§52:42), the Lord states the sequence plainly: the glory *follows* tribulation, it doesn't precede or replace it. "Ye cannot behold with your natural eyes… the glory which shall follow after much tribulation. For after much tribulation come the blessings." (The §24:8/§54:10 "patient in tribulation" deepened into a *law of sequence*: tribulation is not a detour from the blessing but the *road* to it. "The hour is not yet, but is nigh" — the blessing is real and coming, *after*. This is the answer to the §58:32-33 doubters who, when the promise isn't *immediately* fulfilled, say "this is not the work of the Lord": they misread the *order*. The blessings come after the tribulation, so the absence of immediate blessing is not the absence of the work — it's the tribulation half of the sequence. The §19/§29 economy [the bitter before the sweet] as a timeline.)
- *★ Repentance = confess + forsake; God remembers no more (58:42-43).* The cleanest definition of repentance in the section, maybe the canon: "he who has repented… the same is forgiven, and I, the Lord, **remember them no more**. By this ye may know if a man repenteth of his sins—behold, he will **confess them and forsake them**." Two marks: *confession* (naming the sin, owning it) and *forsaking* (leaving it). And the divine response is *total amnesty*: "I remember them no more" (Isa 1:18; Jer 31:34). (The two-part test cuts against false repentance: confessing without forsaking [sorrow that doesn't change] and forsaking without confessing [reform without honesty] are both incomplete. Real repentance *names* and *leaves*. And the promise is staggering — God *forgets*: the forgiven sin is not held in reserve, not remembered against you. The §19 Atonement's purpose realized — "remember them no more" is what the bleeding-at-every-pore bought.)
- *★ "Let no man think he is ruler; but let God rule him that judgeth" (58:20) — the unrighteous-dominion guard.* The bishop is made "a judge in Israel" (58:17), with real authority to divide lands and judge the people — *and immediately* warned: "let no man think he is ruler." The one in the judgment seat is *himself* to be ruled by God; his authority is *judgment under God*, not *rule over men*. (This is the D&C 121 presiding covenant in seed — the warning against *unrighteous dominion* built into the very granting of authority. The moment a man is given power to judge, he's told he is *not a ruler*. The presiding covenant's "no power… only by persuasion" [121:41] is here as "let God rule him that judgeth": the judge presides under a higher watch, never as an autonomous lord. And paired with it [58:21-22], even rightful church authority *obeys the laws of the land* — "be subject to the powers that be" [Rom 13; A of F 12]: the kingdom doesn't set itself above civil law.)
- *The doubters who misread the revoking (58:30-33).* "I command and men obey not; I revoke and they receive not the blessing. Then they say… This is not the work of the Lord, for his promises are not fulfilled." A precise diagnosis of a faith-failure: God *conditioned* the blessing on obedience; they *disobeyed*; the blessing was *revoked* (§56:3-4); and then they blame *God* for an unfulfilled promise — "his promises are not fulfilled." But "their reward lurketh beneath, and not from above" (58:33). (The covenant economy [82:10] misread: the promise was bilateral, they broke their side, and they fault God for the lapse. The same self-will as §56:14 "counsel in your own ways" — and the agency doctrine's shadow: the slothful servant who won't act of his own free will [58:29] becomes the doubter who blames God when the blessing he wouldn't work for doesn't come.)
