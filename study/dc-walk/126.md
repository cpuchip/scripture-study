# D&C 126 — ★ your offering is acceptable; take care of your family

**Setting:** **Nauvoo, Illinois, July 9, 1841 — in the house of Brigham Young**, who had just returned from a nearly two-year mission to England (the famous mission of the Twelve, undertaken while many of them were sick and destitute). Three verses, addressed to one man by name: the Lord *sees* his labor, *accepts* it, and *releases* him from further travel abroad to care for his family.

**Binding idea:** **"My servant Brigham, it is no more required at your hand to leave your family as in times past, for your offering is acceptable to me" (126:1).** The §124:49 principle — *the offering accepted* — made tender and personal: the faithful laborer's years of sacrifice are *seen and accepted*, and the reward is *release to his own household*. "Take especial care of your family from this time, henceforth and forever" (126:3).

**Read:** "Dear and well-beloved brother, Brigham Young, verily thus saith the Lord unto you: My servant Brigham, it is no more required at your hand to leave your family as in times past, for your offering is acceptable to me" (126:1). "I have seen your labor and toil in journeyings for my name" (126:2). "I therefore command you to send my word abroad, and take especial care of your family from this time, henceforth and forever. Amen" (126:3).

**Thoughts:**

- *★ The offering accepted, made personal — the faithful laborer released to his family (126:1-3).* Where §124:49 stated the *principle* (God accepts the willing work), §126 *enacts* it on a single weary man. (Three movements in three verses. First, the labor is *seen*: "I have seen your labor and toil in journeyings for my name" (126:2) — God witnessed every mile of the sick, destitute mission to England; nothing of it was unnoticed. Second, the offering is *accepted*: "for your offering is acceptable to me" (126:1) — the §124:49 acceptance landed on Brigham by name; his sacrifice is *enough*, complete, received. Third — and this is the tender turn — the acceptance *releases* him: "it is no more required at your hand to leave your family… take especial care of your family from this time, henceforth and forever" (126:1,3). The reward for faithful sacrifice is not *more* sacrifice but *permission to come home*. For the project's stewardship and the §24:9 strength-is-calling-bounded thread: the Lord does not run His faithful servants to ruin; the offering accepted means the demand *can be satisfied* — there is a "henceforth" where the laborer is sent to tend his own household, and that tending is now itself the command. The presiding covenant's downward face [§121:41 — no compulsion, only love] in pastoral form: the master who sees the labor and *releases* the servant to rest, rather than extracting endlessly. For Michael, who works in intermittent bursts and carries a family: the section that makes "take especial care of your family" a *commandment*, not a concession — the accepted offering buys the laborer his home.)

- *"Send my word abroad" AND "take care of your family" — both, not either (126:3).* The release is not from the work entirely — "I therefore command you to send my word abroad" *and* "take especial care of your family." (The two held together: Brigham still serves [now by sending the word, directing the work from Nauvoo], but the *form* of service shifts from self-abandoning travel to a sustainable rhythm that includes his household. The §119 durable-law principle applied to a life: not the unsustainable height [endless absence] but the standing rhythm [serve *and* tend home]. The family is not the enemy of the calling; caring for it is now *part* of the calling.)
