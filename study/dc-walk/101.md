# D&C 101 — why Zion fell, the tower unbuilt, and the inspired Constitution

**Setting:** **Kirtland, Ohio, December 16-17, 1833.** The blow had fallen: mobs had driven the Saints from Jackson County; they had lost "household furniture, clothing, livestock," their crops destroyed, their homes gone, with "many threats of death." The main body huddled in Clay County. Into that devastation the Lord answers the question every Saint was asking — *why?* — and gives the path to Zion's eventual redemption: a parable of a watchman who fell asleep, the inspired Constitution, and the importuning of the unjust judge.

**Binding idea:** "I, the Lord, have suffered the affliction to come upon them… in consequence of their transgressions… there were jarrings, and contentions, and envyings, and strifes, and lustful and covetous desires among them; therefore by these things they polluted their inheritances" (101:2,6). Zion was not merely overrun by enemies — she *lost the watch from within*. And yet: "notwithstanding their sins, my bowels are filled with compassion… I will not utterly cast them off" (101:9).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ Why Zion fell — the watch lost from within (101:1-9).* The Lord's answer to *why* is unflinching and *internal*: not "the mobs were wicked" [though they were] but "in consequence of *their* transgressions… jarrings, and contentions, and envyings, and strifes, and lustful and covetous desires" (101:2,6). The Saints "polluted their inheritances" by their *own* contention and covetousness; "in the day of their peace they esteemed lightly my counsel" (101:8). (The §97:25 conditional ["Zion shall escape *if* she observe to do all things"] explained: she did *not*, and *this is why*. The hard doctrine — affliction can come "in consequence of transgressions" — but bounded by two mercies: the chastening *sanctifies* ["they must needs be chastened and tried, even as Abraham… all those who will not endure chastening… cannot be sanctified," 101:4-5], and God's *compassion remains* ["notwithstanding their sins, my bowels are filled with compassion… I will not utterly cast them off; in the day of wrath I will remember mercy," 101:9]. The reprove-then-love [§3/§95/§121:43] at the scale of a lost homeland: the loss was *deserved* in part, and *redemptive* in whole, and *never the end of God's love*. The sober word for any covenant community: enemies without can only break what has already weakened within — "they esteemed lightly my counsel in the day of their peace." The watch is lost in the *peace*, not the crisis.)
- *★★ The parable of the nobleman — the tower unbuilt, the watch not kept (101:43-62).* The section's centerpiece, and the presiding covenant's *watch* doctrine in a story. A nobleman plants twelve olive trees and commands his servants to "set watchmen… and build a **tower**, that one may overlook the land… that mine olive trees may not be broken down when the enemy shall come" (101:44-45). But the servants, "while they were yet laying the foundation," begin to *question the tower*: "what need hath my lord of this tower, seeing this is a *time of peace*? Might not this money be given to the exchangers?" (101:48-49). "While they were at variance one with another they became very slothful, and they hearkened not" — and "the enemy came by night, and broke down the hedge… and broke down the olive trees" (101:50-51). The lord's rebuke: "ought ye not to have… built the tower also, and set a watchman upon the tower… and **not have fallen asleep**, lest the enemy should come upon you? And behold, the watchman upon the tower would have seen the enemy while he was yet afar off" (101:53-54). (This is the presiding covenant's *keep the watch* [Abraham 4:18 — watch what you order; the watchman thread] given its parable: the tower is *the watch*, and the servants lost the vineyard *because they would not build the watch in the time of peace*. Three failures named: they *questioned the cost of vigilance* ["what need… seeing this is a time of peace?" — vigilance feels wasteful when nothing's wrong], they *fell into variance* [contention dissolved the work, §101:6], and they *fell asleep* [the watch unmanned]. The watchman *on the tower* "would have seen the enemy while he was yet afar off" — the whole point of the watch is *foresight*, seeing the threat *afar off* [the project's Foresight & Adjacent-Surfaces discipline; the §82:5 "Watch, for the adversary spreadeth his dominions"]. For the presiding covenant and the project: **the watch must be built in the time of peace**, before the enemy comes — vigilance questioned as wasteful, watch dissolved by internal contention, watchman asleep = the vineyard lost. And then the redemption: "gather… the strength of mine house… and redeem my vineyard" [101:55-56] — which became Zion's Camp [§103/§105]. I flag the keep-the-watch parable as the presiding-covenant landmark of the section.)
- *★★ The inspired Constitution — established to protect agency (101:77-80).* The foundational text on the divine purpose of the Constitution. "The laws and constitution of the people, which I have suffered to be established… should be maintained for the rights and protection of all flesh, according to just and holy principles; that every man may act… **according to the moral agency which I have given unto him**, that every man may be accountable for his own sins… therefore, it is not right that any man should be in bondage one to another. **And for this purpose have I established the Constitution of this land, by the hands of wise men whom I raised up unto this very purpose**" (101:77-80). (God *established* the Constitution — "by the hands of wise men whom I raised up" — and its *purpose* is to protect *agency*: "that every man may act according to the moral agency which I have given unto him." The §93:30-31 [agency is the nature of an eternal intelligence] and §98:5 [the constitutional law is justifiable] reach their political summit: the Constitution is *for agency's sake* — it secures the freedom in which moral agency can operate and accountability can be real, so "it is not right that any man should be in bondage one to another." The chain is exact: God gives moral agency → agency requires freedom → the Constitution secures freedom → therefore the Constitution is God's instrument. For the project's whole agency/stewardship framework: the political order that secures freedom is *sacred precisely because agency is sacred* — the Constitution exists to protect the very moral agency the §58:26 stewardship doctrine runs on. Even spoken to people the constitutional order was *failing* to protect [the mobs in Missouri], the doctrine holds: the *principle* is God's, even when the *practice* fails.)
- *★ The importuning judge — exhaust lawful redress, then God acts (101:81-92).* The path to redress: the parable of the woman and the unjust judge [Luke 18], applied as a *sequence*. "Let them importune at the feet of the judge; and if he heed them not… the governor; and if the governor heed them not… the president; and if the president heed them not, then will the Lord arise… and in his fury vex the nation" (101:86-89). (The §98 "standard of peace" logic applied to *redress*: the Saints are to *exhaust the lawful channels* — judge, governor, president, in order — *before* God acts. The faithful do not skip to vengeance or to divine intervention; they "importune" patiently up the lawful ladder [Luke 18 — "men ought always to pray and not to faint," 101:81], and *only when* every earthly authority has refused does "the Lord arise." This is the presiding covenant's *lawful-channels-first* at the scale of a persecuted people: work the order God established [the Constitution, 101:80], appeal up the chain, and leave the final reckoning to God. And the *purpose* of the importuning is partly mercy: "pray ye… that their ears may be opened… that I may be merciful unto them, that these things may not come upon them" [101:92] — the importuning gives the unjust a *chance to repent* before judgment. For the project: exhaust the lawful, ordered channels before escalating; the patient appeal up the chain is the faithful path, and even the appeal is a mercy to the one appealed to.)
- *The millennium, and "be still and know that I am God" (101:16,23-38).* Woven through: the comfort "**be still, and know that I am God**" [101:16, Ps 46:10 — to a people in chaos, *stillness* and *knowing*]; and the millennial vision — the veil taken off and "all flesh shall see me together" [101:23], the corruptible consumed, "the enmity of all flesh shall cease" [101:26, Isa 11], "Satan shall not have power to tempt any man" [101:28], "no sorrow because there is no death" [101:29], the infant living "as the age of a tree" then "changed in the twinkling of an eye" [101:30-31]. And the disciple's posture: "care not for the body… but care for the soul… seek the face of the Lord always, that in patience ye may possess your souls" [101:37-38, Luke 21:19]. (The persecuted are lifted to see the *end*: the millennial peace where enmity ceases and death is gone — and told, meanwhile, to "be still," care for the *soul* not the body, and "in patience possess your souls." The §97 Zion-the-pure-in-heart and the §76 glory given their *millennial* horizon: this loss is a moment in a story that ends in a transfigured earth where "all flesh shall see me together.")
