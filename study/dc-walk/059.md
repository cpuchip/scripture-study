# D&C 59 — thank the Lord in all things, and the fulness of the earth

**Setting:** **In Zion, Jackson County, Missouri, August 7, 1831** — days after the land of Zion was consecrated and the temple site dedicated. On the very day this revelation came, **Polly Knight** (wife of Joseph Knight Sr.) died — the *first Church member to die in Zion*. Early Saints described the revelation as "instructing the Saints how to keep the sabbath and how to fast and pray." It opens with comfort at a death and unfolds into the Sabbath law, a luminous theology of the *goodness of the earth*, and the naming of ingratitude as the chief offense against God.

**Binding idea:** "Thou shalt thank the Lord thy God in all things" (59:7) — and its inverse: "in nothing doth man offend God… save those who **confess not his hand in all things**, and obey not his commandments" (59:21). Gratitude is the keynote; *ingratitude*, the chief sin. And the Sabbath is given as the rhythm of it — a day to rest, worship, confess, and *rejoice*, keeping "unspotted from the world" (59:9), so that "the fulness of the earth is yours" (59:16).

**Read:** "Blessed are they who have come up unto this land with an eye single to my glory" (59:1); "those that die shall rest from all their labors, and their works shall follow them; and they shall receive a crown in the mansions of my Father" (59:2). "Thou shalt love the Lord thy God with all thy heart, with all thy might, mind, and strength… Thou shalt love thy neighbor as thyself" (59:5-6); the moral law (59:6). "Thou shalt thank the Lord thy God in all things" (59:7). "Thou shalt offer a sacrifice unto the Lord… even that of a broken heart and a contrite spirit" (59:8). "That thou mayest more fully keep thyself unspotted from the world, thou shalt go to the house of prayer and offer up thy sacraments upon my holy day; for verily this is a day appointed unto you to rest from your labors, and to pay thy devotions unto the Most High" (59:9-10); "offer thine oblations and thy sacraments… confessing thy sins unto thy brethren, and before the Lord" (59:12). "This is fasting and prayer, or in other words, rejoicing and prayer" (59:14); "with cheerful hearts and countenances, not with much laughter, for this is sin, but with a glad heart and a cheerful countenance" (59:15). "The fulness of the earth is yours… all things which come of the earth, in the season thereof, are made for the benefit and the use of man, both to please the eye and to gladden the heart; yea, for food and for raiment, for taste and for smell, to strengthen the body and to enliven the soul" (59:16-19); "to be used, with judgment, not to excess, neither by extortion" (59:20). "In nothing doth man offend God… save those who confess not his hand in all things, and obey not his commandments" (59:21). "He who doeth the works of righteousness shall receive his reward, even peace in this world, and eternal life in the world to come" (59:23).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ Ingratitude is the chief offense (59:7, 21).* The most surprising claim of the section: "in **nothing** doth man offend God… **save** those who confess not his hand in all things, and obey not his commandments" (59:21). Two sins, and the first is *ingratitude* — failing to "confess his hand in all things." Paired with the command "thou shalt thank the Lord thy God in all things" (59:7), this elevates *gratitude* to the central spiritual posture: the offense isn't primarily lust or pride or greed but the *failure to see and thank God in everything*. (This reframes the whole moral life around *gratitude*. The ungrateful heart — which takes the good as its due and sees no Giver — is the root offense; from it flow the others. And "his hand in **all** things" is radical: not just the obvious blessings but *all* — the §54:10 tribulation included, the §58:3 "after much tribulation," even Polly Knight's death that very day. To "confess his hand in all things" is to see God *in* the hard providence, not only the easy one. Thanksgiving is not a response to good circumstances; it's a *recognition of God's hand* regardless of circumstance — which is why ingratitude is the deep sin: it's a *blindness to the Giver*.)
- *★ The goodness of the earth — given for joy, not just survival (59:16-20).* This is one of the most *sensuous* and life-affirming passages in scripture. The earth's fulness is given "to please the eye and to **gladden the heart**… for taste and for smell, to strengthen the body and to **enliven the soul**" (59:18-19). Not merely for *use* but for *delight* — beauty, taste, smell, gladness. God made the good things of the earth *to be enjoyed*, "both to please the eye and to gladden the heart." (This is the §49 answer to asceticism at full volume: the created world is not a danger to be renounced but a *gift to be enjoyed* — with the only limit being "with judgment, not to excess, neither by extortion" (59:20). The matter-spectrum's affirmation [§29 "all things spiritual," §49 marriage-ordained]: the physical is *good*, the senses are *God-given*, pleasure rightly ordered is *holy*. The earth is sacramental — it "gladdens the heart" and "enlivens the soul," so enjoying its beauty is itself a form of receiving God's love. And it follows directly from the gratitude command: you can only "thank the Lord in all things" if you *receive* the good things as gifts to be gladdened by, not despised or grasped.)
- *★ The Sabbath: rest, worship, confession — and "rejoicing" (59:9-15).* The Sabbath law is given as the *rhythm* that keeps one "unspotted from the world" (59:9, James 1:27): go to the house of prayer, offer the sacrament, rest, "pay thy devotions," confess sins (59:12). And then a striking redefinition: "this is fasting and prayer, or in other words, **rejoicing** and prayer" (59:14). Fasting *is* rejoicing — the Sabbath's self-denial is *gladness*, not gloom. And the posture: "cheerful hearts and countenances, not with much laughter, for this is sin, but with a **glad heart and a cheerful countenance**" (59:15). (The Sabbath is *joyful sobriety* — glad and cheerful, but not *flippant* [the §43:34 "solemnities of eternity" balanced with cheer]. "Not with much laughter, for this is sin" isn't a ban on joy — it's a ban on *levity*, the frivolous laughter that empties the day of weight. The holy posture is *glad gravity*: a cheerful countenance over a heart that knows the day is sacred. Worship is rejoicing, but reverent rejoicing.)
- *"A broken heart and a contrite spirit" — the new sacrifice (59:8).* The Mosaic sacrifices of animals are replaced by the *inner* sacrifice: "even that of a broken heart and a contrite spirit" (3 Ne 9:20, Christ's own word after the crucifixion ended the old law). What God now wants on the altar is the *heart* — broken, contrite. (The §52:15-16 / §56:18 contrite pattern as *worship*: the sacrifice is no longer something you bring but something you *become* — a heart broken open before God. And it's the Sabbath's center: you go to the house of prayer to lay *that* on the altar.)
- *"Their works shall follow them" (59:2) — the comfort at Polly Knight's grave.* Given the day the first Saint died in Zion, the opening word is *comfort*: "those that die shall rest from all their labors, and their works shall follow them" (Rev 14:13). Death is *rest*, and the faithful's *works go with them* into the next life — nothing of a faithful life is lost at death. (The §51:19 stewardship's reward at the grave: the "faithful, just, and wise steward" enters "the joy of his Lord," and her *works follow her*. To a husband burying his wife in the new Zion, the Lord's first word is that her labors aren't ended but *crowned* — she rests, and her works come with her.)

And **the Sabbath as joyful sobriety** (59:14-15 — "fasting… in other words, rejoicing"; glad and cheerful but "not with much laughter, for this is sin" — reverent gladness). For Michael: the section that says the way you most offend God is *ingratitude* — failing to see His hand in everything — and that the earth's beauty and taste and smell were made *to gladden your heart*; receive them with thanks, and the fulness of the earth is yours.
