# D&C 64 — of you it is required to forgive all men

**Setting:** **Kirtland, Ohio, September 11, 1831.** Joseph was preparing to move to Hiram, Ohio, to resume the translation of the Bible (laid aside during the Missouri journey), and a company of brethren was "earnestly engaged in making preparations" to leave for Zion in October. "At this busy time, the revelation was received" — and its first and longest word is about *forgiveness*: the Saints, fresh from the Missouri tensions (Ezra Booth's apostasy, friction between Edward Partridge and Joseph), are commanded to forgive one another, "lest there remain in them the greater sin."

**Binding idea:** "I, the Lord, will forgive whom I will forgive, but **of you it is required to forgive all men**" (64:10). And the sting that precedes it: "he that forgiveth not his brother his trespasses standeth condemned before the Lord; for there remaineth in him **the greater sin**" (64:9). God reserves to Himself the judgment of *who is forgiven*; of *us* He requires the forgiving of *everyone* — and the one who withholds it carries a sin *greater* than the trespass done to him.

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "Of you it is required to forgive all men" — the asymmetry, and "the greater sin" (64:9-11).* The headline, and one of the sternest mercies in the canon. The structure is an *asymmetry*: "I, the Lord, will forgive whom I will forgive" — *He* retains the judgment of who is finally pardoned, because He alone sees the heart — "but of you it is required to forgive *all men*" (64:10). Your forgiving is not contingent on their repentance, not scaled to the offense, not yours to ration: it is *required*, of *all*. And the reason it's required is the most piercing clause: "he that forgiveth not his brother his trespasses standeth condemned… for there remaineth in him **the greater sin**" (64:9). The unforgiving person's resentment is a *greater* sin than the trespass that provoked it. (This inverts the natural arithmetic of grievance. The wounded party feels himself the lesser sinner — *he* was wronged. But the Lord weighs the *withholding* heavier than the wound: to nurse the offense, to refuse release, is to commit the *greater* sin. This is Matt 6:14-15 [forgive, or be unforgiven] and the unmerciful servant of Matt 18:23-35 [forgiven the ten-thousand-talent debt, he jails his fellow over a hundred pence] — the §42:88 offense law's *interior* side: §42 governed the *procedure* of reconciliation; §64 governs the *heart* that must precede it. And the release valve [64:11] is *handing the judgment back to God*: "let God judge between me and thee" — you forgive not by declaring the other *right* but by surrendering the *verdict* to the only One who can weigh it. Forgiveness is not "you didn't hurt me"; it's "the accounting is God's, not mine." For anyone holding an old wound: the holding is the greater sin, and the way out is to give the judgment up to God.)
- *★ "The Lord requireth the heart and a willing mind" (64:22,34).* Twice in the section the Lord names *what He actually asks for*: "I, the Lord, require the hearts of the children of men" (64:22); "the Lord requireth the heart and a willing mind; and the willing and obedient shall eat the good of the land of Zion" (64:34). Not the outward act alone — the *heart*, and a *willing* mind. (This is the inward-offering thread the walk has tracked from §20:37 [the broken heart] and §59:8,21 [confess His hand / the broken heart sacrifice]: God's requirement reaches *past* compliance to *willingness*. The "willing AND obedient" pairing matters — obedience without willingness is the "dead works" of §22:3, the form without the heart; willingness without obedience is sentiment without fruit. He wants both, and the *heart* is named first. Isa 1:19 "if ye be willing and obedient, ye shall eat the good of the land" — quoted nearly verbatim [64:34]. The whole consecration economy [§42/§51] runs on *this*: a willing heart, not a coerced contribution. And it answers the forgiveness command above — forgiving "all men" is precisely a thing the *heart* must do, not the hands; you cannot perform forgiveness, you must *will* it.)
- *★ "Out of small things proceedeth that which is great" (64:33).* To a tired, busy, harassed little band "laying the foundation of a great work," the encouragement: "be not weary in well-doing… out of small things proceedeth that which is great." (The small-things principle [1 Ne 16:29 "by small means the Lord… bringeth about… great things"; Alma 37:6-7 "by small and simple things are great things brought to pass"]: the kingdom is built not by grand strokes but by the unwearied accumulation of small faithful acts. The foundation-laying is *unglamorous* — stores not sold, farms kept, debts avoided, the daily errands of §64:26-30 — and the Lord names it *the foundation of a great work*. For the unseen laborer [the §62:3 thread]: your small faithful thing is not small to God; it's the seed of the great. "Be not weary" [Gal 6:9] — the temptation in foundation-work is *weariness*, the long middle with no visible result; the answer is the promise that the small *proceeds into* the great.)
- *"As ye are agents, ye are on the Lord's errand" (64:29) — the agency doctrine cast as errand.* The §58:26 agency doctrine recurs, now in a striking phrase: "as ye are *agents*, ye are on the Lord's errand; and whatsoever ye do according to the will of the Lord is the Lord's business." (The steward's agency [§58, the project-source] reframed: the agent acts on his own initiative *and* is "on the Lord's errand" — the two are not in tension. When the agent acts "according to the will of the Lord," his free act *is* the Lord's business being done. This is the exact resolution of the autonomy paradox the workspace lives in: a steward acting of his own free will within granted intent is not freelancing — he's "on the Lord's errand," and his self-directed work *is* his principal's work. Agency and errand fused: the trusted agent doesn't wait to be told each move [§58:29], he acts, and the acting is the errand.)
- *Today is the day; the church as judge of nations (64:23-25,37-43).* Two more threads: (1) the *urgency of "today"* — "now it is called today until the coming of the Son of Man… a day of sacrifice, and a day for the tithing of my people; for he that is tithed shall not be burned" (64:23); "if ye believe me, ye will labor while it is called today" (64:25, John 9:4). The first canonical mention pointing toward *tithing* (fully revealed §119); the "today" of opportunity that closes at the burning (§64:24). (2) Zion *as judge and ensign* — "my church… like unto a judge sitting on a hill… to judge the nations" (64:37); "she shall be an ensign unto the people, and there shall come unto her out of every nation under heaven" (64:42, Isa 2:2-3 / 11:12). Even the bishop, "who is a judge," is held to faithfulness "in their stewardships" or "others shall be planted in their stead" (64:40) — the stewardship-accountability the project knows well.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. **Walk's contribution:** **§64 gives the forgiveness law in its sternest, most merciful form (64:9-11)** — "**of you it is required to forgive all men**," and the one who withholds it "standeth condemned… for there remaineth in him **the greater sin**": the asymmetry is exact — God reserves the judgment of *who* is forgiven (He alone sees the heart), but requires of *us* the forgiving of *everyone*, un-rationed and not contingent on their repentance; and the *withholding* is weighed *heavier than the wound* (inverting the grievance-arithmetic — the offended party feels the lesser sinner, but the resentment is "the greater sin"). The release is *handing the verdict back to God*: "let God judge between me and thee" (64:11) — you forgive not by calling the other right but by surrendering the accounting to the only One who can weigh it (the §42:88 offense law's interior side). Plus **"the Lord requireth the heart and a willing mind" (64:22,34)** — God asks *past* the act for the willing heart (the inward-offering thread, §20:37/§59:8; willing AND obedient, against "dead works"). Plus **"out of small things proceedeth that which is great" (64:33)** — the unwearied foundation-work, the small faithful thing as the seed of the great (be not weary; the unseen laborer is building the kingdom). And the **agency-as-errand** (64:29 — "as ye are agents, ye are on the Lord's errand," the autonomy paradox resolved: the self-directed act within intent *is* the principal's business). For Michael: the section that requires you to forgive *everyone* — and names the refusal "the greater sin," heavier than the wrong done to you — with the way out being not to declare the offender innocent but to give the judgment up to God; and the quiet promise to a tired band laying an unglamorous foundation that "out of small things proceedeth that which is great."
