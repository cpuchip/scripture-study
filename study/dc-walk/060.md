# D&C 60 — bury not the talent

**Setting:** **Independence, Jackson County, Missouri, August 8, 1831** — the day after the Sabbath revelation (§59). The elders who had journeyed to Missouri and witnessed the dedication of the land and temple site wanted to know what to do next — specifically, how to get home. The Lord gives travel directions, and through them a *rebuke*: some of these elders had crossed a thousand miles and *would not open their mouths to preach*, hiding the gift God gave them out of fear of man.

**Binding idea:** "With some I am not well pleased, for they will not open their mouths, but they hide the talent which I have given them, **because of the fear of man**" (60:2). The agency doctrine of §58 (the passive servant is damned) made personal: these elders *buried the talent* (Matt 25:25). "Thou shalt not idle away thy time, neither shalt thou bury thy talent that it may not be known" (60:13). And the means of the journey is left to *their* judgment — "as seemeth you good, it mattereth not unto me" (60:5) — agency, again.

**Read:** "It pleaseth me, that you have come up hither; but with some I am not well pleased, for they will not open their mouths, but they hide the talent which I have given them, because of the fear of man. Wo unto such, for mine anger is kindled against them" (60:1-2). "If they are not more faithful unto me, it shall be taken away, even that which they have" (60:3). "Let there be a craft made, or bought, as seemeth you good, it mattereth not unto me… take your journey speedily for the place which is called St. Louis" (60:5). The leaders preach "without wrath or doubting" (60:7). "Thou shalt not idle away thy time, neither shalt thou bury thy talent that it may not be known" (60:13); preach "not in haste, neither in wrath nor with strife" (60:14); shake off the dust "not in their presence, lest thou provoke them, but in secret" (60:15).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ Hiding the talent from fear of man (60:2) — §58's passive servant made personal.* §58:29 named the failure mode in the abstract: "he that doeth not anything until he is commanded… keepeth it with slothfulness, the same is damned." §60:2 names *who did it*: elders who "will not open their mouths, but… hide the talent… **because of the fear of man**." They had the gift; they buried it (Matt 25:25, the wicked-and-slothful servant who hid his lord's money in the earth). And the reason is the recurring sin: *fear of man* (§3:7 Joseph, §30:1 David). The §58 agency doctrine and the fear-of-man thread converge here: *fear of man is what makes a steward bury his talent instead of being "anxiously engaged."* (And the consequence is Matthew 25's exactly: "it shall be taken away, even that which they have" [60:3] — the buried talent is *removed*. The gift unused is the gift forfeited. For any steward: the agency you won't exercise from fear is the agency you lose. Passivity isn't safe; it's *subtractive* — what you won't risk using is taken.)
- *★ "As seemeth you good, it mattereth not unto me" (60:5) — the agency doctrine in a real decision.* On the practical question of *how* to travel — build a boat or buy one — the Lord says: "as seemeth you good, **it mattereth not unto me**." He doesn't command the means; He leaves it to their judgment. (This is §58:26 in a concrete case: "it is not meet that I should command in all things." On matters of *wisdom and means* — not principle — God *deliberately declines to specify*, handing the choice to the steward's agency [the footnote even tags 60:5 "Agency/Initiative"]. The irony is sharp beside 60:2: the same elders who *wouldn't act* on the thing God *did* command [open your mouths] are now *given* a thing to decide for themselves [the craft]. The lesson cuts both ways — don't bury the talent God gave [act on the command], *and* don't wait for a command where God left it to your judgment [exercise the agency]. "It mattereth not unto me" is the Lord trusting His stewards with the means.)
- *Preach "without wrath… not in haste, neither in wrath nor with strife" (60:7,14).* The repeated qualifier on the preaching: *no contention*. Even the dust-shaking testimony against rejecters is to be done "**not in their presence, lest thou provoke them, but in secret**" (60:15) — the witness against rejection is *not* a public confrontation but a private act before God. (The §52 meek/edifying pattern and the §28:11/§42:88 private-correction principle applied to *missionary* manner: declare boldly but *without wrath*; even the act of testimony-against is done privately, so as not to *provoke*. The opposite of the combative preacher — the elder opens his mouth [against 60:2's fear] but speaks *without strife*. Boldness and gentleness together: fearless to speak, careful not to provoke.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. **Walk's contribution:** **§60 makes §58's agency doctrine personal** — some elders crossed a thousand miles and *would not open their mouths*, hiding "the talent which I have given them, **because of the fear of man**" (60:2): the passive servant of §58:29 named, the buried talent of Matthew 25, the recurring fear-of-man (§3:7/§30:1) exposed as *what makes a steward bury his gift*. The sharp irony: the same elders who *wouldn't act* on what God commanded are *given* what to decide for themselves — act on the command, *and* exercise the agency. For Michael: the section that shows what burying the talent looks like — an elder too afraid of men to open his mouth — and that the gift you won't risk using from fear is the gift you *lose*; while the means God leaves to your judgment ("it mattereth not unto me") is the agency you're meant to exercise.
