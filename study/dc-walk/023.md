# D&C 23 — five men, "under no condemnation"

**Setting:** **Manchester, New York, April 1830** — five short revelations, one each to **Oliver Cowdery, Hyrum Smith, Samuel H. Smith, Joseph Smith Sr., and Joseph Knight Sr.**, who each "earnestly desired to know" his duty. Seven verses; each man gets a sentence or two. A snapshot of the earliest Church assigning its first laborers.

**Binding idea:** To four of the five the Lord says the same striking thing — "thou art under no condemnation" — and then gives each a *specific* duty (preach, exhort, strengthen, take up your cross). Reassurance first, assignment second: they are told they stand clean *before* they are told what to do.

**Read:** To Oliver: "thou art blessed, and art under no condemnation. But beware of pride, lest thou shouldst enter into temptation" (23:1); "make known thy calling… and thy heart shall be opened to preach" (23:2). To Hyrum: "thou also art under no condemnation, and thy heart is opened, and thy tongue loosed; and thy calling is to exhortation, and to strengthen the church… and this because of thy family" (23:3). To Samuel: "thou also art under no condemnation… and thou art not as yet called to preach before the world" (23:4). To Joseph Sr.: "thou also art under no condemnation… thy calling also is to exhortation, and to strengthen the church" (23:5). To Joseph Knight: "thou must take up your cross… pray vocally before the world as well as in secret… it is your duty to unite with the true church" (23:6-7).

**Thoughts:**
- *"Under no condemnation" — clean standing precedes the call (23:1,3,4,5).* The repeated phrase is the section's gift. Before any of these men is handed work, he's told where he *stands*: forgiven, clear, "under no condemnation." The Lord assigns from a position of acceptance, not probation — you don't earn clean standing by the labor; you labor *from* clean standing. (Contrast the world's order: prove yourself, then be accepted. The gospel's order: be accepted, then serve. The §6:20 "arms of my love" as the *ground* of the call.)
- *Oliver alone gets a warning (23:1).* Four men are "under no condemnation" full stop; Oliver gets "but beware of pride, lest thou shouldst enter into temptation." The Lord calibrates — Oliver, the second elder, the most prominent of the five, is the one warned about pride. The reassurance is real *and* the specific danger is named. (Tragically prescient: Oliver's later estrangement [1838] turned partly on exactly this — a pride that couldn't submit. The Lord named the fault eight years early; the naming didn't compel the avoiding. I note this as documented biography, bin-4-adjacent, Michael's to weigh.)
- *Calibrated callings — including "not yet" (23:4).* Each duty fits the man: Hyrum to exhort and strengthen "because of thy family" (his temperament and household); Samuel to strengthen but "not as yet called to preach before the world" (a *delayed* commission — readiness honored, the §11:15 "until you are called"); Joseph Knight, the generous outsider, to "take up your cross" and finally "unite with the true church" (he hadn't yet been baptized). The same desire-to-know-my-duty yields five *different*, fitted answers — the opposite of the §15-16 verbatim twins, and the complement to them: the *most-worth* thing is universal (bring souls), but the *specific assignment* is bespoke.
