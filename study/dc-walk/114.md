# D&C 114 — others shall be planted in their stead

**Setting:** **Far West, Missouri, April 11, 1838** — to **David W. Patten**, of the Quorum of the Twelve, instructing him to settle his affairs for a spring mission "in company with others, even twelve." (History adds a poignant note: Patten would be killed at the Battle of Crooked River months later, in October 1838 — the spring mission never came; §118 would name his replacement.)

**Binding idea:** "Inasmuch as there are those among you who deny my name, others shall be planted in their stead and receive their bishopric" (114:2). The work does not depend on any man's faithfulness — those who fall away are *replaced*; the office outlasts the apostate.

**Read:** "It is wisdom in my servant David W. Patten, that he settle up all his business as soon as he possibly can… that he may perform a mission unto me next spring, in company with others, even twelve including himself, to testify of my name" (114:1). "Inasmuch as there are those among you who deny my name, others shall be planted in their stead and receive their bishopric" (114:2).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ The office outlasts the apostate (114:2).* Amid the Kirtland-and-Far-West apostasy [a third of the Twelve fell in 1837-38], the Lord states a structural mercy: "those among you who deny my name, **others shall be planted in their stead** and receive their bishopric" (Acts 1:20 — Judas replaced by Matthias; 1 Sam 2:35 — a faithful priest raised up for an unfaithful house). (The §81 Gause→Williams and §107:99 thread reaching the Twelve: the *calling* does not fall with the *man*. When an apostle apostatizes, the work is not crippled — "others shall be planted in their stead." The office is what God revealed, not the individual; the apostate's fall *vacates* a seat that is simply *filled* by another. For the project's stewardship: no role is so important that one steward's failure can destroy it — the structure is built so that "others shall be planted." This is the §101 "watch kept" institutionalized: the kingdom is resilient to the loss of any single laborer because the *office* persists. And the irony bracketing Patten himself: he was *not* among the fallen — he died *faithful*, the first apostle-martyr — yet his seat too was filled [§118]; the planting-in-their-stead applies to the *fallen* and the *martyred* alike — the work goes on.)
