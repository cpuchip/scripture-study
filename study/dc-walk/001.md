# D&C 1 — the Lord's preface: a voice to all the world, spoken in the language of the weak

**Setting:** November 1, 1831, a special conference of elders at Hiram, Ohio. The elders had gathered to decide on publishing the accumulated revelations (the Book of Commandments). The Lord gave this section as **His own preface** to that book — not Joseph's introduction, but the Lord's. So the D&C opens with God prefacing God's own book.

**Binding idea:** The preface to a book of revelations given through one unlearned farm boy is addressed to **the whole earth** — and it tells you, up front, *how* the revelations were given: "in their weakness, after the manner of their language, that they might come to understanding" (1:24). The opening section is both the widest possible address and a little theology of how revelation works.

**Read:** "Hearken, O ye people of my church… Hearken ye people from afar; and ye that are upon the islands of the sea" (1:1) — "the voice of the Lord is unto all men, and there is none to escape" (1:2). The voice of warning goes "unto all people, by the mouths of my disciples" (1:4); the rebellious will have "their secret acts… revealed" (1:3). The Lord, "knowing the calamity which should come," called Joseph Smith (1:17), so that "the weak things of the world shall come forth and break down the mighty… that man should not… trust in the arm of flesh" (1:19), "that mine everlasting covenant might be established" and "the fulness of my gospel… proclaimed by the weak and the simple" (1:22-23). Then the key: "these commandments are of me, and were given unto my servants in their weakness, **after the manner of their language, that they might come to understanding**" (1:24) — and the growth that follows: erring made known (1:25), wisdom-seekers instructed (1:26), sinners chastened to repent (1:27), "and inasmuch as they were humble they might be made strong… and receive knowledge from time to time" (1:28). The Church is "the only true and living church… speaking unto the church collectively and not individually" (1:30); "I the Lord cannot look upon sin with the least degree of allowance; **nevertheless**, he that repents… shall be forgiven" (1:31-32). "Search these commandments, for they are true and faithful" (1:37); "though the heavens and the earth pass away, my word shall not pass away… **whether by mine own voice or by the voice of my servants, it is the same**" (1:38); "the Spirit beareth record, and the record is true, and the truth abideth forever and ever" (1:39).

**Thoughts:**
- *The widest address in scripture opens the most American-frontier of books.* These revelations were given in farmhouses in Ohio and Missouri, to a handful of mostly unlettered men — and the Lord's preface speaks to "all men," "people from afar," "the islands of the sea," "all flesh," "kings and rulers." The scale is deliberate and is itself an argument: the smallness of the instrument (a 25-year-old farmer) against the size of the audience (the earth) is the point of verses 19-23 — *the weak things break the mighty so that no one trusts the arm of flesh.* God chose a weak instrument precisely so the reach couldn't be credited to the instrument.
- *★ 1:24 is the scriptural root of period-language reading — and it landed on the walk an hour after we named the principle.* "Given unto my servants… **after the manner of their language, that they might come to understanding.**" God does not dictate in some perfect heavenly idiom; He accommodates His word to the *language and weakness of the receiver*, so the receiver can understand. This is the condescension of revelation — and it is exactly why reading scripture *in the language-register it was given in* (Joseph's 1830s English, the 1828 sense of a word) recovers the meaning: the Lord spoke into that frame on purpose. The footnote ties it to Nephi's identical phrase — "after the manner of their language, that they might come to understanding" (2 Ne 31:3) — and to Ether 12:39 and D&C 67:5. Three witnesses that revelation is *accommodated*, not absolute, in its wording. (This is the textual floor under the principle Michael articulated and we just saved: read in the era's own language because God spoke in the era's own language.)
- *1:24-28 is a model of how revelation and growth actually work.* It's not "perfect words handed down." It's: given in weakness and human language (24) → errors surface and are made known (25) → the wisdom-seeker is instructed (26) → the sinner is chastened toward repentance (27) → the humble are made strong "and receive knowledge from time to time" (28). That last phrase — *from time to time* — is line-upon-line built into the preface. The revelations admit their own human medium ("inasmuch as they erred it might be made known") rather than claiming verbal inerrancy. That's a remarkable thing for a book to say about *itself* on page one: these came through weak men in their own language, and where they erred it would be shown. Honest scripture about the nature of scripture.
- *★ 1:38 — "whether by mine own voice or by the voice of my servants, it is the same."* The prophetic-word principle, stated as the preface's near-final word. The Lord's voice and the authorized servant's voice carry the same weight (footnote → TG "Sustaining Church Leaders," and pairing with 1:14 — rejecting "the voice of his servants" is rejecting the Lord). For *this* project this is a load-bearing text: it is the delegation/stewardship pattern at its source — the servant who speaks in the Lord's name *is* the Lord's voice (cf. Abr 2:6, "a minister to bear my name"; the whole intent-over-instruction frame). The authority flows down through the chain of servants and is "the same" at each link — which is precisely the presiding chain the workspace's covenant describes.
- *Idolatry as self-worship (1:16).* "Every man walketh in his own way, and after the image of his own god, whose image is in the likeness of the world." Not idols of wood and stone — the idol is *the self projected*, "the image of his own god" made "in the likeness of the world." Modern idolatry named in 1831: worshipping a god shaped like yourself and your world. "Whose substance is that of an idol, which waxeth old and shall perish" — set against 1:39's "the truth abideth forever and ever." The idol's substance perishes; truth's substance endures. (A matter-spectrum echo: truth is the enduring substance, the idol is the perishing one.)
- *"The only true and living church… collectively and not individually" (1:30) — note the built-in humility.* The exclusivity claim is real ("the only true and *living* church" — *living* = receiving revelation, an Article-9 church, not a creedal-dead one). But the verse immediately qualifies its own praise: the Lord is "well pleased… speaking unto the church *collectively and not individually*." The pleasure is with the body, not a blanket endorsement of every member. The preface that makes the boldest claim also builds in the disclaimer — the Church is true; that doesn't make each Saint pleasing. Honest.
- *The nevertheless at the hinge (1:31-32).* "I the Lord cannot look upon sin with the least degree of allowance; **nevertheless**, he that repents… shall be forgiven." The project's `nevertheless` principle, in the preface — the word at the turn between God's holiness (which cannot tolerate sin at all) and His mercy (which forgives the repentant). The two are held in one sentence by that single word; the holiness is absolute *and* the mercy is real, and "nevertheless" is the hinge that keeps both true.

**Critical analysis / tensions named:**
- *The book prefaces itself by admitting its human medium (1:24-25).* A skeptic reads "given… in their weakness… inasmuch as they erred" as the text hedging. The faithful reading takes it as honesty: the D&C does not claim verbal inerrancy; it claims that God's *intent* came through accommodated human language and that errors of wording could be corrected (the revelations *were* edited between the 1833 Book of Commandments and the 1835 D&C — the text's own statement anticipates that). Naming it: 1:24 is the Restoration's doctrine of how revelation works (intent through accommodated language), not a loophole.
- *"None to escape… the voice of warning" (1:2-4) vs. agency.* The universality of the *warning* doesn't override agency — "all that will hear may hear" (1:11). The voice reaches all; the hearing is chosen. The cutting-off (1:14) is of those who "will not hear," not of the unreached. The preface holds universal address and individual agency together.
- *Dating/provenance note:* this preface (Nov 1831) was given *for* the publication and placed *first* — so section 1 is chronologically later than many sections it introduces (e.g., D&C 3, 1828). The D&C's numbering is roughly chronological but the preface is positional, like Moses 1 prefacing Genesis. Worth holding through the walk: section order ≈ revelation order, but not strictly.
