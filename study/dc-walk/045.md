# D&C 45 — the Advocate's prayer, and the place of safety

**Setting:** **Kirtland, Ohio, March 7, 1831.** False reports and "foolish stories" were being circulated to keep people from the Church; Joseph records this revelation came "to the joy of the Saints." It is a sweeping vision: Christ as our **Advocate** pleading before the Father, then His own *expanded* retelling of the Olivet discourse (Matthew 24, which He gave "as I stood before them in the flesh"), the signs of His coming, His appearance to the Jews who recognize Him by His wounds, the Millennium, and the gathering of **Zion as a place of safety**. The Lord also commands the New Testament translation (the JST continues).

**Binding idea:** Two pictures anchor the section. First, the **Advocate**: "Listen to him who is the advocate with the Father, who is pleading your cause before him" — and we hear the actual plea: "Father… spare these my brethren that believe on my name, that they may come unto me and have everlasting life" (45:3-5). Second, **Zion**: in a world dissolving into war, the New Jerusalem will be "a city of refuge, a place of safety… the only people that shall not be at war one with another" (45:66,69). The Atonement pleads us *to* Christ; Zion gathers us *into* safety.

**Read:** "Listen to him who is the advocate with the Father, who is pleading your cause before him—saying: Father, behold the sufferings and death of him who did no sin, in whom thou wast well pleased; behold the blood of thy Son which was shed… Wherefore, Father, spare these my brethren that believe on my name, that they may come unto me and have everlasting life" (45:3-5). Christ is "Alpha and Omega… a light that shineth in darkness" (45:7); "unto as many as received me gave I power… to become the sons of God" (45:8). The Olivet discourse: "as ye have asked of me concerning the signs of my coming…" (45:16); Jerusalem's temple "thrown down" (45:18-21); "men's hearts shall fail them" (45:26); "the love of men shall wax cold" (45:27); the fulness of the gospel breaks forth but "they receive it not… because of the precepts of men" (45:28-29); "a desolating sickness shall cover the land. But my disciples shall stand in holy places, and shall not be moved" (45:31-32). "Be not troubled, for, when all these things shall come to pass, ye may know that the promises which have been made unto you shall be fulfilled" (45:35). The Jews "shall look upon me and say: What are these wounds in thine hands and in thy feet? Then shall they know that I am the Lord… I am Jesus that was crucified" (45:51-52); "then shall they weep… because they persecuted their king" (45:53). The ten virgins: "they that are wise and have received the truth, and have taken the Holy Spirit for their guide, and have not been deceived… shall abide the day" (45:57). The New Jerusalem: "a land of peace, a city of refuge, a place of safety for the saints" (45:66); "the only people that shall not be at war one with another" (45:69); "every man that will not take his sword against his neighbor must needs flee unto Zion for safety" (45:68); the righteous come "singing with songs of everlasting joy" (45:71). "Translate [the New Testament]… that ye may be prepared for the things to come" (45:60-61).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ The Advocate's prayer — we hear the actual words (45:3-5).* The Advocate doctrine (1 John 2:1; named at §29:5, §32:3) is here made *vivid*: we are given the *content* of Christ's plea before the Father. And the plea's logic is the Atonement itself — He doesn't argue our merits; He presents *His own*: "behold the sufferings and death of him who did no sin… behold the blood of thy Son which was shed" (45:4). The basis of the appeal is not what *we* have done but what *He* suffered. "Wherefore, Father, **spare** these my brethren" (45:5) — the verb is mercy, asked on the ground of His wounds. (This is the most intimate Christology in the early D&C: the Son standing before the Father, holding up His own blood, pleading *for us by name* ["my brethren that believe on my name"]. The §19 first-person Atonement [what it cost] and §45 the first-person Advocacy [what He does with the cost] — He suffered it, and now He *pleads* it. Our standing before God is not our record but Christ's wounds presented on our behalf.)
- *★ The wise virgins = "have taken the Holy Spirit for their guide, and have not been deceived" (45:57).* This is the section's interpretive gift, and it ties the whole walk's discernment thread to the ten virgins. The *oil* in the lamp (Matt 25) is *the Holy Spirit taken as guide* — accumulated, personal, non-transferable (§33:17, the oil that can't be borrowed). And the wise are defined *also* by what they avoided: "have **not been deceived**." So readiness for the coming is *the Spirit-as-guide plus discernment* — exactly the §28/§43 order-against-deception (the false revelators), the §8:2/§9:8 felt-witness, the §36:2 peaceable signature. The ten virgins is a parable about *who took the Spirit seriously enough to be guided and not fooled*. (This is the discernment thread reaching its eschatological stakes: in a day of "false reports and foolish stories" [the section's setting] and "precepts of men" [45:29], the ones who "abide the day" are those who took the Holy Spirit as their guide and *were not deceived*. The whole period-language/Spirit-distillation discipline — distill the true, reject the counterfeit — is the oil in the wise virgin's lamp.)
- *★ Zion as "a place of safety" — the only non-warring people (45:66-71).* The gathering-as-refuge (§37) reaches its fullest statement. As the world fills with war ("they will take up the sword, one against another," 45:33; "wars… even at your doors," 45:63), the New Jerusalem becomes "a city of refuge, a place of safety… **the only people that shall not be at war one with another**" (45:69). And the magnet is not force but *peace itself*: "every man that will not take his sword against his neighbor must needs flee unto Zion for safety" (45:68). Zion draws the *peace-seekers* of every nation. (This is the §38:27 "be one" people as *refuge* — the economic-and-relational oneness [no robes/rags, no war] becomes the one safe place when the world tears itself apart. The "be one" is not only the *terms* of Zion but its *protection*: a people genuinely at peace internally is the refuge for everyone who refuses to take up the sword. And it's evangelistic by *contrast* — "the inhabitants of Zion are terrible" [45:70] not by arms but by being *unconquerable in their peace*. The wicked "cannot stand" against a people who simply will not war.)
- *"What are these wounds in thine hands and in thy feet?" (45:51-52).* The dramatic recognition: at His coming, the Jews — who awaited a Messiah and crucified the One who came — see the *wounds* and ask. And He answers by *identity*: "These wounds are the wounds with which I was wounded in the house of my friends [Zech 13:6]. I am he who was lifted up. I am Jesus that was crucified. I am the Son of God." The recognition is *by the wounds* — the marks of suffering are the proof of identity. (The §6:37 "behold the wounds which pierced my side" from the other end: there Christ shows His wounds to convince a disciple; here He shows them to a nation that pierced Him. The wounds are how He is *known* — the risen Christ keeps the marks, and they are His credential. The Atonement is not erased by the Resurrection; it is *carried* into glory as identity.)
- *"Be not troubled… ye may know that the promises… shall be fulfilled" (45:35).* Amid the catalog of desolations, the pastoral word: the signs are not for *fear* but for *confidence* — "be not troubled," for the very coming-to-pass of these things *confirms the promises*. The disciple reads the world's unraveling not as abandonment but as *fulfillment* — "my disciples shall stand in holy places, and shall not be moved" (45:32). (The signs are for the faithful a *reassurance*, not a terror: the same events that make the wicked's "hearts fail them" [45:26] tell the watching disciple that God is keeping His word. Standing in "holy places" — gathered, covenant-keeping — is how you read the chaos as promise rather than threat.)
