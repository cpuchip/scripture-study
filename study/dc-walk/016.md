# D&C 16 — the same word to a second brother

**Setting:** **Fayette, New York, June 1829** — to **Peter Whitmer Jr.**, the youngest of the Whitmer sons present, asking the same question his brothers David (§14) and John (§15) asked. Peter Jr. also became one of the **Eight Witnesses**. Six verses — and they are **word-for-word identical to §15**, with only the name changed ("my servant Peter" for "my servant John").

**Binding idea:** Same as §15 — "the thing which will be of the most worth unto you will be to declare repentance unto this people, that you may bring souls unto me, that you may rest with them in the kingdom of my Father" (16:6). The doctrine is identical; what's worth most is bringing souls. The *distinct* thing here is the identity itself: the Lord gave Peter the *same* answer, verbatim, as John.

**Read:** "Hearken, my servant Peter, and listen to the words of Jesus Christ, your Lord and your Redeemer" (16:1). The remainder (16:2-6) repeats §15:2-6 exactly: spoken "with sharpness and with power," "that which no man knoweth save me and thee alone," the long-desired most-worth question, and the answer — declare repentance, bring souls, rest with them.

**Thoughts:**
- *★ The verbatim twin (15 ↔ 16) is itself the teaching.* Why would the Lord canonize two identical revelations to two brothers? Because the *sameness is the message.* Peter Jr. asked his own private question ("many times you have desired… that which would be of the most worth unto you," 16:4) — his own, not borrowed — and received the same answer his brother did. The most-worth thing is not bespoke. There is no special, individualized "your unique purpose" hidden from the next person; the highest calling is the *same* for John and Peter and David and Hyrum: **bring souls to Christ.** (This is the §11 "one call issued identically" point made unmistakable — the Lord literally repeats Himself. The gospel's central work is universal; the variety is in the gifts and circumstances, not in the *worth*.) It's also a quiet rebuke to the modern hunger for a unique destiny: the thing of most worth is the *common* thing, and it loses none of its worth by being common.
- *The Whitmer pattern (14-15-16).* Three brothers, three revelations, three callings — David to "assist" (and become a Three-Witness, §17), John and Peter Jr. to "declare repentance" (and become Eight-Witnesses). The Whitmer family is being *gathered into the work as a household*, each son answered personally yet drawn into the same labor. (The family-as-unit-of-the-Restoration pattern — the Smiths, the Whitmers, the Knights — households converted together; cf. the sealing/family theology of §2.)
- *Peter Jr.'s later faithfulness.* Unlike David and John (both of whom left the Church in 1838), Peter Whitmer Jr. remained faithful until his early death in 1836. The same word landed in three brothers; their lives diverged. The call is identical; the *enduring* (15:6/16:6's "rest with them" is contingent on the same faithfulness §14:11 names) is each man's own. (Noted as documented biography, not a verdict on anyone — bin-4-adjacent, Michael's to weigh.)
