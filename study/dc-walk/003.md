# D&C 3 — the work of God can't be frustrated, but the work of men can

**Setting:** Harmony, Pennsylvania, **July 1828** — the *earliest* revelation in the D&C by date. Joseph had pressed the Lord three times (against two refusals) to let Martin Harris take the 116 manuscript pages (the book of Lehi) to show his skeptical family; Harris lost them. This is the Lord's response — Joseph's **first recorded rebuke**, given through the Urim and Thummim, and it cost him the gift to translate "for a season." (Resolved in section 10.)

**Binding idea:** God's purposes cannot be frustrated — only men's can (3:1,3). A prophet with "many revelations" can still fall if he "boasts in his own strength" and "fears man more than God" (3:4,7). The section that rebukes the prophet by name also renews his call — discipline and restoration in one breath — and ends "**nevertheless, my work shall go forth**" (3:16).

**Read:** "The works, and the designs, and the purposes of God cannot be frustrated, neither can they come to naught" (3:1); "God doth not walk in crooked paths… his course is one eternal round" (3:2). "Remember, remember that it is not the work of God that is frustrated, but the work of men" (3:3) — "for although a man may have many revelations, and have power to do many mighty works, yet if he boasts in his own strength, and sets at naught the counsels of God, and follows after the dictates of his own will and carnal desires, he must fall" (3:4). Joseph's specific failure: "you should not have feared man more than God… yet you should have been faithful" (3:7-8). "Thou art Joseph, and thou wast chosen to do the work of the Lord, but because of transgression, if thou art not aware thou wilt fall. But remember, **God is merciful; therefore, repent**… and thou art still chosen, and art again called" (3:9-10). The pages were delivered "into the hands of a wicked man" (3:12, Harris's misuse). Then the turn: "**Nevertheless, my work shall go forth**" (3:16) — the knowledge of the Savior will still reach the remnant of Lehi, "that they may believe the gospel and rely upon the merits of Jesus Christ" (3:16-20).

**Thoughts:**
- *The pride warning (3:4) is the section's enduring word, and it's aimed at the gifted.* "Although a man may have many revelations, and… power to do many mighty works, yet if he boasts in his own strength… he must fall." Spiritual gifts do not make a person safe — they make the fall *higher*. The danger named is precise: boasting in one's *own* strength + setting at naught *God's counsels* + following *one's own will*. That's the formula for a prophet's fall, and the Lord states it to a prophet. (The same warning runs through the project's own frame — "trust not in the arm of flesh," D&C 1:19; counsel-in-council before acting; the presiding covenant's warning against the steward who stops feeling watched.) Revelation received is not the same as righteousness kept.
- *Joseph's sin was the fear of man (3:7).* Not greed, not malice — he "feared man more than God," yielding to Martin Harris's persistence and his family's pressure. The most ordinary sin: wanting the approval (or relief from the disapproval) of people more than faithfulness to God. "Although men set at naught the counsels of God… yet you should have been faithful" (3:7-8) — the failure of others doesn't excuse your own caving. (Connects to D&C 1:38 from the last section: the *fear of man* is precisely losing hold of "the voice of God" in favor of the voice of men.)
- *★ Discipline then restoration, in one breath (3:9-10) — the pattern D&C 121 will name as principle.* "If thou art not aware thou wilt fall. **But remember, God is merciful; therefore, repent**… and thou art still chosen." The Lord reproves Joseph sharply, then immediately renews the call on condition of repentance. This is the first canonical instance of the very pattern D&C 121:43 will later state as law — "reproving betimes with sharpness… and then showing forth afterwards an increase of love." The rebuke is severe *and* the trust is restored; the sharpness serves the restoration. (That this is the source of the **presiding covenant** makes it a forward-link worth holding — D&C 3 *enacts* with Joseph what D&C 121 *articulates* as the terms of righteous correction.)
- *"His course is one eternal round" (3:2) — the unchanging God against the failing man.* God "doth not walk in crooked paths… neither doth he vary from that which he hath said." The contrast structures the section: the man wavers (fears, transgresses, caves), God does not. The loss of the 116 pages couldn't frustrate God's work because God had already provided for it 2,000 years earlier (the small plates, "for a wise purpose," 1 Ne 9:5 — fully expounded in section 10). The straightness of God's course is what *absorbs* the crookedness of men's failures without being deflected by them.
- *"Nevertheless, my work shall go forth" (3:16) — the double-nevertheless.* The section pivots on the word at the hinge: the failure is real and named (3:5-15), *nevertheless* the work proceeds (3:16-20). The Book of Mormon comes forth anyway, to bring the knowledge of the Savior to the remnant of Lehi. Even the rebuke section ends on the redemptive purpose — the discipline is in service of the mission, not the end of it.

**Critical analysis / tensions named:**
- *★ The canon rebukes its own prophet, by name, in its third (and earliest) section.* This is remarkable and worth naming for what it is: the Restoration's book of revelations *opens its chronology* with the Lord chastising Joseph Smith for a serious failure that cost him the translation gift. A fabricated prophet-figure does not canonize his own rebuke. The same honesty as JS—H 1:28 (Joseph's confessed "levity"); the `include_failures` covenant value at the level of scripture itself. The faithful reading: this honesty *is* a witness — God corrects His prophet, and the prophet preserves the correction. (Critically, it also models that prophetic gift and prophetic infallibility are not the same thing — a point the section makes explicit in 3:4.)
- *"Into the hands of a wicked man" (3:12) — Martin Harris.* Strong language for a man who later mortgaged his farm to fund the Book of Mormon's printing and became one of the Three Witnesses. The "wicked" here is situational (his breaking of "the most sacred promises," 3:13), not a final verdict — Harris repented and remained. The walk should hold the harshness as a description of the act, not the man's eternal state (Harris's later faithfulness complicates any flat reading).
