# D&C 135 — ★ the martyrdom: sealed with his own blood

**Setting:** **Carthage, Illinois, June 27, 1844 — about five o'clock p.m.** Joseph Smith and his brother Hyrum, held in Carthage jail on false charges, were shot by an armed mob of 150–200 men with painted-black faces. The announcement (written by John Taylor, who was himself wounded by four balls in the same room) was added to the 1844 edition of the Doctrine and Covenants, "which was nearly ready for publication when Joseph and Hyrum Smith were murdered." It is the book's narrative climax — the end of the affliction the walk has tracked through Liberty Jail (§121–122) and the deep water Joseph "was wont to swim in" (§127).

**Binding idea:** **"To seal the testimony of this book and the Book of Mormon, we announce the martyrdom" (135:1)** — and "The testators are now dead, and their testament is in force" (135:5). The death is not the defeat of the testimony but its *seal*: a testament takes force only at the testator's death (Heb 9:16-17), and Joseph "has sealed his mission and his works with his own blood" (135:3).

**Read:**
- The killing: "They were shot in Carthage jail, on the 27th of June, 1844, about five o'clock p.m., by an armed mob—painted black—of from 150 to 200 persons. Hyrum was shot first and fell calmly, exclaiming: I am a dead man! Joseph leaped from the window, and was shot dead in the attempt, exclaiming: O Lord my God!" (135:1).
- The acclamation: "Joseph Smith, the Prophet and Seer of the Lord, has done more, save Jesus only, for the salvation of men in this world, than any other man that ever lived in it" (135:3); "...has sealed his mission and his works with his own blood; and so has his brother Hyrum. In life they were not divided, and in death they were not separated!" (135:3).
- Going calmly: "I am going like a lamb to the slaughter; but I am calm as a summer's morning; I have a conscience void of offense towards God, and towards all men. I shall die innocent, and it shall yet be said of me—he was murdered in cold blood" (135:4).
- Hyrum's leaf turned at Ether 12: "...If they have not charity it mattereth not unto thee, thou hast been faithful; wherefore thy garments shall be made clean. And because thou hast seen thy weakness, thou shalt be made strong…" (135:5, quoting Ether 12:37). "The testators are now dead, and their testament is in force" (135:5).
- The reward + the seal: "They lived for glory; they died for glory; and glory is their eternal reward" (135:6). "...their innocent blood on the floor of Carthage jail is a broad seal affixed to 'Mormonism' that cannot be rejected by any court on earth… will cry unto the Lord of Hosts till he avenges that blood on the earth" (135:7).

**Thoughts:**

- *★ The death seals the testimony — "their testament is in force" (135:1,5).* The whole announcement turns on a legal-theological pun that is also a deep truth: "To **seal** the testimony… we announce the martyrdom" (135:1), and "The testators are now dead, and their **testament** is in force" (135:5, Heb 9:16-17 — a will takes effect only when the one who made it dies). (The death is not the *interruption* of the work but its *ratification*: Joseph "has sealed his mission and his works with his own blood" (135:3). A testimony held under threat is one thing; a testimony *sealed in blood* is unanswerable — "their innocent blood… is a broad seal affixed to 'Mormonism' that cannot be rejected by any court on earth" (135:7). For the walk's whole affliction arc: this is where it lands. §121:8 said "if thou endure it well, God shall exalt thee on high"; §122:7 said "all these things shall give thee experience"; §127:2 had Joseph "glory in tribulation"; §135 is the *enduring it well* completed — the affliction-curriculum graduates into the seal. The martyr's blood does not *disprove* the work [as the mob intended]; under the testament-logic it *puts the work in force*.)

- *★ "Calm as a summer's morning" — the curriculum metabolized (135:4).* Joseph's own words going to Carthage: "I am going like a lamb to the slaughter; but I am **calm as a summer's morning**; I have a conscience void of offense towards God, and towards all men. I shall die innocent." (This is the §127:2 "deep water is what I am wont to swim in… glory in tribulation" reaching its final form. The man who, three years earlier, had cried "O God, where art thou?" from Liberty Jail [§121:1] now walks to his death *calm* — not because the danger lessened but because the §122 curriculum *worked*: affliction became "second nature" [§127:2], and the one who learned to swim in deep water meets the deepest water calmly. The "conscience void of offense" is the §135:5 "thy garments shall be made clean" already known — the integrity [§124:15] intact to the end. For the project's theology of trial: the evidence that "all these things shall give thee experience" is *true* is a man who meets his murder "calm as a summer's morning." The curriculum's final exam is death, and he passed it calmly.)

- *★ The brothers not divided — integrity to the end (135:3,5).* "In life they were not divided, and in death they were not separated!" (135:3, 2 Sam 1:23, David's lament for Saul and Jonathan). And Hyrum, the morning he went, read Ether 12:37 — "because thou hast seen thy weakness, thou shalt be made strong" — and "turned down the leaf upon it" (135:4). (Hyrum, loved "because of the integrity of his heart" [§124:15], called to the patriarchal office [§118/§124:91], dies beside his brother with a verse about *weakness made strong* marked in his book. The §124 integrity thread and the §118 office-outlasts-the-fallen reaching their human apex: the two brothers, faithful to the end, "not separated." For the walk: the martyrdom is not Joseph alone — it is *two* faithful men, and the integrity that ran through the Nauvoo sections [§124:15,20] is sealed in their shared death.)
