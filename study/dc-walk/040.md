# D&C 40 — the word received with gladness, then rejected

**Setting:** **Fayette, New York, January 6, 1831** — one day after §39 — to Joseph and Sidney. Joseph's history is blunt: "As James [Covel] rejected the word of the Lord, and returned to his former principles and people, the Lord gave… the following revelation." Three verses: the sequel to §39, recording that the man whose heart was "right before me at this time" (39:8) broke his covenant within a day. The whole James Covel arc — §39 the call, §40 the fall — is the parable of the sower told as history.

**Binding idea:** "He received the word with gladness, but straightway Satan tempted him; and the fear of persecution and the cares of the world caused him to reject the word" (40:2). The seed fell on stony, thorny ground — received with *gladness*, choked by *fear* and *worldliness* almost at once. And the canon preserves the failure, naming the cause exactly, leaving the judgment to God.

**Read (the whole section):** "The heart of my servant James Covel was right before me, for he covenanted with me that he would obey my word. And he received the word with gladness, but straightway Satan tempted him; and the fear of persecution and the cares of the world caused him to reject the word. Wherefore he broke my covenant, and it remaineth with me to do with him as seemeth me good. Amen" (40:1-3).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ The parable of the sower, told as history (40:2).* The Lord's own words map exactly onto Matthew 13: Covel "received the word with **gladness**" (Matt 13:20, the stony ground, "anon with joy receiveth it"), but "**straightway** Satan tempted him; and the **fear of persecution** [Matt 13:21, "when tribulation or persecution ariseth… he is offended"] and the **cares of the world** [Matt 13:22, "the care of this world… choke the word"] caused him to reject the word." The parable isn't illustrated; it is *enacted*, and the Lord narrates it in the parable's own terms. Covel is the stony-and-thorny ground in a single man — joy without root, choked by fear and worldliness "straightway." (The sobering lesson: the *gladness* of receiving the word is not the test. Covel received it *gladly*, his heart was *right* — and still fell within a day. The test is not the warmth of the reception but whether there is *root* and whether the thorns are cleared. A glad "yes" can collapse by nightfall.)
- *★ "He covenanted with me… wherefore he broke my covenant" (40:1,3) — the gravity of a broken covenant.* The heading and §39 both stress that Covel *covenanted to obey* — and §40 names the result in covenant terms: "he **broke** my covenant." This is not merely "he changed his mind"; he *entered a covenant and broke it*. The §39 affirmation ("thine heart is now right") was true precisely because of that covenant; the §40 verdict is grave precisely because the covenant was real. (The covenant frame of this whole project — D&C 82:10, "when ye do not what I say, ye have no promise" — enacted in a single man: Covel made the bilateral commitment, and breaking it dissolved the promised "blessing so great as you never have known" [39:10]. The covenant degrades when the covenant-keeper walks away — not as punishment but as the natural lapse of an unkept agreement.)
- *"It remaineth with me to do with him as seemeth me good" (40:3) — judgment reserved to God.* The Lord does not pronounce Covel's final state. The verdict is *withheld* — "it remaineth with me." A man who broke covenant is left in God's hands, not consigned by the text. (The §10:37 "ye cannot always judge" and §29's reserved judgments: even a clear covenant-breaking is *not* a human's to finalize. The canon records the failure and the cause with total honesty, and then *stops* — leaving the soul to the only Judge. include_failures, paired with judgment-reserved: tell the truth about the fall, and don't presume the eternal sentence.)
- *Why preserve this at all? — include_failures (the whole §39-40 pair).* The Restoration's canon spends *two revelations* on a man who failed — a full warm call (§39) and its collapse (§40). It would have been easy to omit; instead it stands as a permanent warning and a permanent honesty. (The same impulse as §3 [Joseph rebuked], §20:5 [the prophet's relapse in the charter], §30 [David swayed]: the canon refuses a highlight reel. A scripture that records, by name, a man whose heart was right and who still fell to fear in a day, is making a claim about the *reality* of the danger — and about a God who tells the truth about His people. For Michael's teaching covenant, this is `include_failures` at scripture-scale: the failures stay in.)
