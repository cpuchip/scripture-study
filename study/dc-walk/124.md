# D&C 124 — ★ Nauvoo: the proclamation, the house, and the offering accepted

**Setting:** **Nauvoo, Illinois, January 19, 1841.** The Saints, expelled from Missouri under Boggs's extermination order, have drained the swampland of Commerce and built a city — Nauvoo, the headquarters of the Church. Into the new beginning comes a long, pivotal revelation: a proclamation to the kings of the earth, the command to build a temple (with baptism for the dead reserved to it), the principle that resolves the wound of Jackson County (the *offering accepted* when the enemy prevents the work), and the calling of Hyrum to the patriarchal office. It is one of the longest sections in the book; the ordinances it names (washings, anointings, baptisms for the dead) are the temple frame beginning to unfold from the §110 sealing keys — reserved-bin-4, Michael's.

**Binding idea:** **"When I give a commandment to any of the sons of men to do a work unto my name, and those sons of men go with all their might and with all they have… and cease not their diligence, and their enemies come upon them and hinder them from performing that work, behold, it behooveth me to require that work no more at the hands of those sons of men, but to accept of their offerings" (124:49).** God accepts the *willing, diligent work the enemy prevents* — the offering, not the unattainable outcome — and lays the guilt on those who hindered. The wound of Jackson County (the unbuilt Zion temple) is here turned to *consolation*: the labor was accepted though it was stopped.

**Thoughts:**

- *★★ The offering accepted when the enemy prevents the work (124:49-53).* This is the doctrinal heart of the section and its strongest reach into the project. The principle is exact and conditional: *if* a person, commanded to a work, "go with **all their might** and with **all they have**… and **cease not their diligence**," *and* enemies "hinder them from performing that work" — *then* "it behooveth me to **require that work no more** at the hands of those sons of men, but to **accept of their offerings**." (God judges the *labor*, not the *result*: where the diligence was total and the failure was *external* (an enemy, an oppression), the offering is *accepted as if completed*, and the guilt of the unfinished work falls "upon the heads of those who hindered" (124:50) — not on the laborer. This *redeems Jackson County* (124:51 — the unbuilt Zion temple, the Saints driven out) by reframing it: their work was *accepted*, the loss was charged to the mob. For the project's whole theology of intent-over-outcome: the steward who labors "with all their might… and cease not their diligence" but is *hindered by what is outside their power* has *not failed* — the offering is accepted. This is the §122 "experience" principle's positive twin and the §117:8 "what is property unto me?" extended to *work*: God does not require the *outcome* He prevented circumstances from allowing; He requires — and accepts — the *whole-souled diligence*. The boundary is sharp and worth keeping: the acceptance is for the one who went "with all their might… and cease not," *not* for the one who slackened and blamed the enemy. Total diligence + external hindrance = offering accepted; that is the test.)

- *★ Baptism for the dead belongs to the house — the sealing keys cashing out (124:28-41).* The §110 Elijah keys begin to *unfold into ordinances*. The temple is commanded not as a meetinghouse but as the *only place* God "may come to and restore again that which was lost… even the **fulness of the priesthood**" (124:28) — and the headline ordinance is *baptism for the dead*: "For a baptismal font there is not upon the earth, that they, my saints, may be baptized for those who are dead— For this ordinance **belongeth to my house**" (124:29-30), "instituted from before the foundation of the world" (124:33). (The whole temple liturgy is named here for the first time — "anointings… washings… baptisms for the dead… solemn assemblies… oracles in your most holy places" (124:39) — as "things which have been kept hid from before the foundation of the world" (124:41). The Malachi/sealing taproot (§2→§13→§27:9→§110) now puts down its *ordinances*: the hearts of the fathers turned to the children *operationally*, through a font. This runs straight forward to §128 (the doctrine of baptism for the dead) and §138 (the vision of the redemption of the dead). Temple-reserved, bin-4 — I flag the frame, I do not unfold it.)

- *★ Integrity is the security on the system — Hyrum, George Miller, and the man who had none (124:15,20,91-97).* The blessings in §124 turn on one trait named twice: **integrity**. Hyrum is loved "because of the **integrity of his heart**" (124:15); George Miller "is without guile; he may be **trusted** because of the **integrity of his heart**" (124:20). (And Hyrum's reward is *office*: he takes "the office of Priesthood and Patriarch" (124:91), becomes "a prophet, and a seer, and a revelator… as well as my servant Joseph" (124:94), standing in the place Oliver Cowdery forfeited (124:95) — the §118 office-outlasts-the-fallen pattern, with *integrity* as the qualifier that fills the seat. The §41 "character is the security on the system" [Edward Partridge, "no guile"] reaffirmed: trust is *earned by integrity*, and the offices go to the men "without guile." But the section also records the blessing offered to a man who would *not* keep it: William Law, counseled "let him be humble before me, and be **without guile**" (124:97) — who would instead apostatize, found the *Nauvoo Expositor*, and help set in motion Joseph's martyrdom. The canon's `include_failures` at its most poignant: the same revelation that rewards Hyrum's integrity *offers* the identical blessing to Law, conditioned on a humility he refused — the blessing was real, the condition unmet [§82:10, "when ye do not what I say, ye have no promise"]. For the project: integrity is what makes a steward *trustable* with an office; the gift is offered to all, but only kept by the guileless.)

- *The proclamation to the kings — testimony without fear (124:1-11).* The first charge is *outward*: proclaim the gospel "to all the kings of the world… fearing them not, for they are as grass" (124:3,7). (The §1:23 voice-to-all and the §123:6 "leave them without excuse" given a *royal address*: the gospel testifies *up* the power structure, to presidents and kings, unintimidated — "they are as grass, and all their glory as the flower thereof which soon falleth." The §122:9 "fear not what man can do" applied to the *mightiest* men; the testimony owed even to those who can destroy you, precisely because their glory is grass.)
