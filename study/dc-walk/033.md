# D&C 33 — open your mouths and they shall be filled

**Setting:** **Fayette, New York, October 1830** — to **Ezra Thayre** and **Northrop Sweet**, two more called to preach. Joseph's history introduces it with a line worth keeping: "the Lord… is ever ready to instruct such as diligently seek in faith." A missionary call, but one of the richest of the short ones — it carries the eleventh-hour urgency, the apostasy diagnosis, and the great promise to the unprepared preacher.

**Binding idea:** "Open your mouths and they shall be filled" (33:8,10) — the promise that the words will be *given* to the one who simply opens his mouth in faith. The field is white "and it is the eleventh hour, and the last time that I shall call laborers into my vineyard" (33:3) — so the call is urgent, the laborers are inadequate, and the answer to inadequacy is *open your mouth and trust the filling*.

**Read:** The Lord's word "is a discerner of the thoughts and intents of the heart" (33:1, Heb 4:12). "Ye are called to lift up your voices as with the sound of a trump… unto a crooked and perverse generation" (33:2). "The field is white already to harvest; and it is the eleventh hour, and the last time that I shall call laborers into my vineyard" (33:3). "My vineyard has become corrupted every whit; and there is none which doeth good save it be a few; and they err in many instances because of priestcrafts, all having corrupt minds" (33:4). "Even so will I gather mine elect from the four quarters of the earth" (33:6). "Open your mouths and they shall be filled, and you shall become even as Nephi of old" (33:8); "open your mouths and spare not, and you shall be laden with sheaves upon your backs, for lo, I am with you" (33:9). "This is my gospel… they shall have faith in me or they can in nowise be saved; and upon this rock I will build my church" (33:12-13). "Remember the church articles and covenants to keep them" (33:14). "Having your lamps trimmed and burning, and oil with you, that you may be ready at the coming of the Bridegroom—for behold… I come quickly" (33:17-18).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "Open your mouths and they shall be filled" (33:8,10) — the promise to the inadequate.* The most enduring line of the section, and one of the most quoted missionary promises in the D&C. Ezra and Northrop are ordinary men called to declare to "a crooked and perverse generation" (33:2) — and the help offered isn't a script but a *promise of supply*: open your mouth in faith and the words will come ("they shall be filled," repeated three times, 33:8-10). This is §24:6 ("it shall be given thee in the very moment what thou shalt speak") as a *general law of faithful speaking*: the filling follows the opening, not the other way round. You don't wait to feel ready and then speak; you speak in faith and are *made* ready in the act. (For the project's discernment thread: this is the §8:2 mind-and-heart revelation in its *spoken* form — the Spirit fills the mouth opened in faith; but note §32:4's guard still holds — the filling unfolds the *written* word, it doesn't license new doctrine. Open your mouth on the gospel, and it is filled; open it to invent, and you're on Hiram Page's ground.)
- *The eleventh hour, and "priestcrafts" as the apostasy's engine (33:3-4).* "It is the eleventh hour, and the last time that I shall call laborers" — the Restoration is the *final* dispensation (Matt 20's eleventh-hour laborers; Jacob 5's last pruning). And the diagnosis of why a Restoration was needed: the vineyard "corrupted every whit… they err… because of **priestcrafts**, all having corrupt minds" (33:4). The apostasy is named not as mere ignorance but as *priestcraft* — religion practiced for gain and status rather than for God (2 Ne 26:29, the BoM walk's sharp word: "priestcrafts are that men preach and set themselves up for a light… that they may get gain"). The counter to priestcraft is precisely the §32:1 / §31:9 *meekness* — preachers who "open their mouths" to declare the word, not to exalt themselves. (The eleventh-hour urgency and the priestcraft diagnosis together: the field is white *because* the existing laborers were corrupt; the new laborers must be meek, or they reproduce the disease.)
- *The lamps and the Bridegroom (33:17-18).* The section closes on the ten virgins (Matt 25): "having your lamps trimmed and burning, and oil with you, that you may be ready at the coming of the Bridegroom—for… I come quickly." Readiness is the note — the *oil* (the Spirit, accumulated by faithfulness) that can't be borrowed at the last moment. (The §27 future supper from the watching side: the sacrament rehearses the feast; the trimmed lamp prepares for the Bridegroom. And it answers the eleventh-hour urgency personally — the hour is late not only for the world's harvest but for each watcher's readiness.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. **Walk's contribution:** **"open your mouths and they shall be filled" (33:8,10)** — the great promise to the inadequate preacher: the filling *follows* the opening, you speak in faith and are made ready in the act (§24:6 generalized) — with the §32:4 guard still holding (the filling unfolds the *written* word, it doesn't license invention). Plus **priestcraft named as the apostasy's engine (33:4)** — the vineyard corrupted not by ignorance but by religion-for-gain; the counter is the meekness §31-32 keep prescribing, or the new laborers reproduce the disease. And **the lamps and the Bridegroom (33:17)** — readiness is *oil that can't be borrowed*, the eleventh-hour urgency made personal. For Michael: the section that tells two ordinary men the words will be *given* if they'll just open their mouths in faith — and warns that the religion they're replacing fell by preaching for gain, so they must stay meek, lamps trimmed, ready.
