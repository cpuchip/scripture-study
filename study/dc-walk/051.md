# D&C 51 — every man equal according to his wants and needs

**Setting:** **Thompson, Ohio, May 20, 1831.** The eastern Saints were arriving in Ohio, and their settlement "belonged particularly to the bishop's office." Bishop **Edward Partridge** sought instruction on *how* to administer the law of consecration (§42) in practice. This revelation is the operational manual — how to distribute stewardships, secure them by deed, keep the storehouse, and hold property "that ye may be one."

**Binding idea:** Consecration distributes "**every man equal according to his family, according to his circumstances and his wants and needs**" (51:3). "Equal" is *need-based*, not identical: each steward receives a *deeded* portion sufficient for his family, the surplus goes to the storehouse for the poor, and "every man… receive alike, **that ye may be one**" (51:9). The §38:27 "be one" becomes a property system that protects both the individual *and* the poor.

**Read:** Edward Partridge is to "organize this people… according to my laws; if otherwise, they will be cut off" (51:1-2). "Appoint unto this people their portions, every man equal according to his family, according to his circumstances and his wants and needs" (51:3). When he appoints a portion, "give unto him a writing that shall secure unto him his portion, that he shall hold it, even this right and this inheritance in the church, until he transgresses" (51:4); a transgressor "shall not have power to claim that portion which he has consecrated unto the bishop for the poor… but shall only have claim on that portion that is deeded unto him" (51:5) — "all things shall be made sure, according to the laws of the land" (51:6). "Let every man deal honestly, and be alike among this people, and receive alike, that ye may be one, even as I have commanded you" (51:9). "Let the bishop appoint a storehouse… and let all things both in money and in meat, which are more than is needful for the wants of this people, be kept in the hands of the bishop" (51:13). "I consecrate unto them this land for a little season, until I, the Lord, shall provide for them otherwise… and the hour and the day is not given unto them, wherefore let them act upon this land as for years" (51:16-17). "Whoso is found a faithful, a just, and a wise steward shall enter into the joy of his Lord, and shall inherit eternal life" (51:19).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "Equal according to his wants and needs" (51:3) — equality is *equitable*, not identical.* The distribution principle is *need-based equality*: each steward gets a portion "according to his family… his circumstances and his wants and needs." A family of eight and a single man do not receive *identical* portions — they receive *equitable* ones, each sufficient for what their household actually needs. (This corrects a flat reading of "be one" as uniformity: the §38:27 oneness is *equity*, not sameness — "receive alike" [51:9] means each is treated *fairly by need*, not each handed the same amount. The §46 gift-economy principle [each receives a *distinct* portion, all profited] applied to property: distinct stewardships, all sufficient, none in want. "From each according to ability, to each according to need" — but voluntarily, by covenant, under a bishop without guile [§41:11], not by coercion.)
- *★ The deed mechanic — protecting both the steward and the poor (51:4-5).* The genius of the system is in the *legal structure*. Each steward gets "a writing that shall secure unto him his portion" (51:4) — a real, legally-binding deed ("according to the laws of the land," 51:6). So the individual is *protected*: his stewardship can't be arbitrarily stripped; it's *his*, deeded. *But* the *consecrated surplus* (what he gave to the bishop for the poor) stays with the church if he leaves (51:5). So the system protects *both*: the steward keeps his deeded portion (no one can confiscate his livelihood), and the poor keep the surplus (no one can withdraw his consecration and impoverish the storehouse). (This is *not* a commune where the individual is dissolved into the collective — the deed makes the stewardship genuinely *his*, with civil-law protection. Consecration honors private stewardship *and* the common good simultaneously, secured by ordinary law. The §44:4 "organize according to the laws of man" enacted: the kingdom's economy runs *through* civil deeds, not around them.)
- *★ "Act upon this land as for years" though it's "for a little season" (51:16-17).* A quietly profound instruction. The Lord consecrates Ohio to them "for a little season… and the hour and the day is not given unto them" — they *don't know how long* they'll be there. And the counsel is: "**act upon this land as for years**." Don't live provisionally, half-packed, treating the temporary as disposable — *build, plant, invest as if it were permanent*, even knowing it isn't. (This is the discipline of the withheld [§25:4, §48:5] applied to *time*: when you don't know the duration, live *fully in the present* rather than holding back. The pilgrim who knows he's a sojourner [§45:13] still *builds*. Faithfulness isn't measured by how provisionally you hold a temporary assignment but by how *fully* you labor in it — "as for years" — trusting the Lord with the timing. A deep word for any steward of a thing whose end is unknown: don't coast because it's temporary; work it as if it were forever, and let God set the term.)
- *"A faithful, a just, and a wise steward… enter into the joy of his Lord" (51:19).* The whole consecration system culminates in the *parable of the talents* (Matt 25:21,23): the reward of stewardship is to "enter into the joy of his Lord." Property is *stewardship*, and stewardship well-kept is the path to "eternal life" (51:19). (The project's stewardship covenant at its source: you don't *own*, you *steward*; and the faithful-just-wise steward inherits the joy. The §42 consecration economy isn't ultimately about property at all — it's a *training in stewardship* toward the joy of the Lord.)
