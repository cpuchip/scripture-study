# D&C 91 — whoso readeth it, let him understand, for the Spirit manifesteth truth

**Setting:** **Kirtland, Ohio, March 9, 1833.** Joseph was translating the Old Testament and "came to that portion of the ancient writings called the **Apocrypha**" — the books between the Testaments, of mixed authority. He inquired whether to translate them, and received this instruction: a short, profound word on how to read a source that is *part true and part not*.

**Binding idea:** "There are many things contained therein that are true… [and] many things… that are not true, which are interpolations by the hands of men… Therefore, whoso readeth it, let him understand, for the Spirit manifesteth truth; and whoso is enlightened by the Spirit shall obtain benefit therefrom; and whoso receiveth not by the Spirit, cannot be benefited" (91:1-6). A mixed source is profitable — *if read by the Spirit*. The Spirit is the filter that separates the true from the interpolated.

**Read:** "Concerning the Apocrypha—There are many things contained therein that are true, and it is mostly translated correctly" (91:1). "There are many things contained therein that are not true, which are interpolations by the hands of men" (91:2). "It is not needful that the Apocrypha should be translated" (91:3). "Whoso readeth it, let him understand, for the Spirit manifesteth truth" (91:4). "Whoso is enlightened by the Spirit shall obtain benefit therefrom" (91:5). "Whoso receiveth not by the Spirit, cannot be benefited" (91:6).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ The Spirit as the filter for a mixed source (91:4-6).* The Lord does *not* say "burn the Apocrypha" [it has "many things… that are true"] nor "trust it" [it has "interpolations by the hands of men"]. He says: *read it by the Spirit*. "Whoso readeth it, let him understand, for the Spirit manifesteth truth" — the Spirit, not the text's pedigree, is what separates gold from dross *within* a source. (This is the project's source-discernment doctrine at its root. Most sources outside the canon — books, articles, talks, the whole world of human writing — are *exactly* the Apocrypha's case: part true, part the "interpolations of men." The Lord's instruction is not to *avoid* them but to *read them by the Spirit*, which "manifesteth truth" and lets the reader "obtain benefit therefrom." The §50/§52 discernment [light edifies / the pattern of the spirits] applied to *texts*: the Spirit-enlightened reader profits from a mixed source by *receiving the true and leaving the false*; the reader "who receiveth not by the Spirit, cannot be benefited" — without the Spirit, even a mostly-true book can't profit you, because you have no way to tell the true part from the interpolation. For the research-gospel discipline [evaluate non-canonical sources under Restoration discernment]: §91 *is* the warrant — engage the mixed source, read by the Spirit, take the truth. The filter is the Spirit, not the imprimatur.)
- *★ "Not needful that it should be translated" — the canon is sufficient, the Spirit is enough (91:3,6).* Notably, the Lord declines to *fix* the Apocrypha by retranslation: "it is not needful." (The point is not that the Apocrypha is worthless but that a Spirit-led reader *doesn't need* it corrected to profit from it — the Spirit does the correcting *in the reading*. This protects against two errors: treating a mixed source as *all* authoritative [it isn't], and treating it as *all* suspect [it isn't]. The Spirit, applied live, is more reliable than any editorial pass — it "manifesteth truth" verse by verse. For a project that reads widely [§88:118 "the best books"; §90:15 "all good books"]: you don't need every source pre-vetted and corrected; you need the Spirit *as you read*, which sorts the true from the false in real time. The canon is the fixed standard; the wider reading is profitable *through the Spirit's live discernment*, not through pre-certification.)
