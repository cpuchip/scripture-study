# D&C 70 — stewards over the revelations

**Setting:** **Hiram, Ohio, November 12, 1831** — the last of four special conferences (Nov 1-12) on publishing the revelations. The conference had just voted that the revelations were "worth to the Church the riches of the whole Earth," and Joseph called them "the foundation of the Church in these last days." This revelation appoints six men — Joseph, Martin Harris, Oliver Cowdery, John Whitmer, Sidney Rigdon, and W.W. Phelps (the "Literary Firm") — as *stewards* over the revelations: to manage their publication, account for them at the judgment, and live the law of consecration on the proceeds.

**Binding idea:** "I, the Lord, have appointed them, and ordained them to be **stewards over the revelations and commandments** which I have given unto them… and **an account of this stewardship will I require of them in the day of judgment**" (70:3-4). The revelations are a *stewardship* — managed, not owned; and the management will be *accounted for*.

**Read:** "I, the Lord, have appointed them, and ordained them to be stewards over the revelations and commandments which I have given unto them, and which I shall hereafter give unto them" (70:3). "An account of this stewardship will I require of them in the day of judgment" (70:4). "This is their business in the church of God, to manage them and the concerns thereof, yea, the benefits thereof" (70:5). "Inasmuch as they receive more than is needful for their necessities and their wants, it shall be given into my storehouse" (70:7). "This is what the Lord requires of every man in his stewardship, even as I, the Lord, have appointed or shall hereafter appoint unto any man" (70:9). "None are exempt from this law who belong to the church of the living God" (70:10). "He who is appointed to administer spiritual things, the same is worthy of his hire, even as those who are appointed to a stewardship to administer in temporal things" (70:12). "In your temporal things you shall be equal, and this not grudgingly, otherwise the abundance of the manifestations of the Spirit shall be withheld" (70:14). "They have been faithful over many things, and have done well inasmuch as they have not sinned" (70:17). "I, the Lord, am merciful and will bless them, and they shall enter into the joy of these things" (70:18).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ The revelations as a stewardship, accounted at the judgment (70:3-5,9-10).* The word the project knows best, applied to the most sacred property the Church had: the revelations are a *stewardship*. The six are "stewards over the revelations… to manage them and the concerns thereof" (70:3,5) — and "an account of this stewardship will I require of them in the day of judgment" (70:4). (The §42/§51 consecration doctrine extended from land and goods to *the word itself*: even scripture-as-property is *received to manage*, not owned, and the managing is *accountable*. "None are exempt from this law" [70:10] — not the steward, not the bishop, not "the agent who keepeth the Lord's storehouse" [70:11]. This is the exact pattern the walk has called the project's own: received-not-owned, managed-on-trust, accountable-at-reckoning [§58:26-28 the agency doctrine; §64:29 agents-on-the-errand; §47/§69 the record]. The revelations were "worth… the riches of the whole Earth," and the men who held them held them as *stewards who will give an account* — the highest trust is still a trust. For anyone handed something sacred to manage: the value of the thing doesn't make it yours; it makes the accounting heavier.)
- *★ "The laborer… is worthy of his hire" — spiritual work deserves support (70:12-13).* "He who is appointed to administer **spiritual** things, the same is worthy of his hire, even as those who… administer in temporal things; yea, even more abundantly" (70:12-13). (Luke 10:7 / 1 Cor 9:11-14 affirmed: those who labor in the word are *worthy of support* — the spiritual laborer no less than the temporal, and his "abundance is multiplied… through the manifestations of the Spirit" [70:13]. Not priestcraft [§33:4 — preaching *for* money, to be seen]; the difference is direction: priestcraft *sets up itself* and seeks gain *as the goal*; this is the *community sustaining* those who give their labor to the word so they're not crushed by want. The §70:7 limit guards it — "more than is needful for their necessities… shall be given into my storehouse"; the laborer is *supported*, not *enriched*. Worthy of his hire, capped at his need.)
- *★ "In your temporal things you shall be equal… otherwise the manifestations of the Spirit shall be withheld" (70:14).* The consecration principle [§51 equal-by-need] restated with a *spiritual consequence*: equality "not grudgingly," or "the abundance of the manifestations of the Spirit shall be withheld." (The startling link: *economic* inequality among the Saints *throttles spiritual gifts*. Holding back from the equal-by-need order [§51], or doing it "grudgingly," doesn't just hurt the poor — it *withholds the Spirit* from the whole body. The §38:27 "be one" given a teeth-of-consequence: the manifestations of the Spirit flow through a *united* people and clog in a grasping one. This is §50:24's light tied to §51's equality — the light grows in the body that shares, and dims in the body that hoards. For the project's economy of any kind: ungenerous distribution isn't only an injustice; it *chokes the very Spirit* the work runs on.)
- *"Faithful over many things… enter into the joy" (70:17-18).* The close is mercy: these men "have been faithful over many things, and have done well inasmuch as they have not sinned… I, the Lord, am merciful and will bless them, and they shall enter into the joy of these things" (Matt 25:21, the good steward). (The stewardship-with-accounting [70:4] doesn't end in dread but in *joy*: "well done… enter thou into the joy of thy lord." The accountable steward who is faithful *enters the joy* — the reckoning is the doorway to the reward, not only the audit. The §51:19 "joy of his Lord" again: faithful stewardship's end is gladness.)
