# D&C 67 — make one like unto it

**Setting:** **Hiram, Ohio, early November 1831** — a special conference to decide on *publishing the revelations* (the Book of Commandments; W.W. Phelps had just set up the press in Independence). After §1 was received as the preface, "some conversation was had concerning the **language** used in the revelations" — that is, some of the brethren criticized Joseph's imperfect grammar, doubting that a true revelation would come in such rough language. This revelation followed: the Lord's answer to the critique of His word's *vessel*, including the famous challenge to *duplicate* a revelation.

**Binding idea:** "Seek ye out of the Book of Commandments, even the least that is among them, and appoint him that is the most wise among you; or, if there be any among you that shall **make one like unto it**, then ye are justified in saying that ye do not know that they are true; **but if ye cannot make one like unto it, ye are under condemnation if ye do not bear record that they are true**" (67:6-8). Don't critique the language — *reproduce* it. The proof of the revelations' divinity is that the wisest among you cannot make even the *least* of them.

**Read:** "Behold and hearken, O ye elders of my church, who have assembled yourselves together, whose prayers I have heard, and whose hearts I know, and whose desires have come up before me" (67:1). "Mine eyes are upon you, and the heavens and the earth are in mine hands, and the riches of eternity are mine to give" (67:2). "Ye endeavored to believe that ye should receive the blessing which was offered unto you; but behold, verily I say unto you there were **fears in your hearts, and verily this is the reason that ye did not receive**" (67:3). "Your eyes have been upon my servant Joseph Smith, Jun., and his **language** you have known, and his **imperfections** you have known; and you have sought in your hearts knowledge that you might express beyond his language; this you also know" (67:5). "Seek ye out of the Book of Commandments, even the least that is among them, and appoint him that is the most wise among you" (67:6). "If there be any among you that shall make one like unto it, then ye are justified in saying that ye do not know that they are true" (67:7). "But if ye cannot make one like unto it, ye are under condemnation if ye do not bear record that they are true" (67:8). "For ye know that there is no unrighteousness in them, and that which is righteous cometh down from above, from the **Father of lights**" (67:9). "Inasmuch as you strip yourselves from jealousies and fears, and humble yourselves before me, for ye are not sufficiently humble, **the veil shall be rent and you shall see me and know that I am**—not with the carnal neither natural mind, but with the spiritual" (67:10). **Thoughts:**
- *★ "Make one like unto it" — the test of the genuine is reproduction, not polish (67:5-9).* This is the central event, and the §1:24 principle's *defense*. The brethren had grown critical of the *language* — Joseph's grammar was rough, his diction unschooled, and "you have sought in your hearts knowledge that you might express beyond his language" (67:5, i.e., *I could say it better*). The Lord's answer is not to deny the imperfection — He *concedes* it: "his language you have known, and his imperfections you have known" (67:5). The vessel *is* flawed. But then the challenge: take the *least* revelation in the book, appoint your *wisest* man, and **make one like it**. If you can, you're "justified in saying… ye do not know that they are true." If you *cannot* — and you cannot — then to withhold your witness is condemnation (67:7-8). (William McLellin, the educated schoolteacher of §66, the "most wise," *tried* — and Joseph's history records he failed. The proof of a revelation's divinity is *not* the elegance of its language but the *substance* that "cometh down from above, from the Father of lights" [67:9]. This is the exact apologetic for §1:24 ["given after the manner of their language… that they might come to understanding"]: God's word arrives *through* an imperfect human instrument and his period diction, and the criticism that fixes on the *roughness of the vessel* misses that the *content* is divine and *irreproducible*. The period-language principle's other half — §1:24 says *read it in the receiver's frame*; §67 says *don't mistake the frame for the message*. You cannot manufacture revelation by improving the prose; the wise man's polished imitation would still be empty, because what makes it true descends from the Father of lights, not from grammar. For a project that works *through* the imperfect language of an AI instrument: the test of the work is never the fluency of the sentences — it's whether the substance is true and came from above. Smooth language can be empty; rough language can carry the light.)
- *★ "Fears in your hearts… this is the reason that ye did not receive" (67:3).* Before the challenge, a diagnosis. The elders had been *offered* a blessing — the staggering one of 67:10-14, to have the veil rent and *see God* — and "ye endeavored to believe that ye should receive… but there were fears in your hearts, and verily this is the reason that ye did not receive." (The fear-forfeits thread the walk has tracked from §3:7 [fear of man], §9:11 [fear forfeits the gift], §30:1-2, §38:30 ["if ye are prepared ye shall not fear"], §60:2 [the buried talent from fear of man]: fear is the *blocker* of revelation and blessing. They *tried* to believe — "endeavored" — but the trying was undercut by the fear underneath. And note the pairing in 67:10: "strip yourselves from **jealousies and fears**" — the two named together as what must go before the veil parts. Jealousy [the McLellin impulse, "I could say it better"] and fear are the twin disqualifiers; humility is the cure ["ye are not sufficiently humble," 67:10]. The blessing wasn't withheld by God's reluctance but by *their* fear — the gift was *offered* and not *received*. A sobering word: sometimes the unreceived blessing is on our side of the veil, not His.)
- *★ "No natural man can abide the presence of God… except quickened by the Spirit" (67:10-13) — the matter-spectrum on seeing God.* The promised blessing is to see God's face — but conditioned by a doctrine of *capacity*: "no man has seen God at any time in the flesh, **except quickened by the Spirit of God**" (67:11); "neither can any natural man abide the presence of God, neither after the carnal mind" (67:12); "ye are not able to abide the presence of God now… continue in patience until ye are perfected" (67:13). (The matter-spectrum applied to *theophany*: to see God is not merely to have one's eyes opened but to be *raised to a higher state* — "quickened by the Spirit," transfigured enough to *abide* a glory that would otherwise consume the natural man. This is Moses 1:11 ["mine own eyes have beheld God; but not my natural eyes… his glory was upon me; and I beheld his face, for I was transfigured before him"] and the §50:24 light-growth-law: you grow "brighter and brighter until the perfect day," and only the perfected can abide the perfect light. Seeing God is a function of *what you have become*, not of God's willingness to be seen. "When ye are worthy, in mine own due time, ye shall see" [67:14] — the vision waits on the worthiness, the patience, the perfecting. The natural man can't abide the presence not because God bars him but because he is *not yet the kind of being* who can stand in that light — the becoming is the prerequisite. The matter-spectrum's anthropology: glory is a capacity grown, not a gift merely given.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. **Walk's contribution:** **§67 is the defense of §1:24 — "make one like unto it" (67:5-9).** When the brethren criticized the *language* of the revelations (Joseph's rough grammar — "I could express it better"), the Lord conceded the imperfection ("his language… his imperfections you have known") and then issued the challenge: take the *least* revelation, appoint your *wisest* man, and reproduce it — and if you cannot (McLellin, the "most wise," tried and failed), then to withhold your witness is condemnation. The proof of a revelation's divinity is **irreproducible substance "from the Father of lights," not linguistic polish.** This is the period-language principle's other half: §1:24 says *read God's word in the receiver's frame*; §67 says *don't mistake the frame for the message* — God's word comes *through* an imperfect instrument's language, and smooth prose can be empty while rough prose carries the light (directly relevant to a project working through an AI instrument: the test is never fluency, always whether the substance is true and came from above). Plus the **fear-forfeits-the-blessing** diagnosis (67:3 — the elders were *offered* the vision of God's face but "there were fears in your hearts, and verily this is the reason that ye did not receive"; jealousy and fear the twin disqualifiers, humility the cure — the block was on *their* side, not God's reluctance). And the **matter-spectrum on theophany** (67:11-13 — "no natural man can abide the presence of God, except quickened by the Spirit"; seeing God requires being *transfigured* enough to abide the glory, Moses 1:11; the §50:24 light-growth-law — glory is a capacity *grown*, the vision waits on the *becoming*). For Michael: the section that answers everyone who ever doubted a revelation because the *words* were plain — reproduce the least of them, or bear record they're true; what makes them true descends from the Father of lights, not from grammar — and the natural man cannot yet stand in that light, not because God bars him, but because he is not yet the kind of being who can abide it.
