# D&C 55 — the printer, and books for children

**Setting:** **Kirtland, Ohio, June 14, 1831.** **William W. Phelps**, a printer by trade, had just arrived in Kirtland with his family; Joseph inquired concerning him. Six verses calling Phelps to baptism, ordination, and — distinctively — the work of *printing* and *writing books for the Church's children*. Phelps would become the Restoration's principal printer (the Book of Commandments, the Evening and Morning Star) and hymn-writer ("The Spirit of God," "Praise to the Man").

**Binding idea:** Phelps is called, after baptism "with an eye single to my glory" (55:1), not only to preach but to *print and teach the young*: "selecting and writing books for schools in this church, that little children also may receive instruction before me as is pleasing unto me" (55:4). The kingdom needs its *printers and teachers of children* as much as its missionaries — the gospel propagated by *the press* and planted in *the rising generation*.

**Read:** "Thou art called and chosen; and after thou hast been baptized by water, which if you do with an eye single to my glory, you shall have a remission of your sins and a reception of the Holy Spirit" (55:1). Ordained an elder, "to preach repentance and remission of sins by way of baptism" (55:2); "on whomsoever you shall lay your hands, if they are contrite before me, you shall have power to give the Holy Spirit" (55:3). "You shall be ordained to assist my servant Oliver Cowdery to do the work of printing, and of selecting and writing books for schools in this church, that little children also may receive instruction before me as is pleasing unto me" (55:4). Journey to Missouri, "that you may be planted in the land of your inheritance to do this work" (55:5).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "Books for schools… that little children also may receive instruction" (55:4) — the kingdom invests in the young and in the press.* The very first thing the new printer is told, beyond preaching, is to *make books for the Church's children*. The Restoration, weeks into its Ohio period and under persecution, is already commissioning *children's education* — "that little children also may receive instruction… as is pleasing unto me." (Two priorities revealed in one verse: the *press* [printing is a calling, a means of the kingdom — the gospel multiplied in type, as §47 commissioned the *record*] and *the children* [the rising generation taught "before me"]. A movement that, before it has a temple or a city, commissions *books for its children* is a movement playing the long game — the §49:16 "measure of man" filled, then *taught*. The §88 School of the Prophets and the whole education thread begins here, with a printer told to write for the young.)
- *"With an eye single to my glory" (55:1) — the condition on the very baptism.* Phelps's baptism carries a condition rarely attached so explicitly: "*if* you do with an eye single to my glory, you shall have a remission." The §4:5 / §27:2 single eye made the qualifier on the ordinance itself — even baptism's efficacy turns on *pure motive*. (Not the act alone but the *eye single* makes it count — the §41:5 doer and the §4:5 single eye joined: a baptism performed with divided motive doesn't carry the remission the same way. The heart's singleness conditions the ordinance's grace.)
- *"Power to give the Holy Spirit, if they are contrite" (55:3) — the receiver's part.* Phelps will confer the Holy Ghost "on whomsoever you shall lay your hands, **if they are contrite before me**." The ordinance's effect depends not only on the authority of the one conferring but on the *contrition* of the one receiving (the §52:15-16 contrite pattern; §50). The Spirit isn't conferred mechanically; the receiver's broken heart is the condition of the gift. (Authority confers; contrition receives — the same two-sidedness as §50:19-22 [both preacher and hearer must be in the Spirit]. A contrite recipient receives; a hard one doesn't, regardless of the conferring hands.)
