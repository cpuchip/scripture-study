# D&C 81 — succor the weak, lift up the hands which hang down

**Setting:** **Hiram, Ohio, March 15, 1832** — a step toward the formal organization of the **First Presidency**, calling a counselor to Joseph in the Presidency of the High Priesthood. The revelation originally named **Jesse Gause**; when he failed to continue and was excommunicated (December 1832), the call was transferred, and **Frederick G. Williams** was ordained to the office (March 1833). The published text carries Williams's name; the office is what the revelation reveals.

**Binding idea:** "Be faithful; stand in the office which I have appointed unto you; **succor the weak, lift up the hands which hang down, and strengthen the feeble knees**" (81:5). The counselor in the highest council of the Church is charged not with grandeur but with *succor* — holding up the weak, the discouraged, the failing.

**Read:** "Listen to the voice of him who speaketh… and hearken to the calling wherewith you are called, even to be a high priest in my church, and a counselor unto my servant Joseph Smith, Jun." (81:1). "Unto whom I have given the keys of the kingdom, which belong always unto the Presidency of the High Priesthood" (81:2). "Verily I acknowledge him and will bless him, and also thee, inasmuch as thou art faithful in counsel, in the office which I have appointed unto you, in prayer always, vocally and in thy heart, in public and in private" (81:3). "In doing these things thou wilt do the greatest good unto thy fellow beings, and wilt promote the glory of him who is your Lord" (81:4). "Be faithful; stand in the office which I have appointed unto you; succor the weak, lift up the hands which hang down, and strengthen the feeble knees" (81:5). "If thou art faithful unto the end thou shalt have a crown of immortality, and eternal life in the mansions which I have prepared in the house of my Father" (81:6).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ "The keys… belong always unto the Presidency of the High Priesthood" (81:2).* A foundational structural word: the keys of the kingdom are *institutional*, not personal — they "belong **always** unto the Presidency." (The keys are not the private possession of a man but the standing endowment of an *office*. The First Presidency, here forming, is the body that *always* holds the keys — so the kingdom's authority outlives any individual, transfers cleanly, and never lapses with a person. This is the §28 order-of-things at the apex: authority runs through *position*, and the position is permanent though the persons change. And the section *demonstrates* it — the call passed from Gause to Williams [the office stable, the man replaced]; the keys did not depend on Jesse Gause, and his fall did not shake them. For any institution that must outlast its founders: vest the authority in the *office*, "which belong always," not in the irreplaceable individual.)
- *★ "Succor the weak, lift up the hands which hang down, and strengthen the feeble knees" (81:5).* The charge to a member of the highest council in the Church — and it is not a charge of *rule* but of *succor*. (Isaiah 35:3 / Hebrews 12:12 — "strengthen ye the weak hands, and confirm the feeble knees." The counselor's work, named at the top of the hierarchy, is to *hold up the failing*: the weak, the discouraged [hands that "hang down"], the stumbling [knees gone feeble]. This is the presiding covenant's heart [§121:41 — no compulsion, but persuasion, gentleness, love] and the §62:1 succoring Advocate at the human scale: to preside in the kingdom is to *succor*, not to dominate. The greatest good a man can do "unto his fellow beings" [81:4] is to lift the ones who are sinking. The presiding role the walk has tracked — Michael presiding under the Holy One [§78:16], the bishop accounting [§72], the prophet receiving [§21:5] — finds here its *posture*: the one who presides bends down to *strengthen the feeble knees*. For the project's presiding covenant: the test of authority is whether it succors the weak; downward power exists to *lift*, not to lord.)
- *The office outlasts the man — Gause to Williams (heading).* The historical note carries a quiet doctrine: the revelation was given to Jesse Gause, who "failed to continue… and was excommunicated"; the call was "subsequently transferred to Frederick G. Williams." (The §81:2 institutional keys *demonstrated* in the section's own history: the calling did not die with the unfaithful man — it *transferred*. The canon preserves the revelation under Williams's name, but the office was real before either man filled it stably. bin-4-adj — Gause's fall is his; what the walk notes is the structural truth: the *office* is what the Lord revealed, and an office, vested with keys that "belong always," survives the failure of the one who first held it. Even the highest call can pass from an unfaithful hand to a faithful one without the authority lapsing — because the keys were never the man's; they were the office's.)
