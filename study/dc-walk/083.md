# D&C 83 — widows and orphans shall be provided for

**Setting:** **Independence, Missouri, April 30, 1832** — four days after §82, "as the Prophet sat in council with his brethren." A six-verse revelation "in addition to the laws of the church concerning women and children," defining the *claims* of the vulnerable: women on their husbands, children on their parents, and — when those are gone — widows and orphans on the Church storehouse.

**Binding idea:** "The storehouse shall be kept by the consecrations of the church; and widows and orphans shall be provided for, as also the poor" (83:6). The consecration system the walk has tracked exists, finally, for *this*: the widow and the orphan and the poor are *provided for*, by covenant.

**Read:** "In addition to the laws of the church concerning women and children, those who belong to the church, who have lost their husbands or fathers" (83:1). "Women have claim on their husbands for their maintenance, until their husbands are taken" (83:2). "All children have claim upon their parents for their maintenance until they are of age" (83:4). "After that, they have claim upon the church, or in other words upon the Lord's storehouse, if their parents have not wherewith to give them inheritances" (83:5). "The storehouse shall be kept by the consecrations of the church; and widows and orphans shall be provided for, as also the poor" (83:6).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ A descending order of claims — and no one falls through (83:2-6).* The revelation lays out a *structure of care* in concentric rings: a woman has "claim on [her] husband for [her] maintenance"; a child "claim upon [his] parents… until they are of age"; and *when the family cannot provide* — husband "taken," parents "have not wherewith" — the claim passes outward to "the church… the Lord's storehouse" (83:5). (The word is "**claim**" — not charity, not optional benevolence, but a *right*. The widow and orphan don't *beg* the Church; they have a *claim* on it, an entitlement the covenant created. This is the consecration system [§42→§51→§78→§82] revealing its *purpose*: the storehouse, the equal-by-need distribution, the bishop's accounting — all of it exists so that "widows and orphans shall be provided for." The structure is *first* the family [the natural order — husband, parents], *then* the Church as the safety net when the family fails [§75:24's support-the-missionary's-family generalized to *every* failed provision]. No one falls through: if the inner ring [family] breaks, the outer ring [the church storehouse] catches. James 1:27 — "pure religion… is this, To visit the fatherless and widows in their affliction" — given an *institution* and a *funding mechanism* [the consecrations]. The gospel's care for the vulnerable is not a sentiment but a *system with a claim attached*.)
- *The family first, the Church as the net (83:2-5).* The order matters: the *family* bears the first responsibility [husband for wife, parents for children — §75:28 "every man who is obliged to provide for his own family, let him provide"], and the Church catches what the family cannot. (Not a welfare state replacing the family but a *backstop* to it: "if their parents have not wherewith" [83:5] — the storehouse activates *when the family's provision fails*, not instead of it. The §1 Tim 5:8 order — "if any provide not for his own… he hath denied the faith" — paired with the §James 1:27 order — the church provides for those *with no* family to provide. Both are true at once: the man must provide for his own [§75:28], and the church must provide for those who have no one. The widow whose husband is "taken" and the orphan whose parents are gone are precisely the ones the *family* order cannot reach — so the *covenant* order reaches them. Even fellowship is tied to faithfulness [83:2-3 — "if they are not found transgressors they shall have fellowship"], yet the *inheritance* claim under the law of the land remains [83:3] — care without coercion of conscience.)
