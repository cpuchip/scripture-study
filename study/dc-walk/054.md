# D&C 54 — the broken covenant, and mercy for the keepers

**Setting:** **Kirtland, Ohio, June 10, 1831.** The Saints at Thompson were "divided… having to do with the consecration of properties. Selfishness and greed were manifest." **Leman Copley** — the Shaker convert of §49 — had covenanted to consecrate his large farm as a place of inheritance for the Colesville Saints arriving from New York, *then broke the covenant* and reneged. **Newel Knight**, leading the displaced Colesville members, came to ask what to do. The Lord's answer: leave Copley's farm, flee to Missouri, and trust Him through the tribulation.

**Binding idea:** "As the covenant which they made unto me has been broken, even so it has become void and of none effect" (54:4) — a broken covenant dissolves the promise (D&C 82:10). But the *innocent* keepers are not abandoned: "blessed are they who have kept the covenant… for they shall obtain mercy" (54:6). The covenant-breaker forfeits; the covenant-keeper, even when *displaced* by another's breach, is held by mercy.

**Read:** Newel Knight is to "stand fast in the office whereunto I have appointed you" (54:2). "If your brethren desire to escape their enemies, let them repent… and become truly humble before me and contrite" (54:3). "As the covenant which they made unto me has been broken, even so it has become void and of none effect" (54:4). "Wo to him by whom this offense cometh, for it had been better for him that he had been drowned in the depth of the sea" (54:5). "Blessed are they who have kept the covenant… for they shall obtain mercy" (54:6). "Go to now and flee the land… take your journey… into the regions westward, unto the land of Missouri" (54:7-8). "Seek ye a living like unto men, until I prepare a place for you" (54:9). "Be patient in tribulation until I come… and they who have sought me early shall find rest to their souls" (54:10).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ A broken covenant goes "void and of none effect" (54:4) — D&C 82:10 enacted again.* Copley's consecration covenant, broken, *dissolves*: "even so it has become void." This is the covenant principle the whole project rests on — "when ye do not what I say, ye have no promise" (D&C 82:10) — made concrete a second time (the first was §40, Covel's broken covenant). The covenant is *bilateral*; when one side breaks it, the binding lapses. (And note the asymmetry of consequence: the *breaker* gets the millstone-wo [54:5, Matt 18:6 — "better… drowned in the depth of the sea"], because his breach *displaced and endangered the innocent*. Breaking a covenant that only hurts yourself is grave; breaking one that *causes others to stumble* draws the harshest word in the gospel. Copley's reneging didn't just void his own promise — it turned the Colesville Saints out of the inheritance they'd been promised, "by whom this offense cometh.")
- *★ Mercy for the keepers, even when displaced (54:6).* The Colesville Saints did *nothing wrong* — they kept their covenant and were *betrayed* by Copley's breach, left homeless. And the Lord's word to them is "**blessed are they who have kept the covenant… for they shall obtain mercy**." The innocent party, harmed by another's covenant-breaking, is not punished for the disruption — they are *blessed* and shown mercy. (This is tender and just: the covenant economy doesn't make the faithful suffer for the unfaithful's sin. When someone *else's* breach upends your life, your faithfulness still stands and still draws mercy. The displaced keep their standing; the breaker loses his. Justice tracks the *heart*, not the *circumstance* — being turned out by another's sin doesn't diminish you before God.)
- *"Seek ye a living like unto men, until I prepare a place" (54:9) — faithful ordinariness in the interim.* The displaced Saints, fleeing to Missouri, are told something humble: "seek ye a living like unto men" — work, earn, make do, *like ordinary people*, "until I prepare a place for you." No miraculous provision is promised for the gap; they're to *labor like men* in the meantime. (The §51:16 "act as for years" in its hardship form: when the promised place isn't ready, you don't wait idly for deliverance — you *make a living like a man*, faithfully ordinary, trusting God to prepare the place in His time. Faith doesn't exempt you from the day-labor of survival; it sustains you *through* it.)
- *"Patient in tribulation… sought me early shall find rest" (54:10).* The close: the displaced are not promised the trouble will end soon, but *rest to their souls* if they "seek me early" (Prov 8:17; Matt 11:29). The §24:8 / §45:35 comfort — presence and rest *within* tribulation, not exemption from it — given to a betrayed, homeless people. (And "sought me early" — the ones who seek God *first*, *promptly*, in the trouble, find the rest. The §6:23 peace-to-the-mind for a people who lost their inheritance to another's greed: seek Him early, and find rest even on the road.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. **Walk's contribution:** **§54 enacts the covenant economy a second time** — Copley's broken consecration covenant goes "void and of none effect" (54:4 = D&C 82:10, "no promise"), and because his breach *displaced the innocent*, he draws the millstone-wo (54:5, "better… drowned in the depth of the sea").
