# Digest: The Imitation of Christ — Thomas à Kempis

**Author:** Thomas à Kempis (c. 1380–1471), translated by William Benham
**Origin:** Latin, early 15th century; composed within the Devotio Moderna movement
**Status:** After the Bible, the most widely read devotional work in Christian history (745+ editions before 1650)

---

## Overview

*The Imitation of Christ* is a four-book manual of spiritual instruction aimed at those seeking interior transformation through humility, self-denial, and contemplative devotion. Written by a canon regular of the Windesheim monastery, it addresses not theologians but practitioners—those who want to live the Gospel rather than merely study it. The book's four movements trace a path from external discipline (Book I) through inward purification (Book II) to divine consolation (Book III) and finally to the Eucharistic life (Book IV).

---

## Book I: Admonitions Profitable for the Spiritual Life

**Purpose:** Foundational disciplines for beginning the spiritual journey.

### Core Teaching: Imitation Over Information

The opening chapter establishes the book's central thesis:

> "He that followeth me shall not walk in darkness... Let it be our most earnest study, therefore, to dwell upon the life of Jesus Christ. His teaching surpasseth all teaching of holy men, and such as have His Spirit find therein the hidden manna."

Knowledge without transformation is worthless. Chapter II drives this home:

> "Better of a surety is a lowly peasant who serveth God, than a proud philosopher who watcheth the stars and neglecteth the knowledge of himself."

### Key Themes

- **Humility over learning** (Ch. II–III): True understanding comes not from intellectual accumulation but from inner simplicity. "Happy is the man whom Truth by itself doth teach, not by figures and transient words, but as it is in itself."
- **Prudence in action** (Ch. IV): "We must not trust every word of others or feeling within ourselves, but cautiously and patiently try the matter, whether it be of God."
- **Scripture read for profit, not cleverness** (Ch. V): "All Scripture ought to be read in the spirit in which it was written." Seek what is profitable, not what "ministereth to subtlety in discourse."
- **Fleeing vanity and pride** (Ch. VI–VIII): Worldly familiarity and excessive speech are dangers to the soul.
- **Obedience** (Ch. IX): Submission to rightful authority is a discipline of humility.
- **Adversity as medicine** (Ch. XII): Suffering purifies; "he who knoweth not how to bear adversity, knoweth not how to use prosperity."
- **Contemplation of death** (Ch. XXIII–XXIV): Mortality should animate, not paralyze. "Let us prepare ourselves for death, that we may not fear it."

### Structural Note

Book I contains 25 chapters—the longest section. It functions as a rule of life: concrete admonitions for daily conduct, prayer, reading, and community living.

## Book II: Admonitions Concerning the Inner Life

**Purpose:** Moving from external discipline to interior transformation.

### The Kingdom Within

> "The kingdom of God is within you... Turn thee with all thine heart to the Lord and forsake this miserable world."

### Key Themes

- **Inward turning** (Ch. I): The spiritual life is not about external observance but interior reorientation.
- **Lowly submission** (Ch. II): "Make no great account who is for thee or against thee, but mind only the present duty."
- **Peace** (Ch. III): "First keep thyself in peace, and then shalt thou be able to be a peacemaker towards others." Inner peace precedes outer peacemaking.
- **Simplicity and purity** (Ch. IV): "By two wings is man lifted above earthly things, even by simplicity and purity."
- **Loving Jesus above all** (Ch. VII–VIII): The intimate love of Christ is the goal, not self-improvement.
- **The Cross** (Ch. XI–XII): "If any man will come after Me, let him deny himself and take up his Cross and follow Me." The cross is not metaphorical suffering but the daily death of self-will.

### The Nature vs. Grace Distinction

A recurring motif: nature is deceitful, self-seeking, and changeable; grace is simple, God-seeking, and steadfast. The spiritual life is the gradual replacement of natural inclinations with grace-shaped ones.

## Book III: On Inward Consolation

**Purpose:** The voice of God to the purified soul.

### God Speaks Inwardly

This book shifts to a dialogic form—God addressing the soul directly:

> "I will hearken what the Lord God shall say within me."
> "Speak Lord, for thy servant heareth."

### Key Themes

- **Divine intimacy** (Ch. I–IV): God speaks not through "noise of words" but through the quiet of a humble heart. "My Son, hear My words, for My words are most sweet."
- **The power of divine love** (Ch. V): Love transforms more than knowledge. "It is a good life which maketh a man dear to God."
- **Humility guards grace** (Ch. VII–VIII): Even spiritual gifts must be hidden under humility, lest pride corrupt them.
- **Casting care upon God** (Ch. XVII): "Cast all thy care upon Me, and I will give thee rest."
- **Patient suffering** (Ch. XVIII–XIX): Endure injuries silently, following Christ's example.
- **Four things that bring peace** (Ch. XXIII): (1) A good conscience, (2) a faithful heart, (3) a meek spirit, (4) a humble mind.
- **Prayer over reading** (Ch. XXVI): "Humble prayer more deserveth than doth frequent reading."

### Structural Note

Book III is the most contemplative section. It reads like a spiritual diary—intimate, personal, addressed to one who has already done the work of Books I and II.

---

## Book IV: The Sacrament of the Altar

**Purpose:** The Eucharist as the summit and sustenance of the spiritual life.

### The Eucharistic Center

> "Christ must be received with reverence... the greatness and charity of God is shown to men in the Sacrament."

### Key Themes

- **Reverence** (Ch. I): The Eucharist demands awe. "Thou comest unto me, O Lord, the creature to the Creator."
- **Frequent communion** (Ch. III): "It is profitable to Communicate often... for the healing of my soul."
- **Scripture and Sacrament together** (Ch. XI): "The Body and Blood of Christ and the Holy Scriptures are most necessary to a faithful soul."
- **The Eucharist as medicine**: Presented not as a ritual but as spiritual nourishment—"the medicine for spiritual sickness."

### Structural Note

Book IV is the shortest section but the theological climax. The entire journey of imitation culminates in receiving Christ in the Eucharist.

---

## Architecture of the Four Books

| Book | Focus | Movement |
|------|-------|----------|
| I | External discipline | From world to monastery |
| II | Interior purification | From self to God |
| III | Divine consolation | From seeking to being sought |
| IV | Eucharistic union | From practice to sacrament |

The progression is deliberate: you cannot receive consolation (III) without purification (II), and you cannot purify without discipline (I). The Eucharist (IV) is both the goal and the means.

## Tensions & Objections

### The Strongest Objection: Anti-Intellectualism Masked as Humility

The book's most dangerous tendency is its consistent devaluation of intellectual pursuit in favor of what it calls "humility." Consider:

> "Better of a surety is a lowly peasant who serveth God, than a proud philosopher who watcheth the stars and neglecteth the knowledge of himself."

This is not merely cautioning against pride—it is actively disparaging the pursuit of knowledge. The same pattern repeats: "Rest from inordinate desire of knowledge, for therein is found much distraction and deceit." The book treats intellectual curiosity itself as suspect, conflating the sin of pride with the activity of learning.

**Why this matters:** This anti-intellectual bias has real historical consequences. The Devotio Moderna movement, while producing genuine saints, also contributed to a spiritual culture that viewed theological inquiry with suspicion. In modern contexts, this posture can be weaponized to discourage critical engagement with scripture, tradition, or even science—under the guise of "humility."

### The Passivity Problem

The book's emphasis on obedience, submission, and bearing adversity quietly is admirable in a monastic context but dangerous when generalized:

> "Make no great account who is for thee or against thee, but mind only the present duty."

This counsel works well for a cloistered canon but poorly for a citizen facing injustice. The book has no category for righteous anger, prophetic confrontation, or social transformation. Suffering is always to be borne; never is it to be resisted. This is not wisdom—it is a specific ascetic ideal dressed as universal counsel.

### The World-Contempt Trap

> "Forsake this miserable world."

The book's contempt for "the world" is not the biblical distinction between creation and fallen systems but a blanket dismissal of earthly life. This otherworldliness can produce spiritual bypassing: ignoring real suffering, real relationships, and real responsibilities because they are "worldly." The book offers no guidance for those who cannot literally withdraw from the world—most people, most of the time.

### What the Book Gets Right Despite Itself

The book's strongest insights emerge when it focuses on what is universally human: the need for honesty about one's motives, the danger of self-deception, the value of simplicity. These are not anti-intellectual; they are preconditions for any genuine inquiry—spiritual or otherwise.

**The null case, stated plainly:** *The Imitation of Christ* is a brilliant manual for a specific vocation (monastic withdrawal) that has been mistakenly universalized. Its counsel is excellent for those who have already chosen to leave the world; it is incomplete—and potentially harmful—for those who must live in it.

## Actionable Learning

1. **Read scripture for profit, not cleverness.** The book's counsel to read "in the spirit in which it was written" applies beyond scripture: read any text for transformation, not just information.

2. **Distinguish humility from anti-intellectualism.** Humility is honest self-assessment; it is not the abandonment of curiosity. You can pursue knowledge without pride.

3. **Inner peace precedes outer peacemaking.** "First keep thyself in peace, and then shalt thou be able to be a peacemaker towards others." This is psychologically sound: you cannot pour from an empty cup.

4. **Suffering is medicine, not a goal.** The book treats adversity as purifying—but purification is not the same as masochism. Endure what you cannot change; change what you can.

5. **The Eucharist (or your equivalent) as center.** Whether literal communion or some other regular practice of receiving grace, the book's insight stands: spiritual life needs a sacramental center, not just disciplines.
