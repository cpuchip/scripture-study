# Nicomachean Ethics

**Author:** Aristotle

---

## Core argument / thesis

The highest good achievable by human action is happiness (*eudaimonia*), which Aristotle defines not as pleasure, honor, or wealth but as activity of the soul’s rational element in accordance with virtue over a complete lifetime, aided by adequate external goods. Moral virtue is neither innate nor merely theoretical; it is a habituated state of character, acquired by repeatedly doing virtuous acts, that aims at the mean between excess and deficiency relative to the individual and determined by right reason. Because political science governs all other arts and legislates what citizens should do, the inquiry into this human good belongs fundamentally to politics, and justice is its completion in relation to one's neighbor.

## Structure

*Book I* establishes the inquiry: every art and action aims at some good, so there must be a chief good—happiness. Aristotle surveys three candidate lives (the life of pleasure, the political life, and the contemplative life), rejects Plato’s separate Form of the Good by arguing that “good” is predicated in every category and cannot be a single Idea, and offers the “function argument” to show that human good is rational soul-activity expressing virtue. He closes by dividing the soul into rational and irrational parts—the latter split into the vegetative and the appetitive/desiring—and establishing that moral virtue concerns the appetitive part insofar as it can obey reason. *Book II* turns to moral psychology: intellectual virtue is taught, but moral virtue is formed by habit (*ethos*). He defines moral virtue as a state of character concerned with choice, lying in a mean between excess and defect. *Book III* analyzes the conditions of responsibility—voluntary action, involuntary action (including mixed acts done under duress for noble ends), choice (*prohairesis*), and deliberation—and begins applying the mean to specific virtues, examining courage (regarding fear and confidence, and distinguishing five counterfeit forms: civic, experiential, passion-driven, sanguine, and ignorant) and temperance (regarding the bodily pleasures of touch and taste). *Book IV* continues through the moral virtues, cataloguing liberality, magnificence, pride, ambition, good temper, friendliness, truthfulness, and ready wit, showing each as an intermediate between two opposed vices; it adds that shame is a feeling rather than a virtue, and that continence is a mixed state, not virtue proper. *Book V* treats justice as a twofold part of virtue: universal justice, whereby the just is the lawful and the fair and commands the acts of complete virtue toward others; distributive justice, which distributes goods according to geometrical proportion (merit); and rectificatory justice, which restores equality in transactions through arithmetical proportion. It closes with the role of reciprocity—properly proportionate requital, not exact retaliation—and money as a commensurable measure that holds the city together.

## Key passages

> “Every art and every inquiry, and similarly every action and pursuit, is thought to aim at some good; and for this reason the good has rightly been declared to be that at which all things aim.”

*Gloss:* Aristotle opens with the teleological premise that grounds the entire inquiry—all human endeavor is oriented toward some end or good.

> “human good turns out to be activity of soul in accordance with virtue, and if there are more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete. But we must add ‘in a complete life.’ For one swallow does not make a summer, nor does one day; and so too one day, or a short time, does not make a man blessed and happy.”

*Gloss:* Happiness is not a fleeting feeling or a single act but a sustained, lifelong activity of the rational soul expressing excellence.

> “moral virtue comes about as a result of habit, whence also its name (ethike) is one that is formed by a slight variation from the word ethos (habit)... Neither by nature, then, nor contrary to nature do the virtues arise in us; rather we are adapted by nature to receive them, and are made perfect by habit.”

*Gloss:* Moral virtue is cultivated; we have a natural capacity for it, but it is realized only through repeated practice until it becomes settled character.

> “Virtue, then, is a state of character concerned with choice, lying in a mean, i.e. the mean relative to us, this being determined by a rational principle... Now it is a mean between two vices, that which depends on excess and that which depends on defect.”

*Gloss:* Excellence is not an absolute quantity but a relative middle state between the opposing vices of too much and too little.

> “Therefore virtue also is in our own power, and so too vice. For where it is in our power to act it is also in our power to act, and vice versa.”

*Gloss:* Because the origin of action is in the agent, we are responsible for the characters we form through our voluntary deeds.

> “justice is complete virtue, but not absolutely, but in relation to our neighbour. And therefore justice is often thought to be the greatest of virtues, and ‘neither evening nor morning star’ is so wonderful; and proverbially ‘in justice is every virtue comprehended’.”

*Gloss:* Justice is the whole of virtue in its social dimension, governing how we stand in relation to others in a political community.

> “It needs the external goods as well; for it is impossible, or not easy, to do noble acts without the proper equipment... the man who is very ugly in appearance or ill-born or solitary and childless is not very likely to be happy.”

*Gloss:* Happiness is not purely internal; it requires a material and social substrate—wealth, friends, health, political power—that lies partly outside the agent’s control.

## Themes

- **Teleology and the Chief Good.** Every practice and action aims at some end; politics, as the most authoritative art, aims at the highest end—happiness.
- **Activity over Possession.** Happiness is a way of living and doing, not a static condition or external good; even virtue itself is realized only in action.
- **Habituation and Character.** We become just by doing just acts; moral states are produced and destroyed by the same kinds of activities, which is why early upbringing matters profoundly.
- **The Doctrine of the Mean.** Virtue is an intermediate state between excess and deficiency, but this mean is “relative to us,” determined by circumstance and right reason rather than by rigid rule.
- **The Parts of the Soul and the Obedience of Appetite.** Moral virtue belongs to the appetitive/desiring part of the soul insofar as it listens to and obeys reason; temperance is the harmony of appetite with rational principle.
- **Voluntariness and Responsibility.** Praise and blame attach only to voluntary actions and the characters built from them; choice and deliberation are the marks of the moral agent, though mixed acts done under threat complicate the picture.
- **Justice as Proportionate Equality.** Justice binds the community through geometrical proportion in distribution and arithmetical equality in rectification, making it virtue entire in relation to one’s neighbor.
- **The Vulnerability of Happiness to Fortune.** Aristotle insists that happiness requires external goods as equipment for noble action, and that great misfortunes can “crush and maim” it; yet he also claims the truly virtuous man can never become miserable, only fall short of blessedness—a tension left unresolved.

## Tensions & objections

1. **Circularity of the Mean (the strongest formal objection).**  
   Aristotle defines virtue as a mean “determined by a rational principle, and by that principle by which the man of practical wisdom would determine it.” But practical wisdom is itself identified by its success in hitting the mean. Without an independent, non-circular criterion of “right reason,” the doctrine cannot guide the learner; it can only retrospectively ratify the intuitions of those already habituated to virtue. The ethics risks collapsing into conservatism: the good is whatever the good man does, and the good man is whoever does what is praised.

2. **The Naturalistic Gap in the Function Argument.**  
   Aristotle argues that because man has a characteristic function—activity of the rational soul—his good is to perform it well. This moves from a descriptive premise (humans are rational animals) to a normative conclusion (they ought to actualize that rationality excellently). Later philosophers identify this as the naturalistic fallacy: mere biological or metaphysical “function” does not, by itself, generate a moral obligation.

3. **Elitism and the Exclusion of Most Human Beings.**  
   By defining happiness as activity in accordance with *complete* virtue across a *complete* life, and by listing good birth, beauty, wealth, friends, and political power as necessary equipment, Aristotle effectively excludes from happiness women, slaves, the poor, the ill-born, the solitary, and anyone stricken by great misfortune. Happiness becomes an aristocratic achievement rather than a universal human possibility, despite his claim to be studying the human good as such.

4. **The Tension Between Self-Sufficiency and External Vulnerability.**  
   Aristotle calls happiness “self-sufficient” and declares that “no happy man can become miserable,” yet he admits that external goods are necessary equipment and that fortunes like those of Priam can destroy blessedness. He tries to save the doctrine by distinguishing the happy man from the blessed one, but this looks like special pleading: a life of noble suffering may be virtuous, yet calling it “happy” strains both ordinary language and the claim that the chief good is final and self-sufficient.

5. **Hardened Character and the Limits of Voluntariness.**  
   Aristotle insists that vice is voluntary because we are responsible for the acts that formed our character. Yet he concedes that once a man has become unjust, “it is not possible for him not to be so,” comparing him to someone who has thrown a stone and can no longer recover it. If the vicious agent now cannot choose otherwise, punitive blame seems to punish him for a condition he can no longer control, undermining the voluntariness that is supposed to ground moral responsibility.

6. **Indeterminacy in Distributive Justice.**  
   Aristotle states that distributive justice awards goods “according to merit,” but acknowledges that democrats identify merit with free birth, oligarchs with wealth, and aristocrats with virtue. He offers no non-question-begging way to adjudicate between these competing standards; his formal account of geometrical proportion can rationalize almost any existing hierarchy, making the theory politically indeterminate or inherently conservative.

## What's worth learning — and what we could do with it

1. **Run a “function argument audit” on your own activities.** Once a week, ask: what is the distinctive capacity I am exercising right now, and am I exercising it well or poorly? (A programmer reasoning vs. copy-pasting; a parent attending vs. merely present; a citizen deliberating vs. reacting.) This is not mood-tracking; it is a structured review against the specific *kind* of activity each role demands. The substrate could implement this as a periodic prompt that asks the user to tag their recent actions by the rational capacity engaged and rate the quality of that engagement.

2. **Use the “excess–mean–deficiency” frame as a decision heuristic.** When facing a recurring choice (how direct to be in feedback, how much to save, how assertive to be in a meeting), explicitly name the two vices that flank the virtue: e.g., brutality –– candor –– evasion. The value is not that Aristotle gives the answer, but that the triplet forces you to locate your action on a spectrum rather than treating each choice as sui generis. A substrate tool could scaffold this by prompting for the two extremes and the middle term before any significant decision is logged.

3. **Treat character as a system, not a score.** Aristotle’s claim that “moral states are produced and destroyed by the same kinds of activities” means you cannot assess virtue by a single act or even by self-reported values; you must look at the *pattern* over time. This suggests replacing one-off “virtue metrics” with longitudinal habit tracking: what did you actually do when angry, when tempted, when generous opportunities arose? The substrate could model character states as time-series data updated by repeated behavior, warning when a drift toward excess or deficiency is detected across multiple instances.

4. **Build “equipment” deliberately, not as optional prep.** Aristotle’s insistence that happiness needs external goods is not materialism; it is systems thinking. Friendship, health, financial slack, and political standing are not rewards for virtue but *enabling conditions* for it. A concrete practice: once a quarter, audit your “equipment inventory”—which external goods are thin or missing, and which noble acts they are therefore foreclosing?—then schedule one action to repair the bottleneck. A substrate could maintain this inventory and surface conflicts between stated goals and missing prerequisites.

5. **Apply the justice-as-proportion test to distributions you influence.** In any group where you allocate resources, attention, or status (a team, a family, a community), ask: what standard of merit am I actually using (birth, wealth, virtue, need), and would I publicly defend it? Aristotle’s framework does not resolve which standard is correct, but it makes visible the otherwise hidden geometry of distribution. A substrate could require an explicit “merit axiom” whenever a resource-allocation rule is proposed, then flag inconsistency between the axiom and the actual allocation pattern.

6. **Design environments for habituation, not just information.** Aristotle’s central pedagogical insight is that we do not become virtuous by learning what virtue is, but by repeatedly doing virtuous acts until they become second nature. This means any system aiming at human improvement should weight *practice architecture* at least as heavily as *content delivery*. Concrete applications: default rules that make the mean option the easy option; social accountability loops that make repeated defect visible early; spaced prompts that cue reflection at decision points rather than after the fact. The substrate itself should be evaluated not by how much it teaches, but by how effectively it structures the repeated actions that form character.
