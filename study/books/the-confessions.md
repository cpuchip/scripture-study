# The Confessions — Saint Augustine


## Overview & Structure

*The Confessions* (c. 397-400 AD) is Saint Augustine of Hippo's autobiographical prayer to God — thirteen books that move from personal narrative (Books I-IX) to philosophical inquiry (Books X-XI) to meditation on Genesis (Books XII-XIII). Written in his late fifties, it is the first extended autobiography in Western literature and one of the most influential works of Christian thought.

**Structure:**
- **Books I-IV:** Youth — infancy, schooling, the pear-theft, the death of a friend, his mother Monica's tears
- **Books V-VII:** Search — Manichaeism, the turning toward Neoplatonism, the intellectual breakthrough
- **Books VIII-IX:** Conversion — the garden scene at Milan, baptism by Ambrose, Monica's death
- **Books X-XI:** Inquiry — memory, temptation, and the nature of time
- **Books XII-XIII:** Meditation — a running commentary on Genesis 1, creation and the soul's journey home

The opening line sets the tone for everything: *"Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee."* The entire work is an argument — lived, not merely stated — that this is so.

## The Early Years: Sin, Longing, and the Restless Heart

Augustine begins not with his birth but with a doxology — a prayer of praise that immediately raises theological questions about God's transcendence and immanence. God fills heaven and earth but is not contained by them; *"the vessels which Thou fillest uphold Thee not, since, though they were broken, Thou wert not poured out."* This paradox — God wholly present everywhere yet contained by nothing — frames Augustine's lifelong project: how can the finite soul contain the infinite God?

**Infancy and the origins of sin.** Augustine turns to his own infancy, which he remembers nothing of, and observes babies to infer what he was like. He sees not innocence but self-will: the infant who cries when desires are frustrated, the baby who grows envious of a foster-brother at the breast. *"The weakness then of infant limbs, not its will, is its innocence."* This is a radical claim: sin is not primarily about acts but about the orientation of the will, and that orientation is corrupted from the beginning.

**Schooling and misplaced love.** As a boy, Augustine was forced to learn — and he hated it. But his deeper complaint is about what he was forced to learn: the wanderings of Aeneas, the death of Dido. He wept for fictional characters while *"with dry eyes, I endured my miserable self dying among these things, far from Thee."* The inversion is devastating: he could feel grief for a poetic fiction but not for his own spiritual death. He calls classical education a *"hellish torrent"* — not because learning itself is bad, but because it trains the soul to admire the wrong things and to dress vice in eloquence.

**The pear-theft (Book II).** Perhaps the most famous episode: Augustine and his companions steal pears from a neighbor's tree — not because they were hungry or the fruit was desirable, but *"to do what we liked only, because it was misliked."* He analyzes this with unprecedented psychological honesty: *"It was foul, and I loved it; I loved to perish, I loved mine own fault, not that for which I was faulty, but my fault itself."* The theft was not about pears; it was about the pleasure of rebellion itself. This is Augustine's diagnosis of sin at its core: not merely wrong action, but a will that takes pleasure in its own turning-away from God.

**The mother and the father.** Monica, his mother, is portrayed as spiritually vigilant — she *"travailed in birth of my salvation"* and warned him against fornication. His father, still a catechumen, cared only that Augustine become eloquent and successful. The contrast is stark: the father wants a son who can speak well; the mother wants a son who can pray.

## Manichaeism, Neoplatonism, and the Search for Truth

After his schooling, Augustine spent nearly a decade as a Manichean — a dualistic religion that explained evil as the product of a separate dark principle opposing God. It appealed to his intellectual pride: it promised to solve the problem of evil without blaming human will, and it flattered his sense that the Christian Scriptures were too simple and contradictory.

**The failure of Manichaeism.** Augustine eventually came to respect a Manichean teacher named Faustus — and was deeply disappointed by him. Faustus was eloquent but empty of real knowledge. This was the beginning of the end. Augustine writes that he had *"burnt in my youth heretofore, to be satiated in things below"* — the language of addiction, of consuming without ever being filled.

**The Neoplatonic breakthrough (Book VII).** Before the full conversion, there was an intellectual turning. Augustine read *"the books of the Platonists"* (likely Plotinus and Porphyry) and experienced a profound shift: he grasped that God is not a body, not spatially extended, but spiritual and transcendent — yet present everywhere by His power. He came close to God *"by the footsteps of our cattle-nourisher"* (a reference to Moses). The Neoplatonists led him to see that the Good is one, immaterial, and the source of all being.

But they could not finish the work. Two critical gaps remained: they did not teach that the Word became flesh (the Incarnation), and they offered no path of grace — only intellectual ascent. Augustine writes: *"They did not cry out in every town to take our burdens from us."* Philosophy could show him the destination; only Christianity could carry him there.

**Monica's role.** Throughout these years, Monica prayed. Augustine records a dream she had, interpreted by a bishop: she and Augustine were walking on a broad golden floor, and the dream meant they would eventually share the same faith. Her persistence — tears, fasting, prayer — is portrayed not as manipulation but as maternal love aligned with God's own drawing.

## The Conversion: Garden Scene and Beyond

**The garden scene (Book VIII).** Augustine is thirty years old, living in Milan with his mother, tormented by an inner civil war. He wants to convert but cannot bring himself to let go of his concupiscence — his relationship with a concubine he had kept for eleven years. He describes his paralysis vividly: *"My flesh and my blood plotted against my soul."* Two wills, he says — one old, one new; one fleshly, one spiritual — are at war.

The crisis breaks when he hears a child's voice singing *"tolle et lege"* — *"take and read."* He opens Scripture and falls upon Romans 13:13-14: *"Not in rioting and drunkenness, not in chambering and wantonness... but put ye on the Lord Jesus Christ, and make not provision for the flesh."* The light floods in: *"The fear of loneliness fell from my heart."* He converts, is baptized by Ambrose at Easter, and Monica weeps tears of joy.

Note what Augustine emphasizes: the conversion is not self-generated. He tries and fails for years. The child's voice, the passage of Scripture — these are instruments of grace. His famous analysis of the will shows that the problem is not ignorance (he knew what was right) but a divided will that cannot execute what it affirms. Grace does not coerce; it heals the will's paralysis.

**Monica's death (Book IX).** Shortly after the conversion, the family travels to Cassiciacum, then to Rome. Monica falls ill at Piacenza. Her dying words to Augustine: *"My son, by whatever lot it may please God, I desire nothing more from this life."* She dies in peace. Augustine's meditation on her death is one of the most moving passages in the book — grief and gratitude intertwined, the recognition that she has found the rest he is only beginning to seek.

## Key Themes: Time, Memory, and Grace

**Book X: The search in memory.** After the narrative, Augustine turns inward. He searches his memory for God — not as a stored image but as the light by which all remembering is possible. His analysis is remarkably modern: memory is a vast inner space containing not just images but emotions, abstract concepts, and even forgotten things (which must exist somewhere to be recoverable). He distinguishes between knowing and remembering, between the body's sensations and the mind's self-awareness.

The climax of Book X is a startling confession: even after conversion, he is tempted by the prospect of sin — not to commit it, but to test whether grace truly holds him. *"I was in love with being in love with sin, not to sin but to love sin."* This is psychological honesty of a kind rarely matched: the converted person still carries the scars and temptations of the old life. Grace does not erase the battlefield; it changes who holds the ground.

**Book XI: The nature of time.** This is one of the most discussed chapters in the history of philosophy. Augustine asks: if God created time along with the universe, what was God doing "before"? The question is incoherent, he realizes — there was no "before" because time itself did not exist.

His analysis of time's structure is brilliant. The past no longer exists; the future does not yet exist; the present is a dimensionless point. So how can we measure time? His answer: time exists as a *"distentio"* — a stretching — of the mind. The past exists in memory, the future in expectation, the present in attention. *"Perhaps it is only correct to say, there are three times, a present of things past, a present of things present, and a present of things future."* We measure time not in the world but in the soul's own extended attention. This is a profound insight into the subjective nature of temporal experience.

**Grace throughout.** The thread binding all thirteen books is grace. Nothing good in Augustine — not his intellect, not his mother's faith, not his conversion, not his very existence — originates in himself. *"From Thee, O God, are all good things."* Even his sins are used by God for his good, though God did not create the sin itself.

## Tensions & Objections

**The strongest objection: Augustine's account of the will makes moral responsibility mysterious.**

The *Confessions* presents a picture of human agency that is deeply compelling but also deeply troubling. Augustine's great insight — that the will can be divided against itself, knowing the good yet choosing the bad — is psychologically true. But his resolution of that insight leans heavily toward a picture of grace that leaves little room for genuine human cooperation.

Consider the garden scene. Augustine agonizes for years, then a child's voice says *"tolle et lege,"* he reads a verse, and the chains fall off. The narrative presents this as grace — and theologically, it is. But the literary effect is to make conversion look like an event that happens *to* a person rather than something a person *does*. The years of struggle, reading, questioning, and partial turning are real — but the book's dramatic structure compresses the decisive moment into a divine intervention that bypasses the will entirely.

This is not merely a literary concern. It has theological consequences that Augustine himself would later develop into the doctrine of predestination — the idea that God's grace is irresistible and given only to the elect. The *Confessions* does not yet state this fully (that comes in *On the Spirit and the Letter* and *On Grace and Free Will*), but the seed is here: if even the desire to turn to God is itself a gift of grace (and Augustine would come to say it is), then human responsibility becomes a mystery bordering on incoherence. We are praised for what God gave us and blamed for what God did not.

**A second tension: the anti-classical polemic is overdrawn.**

Augustine's denunciation of classical education as a *"hellish torrent"* is rhetorically powerful but intellectually unfair. He learned to read and write through the same classical training he condemns — and he uses the rhetorical skills it gave him to write the *Confessions* itself. His real target is not learning but *misplaced love* — loving the wrong objects with the right intensity. But the book often conflates the two, suggesting that classical culture is inherently corrupting rather than merely capable of being misused. A fairer account would distinguish between the tools (grammar, rhetoric, logic) and the ends to which they are directed.

**A third tension: the portrait of Monica is hagiographic to the point of distortion.**

Monica is presented as nearly flawless — patient, prayerful, spiritually perceptive. But Augustine rarely shows her interiority; she is a function in his story (the praying mother) rather than a full person. Her own doubts, frustrations, and complexities are largely absent. This is not dishonesty so much as genre: the *Confessions* is Augustine's prayer, not a biography of his family. Still, the one-sidedness is worth noting — especially since Monica's own journey of faith likely involved struggles Augustine never witnessed or never chose to record.

## Actionable Learning

**1. Distinguish between the object of love and the intensity of love.** Augustine's central diagnostic is not that we love too much but that we love the wrong things with the right intensity. The restless heart is not a defect; it is a compass. When you feel driven toward something — status, relationships, achievement — ask: is this an object worthy of the energy I'm investing? Misplaced love is not cured by loving less; it is cured by loving better.

**2. Examine the will, not just the intellect.** Augustine's great contribution is showing that knowing the good is not the same as doing it. The divided will — *"I will, but I do not"* — is the real battlefield. Self-examination should target not just false beliefs but competing commitments. What do you reach for when no one is watching? What do you defend when cornered? Those reveal the will's actual orientation more honestly than your stated values.

**3. Time is measured in the mind, not the clock.** Augustine's analysis of time as *distentio animi* (stretching of the mind) has practical implications. We experience time differently depending on our attention: suffering stretches it, joy compresses it. This means you have some agency over your temporal experience — not by controlling events but by shaping what you attend to. Memory, attention, and expectation are the three handles. Train them deliberately.

**4. Confess to someone who cannot be impressed.** Augustine writes to God precisely because God cannot be flattered, manipulated, or shamed into a different verdict. Modern self-reflection often suffers from performance anxiety — even private journaling can become self-branding. Whether that is God, a trusted friend, or simply the discipline of writing without an imagined reader, the point is the same: strip away the performance.

**5. Use the pear-theft test.** When you catch yourself doing something pointless and slightly wrong, ask: what am I actually getting from this? Augustine stole pears he didn't want, to give to pigs he didn't care about. The real object was the rebellion itself. Modern equivalents are everywhere: scrolling to be angry, arguing to feel clever, consuming to feel numb. Name the real object.
