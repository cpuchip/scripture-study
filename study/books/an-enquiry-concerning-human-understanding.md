# An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding

**Author:** David Hume  
**Source:** [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/9662/9662-h/9662-h.htm)

---

## The core argument / thesis

Hume argues that all human reasoning about matters of fact depends on the relation of cause and effect, which we cannot discover by reason *a priori* but only through experience; yet experience itself provides no non-circular rational justification for inferring that the future will resemble the past. Therefore our most essential beliefs about the world rest not on reasoning but on *custom* or *habit*—an instinctive principle of human nature that exposes the narrow limits of the understanding.

---

## Structure

The Enquiry builds its case in three escalating arcs.

1. **Preliminaries (Sections I–III):** Hume first defends “accurate and abstruse” philosophy against popular scorn, then maps the mind’s contents into *impressions* and *ideas*, defending the Copy Principle (all ideas are derived from prior impressions) and the three associative principles of thought—resemblance, contiguity, and causation.
2. **The skeptical engine (Sections IV–VII):** Hume introduces his famous “fork” between relations of ideas and matters of fact, shows that causal knowledge is founded entirely on experience, and then demonstrates that any attempt to justify inductive inference by reason is viciously circular. The “skeptical solution” is that custom, not reason, drives expectation. He then extends this to probability and, crucially, to the idea of *necessary connexion*, arguing that we mistake an internal feeling of expectation for an observed power in objects.
3. **Applications (Sections VIII–IX):** He applies the account of necessity to the debate over liberty and necessity, contending that human actions exhibit the same regular conjunction as physical causes, and that true liberty means absence of constraint, not absence of causal determination. Finally, he shows that animals too learn from experience and custom, confirming that reason is not the source of our empirical beliefs.

## Key passages

> “All the objects of human reason or enquiry may naturally be divided into two kinds, to wit, Relations of Ideas, and Matters of Fact.” *(Sect. IV.20)*
> — Hume’s foundational distinction: necessary truths versus contingent empirical claims.

> “I shall venture to affirm, as a general proposition, which admits of no exception, that the knowledge of this relation is not, in any instance, attained by reasonings a priori; but arises entirely from experience, when we find that any particular objects are constantly conjoined with each other.” *(Sect. IV.23)*
> — The declaration that causal knowledge cannot be discovered by pure reason alone.

> “To endeavour, therefore, the proof of this last supposition by probable arguments, or arguments regarding existence, must be evidently going in a circle, and taking that for granted, which is the very point in question.” *(Sect. IV.30)*
> — The classic statement of the problem of induction: any experiential defense of induction assumes the very uniformity it must prove.

> “This principle is Custom or Habit. For wherever the repetition of any particular act or operation produces a propensity to renew the same act or operation, without being impelled by any reasoning or process of the understanding, we always say, that this propensity is the effect of Custom.” *(Sect. V.36)*
> — Custom identified as the true, non-rational engine of inference from experience.

> “This connexion, therefore, which we feel in the mind, this customary transition of the imagination from one object to its usual attendant, is the sentiment or impression from which we form the idea of power or necessary connexion.” *(Sect. VII.59)*
> — The idea of necessary connection is traced not to any perceived force in nature but to an internal feeling bred by repetition.

> “Custom, then, is the great guide of human life. It is that principle alone which renders our experience useful to us, and makes us expect, for the future, a similar train of events with those which have appeared in the past.” *(Sect. V.36, note)*
> — The blunt conclusion that human action and expectation rely on instinctive habit rather than rational demonstration.

## Themes

- **The limits of reason.** Human understanding is bounded by what can be traced to impressions. Reason alone cannot establish matters of fact, causal connections, or the uniformity of nature.
- **Custom as foundation.** Repetition produces an unavoidable propensity to expect the future to resemble the past. This principle of custom is “the great guide of human life” and the true source of empirical belief.
- **The Copy Principle.** All ideas are copies of impressions; therefore any philosophical term that cannot be linked to an original impression is suspect.
- **Necessity as mental projection.** What we call “necessary connexion” is not observed in objects. It is a sentiment produced in the mind by habitual transition after repeated instances of conjunction.
- **Reconciling liberty and necessity.** Moral responsibility requires that actions flow from durable character; thus “necessity” in human conduct is not opposed to liberty properly understood (absence of external constraint).
- **Continuity of human and animal cognition.** Animals learn from experience and custom rather than reasoning, suggesting that human empirical inference is likewise an instinct rather than a product of rational argument.

---

## Tensions and open questions

- **Is Hume a skeptic or a naturalist?** He exposes the impotence of reason but seems content to let custom carry us forward. Is this genuine epistemic humility, or does it leave us without resources when custom fails?
- **The status of the Copy Principle.** Hume asserts that all ideas derive from impressions, yet the idea of “necessary connexion” is said to come from an internal sentiment—still an impression, but of reflection rather than sensation. Does this stretch the principle, or does it show its flexibility?
- **Induction unresolved.** Hume shows we cannot *justify* induction by reason, but he does not offer an alternative foundation that satisfies the rationalist. Is custom a replacement for justification, or an admission that justification is impossible?
- **Liberty and moral responsibility.** If human actions are as causally determined as physical events, what remains of moral responsibility? Hume argues that predictable character is precisely what makes blame and praise meaningful, but this remains contested.

## What’s worth learning — and what we could do with it

1. **Run the Copy Principle audit on your own vocabulary.** Before accepting a claim as meaningful, trace its key terms back to original impressions (sensory or introspective). For a substrate, this means flagging concepts that cannot be grounded in observed data or internal state as suspect — not meaningless, but requiring explicit operational definition.

2. **Separate “relations of ideas” from “matters of fact” before arguing.** In any dispute, classify whether the disagreement is about definitions, logic, or mathematics (where demonstration applies) or about empirical reality (where only evidence and induction apply). Most futile arguments stem from confusing the two categories.

3. **Treat causal claims as observed regularities plus expectation, not as discovered forces.** When building models or making predictions, replace language about “necessary connections” or “inherent powers” with explicit records of conjunction frequency and confidence intervals. This makes uncertainty visible rather than disguising it as necessity.

4. **Identify the custom behind your expectations.** When you find yourself assuming a pattern will continue, ask: how many repetitions created this habit? Is the sample large enough, or is this a Humean overreach from a few instances? Consciously inspecting the origin of an expectation is the only defense against custom’s errors.

5. **Design systems that learn from repetition without requiring deductive justification.** For empirical learning — whether in personal habits, organizational processes, or substrate behavior — favor accumulated experience and pattern recognition over attempts to build everything from first principles. Reason is a scalpel; custom is the engine that moves us.

6. **Apply the skeptical engine to your own foundations.** Periodically examine whether your most confident empirical beliefs rest on circular reasoning. You cannot ultimately justify induction by induction, but you can become more honest about where your confidence comes from — and more cautious where custom is thin.
