## What's Worth Learning — and What We Could Do With It

Despite the corruption, the readable opening of Part I contains ideas that remain actionable. Here are concrete takeaways a person or this substrate could actually try:

### 1. Audit Your Own "Scholastic Education"

Descartes rejected the scholastic curriculum not because it was false, but because it produced certainty without understanding. **Action:** Pick one belief or methodology you inherited from your field's orthodoxy and ask: "Do I hold this because I've verified it, or because it was taught to me?" Write down the answer before defending the belief.

### 2. Read "the Great Book of the World" Before the Books About It

Descartes turned from libraries to direct observation — travel, conversation, experience. **Action:** For the next research question you tackle, spend your first hour gathering primary observations (data, interviews, field notes) before reading secondary literature. Note what changes in your hypothesis after that first hour.

### 3. Treat "Good Sense Equally Distributed" as a Working Hypothesis

Descartes opens by claiming good sense (reason) is equally distributed — people disagree because they follow different paths. **Action:** When you encounter a strong disagreement, first assume the other party has reached their position through a coherent (if different) chain of reasoning. Map their path before attacking their conclusion. This is a discipline, not a platitude.

### 4. Build a Personal "Method" — Four Rules, Not Forty

The full *Discourse* (unavailable in this corrupted source) lays out four methodological rules: never accept anything as true unless it is clearly and distinctly so; divide difficulties into as many parts as possible; conduct thoughts in an ordered progression from simple to complex; make enumerations so complete that nothing is omitted. **Action:** Write down your own four rules for how you make decisions or evaluate claims. Post them where you'll see them. Review quarterly.

### 5. Provisional Maxims While You're Still Doubting

Descartes adopted temporary moral rules while his philosophical reconstruction was incomplete — obey laws and customs, be firm in action, master oneself rather than fortune. **Action:** Identify one area of your life where you're still uncertain about the "right" approach. Write a provisional rule for it — something good enough to live by while you keep investigating. Revisit it after a set period (e.g., 90 days).

### 6. For This Substrate: Flag Corrupted Sources Before Digesting

This null case demonstrates a real failure mode. **Action:** Add a source-quality check to the book-digesting pipeline. Before attempting a digest, verify that the source text has sufficient unique content (e.g., check for repetition ratios, minimum length thresholds, or completeness markers). Reject corrupted sources early rather than producing misleading digests.
