# A Discourse on Method

**By René Descartes** (1596–1650)
Translated by John Veitch; originally published 1637

---

## Provenance

> "My present design, then, is not to teach the method which each ought to follow for the right conduct of his reason, but solely to describe the way in which I have endeavored to conduct my own."

Descartes frames this not as a textbook but as an intellectual autobiography—a "history" or "tale" in which readers may find "some examples worthy of imitation" alongside others "which it were advisable not to follow." Written in French (deliberately, not Latin) and published in 1637 alongside essays on optics, meteorology, and geometry, it is the foundational text of modern Western philosophy.

---

## Structure

The author's own prefatory note divides the work into six parts:

1. **Considerations touching the Sciences**—why existing education failed him
2. **The principal rules of the Method**—four precepts for conducting reason
3. **Rules of Morals deduced from the Method**—a provisional ethics for living while seeking truth
4. **Foundations of Metaphysics**—proofs for God's existence and the immortality of the soul
5. **Physical questions investigated**—the motion of the heart, medicine, the difference between human and animal souls
6. **What is required for greater advancement in the investigation of Nature**—a call for cooperative scientific enterprise

---

## Part I: The Crisis of Learning

### The Opening Thesis

> "Good sense is, of all things among men, the most equally distributed; for every one thinks himself so abundantly provided with it, that those even who are the most difficult to satisfy in everything else, do not usually desire a larger measure of this quality than they already possess."

This is one of the most quoted opening lines in philosophy. Descartes' point is not that everyone is equally intelligent, but that **reason itself**—the capacity to judge truth from error—is distributed equally. The difference between people lies not in the *possession* of reason but in how they *apply* it:

> "The greatest minds, as they are capable of the highest excellences, are open likewise to the greatest aberrations; and those who travel very slowly may yet make far greater progress, provided they keep always to the straight road, than those who, while they run, forsake it."

### The Disillusionment

Descartes received the finest education available at one of Europe's most celebrated schools. He mastered the curriculum and devoured rare books beyond it. Yet when he finished:

> "I found myself involved in so many doubts and errors, that I was convinced I had advanced no farther in all my attempts at learning, than the discovery at every turn of my own ignorance."

This is the hinge of the entire book. Not that he learned nothing, but that **learning made him more uncertain, not more certain**. The education system produced doubt rather than knowledge.

### His Verdict on the Various Sciences

Descartes runs through the curriculum of his day with a discriminating eye:

- **Languages and the ancients**: Useful in moderation for understanding other writings and avoiding provincialism, but excessive study makes one "a stranger to [one's] native country" and ignorant of the present.
- **Fiction and history**: Fables "lead us to imagine the possibility of events that are impossible"; even faithful histories omit the ordinary details that make truth recognizable, leaving readers prone to "entertain projects that exceed their powers."
- **Eloquence and poetry**: "Gifts of nature rather than fruits of study." A person with strong reason can persuade in "the language of Lower Brittany" without knowing rhetoric.
- **Mathematics**: Delighted him for "the certitude and evidence of their reasonings," yet he was troubled that "foundations, so strong and solid, should have had no loftier superstructure reared on them."
- **Ancient moral philosophy**: Compared to "very towering and magnificent palaces with no better foundation than sand and mud." They praise virtue but offer "no adequate criterion of virtue," and what they call virtue is often "apathy, or pride, or despair, or parricide."
- **Theology**: He reveres it but refuses to subject revealed truths to reason, recognizing they are "above our comprehension" and require "some special help from heaven."
- **Philosophy**: The deepest disappointment. "It had been cultivated for many ages by the most distinguished men, and yet there is not a single matter within its sphere which is not still in dispute, and nothing, therefore, which is above doubt." Where learned men hold conflicting opinions and only one can be true, he "reckoned as well-nigh false all that was only probable."
- **Pseudo-sciences** (alchemy, astrology, magic): He claims sufficient discernment "to escape being deceived."

### The Turn to the "Great Book of the World"

> "I entirely abandoned the study of letters, and resolved no longer to seek any other science than the knowledge of myself, or of the great book of the world."

He traveled, visited courts and armies, interacted with people of all ranks, and—most importantly—reflected on his experiences. His insight:

> "I should find much more truth in the reasonings of each individual with reference to the affairs in which he is personally interested, and the issue of which must presently punish him if he has judged amiss, than in those conducted by a man of letters in his study, regarding speculative matters that are of no practical moment, and followed by no consequences to himself."

**Practical stakes produce better reasoning than abstract speculation.** This is a proto-empiricist insight: knowledge tested by consequences is more reliable than knowledge floating in the study.

## Part II: The Four Rules of Method

*(Summarized from the book's well-known content; the full text was not available in the corrupted source.)*

Descartes derives four precepts from his observation that mathematics achieves certainty through method:

1. **Never accept anything as true that I do not know evidently to be so**—avoid precipitacy and prejudice; admit only what presents itself to the mind "so clearly and distinctly" that there is no occasion to call it into doubt.
2. **Divide each difficulty into as many parts as possible**—analysis: break complex problems into manageable pieces.
3. **Conduct thoughts in an orderly progression**—begin with the simplest objects and rise by degrees to the more complex.
4. **Make enumerations so complete and reviews so general that nothing is omitted**—synthesis: verify completeness.

Rules 1 and 4 are about *epistemic discipline* (what to accept, how to verify). Rules 2 and 3 are about *procedure* (how to proceed). Together they form a method for replacing the confused scholastic tradition with mathematical-style certainty.

The famous **"Cogito"** emerges from this framework: if I doubt everything, the very act of doubting proves I exist as a thinking thing. *"I think, therefore I am"* (Cogito, ergo sum) is the first indubitable truth.

---

## Part III: Provisional Morals

While demolishing old beliefs and rebuilding knowledge from scratch, one must still live. Descartes proposes a **provisional moral code**:

- Follow the laws and customs of one's country, conforming to the opinions of the most judicious people around one.
- Be as resolute as possible in acting on whatever one has determined, and conduct oneself as if those determinations were certain—even if they are only the best available.
- Master oneself rather than fortune; change one's desires rather than the order of the world.

This is a morality of **intellectual honesty and practical resolve**, designed to sustain a life of inquiry without paralysis.

---

## Part IV: Proofs for God and the Soul

Descartes reconstructs metaphysics from the Cogito:

- From the idea of perfection in his mind, he argues that an actually perfect being (God) must exist, since only a perfect being could cause the idea of perfection in an imperfect mind.
- God's existence guarantees that clear and distinct perceptions are true (God is not a deceiver).
- The mind (a thinking thing) is fundamentally distinct from the body (an extended thing), establishing **mind-body dualism**.
- The soul, being non-material, is not subject to bodily corruption and is therefore immortal.

This section is the most theologically conservative in the book and was the one Descartes most worried would offend the Church.

---

## Part V: Physics and the Mechanization of Nature

Descartes turns to the physical world, treating it mechanistically:

- **The heart and circulation**: He offers an account of the heart's motion and blood circulation (independently of Harvey, though less accurately).
- **The difference between humans and animals**: Animals are pure machines—automata without souls. Humans possess a rational soul that interacts with the body (through the pineal gland, in his theory).
- **Optics and light**: He develops a theory of light and refraction that anticipates aspects of modern physics.

The key move: **nature operates by mechanical laws**, not by final causes or hidden qualities. This is the birth of the mechanistic worldview that dominates modern science.

---

## Part VI: The Promise and Limits of Method

Descartes concludes with a vision of what method can achieve:

- Medicine can be greatly improved by understanding the mechanical causes of disease.
- All the arts useful in life can be transformed if we know the properties of all natural bodies.
- He calls for **cooperative scientific enterprise**: multiple researchers working together, sharing findings, to advance knowledge faster than any individual could.

But he also acknowledges limits: the method requires time, patience, and the right temperament. Not everyone is suited to it, and the fruits may take generations to mature.

## Key Ideas Extracted

### 1. Radical Doubt as a Tool
Descartes does not believe we *should* doubt everything in daily life. Rather, he uses **hyperbolic doubt** as a methodological tool to find an unshakeable foundation. If you can doubt it, it's not a proper foundation. What survives the doubt is what matters.

### 2. The Primacy of Method Over Content
The book is less about *what* Descartes concludes than *how* he concludes it. The method—clear and distinct perception, analysis, ordered progression, complete enumeration—is transferable. Any domain that can be made clear and distinct can be known.

### 3. The Democratization of Reason
> "The power of judging aright and of distinguishing truth from error, which is properly what is called good sense or reason, is by nature equal in all men."

This is deeply egalitarian. Intellectual authority does not come from pedigree, institution, or tradition—it comes from correctly applying a method that anyone can learn. This undermined the entire scholastic edifice.

### 4. Mechanism and Dualism
By making the physical world a machine, Descartes freed science from theological interference. But by making the mind non-physical, he created the **mind-body problem** that philosophy has not solved in 400 years.

### 5. The Great Book of the World
The shift from books to direct engagement with reality—travel, experience, reflection—is a proto-empiricist move. Knowledge comes from engaging with the world, not just reading authorities.

---

## The Strongest Objections

### 1. The Cartesian Circle
Descartes proves God exists using clear and distinct perceptions. He then says clear and distinct perceptions are reliable because God exists and is not a deceiver. This is **circular reasoning**: he needs God to guarantee his method, but needs his method to prove God.

### 2. The Arrogance of Starting from Scratch
> "I reckoned as well-nigh false all that was only probable."

Descartes dismisses centuries of accumulated knowledge because philosophers disagree. But disagreement does not imply falsehood—it may imply complexity. The assumption that one individual, starting from scratch, can rebuild all knowledge is hubristic. Modern epistemology recognizes that knowledge is often **socially constructed and cumulative**, not individually discovered from first principles.

### 3. The Problem of Induction in Disguise
Rule 1 demands "evident" certainty before accepting anything. But much of what we know (science, history, even much of daily life) is **probabilistic, not certain**. By demanding mathematical-style certainty, Descartes excludes most genuine knowledge.

### 4. Dualism Creates an Unsolvable Problem
If mind and body are fundamentally different substances, how do they interact? Descartes' answer (the pineal gland) is ad hoc. The mind-body problem remains one of philosophy's greatest failures, arguably a direct consequence of this book.

### 5. The Provisional Morality Is Insufficient
The provisional code—"follow local customs, be resolute, master yourself"—is essentially **moral conformism**. It defers to authority precisely when the method should be most rigorously applied. There is a tension between radical intellectual independence and moral conformity that Descartes never resolves.

### 6. Mechanism Erases Meaning
By reducing nature to machines, Descartes makes the world intelligible but also **disenchanted**. Final causes, purposes, and meanings are expelled from nature. This is scientifically powerful but philosophically impoverishing—it explains how things work but not why they matter.

## What We Could Actually Do With This

### For Individual Thinking
- **Apply the four rules to your own reasoning**: When facing a complex problem, (1) suspend judgment on what isn't clear, (2) break it into parts, (3) start with the simplest elements, (4) verify completeness. This is a genuine thinking tool.
- **Distinguish between doubt as a tool and doubt as a habit**: Radical doubt is useful for foundations; in daily life, probabilistic reasoning is sufficient and often superior.
- **Seek practical stakes**: Descartes' insight that "the issue of which must presently punish him if he has judged amiss" produces better reasoning applies to decision-making. Tie ideas to consequences.

### For the Substrate
- **Method before content**: When researching any topic, first establish *how* to evaluate claims (clear and distinct? sourced? testable?) before accumulating claims.
- **The "great book" principle**: Prioritize direct engagement with sources over secondary summaries. Read the primary text, not the commentary on the commentary.
- **Cooperative enterprise**: Descartes' call for collaborative knowledge-building is the ancestor of open science and the substrate's own design. Multiple agents, shared findings, cumulative progress.
- **Name the provisional**: When the substrate holds a working model that isn't yet certain, mark it as provisional—like Descartes' moral code—and act on it while continuing inquiry.

### For Reading and Study
- **The Descartes test for education**: Does learning make you more certain or more confused? If your education only produces doubt without method for resolving it, it has failed Descartes' standard.
- **Beware the palace on sand**: Grand theories with weak foundations (Descartes' critique of ancient moralists) are still common. Check the foundation before admiring the architecture.
- **The humility of "copper and glass"**: Descartes acknowledges he might mistake "copper and glass" for "gold and diamonds." Intellectual humility—recognizing your own judgments are suspect—is the starting point, not the endpoint, of the method.

---

## Final Assessment

*A Discourse on Method* is the founding document of modern philosophy and, indirectly, modern science. Its central achievement is showing that **knowledge requires a method, not just accumulation**. Its central failure is the dualism it creates and the circularity in its metaphysical foundations.

The book is short, elegant, and still readable. Descartes writes not as a professor but as a traveler describing his path. The invitation is not "agree with me" but "walk this road and see where it leads." Whether one accepts his conclusions or not, the method—the demand for clarity, the discipline of doubt, the commitment to ordered reasoning—remains one of the most important intellectual tools ever devised.

> "Those who travel very slowly may yet make far greater progress, provided they keep always to the straight road, than those who, while they run, forsake it."

This is the book's enduring lesson: **method matters more than speed**.
