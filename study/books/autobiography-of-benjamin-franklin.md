# Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin


## Overview and Context

Benjamin Franklin's *Autobiography* is an unfinished memoir written between 1771 and 1790, covering his life from birth in 1706 to age 42 (1748). Written initially for his son William, it became one of the most influential autobiographies ever published. Franklin tells the story of a boy from humble Boston origins who, through industry, self-education, and practical wisdom, rose to become a successful printer, civic leader, scientist, and eventually one of America's most important statesmen.

The work is remarkable for its plain, conversational style — Franklin writes as if telling his story to a friend. He selected and arranged his material with "the unerring instinct of the journalist for the best effects" (F. W. Pine, editor). The autobiography ends abruptly when Franklin was 42, leaving three decades of his remarkable life unrecorded, including his diplomatic service in France and his role in the American Revolution.

**Source**: Project Gutenberg, ed. Frank Woodworth Pine (1916), based on John Bigelow's standard edition.

## Early Life and Family (1706-1717)

Franklin was born January 6, 1706, in Boston, in a house on Milk Street. His father, Josiah Franklin, was a tallow chandler (candle maker) who had emigrated from England, where the family had lived for at least 300 years in the village of Ecton, Northamptonshire. Josiah married twice and fathered 17 children; Benjamin was the youngest son and second-to-youngest child.

Josiah Franklin proved to be a formative influence. Franklin describes him as a man of "sound understanding and solid judgment in prudential matters." Though constrained by his trade and large family from public office, he was frequently consulted by "leading people, who consulted him for his opinion in affairs of the town or of the church." At his table, Franklin's father "always took care to start some ingenious or useful topic for discourse, which might tend to improve the minds of his children. By this means he turned our attention to what was good, just, and prudent in the conduct of life."

Franklin's mother, Abiah, "had likewise an excellent constitution: she suckled all her ten children." Both parents lived to old age — Josiah died at 89, Abiah at 85. Franklin later placed a marble over their grave inscribed:

> "Without an estate, or any gainful employment, / By constant labor and industry, / with God's blessing, / They maintained a large family / comfortably, / and brought up thirteen children / and seven grandchildren / reputably. / From this instance, reader, / Be encouraged to diligence in thy calling, / And distrust not Providence."

Franklin received only two years of formal schooling, ending at age ten. His father initially intended to devote him "as the tithe of his sons, to the service of the Church," but financial constraints prevented college. Instead, Franklin was destined for trade.

## Apprenticeship and the New England Courant (1717-1722)

At age 12, Franklin was apprenticed to his brother James, a printer who published the *New England Courant* — the second newspaper to appear in America (after the *Boston News-Letter*). The apprenticeship was intended to prevent Franklin from pursuing a career at sea, which his father feared after another son had run off to sea "to his great vexation."

James Franklin proved to be a difficult master. Though brothers, "he considered himself as my master, and me as his apprentice, and, accordingly, expected the same services from me as he would from another, while I thought he demean'd me too much in some he requir'd of me, who from a brother expected more indulgence." Their disputes were often brought before their father, and Franklin "fancy I was either generally in the right, or else a better pleader, because the judgment was generally in my favor. But my brother was passionate, and had often beaten me, which I took extreamly amiss."

Franklin secretly contributed to the *Courant* under a pseudonym, writing anonymous papers that he slipped under the printing-house door at night. "I had the exquisite pleasure of finding it met with their approbation, and that, in their different guesses at the author, none were named but men of some character among us for learning and ingenuity." When he finally revealed himself, "I began to be considered a little more by my brother's acquaintance, and in a manner that did not quite please him, as he thought, probably with reason, that it tended to make me too vain."

A political controversy erupted when one of the newspaper's pieces offended the Massachusetts Assembly. James was imprisoned for a month for refusing to reveal the author. During his brother's confinement, Franklin managed the paper and "made bold to give our rulers some rubs in it." After James's release, the Assembly ordered that "James Franklin should no longer print the paper called the New England Courant." The paper continued under Benjamin's name through a "very flimsy scheme" of forged indentures.

When a fresh difference arose, Franklin "took upon me to assert my freedom, presuming that he would not venture to produce the new indentures. It was not fair in me to take this advantage, and this I therefore reckon one of the first errata of my life."

## Flight to Philadelphia (1723)

Franklin left Boston in 1723 at age 17, fleeing to Philadelphia. He had become "a little obnoxious to the governing party" and his "indiscrete disputations about religion began to make me pointed at with horror by good people as an infidel or atheist." His father now sided with his brother, so Franklin left secretly, arranged by a friend who got him passage on a New York bound ship.

The journey was arduous. At sea, Franklin was becalmed off Block Island. The crew began catching cod, and Franklin — who had adopted vegetarianism after reading the philosopher Matthew Tryon — faced a crisis of conscience:

> "Hitherto I had stuck to my resolution of not eating animal food, and on this occasion consider'd, with my master Tryon, the taking every fish as a kind of unprovoked murder, since none of them had, or ever could do us any injury that might justify the slaughter. All this seemed very reasonable. But I had formerly been a great lover of fish, and, when this came hot out of the frying-pan, it smelt admirably well. I balanc'd some time between principle and inclination, till I recollected that, when the fish were opened, I saw smaller fish taken out of their stomachs; then thought I, 'If you eat one another, I don't see why we mayn't eat you.' So I din'd upon cod very heartily, and continued to eat with other people, returning only now and then occasionally to a vegetable diet. So convenient a thing it is to be a *reasonable creature*, since it enables one to find or make a reason for everything one has a mind to do."

This self-aware irony — recognizing how easily reason serves inclination — is characteristic of Franklin's honest self-portrait.

In Philadelphia, Franklin arrived with little money and no clear prospects. He found work with printer Andrew Bradford, then with a man named Keimer. The autobiography ends its first major arc with Franklin establishing himself in Philadelphia, setting the stage for his rise.

## Years with Keimer and Self-Education

Franklin's years working for Keimer were formative. Keimer's printing-house was modest — "an old shatter'd press, and one small, worn-out font of English." Keimer himself was something of a pretender, composing verses directly into type without copy, and wearing his beard "at full length, because somewhere in the Mosaic law it is said, 'Thou shalt not mar the corners of thy beard.'" He also kept the Seventh day as Sabbath.

Franklin used this period for intense self-education. With Keimer's workers often absent at meal times, Franklin "remained there alone, and, despatching presently my light repast, which often was no more than a bisket or a slice of bread, a handful of raisins or a tart from the pastry-cook's, and a glass of water, had the rest of the time till their return for study, in which I made the greater progress, from that greater clearness of head and quicker apprehension which usually attend temperance in eating and drinking."

He taught himself arithmetic using Cocker's book, studied navigation, and read Locke's *On Human Understanding* and the *Art of Thinking* by Messrs. du Port Royal. He also read Xenophon's *Memorable Things of Socrates*, which introduced him to the Socratic method of questioning.

Franklin adopted this method, "dropt my abrupt contradiction and positive argumentation, and put on the humble inquirer and doubter." He became "very artful and expert in drawing people, even of superior knowledge, into concessions, the consequences of which they did not foresee, entangling them in difficulties out of which they could not extricate themselves."

Though he later abandoned the manipulative aspect, he retained "the habit of expressing myself in terms of modest diffidence; never using, when I advanced any thing that may possibly be disputed, the words *certainly, undoubtedly*, or any others that give the air of positiveness to an opinion; but rather say, I conceive or apprehend a thing to be so and so; it appears to me, or *I should think it so or so*."

Franklin believed this habit "has been of great advantage to me when I have had occasion to inculcate my opinions, and persuade men into measures." He quotes Pope: "Men should be taught as if you taught them not, / And things unknown propos'd as things forgot."

## Method of Self-Improvement

Franklin's approach to self-improvement was systematic and pragmatic. Several key methods emerge from the text:

**1. Temperance as Foundation**: Franklin observed that moderation in eating and drinking produced "greater clearness of head and quicker apprehension" — a cognitive advantage that enabled his intensive self-education.

**2. The Socratic Method**: Rather than arguing dogmatically, Franklin learned to ask questions that led opponents to contradict themselves. This was initially a tool of intellectual combat, but he refined it into a genuine habit of modest expression.

**3. Honest Self-Correction**: Franklin openly acknowledges his failures. He calls his deception of his brother "one of the first errata of my life" — using the printer's term for a mistake. He doesn't excuse himself; he names the error and moves on.

**4. Practical Reasoning**: The cod-fish episode reveals Franklin's understanding that reason often serves inclination. His wry observation — "So convenient a thing it is to be a *reasonable creature*, since it enables one to find or make a reason for everything one has a mind to do" — shows intellectual honesty about human self-deception.

**5. Learning from Observation**: Franklin learned by watching skilled workers handle their tools, which "has been useful to me, having learnt so much by it as to be able to do little jobs myself in my house when a workman could not readily be got, and to construct little machines for my experiments."

The autobiography itself models these methods: Franklin writes plainly, admits his errors, and arranges his story to instruct rather than merely to impress. As the editor notes, Franklin displayed "hard common sense and a practical knowledge of the art of living."

## Business Development

Franklin's rise from apprentice to successful businessman followed a pattern of seizing opportunities and building useful connections. After leaving Keimer, he worked for Governor Keith, who promised to help him establish a printing business — promises that ultimately proved empty. Franklin then went to London, where he worked as a compositor before returning to Philadelphia.

The introduction notes that Franklin eventually set up his own printing house and published *The Pennsylvania Gazette*, "which he made a medium for agitating a variety of local reforms." In 1732 he began issuing *Poor Richard's Almanac*, enriching it with "pithy utterances of worldly wisdom which are the basis of a large part of his popular reputation." In 1758, he printed "Father Abraham's Sermon" in the Almanac, "now regarded as the most famous piece of literature produced in Colonial America."

By 1748, Franklin had achieved sufficient wealth that he sold his business "in order to get leisure for study." This decision — to retreat from active commerce once financial security was achieved — reflects a deliberate prioritization of intellectual and public pursuits over mere accumulation.

Franklin's business success was built on the same principles he applied to self-improvement: industry, frugality, and practical judgment. He understood that reputation and trust were as valuable as capital.

## Public Service and Scientific Work

Though the autobiography proper ends at age 42, the editor's introduction sketches Franklin's later achievements, which give context to the trajectory Franklin was on:

**Civic Institutions**: Franklin "set forth a scheme for an Academy, which was taken up later and finally developed into the University of Pennsylvania; and he founded an 'American Philosophical Society' for the purpose of enabling scientific men to communicate their discoveries to one another."

**Scientific Work**: Franklin "had already begun his electrical researches, which, with other scientific inquiries, he carried on in the intervals of money-making and politics to the end of his life." He "perceived the identity of lightning and electricity and set up the lightning rod. He invented the Franklin stove, still widely used, and refused to patent it." Sir Humphry Davy praised "the precision and clearness of the style in which he described his experiments, as to the results he was able to announce."

**Political Service**: Franklin served as agent for Pennsylvania in London, where he "actively opposed the proposed Stamp Act." His most notable domestic service was "his reform of the postal system." His fame as a statesman "rests chiefly on his services in connection with the relations of the Colonies with Great Britain, and later with France."

The introduction is candid about Franklin's flaws: "his record as an office-holder is stained by the use he made of his position to advance his relatives." This honesty — including the negative alongside the positive — models the kind of clear-eyed assessment Franklin himself practiced.

## Key Themes and Lessons

Several themes run through Franklin's autobiography:

**Industry and Frugality**: From his parents' epitaph to his own practices, Franklin consistently links moral character to practical habits. His father maintained a large family "By constant labor and industry, / with God's blessing." Franklin himself practiced temperance in eating to preserve mental clarity for study.

**Self-Education**: Franklin received only two years of formal schooling but became one of the most learned men of his age through disciplined self-study. He reads Locke, Xenophon, and scientific texts — not passively, but with the goal of practical improvement.

**Honest Self-Assessment**: Franklin doesn't flatter himself. He names his deceptions ("the first errata of my life"), his vanity, his sauciness, and his capacity for self-deception (the cod-fish episode). This intellectual honesty is itself a moral practice.

**Practical Wisdom Over Abstract Theory**: Franklin's Socratic method isn't philosophical speculation — it's a tool for navigating human relationships. His modest expressions ("I conceive or apprehend a thing to be so and so") serve the practical goal of persuasion without provoking opposition.

**Public-Spiritedness**: Even in his youth, Franklin's ambitions extend beyond personal success. He uses the *Courant* to challenge authority, and later his business success funds civic projects. The autobiography implicitly argues that personal improvement and public service are not separate projects but one.

**Providence and Agency**: Franklin's parents' epitaph blesses "constant labor and industry" alongside "God's blessing." Franklin doesn't see these as competing explanations — human effort and divine grace operate together.

## Tensions & Objections

**The strongest objection to Franklin's autobiography is that it presents a theodicy of success dressed as honest self-portrait.**

Franklin's narrative implies that industry, temperance, and practical wisdom naturally produce success. But this is survivorship bias: countless industrious, temperate people failed through no moral fault of their own. Franklin's rise depended on historical contingencies — being in the right place during America's colonial expansion, benefiting from specific connections (Governor Keith's promises, however empty, opened doors), and possessing a charisma the book underplays.

**More specifically:**

**1. Virtue as Instrument**: Franklin's adopted virtues often serve success rather than goodness. His Socratic method begins as a tool for "entangling" opponents "in difficulties out of which they could not extricate themselves, and so obtaining victories that neither myself nor my cause always deserved." He admits this — but the admission itself becomes a rhetorical device that makes manipulation seem like intellectual honesty.

**2. The "Errata" Framing**: By calling his moral failures "errata" — a printer's term for mistakes in text — Franklin aestheticizes his sins. Deceiving his brother becomes a charming typo rather than a breach of trust. This linguistic move is itself a kind of self-flattery.

**3. The Unfinished Story**: The autobiography ends at age 42, carefully avoiding the controversies of his later life: his estrangement from his son William (a Loyalist), his complicated relationship with his common-law wife Deborah, and the moral ambiguities of the Revolution. The book presents a trajectory of ascent without the complications that would follow.

**4. Paternalism**: Franklin's public service assumes he knows what's best for his community. His reforms, however beneficial, carry an implicit claim to superior judgment. The autobiography doesn't seriously engage with the possibility that his interventions sometimes overreached.

**5. Reason Serving Inclination**: Franklin's own cod-fish episode reveals the central tension: "So convenient a thing it is to be a *reasonable creature*, since it enables one to find or make a reason for everything one has a mind to do." If this is true of the vegetarianism he abandoned, how much more true is it of the virtues he claims to have adopted? The autobiography may be less a record of moral perfection than a masterclass in self-justification — which is not to dismiss it, but to read it more carefully.

Franklin himself provides the key to reading him honestly: he knew reason serves inclination. The question is whether the autobiography is an exception to that rule, or its finest example.
