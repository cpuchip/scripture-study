# Digest: Selections from the Writings of Kierkegaard — Søren Kierkegaard


## The Core Argument

This collection, translated by L. M. Hollander and published in the University of Texas Bulletin (1923), presents Kierkegaard's core argument: authentic existence requires the individual to move beyond comfortable social conformity and aesthetic drift into a passionate, personal relationship with the absolute paradox of faith. True Christianity is not inherited by birth into "Christendom" nor achieved through intellectual assent to doctrines; it demands a leap of inward commitment that reason alone cannot justify. The crowd and the public are illusions — only the single individual, standing alone before God, can live authentically.

## Structure

The selections are organized around Kierkegaard's major works and the developmental "spheres" of human existence:

- **The Aesthetic Sphere**: Introduced through works like *Either-Or* and *In Vino Veritas* ("The Banquet"), showing the life of immediacy, pleasure, irony, and boredom — a life without commitment or purpose.
- **The Ethical Sphere**: Represented by Judge William's defense of marriage and duty, where one chooses oneself responsibly and submits to universal moral law.
- **The Religious Sphere**: The highest stage, reached through *Fear and Trembling* and *Philosophic Trifles*, where the individual stands in direct relation to God through faith in the absolute paradox — a relation that transcends both aesthetic pleasure and ethical universals.

The collection also includes Kierkegaard's late polemics against "Christendom" (the established State Church), his analysis of dread (*The Concept of Dread*), and his reflections on indirect communication and the role of the religious author. The biographical introduction frames these works within Kierkegaard's own life struggles — his melancholy, his broken engagement to Regine Olson, and his determination to awaken Denmark from its Christian slumber.

## Key Passages

> "the æsthetic individual is, in reality, a slave to the merest accidents. He is not self-directed, self-propelled; but—drifts."

*Gloss*: The aesthetic life, for all its apparent freedom, is actually bondage to moods, impulses, and circumstances — the opposite of authentic self-direction.

> "the individual, no matter how humble, who would take the trouble to follow him and be his reader, 'my only reader, the single individual.'"

*Gloss*: Kierkegaard's appeal is never to the crowd or the public; truth lands only in the one person who wrestles with it personally.

> "humanity is in apparent possession of the divine truth, but utterly perverts it and, to cap injury with insult, protects and intrenches the deception behind state sanction and institutions."

*Gloss*: Christendom — the established State Church — does not preserve Christianity but destroys it by making faith a matter of birth and social convention rather than passionate personal commitment.

> "Its origin is found in the 'sympathetic antipathy' of Dread—that force which at one and the same time attracts and repels from the suspected danger of a fall."

*Gloss*: Dread (angst) is the dizziness of freedom: the pull toward possibilities that simultaneously terrify us, present even in innocence.

> "faith is born of the same conditions: the resolute acceptance by the individual of the absolute paradox."

*Gloss*: Whether 1800 years ago or today, faith requires the same inward leap — embracing what reason cannot reconcile.

> "he who with fervency and inwardness prays to some false god is to be preferred to him who worships the true god, but without the passion of devotion."

*Gloss*: For Kierkegaard, the inward relation to truth — passionate, personal commitment — is primary. A formally correct belief lived mechanically is spiritually dead; but this is not relativism. Kierkegaard himself was deeply committed to orthodox Christian doctrine. The point is that truth must be *lived*, not merely assented to.

## Themes

- **The Single Individual vs. the Crowd**: Truth is not democratic. "The public" is an abstraction that dilutes responsibility. Authentic existence happens only when one person stands alone before God, accountable for their own choices.

- **The Three Spheres of Existence**: The aesthetic (immediacy, pleasure, boredom), the ethical (duty, marriage, universal law), and the religious (faith, paradox, absolute commitment to God). Each sphere subsumes but transcends the one before it.

- **The Paradox of Faith**: Christianity's core claim — that the eternal God entered time as a lowly human — is rationally absurd. Faith is not belief despite evidence; it is a passionate leap into what reason cannot justify.

- **Dread (Angst) as the Dizziness of Freedom**: Dread is not fear of a specific thing but the vertigo of possibility itself. It accompanies the transition from innocence to guilt and is the condition of both sin and freedom.

- **Critique of Christendom**: The institutionalized church has turned Christianity into a comfortable social inheritance, eliminating the suffering, persecution, and inward passion that define genuine faith.

- **Indirect Communication**: Kierkegaard wrote under pseudonyms and used irony and literary artifice not to deceive but to force readers into active thinking rather than passive reception — modeling the very inwardness he preaches.

## What's Worth Learning

1. **Interrogate your "Christendom"**: Identify the beliefs, habits, or identities you hold by social inheritance rather than personal conviction. Ask: "Would I still hold this if no one around me did?" This applies to religion, politics, career paths, and cultural assumptions.

2. **Distinguish the "how" from the "what"**: Kierkegaard insists the intensity of inward commitment matters more than doctrinal precision. When evaluating your own beliefs or others', notice whether passion and sincerity are present — a formally correct but lifeless belief is, for Kierkegaard, worse than a wrong one lived with fervor.

3. **Sit with dread instead of fleeing it**: Dread (angst) signals freedom and possibility, not just danger. When you feel the dizziness of a major decision — career change, relationship commitment, moral choice — recognize that dread is the condition of authentic choice, not a sign you're on the wrong path.

4. **Resist the crowd's authority**: Kierkegaard's insistence on "the single individual" is a call to personal responsibility. Before accepting a widely held opinion, ask: "Have I arrived at this through my own wrestling, or have I absorbed it from the public?" The crowd dissolves individual responsibility — it is, for Kierkegaard, an abstraction that allows no one to be accountable.

5. **Practice indirect engagement**: Kierkegaard's pseudonymous writings model a way of communicating that forces the reader to think rather than receive. In teaching, writing, or mentoring, consider whether your approach encourages passive consumption or active wrestling with ideas.

6. **Recognize the spheres in your own life**: Map your current commitments onto the aesthetic/ethical/religious framework. Are you drifting aesthetically (chasing novelty, avoiding commitment)? Living ethically but without transcendence (duty without passion)? Or is there a dimension of your life where you've made the "leap" into something that exceeds rational justification?

## Tensions & Objections

## Tensions & Objections

**The strongest objection: Kierkegaard's radical individualism has no reliable internal criterion for distinguishing genuine faith from self-deception.**

If truth is entirely inward, and the crowd's judgment is always suspect, and reason cannot adjudicate the leap — what prevents any subjective conviction from claiming the mantle of "faith"? Kierkegaard's framework makes it impossible to distinguish between a person who has genuinely leaped into faith and one who has merely convinced themselves they have. This is not a minor worry; it is the structural vulnerability of his entire project. The "single individual" standing alone before God has no external check — no community, no tradition, no reason — to test whether their inward passion is authentic or merely self-dramatization.

Kierkegaard anticipates this by insisting that genuine faith produces *works* (love of neighbor, suffering, humility) and that the religious sphere *subsumes* the ethical rather than abolishing it. But this is a thin safeguard. Many movements have claimed passionate inward conviction while producing cruelty, fanaticism, or self-absorption. Without some shared standard beyond the individual's own inwardness, Kierkegaard's ideal of authentic existence risks collapsing into solipsism — or worse, into a form of spiritual narcissism dressed up as faith.

**A second tension: the pseudonymous strategy undercuts its own authority.** Kierkegaard writes indirectly to force readers into active engagement. But if the author cannot speak directly, how does the reader know they are wrestling with Kierkegaard's actual convictions rather than with literary characters whose views the author may himself reject? The strategy demands trust in the reader's discernment — but it also makes it impossible to know what Kierkegaard *himself* believed, which is a problem for anyone trying to learn from him.

**A third tension: the critique of Christendom risks throwing out the communal with the doctrinal bathwater.** Kierkegaard is right that institutional religion can become a comfortable social inheritance. But human beings are not "single individuals" in a vacuum — we are formed by communities, languages, and traditions. Even the most "inward" faith is expressed through shared practices, liturgies, and relationships. Kierkegaard's atomistic picture of the self before God may be rhetorically powerful but psychologically incomplete.
