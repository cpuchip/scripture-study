# The Two Deaths and the Third Witness

*Why the sacrament comes in a pair — body and blood — and where the Spirit belongs. A companion to [Hope, and the Grammar of Pairs](hope-and-the-grammar-of-pairs.md).*

---

## Binding Question

A friend noticed that the sacrament is administered in two parts: the body, and the shed blood. Two emblems, two prayers, two trays. He was right, and the pair is worth taking seriously rather than treating as one rite split for convenience.

But two questions follow. The first: having the Spirit *feels* like a third part, yet it has no tray and no prayer of its own. Where does it belong? The second came from pressing on the pair. Both Gethsemane and Calvary shed blood, so why does only one emblem carry the blood? The answer offered was that maybe they are *different kinds* of blood — "spirit-death blood, physical-death blood." That instinct turns out to be the hinge. The gospel knows two deaths, and the two emblems answer them one apiece.

So: why a pair? What does each emblem carry that the other cannot? Where does the Spirit stand? And do the two blood-sheddings answer two different deaths?

---

## I. The Pair, and the Promise Both Halves Share

Read the two prayers next to each other. Over the bread: we eat "in remembrance of the **body** of thy Son… that they may always have his Spirit to be with them" ([Moroni 4:3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/4.md); [D&C 20:77](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/20.md)). Over the wine: we drink "in remembrance of the **blood** of thy Son, which was shed for them… that they may have his Spirit to be with them" ([Moroni 5:2](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/5.md); [D&C 20:79](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/20.md)). The friend's pair is exactly there in the text: body, then blood.

Now notice where both halves of the pair *end*. Not in two different promises, but in one and the same: "that they may have his Spirit to be with them." Whatever the Spirit is in this structure, it is the thing both emblems point toward. It is not a third emblem standing beside the bread and the water. It is what the pair is *for*.

The Lord makes that grammar explicit when He institutes the ordinance among the Nephites. Over the bread: "if ye do always remember me ye shall have my Spirit to be with you" ([3 Nephi 18:7](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/18.md)). Over the wine, word for word again: "if ye do always remember me ye shall have my Spirit to be with you" ([3 Nephi 18:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/18.md)). The sentence is an *if/then*. The emblems are the "if": our remembrance, our witnessing. The Spirit is the "then" — His return. The third part cannot be placed beside the first two because it is not on the same side of the covenant. We bring the remembrance; He gives the Spirit.

That answers where the Spirit goes in the *prayers*. It does not yet answer what the Spirit *is*, or why two emblems are needed to ask for it. For that, the pair has to be read against the problem it was given to solve.

## II. Two Emblems, Because There Are Two Deaths

The Book of Mormon prophets do not speak of death in the singular. They count two. Jacob names them without flinching: "that monster, death and hell, which I call the death of the body, and also the death of the spirit" ([2 Nephi 9:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/9.md)). He sorts them: the temporal death "is the grave" (9:11); the spiritual death "is hell" (9:12). And he promises both will be undone — "the grave must deliver up its captive bodies, and the bodies and the spirits of men will be restored one to the other; and it is by the power of the resurrection."

Samuel the Lamanite teaches the same two, and traces them to one root. Christ's death "redeemeth all mankind from the first death—that spiritual death; for all mankind, by the fall of Adam being cut off from the presence of the Lord, are considered as dead, both as to things temporal and to things spiritual" ([Helaman 14:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/hel/14.md)). Alma, teaching his son, says it most plainly: "the fall had brought upon all mankind a spiritual death as well as a temporal, that is, they were cut off from the presence of the Lord" ([Alma 42:9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/42.md)).

So the Fall did two distinct things. It sentenced the body to the grave (temporal death: body and spirit pulled apart). And it cut the soul off from God's presence by sin (spiritual death: the person separated from God). Two wounds, not one. Therefore the remedy comes in two motions, and the ordinance that remembers the remedy comes in two emblems. The pair is not repetition for emphasis. It is two answers because there were two deaths.

## III. The Bread Is the Body — the Answer to the Temporal Death

The bread is the emblem of the body, and the body is the thing that dies the temporal death and rises from it. "This is my body which is given for you" ([Luke 22:19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/22.md)). Amulek draws the line straight from that body to ours: "the death of Christ shall loose the bands of this temporal death, that all shall be raised… The spirit and the body shall be reunited again in its perfect form," their "spirits uniting with their bodies, never to be divided" ([Alma 11:42–45](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/11.md)). The grave is the wound; the resurrection is the answer; the body is where the answer happens.

Elder James J. Hamula reads the bread exactly this way. With it "we signify our belief that while that same body was laid to rest in death, it was raised again to life from the grave"; and in taking the bread to ourselves "we acknowledge that, like Christ's mortal body, our bodies will be released from the bonds of death, rise triumphantly from the grave, and be restored to our eternal spirits" ([*The Sacrament and the Atonement*](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2014/10/the-sacrament-and-the-atonement.md), Oct. 2014). The bread is the resurrection in the hand.

Two features of this answer matter for what comes later. First, it is *universal* and *unconditional*: "the resurrection of Christ redeemeth mankind, yea, even all mankind, and bringeth them back into the presence of the Lord" ([Helaman 14:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/hel/14.md)). Everyone is raised; everyone is brought back. Second, the body is the thing that is *raised*, not the thing that is shed. The risen Christ defines His own resurrected frame as "flesh and bones" — "a spirit hath not flesh and bones, as ye see me have" ([Luke 24:39](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/24.md)). Bones, not blood. The body is restored; the blood is shed and gone.

## IV. The Water Is the Blood — the Answer to the Spiritual Death

The other emblem carries the other death. The blood in scripture is almost never about the grave and almost always about sin. "This is my blood of the new testament, which is shed for many for the **remission of sins**" ([Matthew 26:28](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/26.md)). The principle is older than Calvary, written into the law of sacrifice: "the life of the flesh is in the blood: and I have given it to you upon the altar to make an atonement for your souls: for it is the blood that maketh an atonement for the soul" ([Leviticus 17:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/lev/17.md)). John says it in one clause: "the blood of Jesus Christ his Son cleanseth us from all sin" ([1 John 1:7](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-jn/1.md)). The spiritual death is separation from God by sin; the blood is what removes the sin and reconciles.

Hamula reads the water this way, against the bread: with it "we remember the blood Jesus spilled and the spiritual suffering He endured for all mankind," and in taking it "we acknowledge that His blood and suffering atoned for our sins and that He will remit our sins as we embrace and accept the principles and ordinances of His gospel." Where the bread answers the grave, the water answers the guilt.

And unlike the resurrection, this answer is *conditional*. The grave gives up everyone; the cleansing is laid hold of only through repentance. "Nothing entereth into his rest save it be those who have washed their garments in my blood, because of their faith, and the repentance of all their sins" ([3 Nephi 27:19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/27.md)). The unrepentant are raised like everyone else, but then "there cometh upon them again a spiritual death, yea, a second death" ([Helaman 14:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/hel/14.md)). So the two emblems are not two ways of saying the same thing. The bread is the death that is already beaten for all; the water is the death whose defeat still waits on me.

There is a quiet reversal in the water worth naming. Under the law of Moses, no one could *eat* blood: "I will even set my face against that soul that eateth blood" ([Leviticus 17:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/lev/17.md)), because the life was in it and the life belonged to God, poured out on the altar away from man. In the new covenant the Lord takes the cup and says, "Drink ye all of it" ([Matthew 26:27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/26.md)). The life that the law poured out *before* God is now poured *into* the covenant-keeper. The thing once forbidden becomes the thing commanded, because His life is given not merely as a price paid in God's sight but as a gift taken into ours.

## V. Two Kinds of Blood

Here the friend's pressure on the pair pays off. Both the garden and the cross shed blood, so the objection runs: if the blood is one emblem, why did Christ bleed twice? The answer is that the two bleedings are not the same kind, and they line up with the two deaths.

The garden blood is the blood of a *living* body breaking under the weight of sin. "Blood cometh from every pore, so great shall be his anguish for the wickedness and the abominations of his people" ([Mosiah 3:7](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/3.md)). The Lord describes the same hour as a suffering of *spirit* as much as body: it "caused myself, even God, the greatest of all, to tremble because of pain, and to bleed at every pore, and to suffer both body and spirit" ([D&C 19:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/19.md)). And the deepest stroke of it was a withdrawal — He tasted what sinners taste "at the time I withdrew my Spirit" (19:20). That is the spiritual death itself, borne in our place: to be cut off from the presence of God. Gethsemane is where the spirit-death blood is shed.

The cross blood is different. It is the blood of a body actually *dying*. "It is finished: and he bowed his head, and gave up the ghost" ([John 19:30](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/19.md)) — the spirit leaves the body, which is the very definition of the temporal death. Then "one of the soldiers with a spear pierced his side, and forthwith came there out blood and water" (19:34), blood poured from a body that is already dead. Gethsemane bled to answer the spiritual death; Calvary bled in dying the temporal one. The friend's instinct was right: spirit-death blood, and physical-death blood.

This is where the study has to stay honest, because the symmetry is real but not airtight. The spiritual desolation does not stay politely in the garden. The loudest cry of forsakenness, "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" ([Matthew 27:46](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/27.md)), is uttered *on the cross*. The withdrawal of the Father, the heart of the spiritual death, culminates at Calvary, not Gethsemane. So the clean categories are the two *deaths*, not the two *locations*. The garden and the cross are where each death is chiefly borne, and the cross is where the two meet. Push the geography harder than that and it breaks.

What does *not* break is the emblem. Because both bleedings serve the one work of reconciliation, the blood-emblem gathers them both. Hamula remembers, under the single cup of water, "the agony that caused great drops of blood to fall in Gethsemane" *and* "the blood He spilled from His hands, feet, and side while at Calvary." Two bleedings, one emblem, because the blood, wherever it falls, is the answer to sin. The bread, meanwhile, holds the body that was broken and then raised. The blood is what is *shed*; the body is what is *restored*. That is the whole logic of Luke 24:39 — flesh and bones come back, the blood does not. The price is poured out and gone; the victory is raised and kept.

## VI. The Third Part: the Spirit, and Where It Belongs

Now the first question can be answered, and the answer arrives three times from three directions, which is itself the point.

**In the prayers, the Spirit is the promised fruit of the pair.** Both emblems end in it; neither *is* it. We witness, we remember, we covenant — and "ye shall have my Spirit to be with you" ([3 Nephi 18:7, 11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/18.md)). It is the return on the covenant, not a third item on the tray. This is why it cannot be set beside the bread and water: those are what the hand offers; the Spirit is what God gives back.

**But it genuinely is a third, and scripture names it as one.** The friend's felt third is not a misreading. The Lord taught Adam that birth and rebirth are both threefold: "inasmuch as ye were born into the world by **water, and blood, and the spirit**… even so ye must be born again into the kingdom of heaven, **of water, and of the Spirit, and be cleansed by blood**, even the blood of mine Only Begotten" ([Moses 6:59](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md)). And He assigns each its office: "by the water ye keep the commandment; by the Spirit ye are justified, and by the blood ye are sanctified" (6:60). Water, blood, Spirit — three, with three works. The third part is doctrine, not a feeling that overreached.

**And the three stand together under the witness law** — the engine of the [grammar-of-pairs](hope-and-the-grammar-of-pairs.md) study, where the gospel establishes a truth by two or three witnesses and "the form of the doctrine is part of the doctrine." John applies that law to exactly these three: "This is he that came by water and blood, even Jesus Christ… And it is the Spirit that beareth witness," for "there are three that bear witness in earth, the Spirit, and the water, and the blood: and these three agree in one" ([1 John 5:6, 8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-jn/5.md)). The two emblems are the two witnesses you can see and taste. The Spirit is the third witness, received inwardly rather than handled. That is precisely why it feels like a part you cannot place on the table: of the three, it is the one given to you instead of the one held by you.

Why does the Spirit present as a single third thing rather than as two more fruits, one per emblem? Because it is the one gift that seals *both* victories at once. It reverses the spiritual death: it is "the agent who cleanses and purifies us from sin" (Hamula), so that the soul washed in the blood is "sanctified by the reception of the Holy Ghost, that ye may stand spotless before me at the last day" ([3 Nephi 27:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/27.md)): separation healed, presence restored. And it is the earnest of the temporal victory: "the Spirit of him that raised up Jesus from the dead… shall also quicken your mortal bodies by his Spirit that dwelleth in you" ([Romans 8:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rom/8.md)); we are "sealed with that holy Spirit of promise, which is the earnest of our inheritance" ([Ephesians 1:13–14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/eph/1.md)). The same Spirit that cleanses you now is the down-payment on the body that will be raised. One gift, because it is the life on the far side of *both* deaths. The bread raises you; the blood cleanses you; the Spirit is the life that proves both — presence now, as the pledge of presence forever.

## VII. The Sacrament as the Grammar of Pairs, Made Edible

Set against the earlier study, the shape is familiar. There the claim was that gospel truths come bound in pairs and triads because one term cannot stand alone as established doctrine, and the binding *is* the doctrine's proof. The sacrament is that claim turned into an ordinance. Two emblems witness the two deaths conquered; the Spirit is the third that "agrees in one" with them. The witness law is not illustrated by the rite — it is *performed* by it, every week, in bread and water and the gift that follows.

The faith-hope-charity triplet is performed too. Faith looks back and remembers the death: the two emblems, the body given and the blood shed. Hope looks forward to the resurrection life — and hope's whole content, the earlier study found, is exactly this: "ye shall have hope through the atonement of Christ and the power of his resurrection, to be raised unto life eternal" ([Moroni 7:41](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md)). The promised Spirit is that hope made present as an earnest. And charity is what the Spirit pours in: "the love of God is shed abroad in our hearts by the Holy Ghost which is given unto us" ([Romans 5:5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rom/5.md)). Faith you eat, hope you drink, charity you carry out under the Spirit you were given.

Even the order teaches. "The sequence of bread first and water second is not inconsequential," Hamula notes. The bread comes first because the resurrection is the settled ground: everyone is raised, everyone is returned to God's presence, so the real question is not whether you will live but, in his words, "with whom" — "while every one of us will return to the presence of God, not every one of us will remain with Him." The water comes second because it asks the part that is still open: will you be clean enough to *stay*? The bread states the universal victory; the water poses the personal question; the Spirit is the answer carried out the door.

## VIII. Becoming

The two emblems turn out to address the two things a soul actually fears, and it is worth letting them land separately.

The bread answers the grave. Your body will be raised; that is finished, and it is true for everyone you have ever lost and everyone you will. The sacrament does not ask you to earn the resurrection. It hands it to you, broken, and says: this is settled.

The water asks the harder thing, and it is the part that depends on *this* week. You will be raised into God's presence whether or not you are ready for it — and "no unclean thing can dwell… in his presence" ([Moses 6:57](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md)). So the weight of the ordinance is not in the bread, where the victory is already won, but in the water and the Spirit, where the victory is still being laid hold of through repentance. The blood was shed for the remission of *your* sins; the question the cup puts to you is whether you will let it remit them this week, or come to the table guarding something back.

And the Spirit you are promised is the proof, given in advance, that both deaths are coming undone in you. Its presence today is the same Spirit that will quicken your body in the resurrection — the future tense already begun. To leave sacrament meeting with His Spirit is to leave carrying the earnest of everything the two emblems remembered.

A few questions to carry out with it:

- When I take the bread, do I receive the resurrection as already mine, or do I treat the whole ordinance as a verdict still in doubt?
- When I take the water, am I letting the blood remit a specific sin this week, or am I drinking in general while holding one thing back from repentance?
- The Spirit I am promised is the down-payment on my own resurrection. Do I treat it as a mood for the meeting, or as the firstfruits of being raised and brought home?
- For the friend who saw the pair: the third part he felt is real, and it is the one witness of the three that no one can hand him. It is given. The question the sacrament leaves is whether we will keep the covenant that lets it stay.
