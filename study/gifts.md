# Gifts of the Spirit

*Date: 2026-02-20*

---

## Starting Questions

- What are all the gifts of the Spirit named in scripture?
- How do they operate? What activates them?
- Can we pray for and receive *new* gifts — like discernment — that we don't currently have?
- How does the gift of healing work alongside faith to be healed? Are they a matched set?
- Can someone with great faith lift those with lesser faith? ("Help thou my unbelief")
- What role does faith play across *all* the gifts?
- How do the gifts relate to each other — are some foundational, others derivative?

---

## The Three Lists

Scripture gives us three independent catalogs of spiritual gifts. They overlap significantly but each adds something unique. Comparing them side by side reveals what's consistent and what's distinctive.

### The Catalog

| Gift | D&C 46 | 1 Cor 12 | Moroni 10 |
|------|:------:|:--------:|:---------:|
| **To know** that Jesus is the Christ | v.13 | v.3 | — |
| **To believe** on others' words | v.14 | — | — |
| Differences of administration | v.15 | v.5 | v.8 |
| Diversities of operations | v.16 | v.6 | — |
| Word of wisdom | v.17 | v.8 | v.9 |
| Word of knowledge | v.18 | v.8 | v.10 |
| Faith to be healed | v.19 | — | — |
| Faith to heal / gifts of healing | v.20 | v.9 | v.11 |
| Exceedingly great faith | — | v.9 | v.11 |
| Working of miracles | v.21 | v.10 | v.12 |
| Prophecy | v.22 | v.10 | v.13 |
| Discerning of spirits | v.23 | v.10 | — |
| Beholding of angels and ministering spirits | — | — | v.14 |
| Tongues | v.24 | v.10 | v.15 |
| Interpretation of tongues | v.25 | v.10 | v.16 |

Three things stand out immediately:

**1. D&C 46 is the most detailed.** It's the only list that separates "faith to be healed" from "faith to heal" as distinct gifts. It's the only one with "to believe on their words." The Lord gives Joseph Smith a more nuanced catalog than either Paul or Moroni provided.

**2. Moroni adds "beholding of angels and ministering spirits."** Neither Paul nor D&C 46 lists this separately. Moroni treats encounters with angels as a spiritual gift — something given *by the Spirit of Christ* to particular people (v.14). This connects to his teaching in [Moroni 7:29-30](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md) that angels haven't ceased to minister and that they show themselves "unto them of strong faith."

**3. All three emphasize distribution, not accumulation.** The gifts are spread across the body of believers. No one person has them all (except possibly the head — D&C 46:29). Paul's body metaphor makes this vivid: the eye can't say to the hand, "I have no need of thee" ([1 Corinthians 12:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-cor/12.md)).

## How Gifts Operate

### Given by God, Through Faith, for Others

Every list anchors the gifts in the same three realities:

**They come from God:**
> "And all these gifts come from God, for the benefit of the children of God."
> — [D&C 46:26](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/46.md)

> "All these gifts come by the Spirit of Christ; and they come unto every man severally, according as he will."
> — [Moroni 10:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/10.md)

> "But all these worketh that one and the selfsame Spirit, dividing to every man severally as he will."
> — [1 Corinthians 12:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-cor/12.md)

**They require faith:**
> "He worketh by power, according to the faith of the children of men, the same today and tomorrow, and forever."
> — [Moroni 10:7](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/10.md)

And critically, if faith disappears, so do the gifts:
> "If the day cometh that the power and gifts of God shall be done away among you, it shall be because of unbelief."
> — [Moroni 10:24](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/10.md)

**They exist for the benefit of others:**
> "The manifestation of the Spirit is given to every man to profit withal."
> — [1 Corinthians 12:7](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-cor/12.md)

> "To some is given one, and to some is given another, that all may be profited thereby."
> — [D&C 46:12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/46.md)

This is the critical frame. Gifts aren't merit badges for the spiritually advanced. They're tools distributed across the body so that *everyone* benefits. The person with the gift of healing doesn't have it for themselves — they have it for the person who needs healing.

### The Ask-in-Spirit Mechanism

D&C 46 gives us the actual mechanism for how asking for gifts works:

> "He that asketh in the Spirit asketh according to the will of God; wherefore it is done even as he asketh."
> — [D&C 46:30](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/46.md)

This is stunning in its simplicity. When you pray *in the Spirit*, your desires naturally align with God's will. So the prayer is always answered — because asking in the Spirit means you're already asking for what God wants to give. It's not a blank check; it's a tuning mechanism. The Spirit shapes the request before it's even spoken.

## Can We Seek New Gifts?

**Yes. Emphatically. We are commanded to.**

> "Seek ye earnestly the best gifts, always remembering for what they are given."
> — [D&C 46:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/46.md)

> "Covet earnestly the best gifts."
> — [1 Corinthians 12:31](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-cor/12.md)

The word "covet" here is not negative — Paul is using the Greek *zēloute* (be zealous for). He's saying: *pursue* them. And the Lord in D&C 46 says "seek earnestly" — not passively wait.

**Webster 1828 — "gift"**: *"Some quality or endowment given to man by God; a preëminent and special talent or aptitude; power; faculty."* The word carries both senses throughout: something *given* (you can't manufacture it) but also something you can *seek* and be given.

President George Q. Cannon, as quoted by Elder Callister:

> "No man ought to say, 'Oh, I cannot help this; it is my nature.' He is not justified in it, for the reason that God has promised to… give gifts that will eradicate [our weaknesses].… If any of us are imperfect, it is our duty to pray for the gift that will make us perfect."
> — George Q. Cannon, quoted in [Elder Tad R. Callister, "The Atonement of Jesus Christ," April 2019](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2019/04/45callister.md)

This is remarkable. Cannon says we have a **duty** to pray for gifts — not just a permission. And the purpose goes beyond ministry effectiveness. It's about *perfection* — becoming like God. Elder Callister puts it directly: "These gifts are attributes of godliness; therefore, each time we acquire a gift of the Spirit, we become more like God."

So yes — if you lack discernment, you can pray for discernment. If you lack faith to heal, you can seek it. The command is explicit. The mechanism is clear (D&C 46:30). The purpose is not sign-seeking but becoming.

### The Guardrails

But there are two guardrails on seeking gifts:

**1. Remember what they're for.** D&C 46:8 says "always remembering *for what they are given*" — they're not trophies. They're for the benefit of others (v.9, 12, 26). Asking for a gift to consume it on your own desires is explicitly warned against (v.9, cross-referencing [James 4:3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/james/4.md)).

**2. Beware counterfeits.** D&C 46:7 warns about being "seduced by evil spirits, or doctrines of devils." The context for seeking gifts includes a warning about imitation. That's why "discerning of spirits" is itself a gift (v.23), and why bishops specifically receive the responsibility to discern all gifts "lest there shall be any among you professing and yet be not of God" (v.27).

## The Healing–Faith Pair

D&C 46:19-20 places these side by side as clearly distinct gifts:

> "And again, to some it is given to have **faith to be healed**;"
> — [D&C 46:19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/46.md)

> "And to others it is given to have **faith to heal**."
> — [D&C 46:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/46.md)

These are a matched set — but they're given to *different people*. One person has the gift of healing; another person has the gift of being healed. Both require faith, but they're different kinds of faith operating from different positions.

### The Protocol: D&C 42:43-52

The Lord gives a detailed protocol for how this works in practice:

> "And whosoever among you are sick, and have not faith to be healed, but believe, shall be nourished with all tenderness, with herbs and mild food, and that not by the hand of an enemy."
> — [D&C 42:43](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/42.md)

Notice the layers here:
1. If you have **faith to be healed** — you can be healed
2. If you don't have that gift but you **believe** — you are nourished with tenderness
3. Either way, **elders lay on hands** — the priesthood blessing is given regardless (v.44)

Then the remarkable qualifier:

> "He that hath faith in me to be healed, and is not appointed unto death, shall be healed."
> — [D&C 42:48](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/42.md)

"Not appointed unto death." This is a clause that humbles every healer. Faith to heal is real, but it operates within God's will and timing, not ours. The gift of healing doesn't override the Lord's purposes.

And then this beautiful cascade:

> "He who hath faith to see shall see. He who hath faith to hear shall hear. The lame who hath faith to leap shall leap."
> — [D&C 42:49-51](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/42.md)

Faith to leap. Not just faith to walk — faith to *leap*. The gift matches the asking.

### Paul Perceives Faith

In [Acts 14:9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/acts/14.md), Paul sees a man crippled from birth, and "perceiving that he had faith to be healed," commands him to stand. The man leaps and walks.

Two gifts operate together here: Paul's gift of *discernment* perceives the man's gift of *faith to be healed*. Paul doesn't just heal randomly. He sees faith — and then acts. Discernment and healing work in tandem.

### "Thy Faith Hath Made Thee Whole"

The woman with the issue of blood in [Mark 5:25-34](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/mark/5.md) reaches through the crowd and touches Jesus's garment. She's healed instantly. Jesus feels "virtue" go out of Him and turns to find her.

> "Daughter, thy faith hath made thee whole; go in peace, and be whole of thy plague."
> — [Mark 5:34](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/mark/5.md)

He doesn't say "My power healed you." He says *your faith* made you whole. The power came from Christ, but the channel was her faith. She had the gift of faith to be healed — and she exercised it by reaching.

### Elder Bowen's Witness

Elder Bowen's grandfather received a patriarchal blessing promising "the gift of healing":

> "The patriarch promised me the gift of healing. He said, 'The sick shall be healed. Yea, *the dead shall be raised under your hands*.'"
> — [Elder Shayne M. Bowen, "Miracles, Angels, and Priesthood Power," April 2024](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2024/04/31bowen.md)

When his mother died, he placed his hands on her head and reminded the Lord of the promise. She opened her eyes and said, "Grant, raise me up. I have been in the spirit world, but you have called me back." The patriarchal blessing didn't just predict — it *conferred* the gift. And the gift operated through faith, reminding God of His own promise.

## "Help Thou Mine Unbelief" — Can Greater Faith Lift Lesser Faith?

This is one of the most human moments in scripture. A father brings his demon-possessed son to the disciples, who can't cast it out. Jesus arrives.

> "If thou canst do any thing, have compassion on us, and help us."
> — [Mark 9:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/mark/9.md)

> "Jesus said unto him, If thou canst believe, all things are possible to him that believeth."
> — [Mark 9:23](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/mark/9.md)

> "And straightway the father of the child cried out, and said with tears, Lord, I believe; help thou mine unbelief."
> — [Mark 9:24](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/mark/9.md)

The father's faith is incomplete. He knows it. He doesn't pretend otherwise. But he brings what he has — a broken, honest, tearful belief — and asks Christ to bridge the gap. And Christ does. The boy is healed.

This suggests something profound about how gifts operate across people: **imperfect faith, honestly offered, is enough to access the gifts of someone with greater faith.** The father didn't have the faith to cast out the demon himself. But he had enough faith to bring his son to Jesus. The Son's perfect faith did the rest.

### D&C 46:14 — The Gift of Believing

This may be the most overlooked gift in all three lists:

> "To others it is given to believe on their words, that they also might have eternal life if they continue faithful."
> — [D&C 46:14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/46.md)

Some people's spiritual gift is *to believe the testimony of others*. Not everyone needs to have the gift of knowing — some receive eternal life by trusting those who do know. This is itself a gift of the Spirit. Not a lesser gift. A *different* gift, given for the same ultimate purpose.

This reframes the "help thou my unbelief" pattern. The father in Mark 9 may have had exactly this gift — the gift of believing on the words of others. He'd heard enough about Jesus to bring his son. His faith was real, even if incomplete. And it was *enough*.

### D&C 42:52 — Bearing Each Other's Infirmities

> "And they who have not faith to do these things, but believe in me, have power to become my sons; and inasmuch as they break not my laws thou shalt bear their infirmities."
> — [D&C 42:52](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/42.md)

Those who lack faith for healing aren't rejected — they're carried. The community bears their infirmities. This is 1 Corinthians 12:26 in practice: "whether one member suffer, all the members suffer with it." The body compensates for what individual members lack. Greater faith doesn't just lift the person who has it — it creates a spiritual environment where those with lesser faith can still be blessed.

## Discernment

**Webster 1828 — "discernment"**: *"The power or faculty of the mind by which it distinguishes one thing from another; power of viewing differences in objects, and their relations and tendencies; penetrative and discriminate mental vision."*

**Webster 1828 — "discern"**: *"To see and identify by noting a difference or differences; to note the distinctive character of; to discriminate; to distinguish."*

The historical definition emphasizes *distinguishing between things*. Not vague spiritual feelings — precise differentiation. Seeing what's really there versus what appears to be there.

### Moses 1: The Prototype

Moses gives us the clearest example of discernment in action. After seeing God face to face, Satan appears and says, "Moses, son of man, worship me" ([Moses 1:12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/1.md)).

Moses's discernment is immediate and specific:

> "Who art thou? For behold, I am a son of God, in the similitude of his Only Begotten; and where is thy glory, that I should worship thee? For behold, I could not look upon God, except his glory should come upon me, and I were transfigured before him. But I can look upon thee in the natural man. Is it not so, surely?"
> — [Moses 1:13-14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/1.md)

And the mechanism:

> "Blessed be the name of my God, for his Spirit hath not altogether withdrawn from me, or else where is thy glory, for it is darkness unto me? And I can judge between thee and God."
> — [Moses 1:15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/1.md)

The gift of discernment operates through the Spirit. Moses can "judge between" God and Satan *because* God's Spirit hasn't withdrawn from him. Without the Spirit, discernment fails. With it, the difference between light and darkness is unmistakable — Moses has seen real glory, and Satan's counterfeit is obvious by comparison.

### Paul Discerns at Philippi

In [Acts 16:16-18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/acts/16.md), a woman possessed by "a spirit of divination" follows Paul and his companions, crying, "These men are the servants of the most high God, which shew unto us the way of salvation." What she says is *technically true* — but the spirit behind it is wrong. Paul discerns the source: "being grieved, turned and said to the spirit, I command thee in the name of Jesus Christ to come out of her."

Discernment doesn't just detect *what* is wrong. It detects *what spirit is behind* something that might look right. The woman's words were accurate. The spirit animating them was not. This is why "discerning of spirits" is the scriptural phrase — the gift is about identifying the source, not just the surface.

### D&C 46:27 — Bishops and Discernment

> "And unto the bishop of the church, and unto such as God shall appoint and ordain to watch over the church and to be elders unto the church, are to have it given unto them to discern all those gifts lest there shall be any among you professing and yet be not of God."
> — [D&C 46:27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/46.md)

Church leaders are specifically given discernment to judge the authenticity of spiritual gifts. This is a check on the system — because counterfeits exist (D&C 46:7). Every genuine gift has an imitation. The gift of discernment is the immune system of the body of Christ.

## The Two Greatest Gifts Nobody Talks About

D&C 46:13-14 lists two gifts that appear in no other catalog:

> "To some it is given by the Holy Ghost to **know that Jesus Christ is the Son of God**, and that he was crucified for the sins of the world."
> — [D&C 46:13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/46.md)

> "To others it is given to **believe on their words**, that they also might have eternal life if they continue faithful."
> — [D&C 46:14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/46.md)

Testimony is a gift of the Spirit. Not just a product of study or reasoning — a gift, given by the Holy Ghost, as real as tongues or healing. And believing someone else's testimony is *also* a gift. Both lead to eternal life. Both are distributed by the same Spirit.

This means the investigator sitting in sacrament meeting who feels something when Brother Smith bears his testimony — that feeling isn't lesser than Brother Smith's sure knowledge. It's a different gift given by the same Spirit. The one who knows and the one who believes on his words are both operating within the system of spiritual gifts.

## Faith: The Substrate of All Gifts

Mormon makes the definitive statement:

> "It is by faith that miracles are wrought; and it is by faith that angels appear and minister unto men; wherefore, if these things have ceased wo be unto the children of men, for it is because of unbelief, and all is vain."
> — [Moroni 7:37](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md)

Faith isn't one gift among many — it's the soil all gifts grow in. Without faith, the gifts cease. Moroni's logic chain is devastating: if miracles have stopped → faith has stopped → "there had been no redemption made" (v.38). The existence of spiritual gifts is evidence of faith, which is evidence of a living redemption.

But faith alone isn't sufficient. Moroni gives the full chain:

> "Wherefore, there must be faith; and if there must be faith there must also be hope; and if there must be hope there must also be charity."
> — [Moroni 10:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/10.md)

And Paul, right after listing the gifts in 1 Corinthians 12, pivots to "a more excellent way" — [1 Corinthians 13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-cor/13.md):

> "Though I have the gift of prophecy, and understand all mysteries, and all knowledge; and though I have all faith, so that I could remove mountains, and have not charity, I am nothing."
> — [1 Corinthians 13:2](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-cor/13.md)

You can have *every gift on the list* — prophecy, knowledge, mountain-moving faith — and without charity, it's nothing. Charity isn't a gift among gifts. It's what makes the gifts matter. Mormon says it's "the pure love of Christ" ([Moroni 7:47](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md)). And he gives us the way to get it:

> "Pray unto the Father with all the energy of heart, that ye may be filled with this love."
> — [Moroni 7:48](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md)

Even charity — the greatest attribute — is something you *pray for*. Just like the other gifts. Just like the Cannon quote says.

## Synthesis

### The Architecture of Gifts

The gifts of the Spirit aren't a buffet where you pick what sounds useful. They're an architecture:

**Foundation**: Faith, hope, charity — the triad that underlies everything (Moroni 10:20-21)

**Infrastructure**: Testimony — knowing Christ is the Son of God, *or* believing on the words of those who know (D&C 46:13-14). This is the foundation most people don't realize is a gift.

**Distribution**: Different gifts to different people, "that all may be profited thereby" (D&C 46:12). The body metaphor isn't decoration — it's the operating principle.

**Mechanism**: Asking in the Spirit aligns your desire with God's will, so "it is done even as he asketh" (D&C 46:30).

**Safeguard**: Discernment — both as an individual gift (v.23) and as an institutional responsibility given to bishops (v.27).

### The Healing Ecosystem

Healing isn't a solo act. It's an ecosystem:
- One person has **faith to heal** (D&C 46:20)
- Another has **faith to be healed** (D&C 46:19)
- Paul **discerns** who has faith to be healed before acting (Acts 14:9)
- Those who lack faith to be healed are **nourished with tenderness** by the community (D&C 42:43)
- The community **bears the infirmities** of those who believe but can't yet exercise full faith (D&C 42:52)
- All of this operates within God's will — "not appointed unto death" (D&C 42:48)

Greater faith lifts lesser faith. That's the Mark 9:24 pattern — "help thou mine unbelief." But also the D&C 46:14 pattern — believing on the words of those who know. The system is designed so that no one needs every gift, because the body shares its gifts across all its members.

### Seeking vs. Sign-Seeking

D&C 46 draws a razor-thin line between seeking gifts (commanded) and seeking signs (condemned):

- v.8: "Seek ye earnestly the best gifts"
- v.9: "Not for a sign that they may consume it upon their lusts"

The difference: **purpose**. Seeking gifts to serve = faith. Seeking signs to validate yourself = consumption. Same spiritual territory, opposite directions.

## Becoming

This study lands in several places:

**1. Identify my gifts.** What gifts of the Spirit do I already have? Which ones have I been exercising without recognizing them as spiritual gifts? D&C 46:10 says to "always retain in your minds what those gifts are." I should know mine.

**2. Seek what I lack — specifically.** Not vaguely "more of the Spirit," but specific gifts. If I need discernment, pray for discernment. If I need faith to heal my family, seek that gift. Cannon says it's my *duty*. D&C 46:8 says to seek *earnestly*.

**3. Remember the purpose.** Every gift is "for the benefit of the children of God" (D&C 46:26). If I receive a gift and use it only for myself, I've missed the point. The gifts are tools for building the body.

**4. Charity first.** Before any specific gift, pray for charity — "with all the energy of heart" (Moroni 7:48). Without it, every other gift is sounding brass (1 Cor 13:1). Charity is the soil. Plant the gifts there.

**5. Bear others' infirmities.** When someone around me has less faith — in a trial, in a crisis of testimony, in a moment of weakness — my role is to bear their infirmity (D&C 42:52), not to judge their lack of faith. The body compensates for what individual members can't do alone.

**6. Don't pretend to more faith than I have.** The father in Mark 9 was honest: "I believe; help thou mine unbelief." That honest, tearful offering was enough. If I lack faith, I can say so and bring what I have. Christ bridges the gap.
