# Is There Gospel in Genshin Impact?

*A study for a Genshin player who also loves the gospel — the true things Teyvat keeps reaching for, and how to tell the gold from the rest.*

---

## Start here

Yes. There's a lot in Genshin Impact that rhymes with the gospel — more than you might expect from a game that wasn't trying to teach it. That last part is the interesting bit, so let's say it plainly up front.

Nobody at the studio that made Genshin sat down to write scripture. They built a beautiful fantasy world out of Greek myths, Chinese history, Norse legends, and a lot of imagination. And *still* the story keeps bumping into gospel-shaped truths — a god who gives up everything to save her people, hope that refuses to die, freedom that can't be forced, the worth of one single person against all of heaven.

Why would that happen by accident? Because it isn't really an accident. There is one true story underneath all the good stories: a God who loves you, a rescue bought by sacrifice, a search for the lost that never gives up. And good storytellers keep leaning toward it the way a plant leans toward a window, even when they don't know that's what they're doing. Scripture even explains why: "the true Light, which lighteth every man that cometh into the world" ([John 1:9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/1.md)). A little of Christ's light is in every person. So when people make something beautiful, some of the true pattern leaks in.

That means you can enjoy the echoes *and* know where the real music is coming from. This study is a tour of the echoes, a tool for testing them, and an honest look at where Teyvat and the gospel part ways.

## The tool: how to test any story

Before the fun part, here's the most useful thing in this whole study — a tool you can use on every game, show, song, and idea for the rest of your life. It comes from Moroni, and it's simple.

> "That which is of God inviteth and enticeth to do good continually; wherefore, every thing which inviteth and enticeth to do good, and to love God, and to serve him, is inspired of God." ([Moroni 7:13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md))

You don't have to be a scripture scholar to use it. You already have what you need: "the Spirit of Christ is given to every man, that he may know good from evil" ([Moroni 7:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md)). So the test for anything is just: *does this invite me toward good (toward being kind, brave, honest, loving God), or away from it?*

And here's the part that gives you permission to enjoy Genshin without guilt. Moroni doesn't say "throw out anything that isn't scripture." He says the opposite:

> "Search diligently in the light of Christ that ye may know good from evil; and if ye will lay hold upon every good thing, and condemn it not, ye certainly will be a child of Christ." ([Moroni 7:19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md))

*Lay hold upon every good thing.* Wherever it is. A game can be like fruit with seeds — you eat the good fruit and you don't swallow the seeds. Paul said the same thing a different way: "if there is anything virtuous, lovely, or of good report or praiseworthy, we seek after these things" ([Articles of Faith 1:13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/a-of-f/1.md)). So let's go find the good things.

## The echoes

### 1. Hope against despair

Teyvat is a wounded world. Five hundred years ago a Cataclysm tore it up, the gods are fading, and there's a lot of grief under the pretty scenery. But the thread that pulls the whole story forward is hope — characters who keep going, keep believing tomorrow can be better, keep refusing to quit.

That's not just a nice mood. It's close to a gospel law: "if ye have no hope ye must needs be in despair; and despair cometh because of iniquity" ([Moroni 10:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/10.md)). Hope isn't a feeling you're stuck waiting for. It's something you *choose* and fight to keep, and losing it is dangerous. Genshin makes hope the hero's real weapon — stronger than any Vision. The gospel agrees, and tells you where the unbreakable kind comes from.

### 2. A god who becomes small to save her people (Fontaine)

This is the big one, and if you've played through Fontaine you already felt it.

Long ago the people of Fontaine were placed under a terrible prophecy: one day the waters would rise, and everyone would dissolve. The god of justice, Focalors, inherited that doom and could not simply wave it away. So she did something staggering. She split off a piece of herself and became a *human*, Furina, who lived among the people for five hundred years, performing as their confident god, carrying a secret so heavy she couldn't tell a single soul, held together only by the people's faith in her. And at the very end, Focalors gave up her own godhood, sacrificing her own divine life, to break the prophecy and save her people and set them free.

Notice the shape of that, because it is the shape of the gospel's very center:

- A God who becomes one of us: "the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us" ([John 1:14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/1.md)).
- A God who sets aside His glory to do it: He "made himself of no reputation, and took upon him the form of a servant… he humbled himself, and became obedient unto death" ([Philippians 2:7–8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/philip/2.md)).
- A rescue paid for by laying down His own life: "Greater love hath no man than this, that a man lay down his life for his friends" ([John 15:13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/15.md)).
- A Savior who carries the burden Himself: "he will take upon him the pains and the sicknesses of his people… that he might take upon him the sins of his people" ([Alma 7:11–13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/7.md)); "the Lord your Redeemer… suffered the pain of all men… And he hath risen again from the dead" ([D&C 18:11–12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/18.md)).

Here's the honest part, and it matters. Furina's story is a beautiful *shadow* of the real thing, not the real thing itself. Christ's sacrifice wasn't a lonely god outsmarting a cruel heaven — it was a loving Father and a willing Son who planned your rescue together, out of love, before the world even began. And it wasn't a story. It actually happened, in history, for *you*. So when Fontaine's ending made you cry, pay attention to that feeling. That ache is real, and it's pointing at something real. You were feeling the shape of the true rescue.

### 3. Freedom that won't be forced (Mondstadt)

Venti, the god of freedom, is the strangest kind of god: he refuses to rule. He won't sit on a throne and give orders. He wants his people free to choose for themselves, even to make mistakes.

That's not a weakness in the story. It's the whole point, and it's deeply gospel. "They are free to choose liberty and eternal life… or to choose captivity and death" ([2 Nephi 2:27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)). The God of the gospel could force everyone to be good. He refuses, on purpose, because love that's forced isn't love and goodness that's forced isn't goodness. Real power, He taught, works "by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned" — never by compulsion ([D&C 121:41](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)). Venti is a little picture of the biggest truth about God: the one Being with the most right to command chooses instead to set you free.

### 4. A God who keeps His word (Liyue)

In Liyue, everything runs on contracts, and Zhongli, the God of Contracts, keeps his word absolutely, even when it costs him dearly. A promise is sacred. Your word is who you are.

The gospel is built on exactly this, except the contracts are called covenants, and the astonishing thing is that God binds *Himself* by them: "I, the Lord, am bound when ye do what I say; but when ye do not what I say, ye have no promise" ([D&C 82:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/82.md)). Think about that. The most powerful Being there is chooses to be *held* to His promises to you. Zhongli is a good picture of it — the God of Contracts, whose greatest contract is your salvation, and who has never once broken His word.

### 5. The worth of one person (the whole game)

Genshin's gods are unusual, and a writer at GamesRadar put his finger on why: they "don't intrinsically deserve the worship of mortals; in fact, they explicitly need the help of their people." Over and over the story insists that ordinary mortals matter — that one person's choices count for something even against the will of heaven.

The gospel says the same, in seven words: "the worth of souls is great in the sight of God" ([D&C 18:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/18.md)). But notice a difference worth keeping. Genshin often lifts people up by making the gods look small. The gospel lifts you up for a better reason: not because God is small, but because God is truly great *and* He calls you His child. You don't matter because heaven is weak. You matter because Heaven loves you.

### 6. The trap of a frozen "eternity" (Inazuma)

Raiden, the god of eternity, tries to freeze her whole nation in an unchanging, perfect moment — no change, no risk, no loss, forever. And the story slowly reveals that her "eternity" has become a kind of death. Nothing grows in it. She has to learn that living forever and never changing are not the same thing.

That's one of the smartest things in the game, and it's a perfect mirror for a real word people get confused. "Eternal life" in the gospel is not a frozen museum. It's the opposite — it's *knowing* God ("this is life eternal, that they might know thee the only true God," [John 17:3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/17.md)) and never stopping growing: "whatever principle of intelligence we attain unto in this life, it will rise with us in the resurrection" ([D&C 130:18–19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/130.md)). Raiden's false eternity is a warning; the real one is an adventure that never runs out.

### 7. The search for the lost (the Traveler — you)

The whole game starts because you are searching. The Traveler crosses an entire world, nation by nation, looking for one lost person — a twin taken away. That longing is the engine of everything you do.

And that longing is the gospel in miniature. The story Jesus told most about Himself was this one: "having an hundred sheep, if he lose one of them… go after that which is lost, until he find it? And when he hath found it, he layeth it on his shoulders, rejoicing" ([Luke 15:4–6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/15.md)). Every hour you spend as the Traveler, you are living a small version of the biggest truth there is: Someone will cross a whole world to find the one they love. In the game, that someone is you. In real life, that someone is also looking for you.

## Where Teyvat and the gospel part ways

A good disciple keeps the gold *and* notices what isn't gold. None of this is a reason to panic or stop playing — it's just playing with your eyes open, which is what Moroni asked us to do. Three honest notes:

**Many gods, not one.** Teyvat has seven Archons, a floating heaven, and mysterious powers above even them — all invented for the story. The real God is one loving Father, not a committee of powerful characters who can be "made or unmade." Interestingly, the game itself often hints that its gods aren't quite trustworthy, and that "heaven" might not be fair. That instinct is half-right: the gods of Teyvat aren't the real thing. The gospel just tells you the real One is *good*.

**The names.** This one's worth knowing, and worth a chat with your parents. A lot of the Archons' true names (Barbatos, Morax, Focalors, Beelzebul, and others) are borrowed from an old, old list of demon names, from a book called the *Ars Goetia*. Tons of fantasy stories borrow spooky old names for flavor; it's decoration, and the game isn't asking anyone to worship or call on anything. But it's the kind of thing a wise player *knows* about instead of not knowing.

**Who does the saving.** In Teyvat, the big hope is often that humans will rise up, defy fate, and even overthrow heaven. That's thrilling, and part of it rhymes with the gospel's love of freedom. But the gospel's hope is not that we defeat God and climb over heaven. It's that God is good, and *He* comes down to lift us — which, funnily enough, is exactly the Fontaine story done for real: heaven didn't get overthrown, Heaven came down.

## Becoming

So here's what to do with all this.

**Use the test on everything.** Not just Genshin — every show, song, book, and idea a friend hands you. Ask Moroni's question: *does this invite me toward good, toward loving God, toward being kind and brave and honest?* Keep what passes. Leave what doesn't. You'll be doing that your whole life, so it's worth getting good at now.

**Let the echoes point you home.** When Fontaine's sacrifice makes you cry, when Venti's freedom makes you glad, when the search for a lost sibling tugs at your heart — those feelings are real, and they're compasses. A great story can make you homesick for a place you've never been. Teyvat does it on purpose. And the home it's making you homesick for is a real one.

A few things to think about, or talk over with Mom or Dad:

- Which echo is your favorite — the sacrifice, the freedom, the search, or another one? Why that one?
- Genshin's gods often seem far away or unfair. How is the real God different from that?
- The Traveler crosses a world to find one lost person. If Heaven is doing that for you, what does that change about how you see yourself?

You don't have to choose between loving a beautiful game and loving the gospel. You can walk through Teyvat the way a disciple walks anywhere — picking up every good thing, keeping the gold, and letting the shadows point you back to the light they came from.
