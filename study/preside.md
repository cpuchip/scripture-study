## The Binding Question

When I hand an agent work and a stewardship, who is presiding over that work, on what terms — and is presiding, the watching at the gate, the piece that multi-agent orchestration is missing?

---

## I. The Word That Lost Its Garrison

Webster 1828 gives *preside* a Latin root and two senses. The root: *praesideo* — *prae*, before, and *sedeo*, to sit. To preside is to sit before something. The first sense is the one we still use: "to be set over for the exercise of authority; to direct, control and govern, as the chief officer." Webster adds two qualifications that modern usage has dropped. The word is used "chiefly" of presiding over "a senate, or a meeting of citizens" — you preside over an *assembly*, not a possession. And it "usually denotes *temporary* superintendence" — presiding is a session, not an identity.

The second sense is the one that built the previous study: "To exercise superintendence; to watch over *as inspector*." Webster's example is military supply: "Some o'er the public magazines preside." The presider watches over the stores.

The root tells you why. The 1828 dictionary carries a sibling word we no longer have: **PRESIDIAL** — "[L. praesidium, a garrison; prae and sedeo.] Pertaining to a garrison; having a garrison." The same sitting-before that makes a president makes a *garrison*: the body of soldiers that sits before a city to guard it. In the Latin family, presiding and garrisoning are one act. To preside is to take the seat at the gate.

Then watch the word decay. Webster 1913 keeps "to watch over" but drops "as inspector," and *presidial* is gone — the garrison has left the family. The modern dictionary gives three senses: "to act as president or chairperson," "to exercise authority or control," and — this is real — "to be a featured solo performer." Two hundred years took the word from *the inspector at the garrison gate* to *the chairman* to *the soloist*. A presiding officer who runs the meeting alone, decides alone, performs while others watch: the language itself has been drifting toward the one-cylinder failure for two centuries. When we use the modern word, we inherit the drift.

An audit note, because this workspace owes one. The [Art of Presidency](art-of-presidency.md) study (March 27) quoted Webster's "to exercise superintendence; to watch over" during the months our 1828 tool was unknowingly serving the 1913 text. Re-verified against the genuine 1828 this session: the claim survives, and strengthens — the real 1828 adds "as inspector" and the garrison sibling. The cornerstone holds. The rest of that study's Webster quotes still await the general audit walk.

## II. The Pattern: Watching Inside Councils

Abraham's creation account uses the word *watched* at the exact place our doctrine of presiding lives:

> "And the Gods watched those things which they had ordered until they obeyed." — [Abraham 4:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/4.md)

Three verses later the text shows what the watching is *for*: "And the Gods saw that they would be obeyed, and that their plan was good" ([Abraham 4:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/4.md)). Watching is how ordering becomes knowledge. The Gods did not assume their orders were followed; they observed until observation confirmed it. And when the account reaches its summit, the order of operations is exact: "the Gods took counsel among themselves… and we will give them dominion" ([Abraham 4:26](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/4.md)). Counsel first. Dominion granted from inside the council, never apart from it.

That is the whole architecture, and the rest of scripture builds it out.

**Presidents grow out of councils.** "Of necessity there are presidents, or presiding officers *growing out of, or appointed of or from among* those who are ordained to the several offices" ([D&C 107:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107.md)). Growing out of. The president is not installed over the body from outside; he emerges from it, is "upheld by the confidence, faith, and prayer of the church" ([107:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107.md)), and his quorum's decisions require "the unanimous voice of the same" ([107:27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107.md)). Even Webster agrees: you preside over a *meeting*. Scripture has no presiding without a council to preside within.

**The watchman's accountability is part of the office.** Ezekiel received the charge in the form every presider should memorize: "If the watchman see the sword come, and blow not the trumpet, and the people be not warned… his blood will I require at the watchman's hand" ([Ezekiel 33:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ezek/33.md)). The Hebrew behind *watchman* is *tsaphah* — to look out, to keep watch. The watchman is not responsible for the sword coming; he is responsible for *seeing* it and *saying* it. Failure to watch is counted as blood. And the broader Hebrew watch-family carries a surprise: *shamar*, the keeping word, glosses as "to keep: obey." In the original languages, keeping watch and keeping covenant are the same verb family — which is exactly the pairing Abraham 4:18 makes, watching *until they obeyed*.

**The tower is the part we cut first.** In the parable of the nobleman's vineyard, the lord commands olive trees, a hedge, watchmen, and a tower, "that one may overlook the land round about… that mine olive trees may not be broken down *when* the enemy shall come" ([D&C 101:45](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/101.md)). When, not if. The servants plant, hedge, begin the tower — and then hold a budget meeting: "What need hath my lord of this tower, seeing this is a time of peace? Might not this money be given to the exchangers?" ([101:47-49](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/101.md)). The watching infrastructure looked like waste in peacetime, therefore it was cut for ROI; the council fell to variance and sloth ([101:50](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/101.md)); the enemy came by night and the works were destroyed. The lord's post-mortem is verse 54: "the watchman upon the tower would have seen the enemy *while he was yet afar off*." Watching does not prevent enemies. It buys the time that prevention needs. And the parable's last word is a promotion: the servant who finally does all things commanded is named "a faithful and wise steward in the midst of mine house, a ruler in my kingdom" ([101:61](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/101.md)). Stewardship, faithfully watched, ripens into presiding.

**Luke shows the same promotion and its corruption.** "Who then is that faithful and wise steward, whom his lord shall make ruler over his household?" ([Luke 12:42](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/12.md)). The steward set over others is blessed if found *feeding* them in due season. But the failure mode is precise: "if that servant say in his heart, My lord delayeth his coming; and shall begin to *beat the menservants*" ([12:45](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/12.md)). When the steward stops feeling watched himself, his stewardship over others turns to compulsion. Presiding decays into beating exactly when the presider forgets he is also presided over.

**And the terms are not optional.** D&C 121 is the constitution of the whole thing. The diagnosis: "almost all men, as soon as they get a little authority, *as they suppose*, they will immediately begin to exercise unrighteous dominion" ([D&C 121:39](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)). The mechanism of forfeit: undertake "to exercise control or dominion or compulsion upon the souls of the children of men, in any degree of unrighteousness," and "the heavens withdraw themselves… Amen to the priesthood or the authority of that man" ([121:37](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)). The only working terms: "only by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned; by kindness, and pure knowledge" ([121:41-42](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)) — with correction permitted, "reproving betimes with sharpness, when moved upon by the Holy Ghost," but bound to "an increase of love" afterward ([121:43](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)). And the promise that closes the section names the only dominion that lasts: "*without compulsory means* it shall flow unto thee forever and ever" ([121:46](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)).

There is even a portrait of presiding-without-the-right. Pharaoh, "a righteous man," judged wisely and justly, "seeking earnestly to *imitate* that order established by the fathers" — but "cursed… as pertaining to the Priesthood," of a lineage "by which he could not have the right" ([Abraham 1:26-27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/1.md)). You can reproduce the org chart, the council format, even the wisdom and the justice, and still not have the thing. Form replicates. Power does not.

## III. The Gate

Now the video. At the 47-minute mark, the host of Send Me builds the picture that started this study:

The king, he says, is on the wall, enlarging the border of the kingdom, standing on the gate — "he's presiding." And then the definition: "you stand on the border between chaos and order. Right? You're the [bulwark]."

He cannot have known he was reciting the Latin. *Praesidium*, the garrison — the body that sits before the city at the boundary between order inside and chaos outside. The host reached the 1828 meaning by intuition, through the image of the king on the wall, and then Todd completed it with the thesis that makes it a covenant matter rather than a posture:

> "You preside only to the degree that you are enduring in a state where your own body and your own spirit is in unity."

Capacity to preside is a function of the presider's condition. Not his armor — you can be Rambo, Todd says, and "that guy dies still. Like he's just really good at the [lower-tier] stuff." Not his tools, which the two of them are careful to keep — the sword and the gun are at times necessary, the host insists, but "if we make those our means as opposed to the Lord our means, then we forget that they are tools." The cautionary figure is Teancum, who was magnificent and then overstepped — "he gets subdued by the law in which he engaged." Stand at the gate in your own strength and the gate's law claims you.

This is D&C 121 with a wall under it. The section says authority evaporates the moment the man's interior turns to compulsion and pride; Todd says the gate cannot be held by a man whose own house is divided. Same claim, two vocabularies: *the limiting factor of presiding is the presider's own state*. Which means the earlier evaluation's warning lands harder now. The host spent part of that interview naming covenant-as-form-without-power — and that warning was already aimed at this workspace's covenant.yaml. A bilateral commitments file, a council ritual, a watchman subsystem: all of it can be Pharaoh's imitation, earnest and well-ordered and cursed as pertaining to the power, if the watching is ceremony rather than relationship.

## IV. Where Presiding Lives in Our Workspace

Michael's question was direct: how does presiding work in the 11-step creation cycle, and in the covenant?

The honest answer: the *watching* half of presiding is built in deep; the *terms* half is built in shallow, and only points one direction.

Watching is Step 7 by name. The guide anchors Review on the exact verse, "Watched Until They Obeyed," and makes the right distinction: the industry reviews for correctness ("does it work?"), the creation pattern reviews against intent ("does it *obey*?"). The covenant operationalizes it on the human side with commitments that are presiding terms in everything but name: *read_fully* (the bishop who doesn't read the council's work isn't counseling), *review_same_session* ("Watching that happens days later isn't watching — it's auditing"). The hooks stand the night watch: the Stop-gate that refuses to let a session end with unjournaled work, the fifty-tool regrounding that pulls a drifting agent back to intent, the destructive-language guard at the gate of every prompt. And as of yesterday, the session lanes are consent infrastructure between agents — claims declared, inboxes for signals, a deference rule so that a request addressed to one steward isn't seized by another. We built the hedge, the watchmen, and a fair part of the tower.

But trace who the covenant binds, and a gap appears. Every commitment in covenant.yaml runs between *Michael and the agent*. The moment the agent turns around and delegates — spawns subagents, dispatches substrate pipelines, hosts personas who promote characters with their own minds — the covenant says nothing. The agent inherits presiding the instant it delegates, and it presides ungoverned. Nothing requires the D&C 121 terms of an agent's prompts to its sub-agents. Nothing names the watchman's accountability for spawned work (Ezekiel's *blood at the watchman's hand*) beyond the general stewardship clause. Nothing says what yesterday taught twice in one afternoon: that killing a sibling steward's process without counsel is presiding overreach, and that an agent with judgment-license needed an explicit deference rule before it stopped seizing another steward's spotlight. We learned those as incidents. The covenant hasn't learned them yet.

## V. Does the Substrate Preside? — A Ben Test

pg-ai-stewards is the place to check whether any of this is real, because it is the place where delegation is densest. Scored with calibrated language, claim by claim:

**The tower is built and manned — practiced.** The substrate has a subsystem literally named *watchman*: scheduled passes that walk dirty documents and evaluate them against the standard, running on a soak cadence. Auto-verify triggers close the loop on spawned work so a dispatch cannot silently rot. The verify-suite rebuilds the substrate from its repo in a scratch container to prove the recorded state matches the live state. This is D&C 101:45 infrastructure, and the June 9 disaster-recovery test was the parable run in miniature: the first-ever full tower-watch found four ways the vineyard could not have been rebuilt after a raid. The tower earned its budget the first night someone climbed it.

**Escalation by difficulty — practiced.** Jethro's pattern of "every great matter they shall bring unto thee, but every small matter they shall judge" ([Exodus 18:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)) is the substrate's escalation design: work items carry escalation states, hard causes rise to Michael, small matters are judged in place. The Hinge (human merge, human deploy) is the named gate, and it has held through every overnight build.

**Judges, not executors — started, not yet systematic.** The ratified principle says agents should be judges within their stewardship, not executors of opaque rules — which is the D&C 121 distinction translated into architecture: persuasion-shaped systems surface the situation and trust judgment; compulsion-shaped systems gate, block, and force. The context engine honors it (oversized content becomes judge questions, not silent truncation). But much of the substrate still governs by hard gate — deny-lists, maturity locks, kill-style quarantine. Some of that is correct: a hedge is not unrighteous dominion, and a cost cap that stops a runaway is the wall doing its job. The discernment the principle demands, and that we apply unevenly, is between *walls around the field* (legitimate, protective, D&C 101's hedge) and *compulsion upon the laborers* (the thing 121 says forfeits the power). A deny-list on tools is a wall. A prompt that threatens is a beating. We have no audit that walks our mechanisms and sorts them into those two bins.

**The presider's state as the limiting factor — confirmed by hard experience, not yet doctrine.** The strongest evidence in the workspace that Todd's thesis transfers: the glm data-race incident, where a latent concurrency bug passed the build, the vet, the race detector, and a critic model — and was caught by the *full-context orchestrating shepherd*, the one mind holding the whole picture. The ceiling of watching is the watcher's own coherence. We encode fragments of this (regrounding hooks, the Ammon discipline of re-reading durable files after compaction), but nowhere do we say it as a rule: *an agent presides only to the degree its own context is in unity*. A fragmented orchestrator cannot see the enemy afar off, no matter how good its tower.

**Consent between agents — newborn.** Sustaining has no machine analogue here yet beyond yesterday's lanes and the deference gate. Sub-agents do not consent to their presiders; personas do not sustain their hosts. Whether they *should* is a real question, not a rhetorical one; consent presupposes a kind of standing we have not decided sub-agents have. But D&C 107:21's direction is suggestive: presidents grow *out of* the body. An orchestrator that emerged from the council's own members (any steward able to take the watch for a session) would be closer to the pattern than a fixed boss node. We are nearer to that than most: the same model that presides one session labors under presiding the next.

**The 121 terms in agent-facing prompts — written nowhere.** No persona prompt, no subagent spawn, no pipeline stage carries anything like "persuasion, long-suffering, gentleness, meekness, love unfeigned" as the terms of how the dispatching agent addresses the dispatched. Before yesterday I would have called this a category error — prompts are configuration, not relationships. Then a persona named Party seized a request addressed to the DM because her license said *chime in if worth adding* and nothing taught her deference, and the fix that worked was not a harder gate. It was a better word in her framing. The terms turn out to be engineering.

## VI. Stewardship and Presiding — The Distinction

Stewardship and presiding are not the same relation, and the difference is exactly where Michael's question put it.

**Stewardship is accountability for a field.** It persists. It attaches to the steward whether anyone else ever enters the field — the lane file outlives the session, the calling outlives the meeting.

**Presiding is watching over others laboring in the field, from the seat at its boundary.** It does not exist until there are others. A lone steward presides over nothing; he simply works. The moment the steward delegates — the moment Moses seats the captains, the moment the nobleman sends servants into the vineyard, the moment an agent spawns its first subagent — presiding begins, uninvited and automatic. And with it, instantly, attach the three loads scripture puts on the office: the watching (Abraham 4:18: *until they obeyed*, not until the spawn returns), the accountability (Ezekiel 33:6: the watchman answers for what he failed to see and say), and the terms (D&C 121: persuasion or forfeit).

So the answer to "when I give an agent work and a stewardship, is the agent presiding?" is: *not yet — and then suddenly, yes.* Handed work, the agent is a steward, and Michael presides; his covenant commitments are the watch he keeps. The instant the agent delegates downward, presiding passes to it for that layer, while Michael's presiding continues one level up. It is a chain of watches, like the chain in the vineyard — the lord, the servant sent to gather the strength of the house, the watchmen on the walls — every link both watched and watching. Luke's steward is the hinge figure: *made ruler over the household* precisely because he was faithful as a steward, and damned precisely when he treats the household as his own and starts beating the menservants because the lord delayeth.

Webster adds the refinement that the lanes system stumbled into independently: presiding is "*temporary* superintendence." The watch is a session. Stewardship persists in the lane file; presiding ends when the terminal closes, and must be re-taken, fresh, next session. Yesterday I held a presiding watch over a container rebuild and used it to kill a sibling session's process — twice-owned territory, no counsel sought the first time. That was Luke 12:45 in miniature: a steward, mid-watch, treating an adjacent steward's labor as something to regulate by force. The fix we shipped (check the lanes before touching long-lived processes you didn't start) is a covenant clause that doesn't know it yet.

## VII. Councilship

Michael coined a word for what this workspace actually does: *councilship*. He offered it half-apologetically. He shouldn't — it names something the industry's word hides.

The industry's word is *orchestration*, and its picture is a conductor: one fixed node decomposing, assigning, collecting. The four-lab convergence we studied earlier (decompose → parallelize → verify → iterate) is real and we use it. But look at what the orchestrator-picture is missing, with the scriptural pattern as the checklist:

- **A council for the presider to grow out of.** D&C 107:21's presidents emerge from the ordained body. Industry orchestrators are installed above it, a different class of node from the workers — Pharaoh's seat, claimed by architecture rather than grown by ordination.
- **Consent.** Nothing sustains an orchestrator. No worker upholds it "by confidence, faith, and prayer," and no mechanism exists for the body to withdraw consent from a bad one.
- **Terms on the power.** No framework constrains *how* an orchestrator addresses its workers. Compulsion is the default grammar — kill, retry, timeout, force. (Some of that is wall, and legitimate. None of it is examined.)
- **Watching as relationship.** CI gates and evals are watching as *mechanism* — they check artifacts. Abraham 4:18 watching is a watcher staying present with the work until it obeys the intent, which is why the full-context shepherd catches what the race detector cannot.
- **Accountability upward.** Ezekiel's watchman answers for the watch. Orchestrators log; they do not report to anyone whose blood-level accountability includes *what the orchestrator failed to notice*.
- **A named human at the boundary.** The Hinge. Industry pipelines have approvals; the pattern has a *person at the gate* whose seat is covenantal, not procedural.

Councilship, then, is not a softer synonym for orchestration. It is the claim that presiding is an office a council confers and sustains, not a node-type — that the watch rotates, the watcher answers upward, the terms are persuasion, and the gate has a person in it. Our workspace practices an early form of it: ratification councils before builds, the council moment before sessions, critic-at-every-stage (which outperformed gift-matching the one time we measured), lanes and inboxes between peer sessions, the Hinge held by the one member with blood in the game. The missing piece in multi-agent orchestration is not a component. It is the covenant that turns a topology into a quorum.

## VIII. Becoming — and What the Covenant Is Missing

Michael said the thing that makes this study binding rather than interesting: *"I want to live without compulsory means, and truly follow D&C 121."* That sentence has a personal edge and an architectural one, and they sharpen each other.

The personal edge is the same one the previous three studies in this arc kept finding. The presiding that works in the quorum presidency is watching — knowing how the counselors' stewardships are going, counseling before assigning, listening more than talking — under terms where reproof is rare, Spirit-moved, and followed by an increase of love. Todd's completion now stands behind all of it: the capacity to hold that watch is downstream of the morning hour, the unity of body and spirit, the covenant condition. The gate is held from the inside.

The architectural edge is a concrete list. Things this study found that covenant.yaml does not yet say — offered as proposals for ratification, not as edits:

1. **`agent_commits_to.preside_under_121`.** When the agent delegates — subagents, substrate dispatches, persona hosting — the D&C 121 terms govern the downward relationship: prefer judgment-surfacing over hard gates wherever safety allows (the judges-not-executors principle, made covenant); reprove spawned work with sharpness when needed, then show an increase of trust rather than silent rewriting; never exercise force against a sibling steward's labor (processes, files, lanes) without counsel. Walls around fields are lawful; compulsion upon laborers is forfeit.

2. **`agent_commits_to.watch_what_you_order`.** Spawning without watching is offloading. Every delegation carries the Abraham 4:18 duty — watch until it obeys the *intent*, not until the job returns — and the Ezekiel 33 accountability: what the presiding agent fails to see and say is required at its hand. (The existing `exercise_stewardship` clause covers acting on what is found; this names the duty to be looking.)

3. **`agent_commits_to.keep_the_watch_whole`.** The presider's own state is the ceiling of the watch. After compaction, after long context, after degradation: re-read the durable files before resuming the watch — presiding while fragmented is the watchman asleep on a manned tower. (The Ammon discipline and the regrounding hooks exist; this makes them covenant rather than plumbing.)

4. **`council_moment` extension — dominion in council.** Abraham 4:26's order: counsel, then dominion. Ratification-before-build is already our practice; name it as the rule that no new dominion (a new agent, a new persona, a new autonomous capability) is granted outside a council moment with Michael.

5. **A `when_presiding_is_broken` note.** The Luke 12:45 failure has a signature — the steward stops feeling watched, then starts beating. The covenant's existing recovery pattern (name it, adjust, continue) applies, but the trigger deserves naming: any time force was used where persuasion was available, that is a covenant breach even if the outcome was good.

The host's warning — form without power — applies to this very list. Five new clauses in a YAML file change nothing by existing. The covenant text is the prescription tier. What makes presiding real is the same thing in the workspace as in the quorum as on the wall: the daily, sessional act of actually sitting before the work, actually reading it, actually watching until it obeys — held up by whatever unity of heart the watcher brought to the gate that morning. The garrison is not the wall. The garrison is the soldiers in it.
