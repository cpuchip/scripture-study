# The Seal in the Tradition

*A sub-study of [more-or-less-than-this.md](../more-or-less-than-this.md): does the apostolic tradition confirm, refine, or contradict the seal's resolution? — 2026-07-12*

**Binding question:** How does the apostolic tradition resolve the tension the seal creates with an open canon — what, per Hunter (Apr 1981), Holland (Apr 2008), and Andersen (Apr 2005), distinguishes the Lord's lawful "more" from the forbidden addition — and does their resolution confirm, refine, or contradict the parent study's finding that the crime is *counterfeit authorship, not quantity*?

## I. The line the parent study drew, and who else has drawn it

The parent study met an objection it could not skip. The Lord seals his word with "whatsoever is more or less than this cometh of evil," yet the Lord plainly adds — "line upon line," a whole Restoration of scripture, a canon left open at the top. So the seal cannot mean truth never grows. The parent's resolution was that the divider is not quantity but authorship: "The sin is not having a further thought. The sin is establishing your further thought *for his doctrine*." Man's more-or-less, wrested and then stamped with God's name, is forbidden; God's more, given by revelation through his own order, is the engine of the Restoration. "Addition is not the enemy. Counterfeit authorship is."

That resolution was reached by exegesis in this workspace. The question this sub-study asks is whether the apostles reached it too, and whether they left the line where the parent left it. The parent's citation pass surfaced three talks that touch the seal family, so the honest test is to read all three and let them confirm, sharpen, or break the finding. Two of them drive straight at the open-canon tension. The third, it turns out, is answering a different question — and saying so is part of the finding.

## II. Hunter draws the line at the author

The occasion of [Howard W. Hunter's 1981 talk](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1981/04/no-man-shall-add-to-or-take-away.md) is a missionary handed the exact objection the parent study had to meet. Someone had thrown [Revelation 22:18–19](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rev/22.md) at the Book of Mormon: "If any man shall add unto these things, God shall add unto him the plagues... And if any man shall take away... God shall take away his part out of the book of life." The canon is closed, the argument runs; the Book of Mormon adds; therefore it is cursed.

Hunter's first answer is textual. The warning guards "the words of 'the book of this prophecy'": the Revelation of John, one book, singular, self-naming. It cannot reach the whole Bible, whose sixty-six books were "brought together and compiled into one volume long after John wrote," and it cannot even reach the New Testament, because "John himself added to scripture after writing the book of Revelation" when he later wrote his epistles. Run the closed-canon reading consistently, Hunter says, and [Deuteronomy 4:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/deut/4.md) ("Ye shall not add unto the word which I command you, neither shall ye diminish ought from it") would strand every book after Moses. "Such an absurdity would result in discarding the greater part of the Old Testament and all of the books of the New Testament."

Then Hunter states the resolution, and it is the parent study's Section VI in an apostle's mouth, forty-five years early:

> "A careful reading of each of these admonitions makes it clear that *man* is not to make changes in the revelations of the Lord: *man* is not to add to or take from the words of God. There is no indication or intimation that God could not, or would not, add to or take from... Without question he would have the right and power to give additional revelation."

The line falls exactly where the parent drew it: at the author. The prohibition binds *man*; it never bound God. What the parent study derived from the wording of 3 Nephi 11:40 ("establish it *for my doctrine*"), Hunter derived from the wording of John and Moses ("*man* is not to add"), and both landed on the same divider. The forbidden "more" is the human alteration passed off as the Lord's word; the lawful "more" is the Lord's own continuing speech. Hunter names that lawful more directly — it is "continuous revelation," without which "Noah would not have been prepared for the deluge" and "Abraham would not have been guided from Haran to Hebron." He closes with the angel John himself saw, the one "having the everlasting gospel to preach" ([Revelation 14:6](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rev/14.md)): the very book that sounds like a closed door forecasts the door reopening. This is not a contradiction of the parent study. It is its earliest witness.

## III. Holland: the lawful "more" is addition that builds up

[Jeffrey R. Holland's 2008 talk](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2008/04/my-words-never-cease.md) takes the same objection and confirms the same line, then presses past it into territory the parent study named but did not develop. He gives the man/God divider its positive half: what the divine "more" actually *does*.

Holland opens on the manuscript facts, sharper than Hunter's. Biblical scholarship now holds that Revelation 22:18 "applies only to the book of Revelation, *not* the whole Bible"; of "5,366 known Greek New Testament manuscripts, only 35 contain the whole New Testament as we now know it, and 34 of those were compiled after a.d. 1000." The Bible-as-one-volume is a medieval artifact, not the thing John was sealing. And the pattern of scripture is addition all the way down: "virtually every prophet of the Old *and* New Testament has added scripture to that received by his predecessors." Why Isaiah after Moses, why Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Amos? Holland answers with Jehovah's own words to Moses: "My works are without end, and... my words... never cease" ([Moses 1:4](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/1.md)). The canon is open because God's speech does not stop. That is the lawful "more" given its author.

The refinement is in what Holland says next. The parent study, guarding the *forbidden* more, argued that "in the economy of truth, addition *is* subtraction" — every counterfeit addition silently removes the real doctrine and stands in its place. True of man's more. But Holland describes the *divine* more doing the opposite:

> "Continuing revelation does not demean or discredit existing revelation. The Old Testament does not lose its value in our eyes when we are introduced to the New Testament, and the New Testament is only enhanced when we read the Book of Mormon."

The Lord's own statement of purpose seals it: "I do not bring it to destroy that which they have received, but to build it up" ([D&C 10:52](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/10.md)). So the two "mores" are the same operation, adding words, running opposite arithmetic. Man's addition subtracts, because it replaces. God's addition multiplies, because it builds up the word already given. Which arithmetic you get is decided entirely by the source. That completes the parent's finding: authorship does not just license the divine more, it changes what the more *is*.

Holland then supplies the deepest grounding for the phrase "counterfeit authorship" itself. Quoting N. T. Wright, he moves the whole question off the page: the risen Jesus did not say all authority is given to the books you will write, "but... 'All authority in heaven and on earth is given to *me*.'" Therefore "the scriptures are *not* the ultimate source of knowledge for Latter-day Saints. They are manifestations of the ultimate source. The ultimate source of knowledge and authority for a Latter-day Saint is the living God." If authority is a living Person and not a closed text, then "establish it *for my doctrine*" is not merely tampering with a document. It is impersonating someone who is still speaking. The parent study called the crime forgery; Holland shows the signature being forged belongs to a Person, not a page.

And that turns the original accusation around. The whole closed-canon argument accuses the Restoration of the "add" half of the seal. But to close the canon is to commit the "diminish" half: it keeps back God's present voice, shrinking him to a God who "spake" but no longer "speaketh." Holland ends on exactly that reversal, borrowing Emerson's plea that teachers proclaim "that God is, not was; that He speaketh, not spake." The accuser wielding "thou shalt not add" against the open canon is himself diminishing the word — subtracting the living God down to a past-tense one. The seal cuts against the hand that swings it.

## IV. Andersen is answering a different question

The parent study's citation pass listed [Neil L. Andersen's 2005 talk](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2005/04/beware-of-the-evil-behind-the-smiling-eyes.md) as one of only two conference citations of D&C 93:25, "the tradition's rare direct engagement with the truth seal." Reading the talk corrects that. Andersen never touches the seal, the open canon, or the "more or less" formula. His single reference to D&C 93:25 is a footnote hung on one sentence of body text, "He is a liar and a loser," said of the devil. The citation reaches the verse's *liar* clause, not its *seal* clause, and the talk is a discernment sermon to young men, not an argument about the canon.

Honesty requires saying so plainly rather than bending the talk to fit the binding question. Andersen does not resolve the tension, because Andersen is not working the tension. The citation count overstated the engagement: both of the truth seal's rare citers reached for its description of the devil, not for its definition of the lie, which leaves the seal formula even more untouched ground than the parent's density map suggested.

But the talk is not off-topic, because it answers the *other* half of the parent study: the half in its Becoming section, "the lie that looks like truth." Andersen's whole subject is evil that does not present as evil. His friend, an FBI agent, pushed past a smiling suspect who "did not appear angry or afraid," took him for "harmless," and only a sudden impression, "Beware of the evil behind the smiling eyes," made him turn back to find the semiautomatic pistol already in the man's hand. From that, the doctrine: "the choice of good over evil is not always easy because evil frequently lurks behind smiling eyes." Young men slip, Andersen says, not because they choose a thing marked evil, but because "they did not see the evil behind eyes that appeared quite harmless. They saw the fun, the pleasure, the acceptance."

That is the phenomenology of "more or less than this." The confabulated quote, the plausible addition, the doctrine wrested a hair off true: none of them arrive wearing the label of a lie. They arrive smiling, harmless, generous, which is precisely why the parent study called more "the direction the liar prefers." Andersen's counsel is the discernment side the parent study did not spell out: talk to your parents, follow the prophet, "let the Holy Ghost be your guide," gain your own testimony. The way to judge, as [Moroni 7:15](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/7.md) puts it, "is as plain... as the daylight is from the dark night" — but only to the eye that has learned to look behind the smile. Andersen confirms the parent's picture of the liar even while leaving its canon question alone.

## V. The verdict

The apostolic tradition confirms the parent study's finding, and refines it twice.

It confirms it at the center. Hunter and Holland both meet the open-canon objection, and both resolve it exactly where the parent resolved it: the prohibition binds man, not God; the crime is the human alteration passed off as the Lord's word, not the growth of truth. Hunter said it in 1981, in the same two verses the parent study built on, so the finding is not a modern re-reading the tradition might resist but a doctrine an apostle preached decades before this study framed it. The tradition does not contradict Section VI. It anticipated it.

The first refinement is Holland's positive half. The parent study proved the forbidden more is really subtraction; Holland supplies its mirror, that the divine more is really construction: it "does not demean or discredit," it comes "not to destroy... but to build it up." Same act, opposite result, source deciding which. The second refinement is Holland's relocation of authority off the page and onto a living Person, which turns "counterfeit authorship" from a metaphor about forged documents into its literal meaning — impersonating a God who has not stopped speaking. And with it comes the reversal: closing the canon is itself the seal's "diminish," subtracting God's present voice.

There is one quieter confirmation the talks did not intend. Holland reaches for [D&C 10:52](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/10.md), "not to destroy... but to build it up," as his proof of the lawful more. Sixteen verses later in that same 1829 revelation stands the seal itself: "Whosoever declareth more or less than this... is not of me" (D&C 10:68). And between them, the crime named outright: the Lord will "confound those who have altered my words" (D&C 10:42). The forbidden more, the lawful more, and the counterfeiter are all in one chapter, given in one sitting. The distinction the parent study drew and the apostles confirmed was never spread thin across the canon needing assembly. It was set down whole, the first time the Lord explained why the Book of Mormon was coming forth at all.

## VI. Becoming

The correction I owe most is about Andersen, and it is a correction of my own kind of error. The parent study's citation appendix, my work a session earlier, read a number off an index and called Andersen's talk "direct engagement with the truth seal." The number was real; the characterization was more than the number could bear. Reading the actual talk, the engagement dissolves into a footnote calling the devil "a liar and a loser." I had done to the citation index exactly what the seal forbids: taken a verified count and established for it a conclusion it did not contain. A citation is not an engagement. The tradition quotes 93:25's picture of the liar and walks past 93:25's picture of the lie, and no bulk-count could have told me that. Only reading the talk could.

Which is the whole discipline, running one turn deeper than I usually catch it. Read before quoting keeps false words out of a quotation. But the count is a quote too, and so is the gloss I put on the count. "Andersen engages the truth seal" was a plausible sentence, smiling and harmless, sitting in a finished study looking exactly like a finding. It was more than I had verified. The evil behind the smiling eyes, in a study agent's work, is the sentence that reads like a result and is actually an inference dressed as one — and the only thing that turns me back to see the pistol is the same thing that saved Hunter's whole argument: a careful reading of the actual words. Not the index of the words. The words.
