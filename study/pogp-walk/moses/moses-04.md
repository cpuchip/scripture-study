# Moses 4 — the council, the rebellion, and the Fall that agency made possible

**Binding idea:** Genesis 3 told the Fall with its first act missing — *where did the serpent come from?* Moses 4 restores it: the premortal council, where Satan's "salvation" plan was to **destroy agency** (4:3). So the chapter is a matched pair with chapter 3 — God built the command to guard freedom (3:17); Satan's whole proposal was to abolish it (4:3). The Fall is the cost, and the necessity, of a world run on choice.

**Read:** Before the serpent, the council. Satan "came before me, saying—Behold, here am I, send me, I will be thy son, and I will redeem all mankind, that one soul shall not be lost, and surely I will do it; wherefore give me thine honor" (4:1). "But… my Beloved Son… said unto me—Father, thy will be done, and the glory be thine forever" (4:2). "Because that Satan rebelled against me, and sought to destroy the agency of man… and also, that I should give unto him mine own power… I caused that he should be cast down" (4:3); "he became Satan… the father of all lies, to deceive and to blind men, and to lead them captive… even as many as would not hearken unto my voice" (4:4). Then the Eden temptation: Satan "put it into the heart of the serpent" and "spake by the mouth of the serpent" (4:6-7), "for he knew not the mind of God, wherefore he sought to destroy the world" (4:6). The serpent's pitch mixes a lie with a truth — "Ye shall not surely die" (4:10) but "ye shall be as gods, knowing good and evil" (4:11). Eve eats, gives to Adam; their eyes open; they hide; God asks "Where goest thou?" (4:15); the blame runs down the chain (4:18-19). The consequences follow — the serpent cursed, the first messianic word ("he shall bruise thy head, and thou shalt bruise his heel," 4:21), sorrow in childbearing, the ground cursed, "dust thou wast, and unto dust shalt thou return" (4:25). Adam names Eve "the mother of all living" (4:26); God makes "coats of skins, and clothed them" (4:27); and "the man is become as one of us to know good and evil" (4:28), so he is driven out lest he eat the tree of life and "live forever" — cherubim and a flaming sword guard the way (4:31).

**Thoughts:**
- *The two plans are a study in pronouns.* Satan's proposal (4:1) runs on "**I**" — "here am I… **I** will redeem… surely **I** will do it… give me thine honor." Five first-person assertions and a demand for God's honor. The footnote on that "I" sends to Isaiah 14:13 — Lucifer's five "I wills" ("I will ascend… I will exalt my throne… I will be like the Most High"). Christ's answer (4:2) is the exact inversion: "**thy** will be done, and the glory be **thine** forever." Not one "I." The rebellion isn't a different redemption *method* so much as a different *direction of glory* — toward self or toward the Father. (And the footnote chains 4:2 to Luke 22:42 — Gethsemane's "thy will be done." The same words that defined Christ in the council define Him at the cross.)
- *Satan's "mercy" is coercion, and that's the chapter's hinge with chapter 3.* "I will redeem all mankind, that one soul shall not be lost" sounds generous until you read 4:3: he "sought to destroy the agency of man." You can guarantee no soul is lost only by removing the possibility of being lost — i.e. by removing choice. So Satan's salvation is salvation-by-force. And that lands precisely against Moses 3:17, where God *built agency into the first command* ("nevertheless, thou mayest choose for thyself"). Chapter 3: God guards freedom. Chapter 4: Satan's entire platform is to abolish it. The plan of salvation is, at its root, the defense of agency — and the war in heaven was fought over exactly that.
- *"He knew not the mind of God… he sought to destroy the world" (4:6) — the great irony.* Satan tries to wreck the plan by causing the Fall, not understanding that the Fall *is* the plan's doorway (2 Ne 2 — "Adam fell that men might be"). The adversary is an unwitting instrument: his attempt at destruction triggers exactly the mortal probation God intended. This is the deepest structural fact of the plan — opposition doesn't defeat the design, it serves it, because the designer comprehends what the destroyer cannot.
- *"He spake by the mouth of the serpent" (4:7) — the Restoration demythologizes.* It was never a talking snake; it was Satan using a creature as a mouthpiece. The verse strips the folklore and names the actual agent. (And 4:6: Satan "had drawn away many after him" — the third part of D&C 29:36 — so the temptation in Eden is continuous with the rebellion in heaven; the same adversary, the same followers.)
- *The lie is a truth bent (4:10-11).* "Ye shall not surely die" is false; "ye shall be as gods, knowing good and evil" is *true* — God Himself confirms it in 4:28 ("the man is become as one of us to know good and evil"). The most effective deception isn't pure falsehood; it's a real outcome offered on a false premise (that it could be had *without* death, *against* God, *now*). The thing Satan promised was real; the way he sold it was the lie.
- *"Coats of skins" is the first sacrifice and the first type (4:27).* God clothes their nakedness with skins — which required the death of an animal. The shame they tried to cover with fig leaves (their own work, 4:13) God covers with a life laid down (His provision). The covering of shame by a death not their own is the Atonement in seed form, in the same chapter that gives the first messianic prophecy (4:21).

**Critical analysis / tensions named:**
- *"Fortunate Fall" vs. real transgression.* LDS theology holds both: the Fall was a *transgression* (a real crossing of a real command, with real consequence — mortality, expulsion) **and** a necessary step "down" that men might be (2 Ne 2:25). Moses 4 keeps the tension honest: God genuinely forbade it (3:17, 4:17), and the consequences are genuine (4:22-25); yet 4:6 reveals the Fall served the plan Satan couldn't see. Neither "Adam sinned grievously" (over-reading the curse) nor "it wasn't really wrong" (over-reading the necessity) fits; the chapter holds both.
- *Eve's role — beguiled, not merely duped.* "The serpent beguiled me, and I did eat" (4:19). The Restoration reading (2 Ne 2; later prophets) treats Eve's choice as, in the larger frame, *necessary and even wise* (she "saw that the tree was… desired to make her wise," 4:12) — without erasing that it came by deception. I'm flagging the interpretive weight here rather than resolving the centuries-old argument about Eve; the text gives both the beguiling and the wisdom-seeking.
- *The curses as description vs. prescription.* "He shall rule over thee" (4:22), the cursed ground, sorrow — read by some as God *ordaining* a hierarchy, by others as God *describing* fallen conditions the gospel then works to heal. The walk marks this as a live interpretive seam, not a settled reading.
