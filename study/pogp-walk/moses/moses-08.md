# Moses 8 — Noah, the covenant line, and "it repented Noah" (not the Lord)

**Binding idea:** The Book of Moses ends where Genesis 6 pivots to the ark — but first it restores what Genesis left ambiguous. The "sons of God" who marry the "daughters of men" aren't angels; they're the covenant line of Noah, and the sin is the covenant *sold* and its title *appropriated by apostates* (8:13-21). And in a single, easily-missed edit, the JST moves the grief: where Genesis says "it repented the Lord that he had made man," Moses says "**it repented Noah**" (8:25). God does not regret His children. The prophet grieves; God is constant.

> **Note:** Noah's grace + the prophet-pattern are worked in our Come Follow Me studies **`cfm-20260215-noah-found-grace`** and **`cfm-20260215-prophets-noah-parallel`**. This note digests the chapter and records the walk's distinct observations; it links rather than re-deriving.

**Read:** Methuselah, Enoch's son, "was not taken" with Zion "that the covenants of the Lord might be fulfilled… that Noah should be of the fruit of his loins" (8:2) — the remnant preserved (7:52). The line runs Methuselah → Lamech → Noah, who begets Japheth, Shem, and Ham. "Noah and his sons hearkened unto the Lord… and they were called the sons of God" (8:13). But "the sons of men saw that those daughters were fair, and they took them wives, even as they chose" (8:14), and "the Lord said unto Noah: The daughters of thy sons have sold themselves" (8:15). The Lord ordains Noah "after his own order… even as it was given unto Enoch" (8:19), and Noah preaches: "Believe and repent of your sins and be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ, the Son of God, even as our fathers" (8:24). But the people answer with a boast: "Behold, we are the sons of God; have we not taken unto ourselves the daughters of men?… our wives bear unto us children, and the same are mighty men… men of great renown" (8:21) — and "they hearkened not." "It repented Noah, and his heart was pained that the Lord had made man on the earth, and it grieved him at the heart" (8:25); the Lord decrees destruction "for it repenteth Noah that I have created them… and he hath called upon me; for they have sought his life" (8:26). "Thus Noah found grace… a just man, and perfect in his generation; and he walked with God, as did also his three sons" (8:27). "The earth was corrupt before God, and it was filled with violence… The end of all flesh is come before me… I will destroy all flesh from off the earth" (8:28-30).

**Thoughts:**
- *The "sons of God / daughters of men" mystery is dissolved (8:13-15, 21).* Genesis 6:1-4 — "the sons of God came in unto the daughters of men… the same became mighty men… men of renown" — has fueled millennia of speculation about angels mating with humans and siring a race of giants (the Nephilim). Moses 8 demythologizes it completely: "the sons of God" are *Noah's faithful line* — "Noah and his sons hearkened unto the Lord… and they were called the sons of God" (8:13). The sin is not angelic miscegenation; it's *the covenant daughters marrying out of the covenant* ("the daughters of thy sons have sold themselves," 8:15) and apostates *claiming the covenant title*. The "mighty men of renown" are simply their children (8:21) — boasted of by the wicked, not demigods. The whole strange passage turns out to be about a covenant community losing its boundary by intermarriage and pride. (The footnote on 8:14 "wives" sends to TG "Marriage, Interfaith" — the editors read it the same way.)
- *The apostates weaponize the covenant title (8:21).* The chilling detail: when Noah preaches repentance, the wicked answer, "Behold, **we are the sons of God**; have we not taken unto ourselves the daughters of men?" They appropriate the exact title 8:13 gave to the faithful — and use it as a *justification* for the very intermarriage that broke the covenant. "We're the chosen people; surely we can do as we please." The title meant to mark covenant faithfulness becomes a license for covenant-breaking. (This is the same move as Satan's "I am the Only Begotten" in Moses 1:19 — the counterfeit always steals the true name.)
- *The gospel is unbroken from Adam to Noah (8:19, 24).* "The Lord ordained Noah after his own order… even as it was given unto Enoch" (8:19), and Noah preaches "Believe and repent… and be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ, the Son of God, **even as our fathers**" (8:24). This closes the gospel-from-the-beginning thread that ran Adam (5:8, 6:52) → Enoch (6:32, 7:11) → Noah (8:24): the *same* gospel, the *same* baptism in Christ's name, the *same* priesthood order, across three dispensations and the whole antediluvian age. The Restoration's claim isn't that Christianity is new in the meridian of time; it's that it is *original* — Noah preached Jesus Christ by name (8:24) two and a half millennia before Bethlehem. (Gospel-pattern-is-eternal, in its strongest single statement.)
- *"It repented Noah," not the Lord (8:25) — the JST protecting God's character.* Genesis 6:6 reads: "it repented the LORD that he had made man… and it grieved him at his heart." That verse has troubled readers forever — does God *regret*? Does the omniscient Creator change His mind? Moses 8:25 reassigns it: "it repented **Noah**, and his heart was pained that the Lord had made man… and it grieved **him** [Noah] at the heart." The grief is the *prophet's*, not a divine regret. And 8:26 makes the destruction partly responsive to Noah's plea ("he hath called upon me; for they have sought his life"). This is theologically of a piece with Moses 7: the God who *weeps* over His children (7:28) does not *regret making* them (8:26 keeps the regret out of God's mouth). God's sorrow is grief-over-their-suffering, never regret-over-their-existence. The one-word change (the Lord → Noah) guards the constancy of God established two chapters earlier.
- *Noah is the Enoch who stays (8:27).* "He walked with God" — Enoch's defining phrase (6:34, 7:69), now Noah's. But Enoch's Zion was *taken up* (7:69); Noah's calling is to *remain* and carry the seed through the judgment. Methuselah "was not taken" precisely so the line would survive (8:2). Two ways to walk with God: be translated out of a wicked world (Enoch), or be preserved *through* its destruction (Noah). The remnant theology (a seed "always… found among all nations," 7:52) requires the second kind.

**Critical analysis / tensions named:**
- *The book ends mid-narrative, and that's the point (8:30).* "The end of all flesh is come before me… I will destroy all flesh" is Genesis 6:13. The "Selections from the Book of Moses" stop *exactly* on the eve of the flood and never carry the ark, the deluge, or the rainbow covenant (those continue in the JST of Genesis, not canonized in the PoGP). So the Book of Moses is precisely what its heading says — *selections*: the visions (Moses 1), the restored creation/Fall (2-4), and the antediluvian gospel (5-8), handed back to Genesis at the flood. Honest to name the boundary: the PoGP gives us the lost *front matter and theology* of Genesis, not a parallel Genesis.
- *"My Spirit shall not always strive with man" (8:17) is the terminal-state doctrine again.* The withdrawal of the Spirit before judgment — the footnote chains to 2 Ne 26:11 and Ether 2:15, and it's the same doctrine the BoM walk found at the *end* of both Nephite and Jaredite civilizations ("the Spirit of the Lord had ceased striving," Ether 15:19). The flood is the antediluvian instance of the pattern the whole canon repeats: a people can strive against the Spirit until it withdraws. Cross-walk link.
- *The 120 years (8:17) — mercy in the warning.* "Yet his days shall be an hundred and twenty years" — read by some as a new human lifespan cap, by others (and the immediate context fits) as the *grace period* before the flood: 120 years of Noah's preaching before judgment. The chapter frames it as time-to-repent ("if men do not repent, I will send in the floods"). Flagging the interpretive options; the context leans toward probation-window.
