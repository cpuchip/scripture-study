# Moses 1 — "Man is nothing" and "this is my work and my glory"

**Binding idea:** The God for whom worlds are without number has staked His whole glory on one thing — *man*. The chapter crushes Moses with scale, then names worth; the two poles ("man is nothing," v10 / "this is my work and my glory… man," v39) don't cancel — they combine.

**Read:** A freestanding vision (June 1830) Joseph received as the front door to the Book of Moses — not part of Genesis, but the prologue placed before it. Moses is caught up to "an exceedingly high mountain," sees God "face to face," and endures it only because "the glory of God was upon Moses" (v1-2). God names Himself — "I am the Lord God Almighty, and Endless is my name" (v3) — calls Moses "my son… in the similitude of mine Only Begotten" (v6), and shows him "the world and the ends thereof, and all the children of men" (v8). Then the glory withdraws, "Moses was left unto himself," and "he fell unto the earth" (v9); for "many hours" he can't recover his natural strength, and concludes: "Now, for this cause I know that man is nothing, which thing I never had supposed" (v10). In that emptied state Satan comes — "Moses, son of man, worship me" (v12) — and Moses, who has just seen the real glory, answers by its absence: "where is thy glory, that I should worship thee?… it is darkness unto me… And I can judge between thee and God" (v13-15). Satan escalates to open imposture — "I am the Only Begotten, worship me" (v19); Moses fears, sees "the bitterness of hell," "Nevertheless, calling upon God, he received strength," and expels him "In the name of the Only Begotten" (v20-21). Filled with the Holy Ghost, Moses asks *why* and *by what* the worlds were made, and God answers: "by the word of my power, have I created them, which is mine Only Begotten Son" (v32); "worlds without number have I created… and by the Son I created them" (v33); they are innumerable to man "but all things are numbered unto me" (v35). The summit: "For behold, this is my work and my glory—to bring to pass the immortality and eternal life of man" (v39). It closes with the chapter predicting its own loss and restoration — men will "esteem my words as naught and take many of them from the book," so "I will raise up another like unto thee" (v40-41).

**Thoughts:**
- *The architecture is scale-then-worth, and it's deliberate.* The whole first movement empties Moses out: worlds without number, heavens that "cannot be numbered unto man," and a man who, unglorified, falls to the dirt for hours. Only after that does v39 land — and it lands harder *because* of the emptying. The same God for whom creation is innumerable has exactly one "work and glory," and it is the immortality and eternal life of the creature who just learned he is "nothing." The verse is not consolation tacked onto despair; it is the answer the despair was set up to receive. Read v10 and v39 as a single sentence with a long pause in the middle.
- *"Man is nothing" is physics, not self-loathing.* The mechanism is exact: Moses endures God's presence only while "the glory of God was upon Moses" (v2); remove it and "he fell" (v9). So "nothing" means *nothing-when-left-unto-himself* — man unsupported by glory collapses. This is the matter-spectrum lesson in narrative form: our capacity to stand in glory is borrowed, not native. Humility here is accurate measurement, not low self-esteem. (Cf. Ether 3:2, Hel 12:7 in the footnotes — the same "we are nothing" said from the dust.)
- *The Satan scene is a discernment manual, and its single criterion is glory.* Moses' refrain — "where is thy glory?" (v13), "where is thy glory, for it is darkness unto me?" (v15) — is a usable test, and he can run it *because he just saw the genuine article.* You discern the counterfeit by having known the real. Note where the temptation falls: immediately after Moses learns both his nothingness (v10) and his sonship (v13). The adversary attacks at the seam between "I am nothing" and "I am a son of God" — exactly the place a person is most disoriented about their own worth.
- *Satan's actual move is imposture, not opposition (v19).* He doesn't offer an alternative to Christ; he claims to **be** Christ — "I am the Only Begotten, worship me." He seizes the very title Moses was just told he is only in the *similitude* of (v6, 13, 16). The deepest lie isn't anti-Christ-as-opposite but anti-Christ-as-imitation — and the footnote sends to JS—M 1:6 and the "False Christs" of the last days. Moses' whole defense is that he has seen the original and this has no glory.
- *The "nevertheless" sits exactly where it always sits (v20).* "Moses began to fear… he saw the bitterness of hell. **Nevertheless, calling upon God, he received strength.**" Textbook instance of the word at the hinge between ruin and rescue. And the deliverance escalates: Moses commands Satan three times (v16, 18, 21), and only the third — "*In the name of the Only Begotten*" — fully expels him. Power over the adversary is invoked in the Name, never self-generated. The first two commands are Moses' resolve; the third is borrowed authority, and that's the one that works.
- *Christ is the Creator here, and that's the chapter's load-bearing Christology (v32-33).* The Father speaks; the *creating* is done "by the word of my power… which is mine Only Begotten Son," "by the Son I created them." Worlds without number, made by Christ. This is the PoGP's opening deposit in the is-Christ-God / Christ-as-Creator line, and it's the direct setup for Moses 2:1 ("by mine Only Begotten I created"). The footnotes chain it to John 1:3 and Heb 1:2 — "all things were made by him."
- *"Endless is my name" means the name is the nature (v3).* Not a label — a description: "without beginning of days or end of years." The footnote to D&C 19:10 is the silver platter: "Endless" and "Eternal" *are* God's name, which is why "endless punishment" reads as "God's punishment," not "punishment without end." And it rhymes forward to v38 — "there is no end to my works, neither to my words" — the Endless God's works share His endlessness.
- *This chapter knows it is a restored text (v40-41).* It predicts its own deletion from the Bible ("take many of them from the book which thou shalt write") and its recovery through "another like unto thee" — the seer "like unto Moses" of 2 Ne 3:9, i.e. Joseph. So the very first chapter of the Book of Moses frames the entire Pearl of Great Price project: plain and precious things lost, restored by a latter-day prophet. v42's gate — "Show them not unto any except them that believe" — is faith-restriction, not secrecy-for-control (the opposite of the Jaredite secret combinations' "worketh in the dark").

**Critical analysis / tensions named:**
- *"No God beside me" (v6) inside a book of plurality.* The same verse names the Father, the Only Begotten, and the Savior. Standard resolution: the Father is speaking, and "no God beside me… him only shalt thou serve" (cf. v15) is about *whom Moses worships*, not a metaphysical denial of the Son whom the verse simultaneously calls "the Savior." The PoGP holds monotheism-of-worship within a plurality of divine persons (made explicit in Abr 4). Flagging, not resolving — it's a real seam, handled the same way across our `is-jesus-christ-god` work.
- *Don't let v10 flatten into a worthlessness doctrine.* The chapter's own arc forbids it; v39 is v10's answer. Any reading of "man is nothing" that doesn't carry to "man is my work and my glory" has stopped reading too early.
- *Provenance:* Moses 1 was never part of Genesis — it's a vision Joseph received (June 1830) and prefixed to the JST/Book of Moses. Reading it as a deliberately-placed *prologue* (the creation account of Moses 2–4 sits *inside* this vision) is the honest frame.
