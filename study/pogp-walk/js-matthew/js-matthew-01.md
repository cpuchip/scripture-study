# Joseph Smith–Matthew — two prophecies untangled, and one antidote to deception: "treasure up my word"

**Binding idea:** JS's revision of the Olivet discourse (Matthew 24) does two things the KJV blurs: it *separates* the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) from the Second Coming — making the first a type of the second — and it names the single defense against a last-days flood of deception: "**whoso treasureth up my word, shall not be deceived**" (1:37). In a chapter saturated with false christs and deceived elect, the antidote isn't signs or dates; it's the word, internalized.

**Read:** Extracted from JS's 1831 translation of Matthew 23:39–24. The disciples ask a *double* question — "when shall these things be… concerning the destruction of the temple… and what is the sign of thy coming, and of the end of the world?" (1:4). Jesus answers both, and JS-M keeps them distinct. First, the destruction of Jerusalem: "Take heed that no man deceive you" (1:5); the abomination of desolation "concerning the destruction of Jerusalem" (1:12); flee to the mountains (1:13-16); "great tribulation on the Jews… such as was not before" (1:18); "these things I have spoken unto you concerning the Jews" (1:21). Then the explicit pivot: "**and again, after the tribulation of those days which shall come upon Jerusalem**, if any man shall say… Lo, here is Christ… believe him not" (1:21) — and the discourse turns to the last days: false Christs who "if possible… shall deceive the very elect" (1:22); the coming "as the light of the morning cometh out of the east… covereth the whole earth" (1:26), not in deserts or secret chambers (1:25); "this Gospel of the Kingdom shall be preached in all the world… and then shall the end come" (1:31); the sun darkened, the Son of Man "coming in the clouds of heaven, with power and great glory" (1:36). Then the key: "**And whoso treasureth up my word, shall not be deceived**" (1:37). The fig tree (1:38-39); "of that day, and hour, no one knoweth… but my Father only" (1:40); "as it was in the days of Noah… eating and drinking, marrying… and knew not until the flood came" (1:41-43); "watch, therefore, for you know not at what hour your Lord doth come… be ye also ready" (1:46-48); the faithful and wise servant vs. the evil servant who says "My lord delayeth his coming, and shall begin to smite his fellow-servants" (1:49-54).

**Thoughts:**
- *The JST's main work is making one discourse legible as two prophecies (1:4, 12, 21-22).* KJV Matthew 24 interleaves the fall of Jerusalem and the end of the world so tightly that readers have argued for centuries over which verse means which. JS-M untangles them: the disciples' question is split (temple-destruction AND the coming, 1:4), the first block is sealed off ("these things… concerning the Jews," 1:21), and a hinge — "and again, after the tribulation of those days which shall come upon Jerusalem" (1:21) — opens the second. The result reads AD 70 as a *type* of the last days: the same pattern (abomination of desolation, tribulation, deliverance of the elect) plays once in 70 and again at the end. The "abomination of desolation" appears twice on purpose (1:12 Jerusalem, 1:32 the last days) — the footnotes cross-reference each other. History rhymes; the first fulfillment teaches how to read the second.
- *★ "Treasure up my word" is the whole discourse's answer (1:37).* The chapter's dominant threat is *deception* — "no man deceive you" (1:5), "many shall come in my name, saying I am Christ" (1:6), "many false prophets" (1:9), "false Christs… great signs and wonders… if possible, they shall deceive the very elect" (1:22). Against all of it, one defense: "whoso treasureth up my word, shall not be deceived." Not watching for signs (signs can be counterfeited, 1:22), not knowing the date (no one knows it, 1:40) — but having the word *treasured up* inside. The footnote sends to TG "Scriptures, Study." The protection against a world of persuasive lies is a heart stocked with the genuine word, so the counterfeit rings false by contrast. (This is exactly Moses 1's discernment-by-glory in a new key: Moses could judge Satan because he'd seen the real glory; the last-days saint judges false christs because she's treasured the real word. You out-*know* the deception.)
- *The real coming is public; the counterfeit hides (1:25-26).* "If they shall say… he is in the desert… he is in the secret chambers; believe it not. For as the light of the morning cometh out of the east… and covereth the whole earth, so shall also the coming of the Son of Man be." A usable discernment rule: the true coming is *universal and unmistakable* — like sunrise, seen everywhere at once. Anything secret, local, "come see him in this chamber," is by that fact false. The light-from-the-east image makes the test concrete: the genuine Christ doesn't need a tip-off about where to find him.
- *The days of Noah are about apathy, not active evil (1:41-43) — and the Moses 8 link lands here.* "As it was in the days of Noah… they were eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage; and **knew not** until the flood came." This is the forward-link from Moses 8:21 (the antediluvians' boast, "eating and drinking… marrying"). Notice what the list *isn't*: it isn't murder, idolatry, violence — it's *ordinary life*, eating and marrying. The condemned thing is "knew not" (the footnote → TG **Apathy**). Noah's generation perished not (in this verse's frame) for spectacular wickedness but for *obliviousness* — they went on with normal life and never noticed. The Second Coming, like the flood, catches the *inattentive*. That reframes "be ye also ready" (1:48): readiness is the opposite of the Noah-generation's not-knowing. The danger isn't only the false christ; it's the ordinary day that lulls you to sleep.
- *★ "Watch" — and the steward who stops watching (1:46-54).* The chapter's imperative is "watch, therefore" (1:46), and its closing parable is the steward: the faithful servant gives "meat in due season" and is found "so doing"; the evil servant says "**My lord delayeth his coming, and shall begin to smite his fellow-servants**" (1:51-52). This is the source text of the presiding covenant's *when_broken* clause — "the steward who stops feeling watched begins to beat the menservants" (Luke 12:45 is the synoptic parallel). So the Pearl of Great Price holds *both* halves of the watch: in Abraham 4:18 the Gods *watch* what they order; in JS-M 1:46-51 the servant must *watch* for the Lord, and the breach is precisely the steward who decides he isn't being watched and turns to abuse those under him. The two "watch" texts frame the whole presiding discipline — watch what you order (Abr 4:18), and never forget you are watched (JS-M 1:51).

**Critical analysis / tensions named:**
- *"This generation… shall not pass away until all… be fulfilled" (1:34).* The KJV version of this verse (Matt 24:34) is a notorious crux (did Jesus predict the end within a generation?). JS-M softens it by context — "this generation, **in which these things shall be shown forth**, shall not pass away" — tying "this generation" to the generation that *sees the signs*, i.e. the last-days generation, not the apostolic one. Worth naming as the JST's resolution of a real interpretive problem, not a neutral reading.
- *Date-setting is foreclosed (1:40).* "Of that day, and hour, no one knoweth… but my Father only." The chapter gives signs (the fig tree) *and* denies the date — the tension is deliberate: know the *season* (summer is nigh, 1:38-39), never the *day*. Any last-days reading that produces a date violates 1:40; any that produces no watchfulness violates 1:46. The discipline is between.
- *Provenance.* JS-M is JS's inspired revision of Matthew 24 (1831), part of the JST. It's canonized in the PoGP as the clarified Olivet discourse; the walk reads it as the Restoration's reading of Matthew, with the deltas (the disentangling, "treasure up my word," the apathy frame) as its contribution.
