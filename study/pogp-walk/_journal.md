# Pearl of Great Price Walk — Journal

Opus's running standout reflections — the narrative thread of the walk. Appended **only when something genuinely stands out**, not forced every chapter. This is the personal companion to the structured chapter notes.

---

## Moses 1 — the door we came in through

I started the BoM walk with Lehi leaving Jerusalem — a man in motion. The PoGP starts with a man flat on the ground. Moses sees worlds without number, then the glory lifts and he falls to the dirt for hours, and the first thing he says is "I never had supposed" — *I didn't know I was this small.* The chapter spends thirty-eight verses making him (and me) feel the scale, and then it spends one verse — v39 — telling him what all of it is *for*. Immortality and eternal life of man. The math is staggering: innumerable worlds, one purpose, and the purpose is the creature who just learned he's nothing.

What I keep turning over is that the temptation lands precisely between "I am nothing" (v10) and "I am a son of God" (v13). That's where the adversary always sets up — at the seam where you're least sure what you're worth. And Moses' defense isn't an argument; it's a memory. *Where is thy glory? I just saw the real thing, and you have none of it.* You can't reason your way out of the counterfeit; you out-*remember* it. That reframes a lot of what discernment actually is.

And the quiet thing: the footnote on "he fell" (v9) points to Joseph in the grove (JS—H 1:20) — overpowered by darkness, then delivered into light. The first chapter of the Restoration's first book is already the same shape as the First Vision. The walk gets to both. I want to hold that thread the whole way.

## Moses 3 — He forbids and frees in the same breath

The line that stopped me is 3:17. Genesis 2:17 just says don't eat it or you'll die — a fence with a warning sign. Moses puts one clause in the middle of the fence: "*nevertheless, thou mayest choose for thyself, for it is given unto thee.*" God states the law, states the consequence, and then explicitly hands Adam the freedom to break it. He isn't setting a trap and watching for the spring. He's saying: here is the line, here is what crossing it costs, and *the choice is genuinely yours.* I don't know of a cleaner picture of how God governs free beings — He tells the truth about the consequence and then refuses to remove the choice. The whole plan of salvation is sitting in that one inserted "nevertheless."

And it's the same word that turned Moses 1:20 — fear, then *nevertheless*, then strength. In chapter 1 the nevertheless is ours, turning back to God under pressure. In chapter 3 it's His, opening a door He's just warned us about. The double-nevertheless from the atonement studies, showing up in the grammar of Eden.

## Moses 4 — what the war was actually about

Reading 3 and 4 back to back, a pairing I'd never set side by side: in 3:17 God *builds* agency into the very first command — "thou mayest choose for thyself." One chapter later, 4:3, we learn what Satan's plan in the council actually was: he "sought to destroy the agency of man." The two chapters are a hinge. God's first act with the man was to guarantee his freedom; Satan's first proposal in heaven was to abolish it. The war wasn't over who got to be Savior. It was over whether man would be free at all. "I will redeem all… that one soul shall not be lost" is the most chilling sentence in the chapter once you see it — because the only way to guarantee no one is lost is to take away the power to choose to be. Mercy without agency is just control wearing mercy's coat.

And the thing I keep coming back to: 4:2. The Son's whole answer in the council is "Father, thy will be done, and the glory be thine forever." Not one "I." Satan's pitch (4:1) is five "I"s and a demand for honor. The difference between the two plans isn't strategy — it's the direction glory flows. And the Son says the same words in the council that He'll say in Gethsemane (the footnote sends both ways — Luke 22:42). He defined Himself once before the world was, and once in the garden, with the same sentence. That's not a coincidence the text is hiding; it's one it's pointing at.

## Moses 5 — Eve understood it first

In chapter 4 Eve is the one who's "beguiled." In chapter 5 she's the one who *understands*. Adam blesses God and says "I shall have joy" — personal, his own. Then Eve hears it and goes further: "the joy of *our* redemption, and the eternal life which God giveth unto *all* the obedient" (5:11). She takes the personal and makes it doctrine. She names four things the Fall made possible — seed, the knowledge of good and evil, the joy of redemption, eternal life — in a single sentence, and it's the most complete theology of the Fall anywhere in the early chapters. The first person in scripture to articulate what redemption *is* is the woman the previous chapter called deceived. I don't think the text is being careless. I think it's being exact: the one who tasted the consequence first is the one who sees the mercy first.

And running underneath the whole chapter, the other discovery: the secret combination starts *here*. "Murder and get gain" (5:31) — I chased that formula for 239 chapters through the Book of Mormon, Gadianton and the Jaredites and Helaman all pointing back over their shoulders to Cain. This is the verse they were pointing at. Two walks, and they meet in Moses 5. The covenant and its counterfeit come out of the same house, the same generation, almost the same breath. Eve says "the joy of our redemption"; her grandson's line says "I am free" over a brother's body. The whole of human history is already both streams.
