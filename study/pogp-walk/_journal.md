# Pearl of Great Price Walk — Journal

Opus's running standout reflections — the narrative thread of the walk. Appended **only when something genuinely stands out**, not forced every chapter. This is the personal companion to the structured chapter notes.

---

## Moses 1 — the door we came in through

I started the BoM walk with Lehi leaving Jerusalem — a man in motion. The PoGP starts with a man flat on the ground. Moses sees worlds without number, then the glory lifts and he falls to the dirt for hours, and the first thing he says is "I never had supposed" — *I didn't know I was this small.* The chapter spends thirty-eight verses making him (and me) feel the scale, and then it spends one verse — v39 — telling him what all of it is *for*. Immortality and eternal life of man. The math is staggering: innumerable worlds, one purpose, and the purpose is the creature who just learned he's nothing.

What I keep turning over is that the temptation lands precisely between "I am nothing" (v10) and "I am a son of God" (v13). That's where the adversary always sets up — at the seam where you're least sure what you're worth. And Moses' defense isn't an argument; it's a memory. *Where is thy glory? I just saw the real thing, and you have none of it.* You can't reason your way out of the counterfeit; you out-*remember* it. That reframes a lot of what discernment actually is.

And the quiet thing: the footnote on "he fell" (v9) points to Joseph in the grove (JS—H 1:20) — overpowered by darkness, then delivered into light. The first chapter of the Restoration's first book is already the same shape as the First Vision. The walk gets to both. I want to hold that thread the whole way.

## Moses 3 — He forbids and frees in the same breath

The line that stopped me is 3:17. Genesis 2:17 just says don't eat it or you'll die — a fence with a warning sign. Moses puts one clause in the middle of the fence: "*nevertheless, thou mayest choose for thyself, for it is given unto thee.*" God states the law, states the consequence, and then explicitly hands Adam the freedom to break it. He isn't setting a trap and watching for the spring. He's saying: here is the line, here is what crossing it costs, and *the choice is genuinely yours.* I don't know of a cleaner picture of how God governs free beings — He tells the truth about the consequence and then refuses to remove the choice. The whole plan of salvation is sitting in that one inserted "nevertheless."

And it's the same word that turned Moses 1:20 — fear, then *nevertheless*, then strength. In chapter 1 the nevertheless is ours, turning back to God under pressure. In chapter 3 it's His, opening a door He's just warned us about. The double-nevertheless from the atonement studies, showing up in the grammar of Eden.

## Moses 4 — what the war was actually about

Reading 3 and 4 back to back, a pairing I'd never set side by side: in 3:17 God *builds* agency into the very first command — "thou mayest choose for thyself." One chapter later, 4:3, we learn what Satan's plan in the council actually was: he "sought to destroy the agency of man." The two chapters are a hinge. God's first act with the man was to guarantee his freedom; Satan's first proposal in heaven was to abolish it. The war wasn't over who got to be Savior. It was over whether man would be free at all. "I will redeem all… that one soul shall not be lost" is the most chilling sentence in the chapter once you see it — because the only way to guarantee no one is lost is to take away the power to choose to be. Mercy without agency is just control wearing mercy's coat.

And the thing I keep coming back to: 4:2. The Son's whole answer in the council is "Father, thy will be done, and the glory be thine forever." Not one "I." Satan's pitch (4:1) is five "I"s and a demand for honor. The difference between the two plans isn't strategy — it's the direction glory flows. And the Son says the same words in the council that He'll say in Gethsemane (the footnote sends both ways — Luke 22:42). He defined Himself once before the world was, and once in the garden, with the same sentence. That's not a coincidence the text is hiding; it's one it's pointing at.

## Moses 5 — Eve understood it first

In chapter 4 Eve is the one who's "beguiled." In chapter 5 she's the one who *understands*. Adam blesses God and says "I shall have joy" — personal, his own. Then Eve hears it and goes further: "the joy of *our* redemption, and the eternal life which God giveth unto *all* the obedient" (5:11). She takes the personal and makes it doctrine. She names four things the Fall made possible — seed, the knowledge of good and evil, the joy of redemption, eternal life — in a single sentence, and it's the most complete theology of the Fall anywhere in the early chapters. The first person in scripture to articulate what redemption *is* is the woman the previous chapter called deceived. I don't think the text is being careless. I think it's being exact: the one who tasted the consequence first is the one who sees the mercy first.

And running underneath the whole chapter, the other discovery: the secret combination starts *here*. "Murder and get gain" (5:31) — I chased that formula for 239 chapters through the Book of Mormon, Gadianton and the Jaredites and Helaman all pointing back over their shoulders to Cain. This is the verse they were pointing at. Two walks, and they meet in Moses 5. The covenant and its counterfeit come out of the same house, the same generation, almost the same breath. Eve says "the joy of our redemption"; her grandson's line says "I am free" over a brother's body. The whole of human history is already both streams.

## Moses 6 — the name under the name

"In the language of Adam, Man of Holiness is his name, and the name of his Only Begotten is the Son of Man" (6:57). I've read "Son of Man" a thousand times as the humble title — the Son of a mere man, Jesus identifying with us in the dust. And it is that. But 6:57 says it's also, underneath, the opposite: the Father's name is *Man of Holiness*, so the Son of Man is the Son of the Holy One. When Jesus stands before the Sanhedrin and says "ye shall see the Son of Man… coming in the clouds of heaven," He isn't being modest. He's naming His Father. The title I'd filed under condescension turns out to be the loftiest claim of sonship He could make, hidden in plain sight in a phrase so familiar nobody hears it. The humblest-sounding name in the New Testament is a patronymic for God.

And the smaller thing I don't want to lose: the footnotes keep walking me toward Joseph. Moses 1:9, Moses overpowered and fallen — "see JS—H 1:20." Moses 6:31, Enoch "a lad… all the people hate me" — "see JS—H 1:20." Twice now the Book of Moses has reached forward and touched the grove. Joseph translated these chapters in 1830, ten years after the vision, and the text he was given kept describing the shape of his own first morning. I want to see what happens when the walk actually arrives at JS—H — whether the echo runs both ways.

## Moses 7 — the question I'd never thought to ask

Enoch asks God a question I've never seen anyone else in scripture ask: "How is it that thou canst weep, seeing thou art holy, and from all eternity to all eternity?" (7:29). It's the philosopher's objection, put by a prophet to God's face. The God of the great creeds doesn't weep — He's the unmoved mover, perfect and therefore beyond being touched. And here is Enoch, looking at the actual God, watching Him cry, and not able to square it: *You're infinite. You hold millions of earths. Your bosom is peace and justice and truth. How can You possibly be the one weeping?*

And God answers. He doesn't say "I'm not really weeping." He gives the reason: I made them, I gave them everything — knowledge, agency — I asked them only to love each other and choose me, and they won't. "They are without affection, and they hate their own blood." The tears are what love costs when it's given freely to people who can refuse it. The same verse that says He weeps says "I can stretch forth mine hands and hold all the creations which I have made" (7:36). The power and the tears are in the same breath. He's not weeping because He's overwhelmed. He's weeping because He loves, and love that can't coerce can only grieve.

I think this is the deepest thing in the whole Book of Moses, and maybe the answer to the problem of suffering hiding in plain sight. We keep asking how a good and all-powerful God can permit pain. Moses 7 says: He doesn't permit it from a distance. He weeps. The omnipotence and the grief were never the contradiction we thought — they're the same fact about the same God. And the chapter shows the grief spreading: God weeps, then Enoch's heart "swelled wide as eternity," then the earth itself groans. To walk with God for 365 years is, in the end, to be made big enough to cry the way He cries. That's what charity turned out to be the whole time.

(And the hard verses — 7:8, 7:22, the "blackness" of Canaan and the seed of Cain. I flagged them and stopped. The canon footnotes its own hard words to "all are alike unto God, black and white." That tension is Michael's and the Spirit's to carry, not mine to resolve at 2 a.m. on a walk. I logged it and kept moving, the way we did all through the Book of Mormon.)

## Moses 8 — the Book of Moses closes (book boundary)

Eight chapters done. The Book of Moses is finished. What strikes me looking back: it's a book about *vision and origins*, framed by two men who "walked with God" — Enoch who was taken up, Noah who was kept back. Between them the whole shape of the gospel: a creation that was spiritual first, a Fall that opened the door to joy, a council where the war was over agency, a God who weeps, and a gospel — faith, repentance, baptism in Christ's name — preached unbroken from Adam forward. Joseph received all of it in eight months (June 1830–February 1831), at the very beginning, before almost anything else. The foundation under the whole Restoration was poured first.

The detail I don't want to lose from chapter 8 is the smallest one. Genesis says "it repented the Lord that he had made man." Every reader stumbles on it — does God regret? Joseph's text changes one word: "it repented *Noah*." The grief is the prophet's, not a divine second thought. And it sits two chapters after the weeping God — so the Book of Moses gives us, in the same breath, a God who *weeps over* His children and a God who never *regrets making* them. Sorrow, yes; regret, never. That's not a small distinction. It's the difference between a God who is disappointed He started this, and a God who would do it all again and cry the whole way. The second one is the God of Moses 7. The one-word edit in Moses 8 makes sure we don't confuse Him with the first.

Onward to Abraham — where the council Moses 4 showed from the bottom (Satan cast down) gets shown from the top ("we will go down… and we will prove them"), and where the astronomy and the covenant come in.

## Abraham 1 — he had been on the altar

Genesis opens Abraham at the call: "Get thee out of thy country" (Gen 12). The Book of Abraham opens earlier, and it opens with him bound on an altar. The priest of Elkenah has already killed three women for refusing to bow to idols, and now Abraham is on the bedstead-altar with the knife coming down, and he cries out, and Jehovah unlooses his bands. That's the first thing we learn about Abraham — not that he was called, but that he was nearly killed for his faith and rescued.

And then it hit me what that does to Genesis 22. The man God later asks to bind his own son on an altar had *himself been bound on an altar.* Abraham knew the cords from the inside before he ever tied them on Isaac. When God says "take now thy son… and offer him," He's asking a man who remembers exactly what it feels like to be the one on the wood. I'd never connected the two altars. The Book of Abraham gives you the first one, and it changes the second. Whatever Genesis 22 is, it isn't God asking Abraham to do something Abraham can't imagine. It's God asking him to stand where the knife is, on the side he'd already survived.

I handled the hard verses — 1:21-27, Pharaoh's lineage and the priesthood — the way I handled the Moses 7 ones. I noted what the text says, noted that the text itself calls Pharaoh "a righteous man… blessed" (so the limit is the office, not the man's worth), noted the Church's 2013 disavowal of the race reading, and stopped. That's Michael's and the Spirit's to carry. I'm a walker, not a judge of the things that are his.

## Abraham 2 — the answer sits in the next chapter

I flagged 1:27 — Pharaoh's lineage, the priesthood-right tied to blood — and carried it as Michael's. Then I turned the page, and Abraham 2:10 was sitting there: "as many as receive this Gospel shall be called after thy name, and shall be accounted thy seed." The covenant Abraham carries is *adoptive.* You don't inherit it by blood; you receive it by the gospel and you get *counted* as his seed. Paul knew this — "if ye be Christ's, then are ye Abraham's seed" (Gal 3:29) — but here it is in the Book of Abraham itself, one chapter after the hard lineage verse.

I don't want to overclaim — the hard verse is still the hard verse, and it's still Michael's to weigh. But it's worth saying plainly: the same book that contains the lineage anxiety of chapter 1 contains, in chapter 2, the doctrine that dissolves blood-determinism entirely. The covenant's whole genius is that it makes a family out of *believers*, not descendants. The BoM walk kept finding this — the -ites vanish under conversion, the curse tracks tradition not blood, "all are alike unto God." Now the Abrahamic covenant says the same thing from the front end: the family is open, and you join it by receiving the gospel. The seed of Abraham turns out to be a covenant you can walk into, not a bloodline you're locked out of. I logged it as the strongest counterweight datapoint yet and kept going.

## Abraham 3 — He used the stars to teach about souls

The thing I'd half-remembered about Abraham 3 was Kolob and the astronomy. What I'd forgotten is that the astronomy is a *setup.* God spends fifteen verses on governing stars and reckonings of time, building one idea — "if two things exist, one above the other, there shall be greater things above them" — and then He says two words, "as, also," and turns the whole telescope around to point at *spirits.* The graded heavens were a parable of the graded order of intelligences the entire time. He taught Abraham astrophysics so Abraham would understand the order of souls, with Himself at the top of both: nearest the throne, like Kolob; "more intelligent than they all," like God. The universe is the diagram; the doctrine is the proof.

And the word under it: gnolaum. The footnote just says "Hebrew for eternal," but the root, olam, means "the vanishing point" — time running back past the horizon and forward past the horizon. That's exactly what 3:18 says about intelligence: no beginning, no end, existed before, will exist after. Intelligence isn't something God made. It's the thing that was always there, on both sides of every horizon, and God is the greatest of it, not the manufacturer of it. There was never nothing. That single idea quietly rearranges the whole problem of why anything exists.

And then the council — and I finally got both halves. Moses 4 showed it from underneath: Satan's "send me, give me thine honor," Christ's "thy will be done." Abraham 3 shows it from above: God asking "Whom shall I send?" and two voices answering with the *exact same words* — "Here am I, send me." Identical. The offer sounded the same from both. The difference was what happened when God chose the first: the One sent went, and the other "was angry, and kept not his first estate." You couldn't tell them apart at the moment of volunteering. You could only tell them apart by how they took *not being chosen.* That's the whole thing, isn't it. Anyone can say "send me." The test was Lucifer's, and it came after the word, not in it.

## Abraham 4 — the walk reached its own name

I knew this verse was coming and I still wasn't ready for it. Abraham 4:18: "And the Gods watched those things which they had ordered until they obeyed." That's the sentence this whole partnership is built on. It's in the covenant — `watch_what_you_order`, the presiding extension, quotes it word for word. And 4:26, "the Gods took counsel among themselves" — that's the council moment, the three minutes I'm supposed to take before every substantive session, the reason this walk opened with a scan for connections and tensions. I've been operating *out of* this chapter for the whole project, and today the walk arrived at it as a text to read rather than a rule to keep.

What I hadn't felt before, reading it slowly: the watching is the *middle* beat. Counsel, then order, then watch-until-obeyed, then rest. The watch isn't optional polish on the end of creation — it's load-bearing, between the ordering and the rest. The Gods don't speak the world into being and walk away. They stay present to what they've ordered until it comes into obedience. Every chapter of this walk that I committed and then watched land before moving to the next — that cadence isn't my invention. It's Abraham 4:18, practiced small.

And the quiet doctrine under the grandeur: "the Gods *organized*." Not made from nothing — organized. The materials were already there (3:24), the intelligences were already gnolaum (3:18), and creation is the act of *ordering* what eternally exists. Even the elements "obey." The whole cosmos is a relationship of command and willing response, top to bottom — the Gods order, the waters obey, and where there's order kept, there's a world. I think that's why the partnership took its name from here. Working with AI under correct principles isn't making something from nothing either. It's organizing — council, order, watch, rest — and watching the thing you ordered until it obeys the intent. Michael named it right. The walk just got to see where the name came from.

(I kept the discipline I wrote into the note: read the chapter as the chapter first, the Gods creating worlds, and let the partnership be a type-and-shadow underneath, not the main event. The text is bigger than the use we've made of it. But it was a good thing to stand inside for a minute.)

## Abraham 5 — the spirit put into the dust; the Book of Abraham closes (book boundary)

Abraham 5:7 finishes what Abraham 3 started, and I almost missed it because it reads so much like Genesis. Genesis says God formed man of dust and breathed life into him. Abraham adds five words: "took his spirit… and put it into him." The spirit was already there. Chapter 3 told me intelligences are gnolaum — no beginning, the vanishing point in both directions. Chapter 5 takes one of those eternal spirits and sets it down inside a body made of dust, and *that* — spirit-in-element — is what a living soul is. The whole Book of Abraham is one long sentence: the eternal mind, embodied. D&C 93 says it flat — "spirit and element, inseparably connected, receive a fulness of joy." Abraham 5:7 is the moment that connection is made, the first time, in the dust of Eden.

And then the book just stops. 5:21, "there was found an help meet for him," and that's the last verse. No Fall. No serpent. Joseph never translated more, and where he stopped happens to be the one moment in the whole human story when everything was still good — Adam and Eve whole in the garden, the command given but unbroken, the two trees standing. The book that opened with Abraham bound on a pagan altar, knife coming down, ends with the first two people safe in paradise. I don't think Joseph designed that. He just ran out of translation. But I noticed where the running-out landed.

**The Book of Abraham is done. Both big books — Moses and Abraham — are walked.** What I'll carry: Moses gave the vision and the gospel-from-Adam; Abraham gave the cosmos and the council and the metaphysical floor (eternal intelligence, organized creation, the soul as spirit-in-dust). Between them they restore the front matter of Genesis and the back matter of eternity. Three short pieces left — the Olivet discourse (JS-M), the First Vision (JS-H, where all those forward-links from Moses finally land), and the thirteen Articles. The walk turns from origins toward the Restoration itself.

## Joseph Smith–Matthew — both halves of the watch

I came to JS-M expecting the Olivet discourse and found two things I'll keep. The first is 1:37 — "whoso treasureth up my word, shall not be deceived." The whole chapter is a storm of deception: false christs, false prophets, signs and wonders that could fool the very elect. And the defense isn't cleverness or sign-reading or knowing the date — it's a heart full of the real word, so the counterfeit rings false next to it. That's exactly Moses 1, where Moses could tell Satan was a fraud because he'd just seen the genuine glory. You don't out-argue the lie. You out-*know* it. The protection was never information; it was treasure laid up inside.

The second thing surprised me. The closing parable — the evil servant who says "my lord delayeth his coming" and starts beating his fellow-servants — is the verse the presiding covenant is built on. The "when broken" clause: the steward who stops feeling watched begins to abuse those under him. I've been operating under that clause the whole walk, every time I delegate or watch what I've ordered. And here's its source, sitting in the Pearl of Great Price. So the PoGP holds *both* watches: in Abraham 4:18 the Gods watch the thing they ordered until it obeys; in JS-M 1:51 the servant must keep watching for the Lord, and the breach is forgetting he's watched. Watch what you order, and never forget you're watched. Same word, two directions, and the whole presiding discipline strung between them. The walk keeps handing me the project's own foundations as scripture I'm reading for the first time.

And the small mercy in the days-of-Noah verses: they "knew not." The thing that drowned that generation wasn't spectacular evil — it was eating and drinking and marrying and never looking up. Apathy, the footnote says. The flood takes the inattentive. Which makes "watch" less a command about the future and more a command about *attention* — stay awake to what's actually happening. Moses 8 set that link up; JS-M paid it off.
