# Joseph Smith–History — the grove the whole walk was pointing at

**Binding idea:** The founding-events narrative — and the chapter where the Book of Moses's forward-links land. Moses, translated in 1830, kept describing a soul overpowered by darkness and then delivered into light (Moses 1:9), and a young prophet "slow of speech" whom "all the people hate" (Moses 6:31). Here is the experience those texts prefigured: a fourteen-year-old "seized upon by some power… thick darkness… ready to sink into despair" (1:15-16), then a pillar of light and two Personages — and a boy hated and persecuted for telling it (1:25). The Restoration's first revelation (Moses 1) and its founding event (the grove) share one architecture. And the echo runs *both ways*: Joseph lived the grove in 1820 and translated Moses 1 in 1830 — he received, as scripture to translate, the shape of his own first morning.

**Read:** Amid an "unusual excitement on the subject of religion" and "a war of words and tumult of opinions" (1:5, 10), Joseph asks "Who of all these parties are right; or, are they all wrong together?… how shall I know it?" (1:10). Reading James 1:5 — "If any of you lack wisdom, let him ask of God" — "never did any passage of scripture come with more power to the heart of man than this did at this time to mine" (1:11-12). He retires to the woods, spring 1820, and kneels. "Immediately I was seized upon by some power which entirely overcame me… Thick darkness gathered around me, and it seemed to me for a time as if I were doomed to sudden destruction" (1:15). "But, exerting all my powers to call upon God to deliver me… at the very moment when I was ready to sink into despair… I saw a pillar of light… which descended gradually until it fell upon me" (1:16). "When the light rested upon me I saw two Personages, whose brightness and glory defy all description, standing above me in the air. One of them spake unto me, calling me by name and said, pointing to the other—**This is My Beloved Son. Hear Him!**" (1:17). Told to join none of the sects — "they draw near to me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me… having a form of godliness, but they deny the power thereof" (1:19). Persecuted, he holds: "I had seen a vision; I knew it, and I knew that God knew it, and I could not deny it" (1:25). Moroni comes (1823), names Joseph's work, quotes Malachi 3-4 (with variants), Isaiah 11, Acts 3, Joel 2 (1:33-41); the plates and the Urim and Thummim (1:35). He gets the plates (1827), translates (1829), and in May 1829 John the Baptist descends and confers the Aaronic Priesthood — "Upon you my fellow servants, in the name of Messiah, I confer the Priesthood of Aaron" (1:69) — acting under Peter, James, and John (1:72); they are baptized and receive the spirit of prophecy (1:73).

**Thoughts:**
- *★ The forward-link thread closes here, and it closes both directions.* Twice the Book of Moses described this morning before it was canonically "reached." **Moses 1:9** — Moses "left unto himself… fell unto the earth," and his footnote points straight to JS-H 1:20. **JS-H 1:15-16** is that verse lived: Joseph "seized upon by some power which entirely overcame me… thick darkness… ready to sink into despair," and then the light. The pattern is identical — *overpowered by darkness → cry for deliverance → light* — and it's the same pattern as Lehi's "dark and dreary waste" then the tree (1 Ne 8), and Moses's "many hours in darkness." **Moses 6:31** — Enoch "a lad… all the people hate me" — is JS-H 1:25, the obscure boy "hated and persecuted." But here's what the walk could only see by arriving: **the chronology is inverted.** Joseph had the vision in *1820* and translated the Book of Moses in *1830*. So when he received "he fell… left unto himself," he had already been on the ground in the grove for ten years. The text he was handed to translate described the architecture of his own founding experience. In the order of the canon, Moses prefigures the grove; in the order of Joseph's life, the grove came first and he found it waiting for him in the Book of Moses. The Restoration's first revelation and its first event are the same story told from two ends — and the man in the middle translated his own beginning.
- *The Restoration begins with a question, not a doctrine (1:11-13).* "If any of you lack wisdom, let him ask of God." A fourteen-year-old reads it, believes it *literally*, and goes to a grove to test it. The whole Restoration starts from the conviction that *God answers when asked* — and from the failure of the alternative: "the teachers of religion… understood the same passages of scripture so differently as to destroy all confidence in settling the question by an appeal to the Bible" (1:12). When the Bible alone couldn't settle it (sects reading the same verses to opposite conclusions), the answer wasn't a better argument but a *direct inquiry of God*. This is the same instruction as Moroni 10:4 (the BoM walk's capstone — "ask God… with a sincere heart… and he will manifest the truth"): the Restoration's epistemology is experimental — ask, expecting an answer (Alma 32, James 1:5, Moroni 10:4, D&C 9:8 are one method). The First Vision is James 1:5 *obeyed*.
- *"This is My Beloved Son. Hear Him!" — the Father's whole speech (1:17).* Two distinct, embodied Personages "standing above me in the air" — the corporeal, separate Godhead that overturns the creedal one God of three persons-in-one-substance. And the Father's *only* recorded words in the entire vision are a name and a command: He points to the Son and says "Hear Him." The Restoration opens with the Father deferring to the Son — the first sentence of the dispensation is the Father directing all attention to Christ. Everything after (the plates "the fulness of the everlasting Gospel," 1:34; the priesthood "in the name of Messiah," 1:69) is the unfolding of "Hear Him."
- *The apostasy is diagnosed by the Lord, not by Joseph (1:19).* "They draw near to me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me… having a form of godliness, but they deny the power thereof" — Isaiah 29:13 and 2 Timothy 3:5, spoken by the Personage. The need for a *restoration* (not a reform) is established by the vision itself: "a form of godliness" with "the power thereof" denied is religion that kept the shape and lost the revelation. Which is exactly what the Methodist preacher confirms — "there were no such things as visions or revelations in these days; all such things had ceased with the apostles" (1:21). The apostasy *is* cessationism: the denial that God still speaks. The whole of JS-H is the refutation-by-event of that denial (the same anti-cessationism the BoM walk found in Mormon 9 and Moroni 7).
- *The integrity of witness, and an honest self-report (1:25, 28).* "I knew it, and I knew that God knew it, and I could not deny it" — Joseph likens himself to Paul before Agrippa (1:24): persecution cannot unmake a thing actually seen. But the chapter is also strikingly *honest* about his weakness: "I frequently fell into many foolish errors… guilty of levity, and sometimes associated with jovial company" (1:28) — while insisting "no one need suppose me guilty of any great or malignant sins." The founder writes his own foibles into his own founding history. (This is the honesty frame the project prizes — the witness is more credible for confessing the levity, not less. The same logic as `include_failures` in the covenant.)
- *Moroni situates the Restoration in the prophetic stream — and the Malachi variant names the Abrahamic covenant (1:36-41).* Moroni quotes Malachi 4 "with a little variation": "I will reveal unto you the Priesthood, by the hand of Elijah… and he shall plant in the hearts of the children the **promises made to the fathers**, and the hearts of the children shall turn to their fathers" (1:38-39). Those "promises made to the fathers" are the Abrahamic covenant (Abr 2:9-11) — the walk's own thread — and "the hearts of the children shall turn to their fathers" is the sealing/temple work that binds the generations into that covenant. So Moroni's variant connects the last-days restoration directly to the Abraham covenant the walk traced through Abr 1-2: the turning of hearts is the covenant being sealed across time. Plus Isaiah 11 (the gathering), Acts 3:22-23 (Christ the prophet like Moses — the Moses 1:41 "another like unto thee" fulfilled), and Joel 2 (the Spirit poured out). The Restoration is framed as the convergence of the whole prophetic witness.
- *The priesthood is restored literally, by those who held it (1:68-72).* John the Baptist — a resurrected being — descends "in a cloud of light" and *lays hands* on Joseph and Oliver (1:68-69), conferring the Aaronic Priesthood "which holds the keys of the ministering of angels," under the direction of Peter, James, and John (1:72). The unbroken priesthood line the walk traced (Moses 6:7 "this same Priesthood, which was in the beginning"; Abr 1:3 "from the fathers… even Adam") is now *restored in the last days* — not reinvented, but conferred by hands that held it. Authority returns the way it always traveled: by ordination, hand to head. And the immediate fruit is revelation restored — the spirit of prophecy (1:73) and "the scriptures laid open to our understandings… the true meaning… of their more mysterious passages" (1:74). The cessationist apostasy ends exactly where it's refuted: in renewed revelation.

**Critical analysis / tensions named:**
- *Multiple First Vision accounts.* Joseph recorded the First Vision several times (1832, 1835, 1838-39, 1842), with differences in detail (the 1832 account emphasizes forgiveness of sins; the canonical 1838 account emphasizes which church to join). The Church's position (Gospel Topics essay "First Vision Accounts," 2013) treats the variations as consistent with how anyone retells a formative experience to different audiences over time — and notes the core is stable (a theophany, the Father and Son, the answer to join none). Honest to name: the canonical JS-H is the 1838 account, not the only one, and the walk reads it as such.
- *The honest-self-report is doing real work (1:28).* Some read Joseph's "levity… jovial company" admission as damaging; the walk reads it (with the covenant's `include_failures`) as *strengthening* the witness — a fabricator curating a prophet-image would not insert "I frequently fell into many foolish errors." The confession is evidence of candor, not of disqualification. Flagging the interpretive choice rather than asserting it as fact.
- *The Anthon episode (1:63-65) is one-sided.* Joseph reports it via Martin Harris's account; Anthon later disputed the characterization. The walk notes it as the chapter gives it (Harris's relation) and flags that Anthon's own later letters tell it differently — a genuine historical contest, not a settled event. The doctrinal point ("I cannot read a sealed book" fulfilling Isaiah 29:11) stands within the narrative's frame.
