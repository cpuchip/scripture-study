# Abraham 5 — the spirit put into the dust, and the book that ends in Eden

**Binding idea:** The creation cycle closes with rest (5:2-3, "we will rest… from all our work which we have counseled") — and with the verse that completes the arc from Abraham 3: "the Gods formed man from the dust of the ground, and **took his spirit (that is, the man's spirit), and put it into him**… and man became a living soul" (5:7). The eternal intelligences of chapter 3 are embodied in chapter 5. A soul is a pre-existing spirit placed into organized dust. And then the Book of Abraham simply… ends — at 5:21, in Eden, before the Fall.

> **Facsimile 3 is folded in here** (the close of the book). It depicts Abraham in Pharaoh's court, on the throne, reasoning astronomy to the Egyptians — the fulfillment of 3:15 ("I show these things… that ye may declare all these words"). **★ 2026-06-14: I've since downloaded and looked at the actual image** and read the verbatim Explanations (Fig. 1 Abraham enthroned "with a crown… representing the Priesthood"; Shulem, Olimlah; a band of stars overhead). It stages the **Abr 1:27 ↔ 2:10 reversal** — the rightful priesthood-holder enthroned in Pharaoh's seat, teaching the court whose blood couldn't hold the priesthood. **Read:** "And thus we will finish the heavens and the earth, and all the hosts of them" (5:1). "The Gods said among themselves: On the seventh time we will end our work, which we have counseled; and we will rest… and sanctified it" (5:2-3). They form "the generations of the heavens and of the earth," the plants "before it was in the earth" — the spiritual creation again (5:4-5, cf. Moses 3:5). Then man: "the Gods formed man from the dust of the ground, and took his spirit (that is, the man's spirit), and put it into him; and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and man became a living soul" (5:7) — "the man, whose spirit they had put into the body which they had formed" (5:8). Eden, the two trees, the river; the command — "thou shalt not eat of it; for in the time that thou eatest thereof, thou shalt surely die" — and a note: "I, Abraham, saw that it was after the Lord's time, which was after the time of Kolob; for as yet the Gods had not appointed unto Adam his reckoning" (5:12-13). "The Gods said: Let us make an help meet for the man" (5:14); the deep sleep, the rib, "formed they a woman, and brought her unto the man" (5:16); "bone of my bones… they shall be one flesh" (5:17-18); "and for Adam, there was found an help meet for him" (5:21) — and there the book ends.

**Thoughts:**
- *5:7 is the capstone of the whole Book of Abraham, and it closes the arc from chapter 3.* Genesis 2:7 and Moses 3:7 both say: dust + breath = living soul. Abraham 5:7 inserts the decisive clause: "and **took his spirit (that is, the man's spirit), and put it into him**." The "man's spirit" is the *pre-existing* spirit — one of the eternal intelligences "organized before the world was" (3:22), gnolaum, with "no beginning… no end" (3:18). So the living soul is the *union* of an eternal spirit with organized dust. The footnote sends to D&C 93:33 — "the spirit and the body are the soul of man" / "spirit and element, inseparably connected, receive a fulness of joy." This is the clearest scriptural statement of the embodiment doctrine, and the Book of Abraham is uniquely positioned to make it: it spent chapter 3 establishing that spirits are eternal, and it spends 5:7 putting one of them into a body. The matter-spectrum runs the length of the book — intelligence (3:18) → spirit (the man's spirit) → element (the dust) → living soul (5:7). Genesis gives you the dust and the breath; Abraham gives you the spirit that was there before the dust.
- *The rest is rest from counseled work (5:2-3).* "We will rest… from all our work which we have counseled." Twice the rest is tied to *counsel* — "which we have counseled," "which they counseled among themselves." The Sabbath isn't rest from labor-as-toil; it's rest from a *deliberated, counseled* creation. The whole cycle was a council (4:26), and the rest is the council's completion. This finishes the Abraham 4-5 pattern on the page: counsel (4:1, 26) → order (the six periods) → watch-until-obeyed (4:18) → **rest** (5:2-3). The pattern the project is named for is now whole in the text.
- *Eden ran on Kolob's time (5:13).* "I, Abraham, saw that it was after the Lord's time, which was after the time of Kolob; for as yet the Gods had not appointed unto Adam his reckoning." Before the Fall, Adam was on *celestial* time — the 1000-years-as-a-day reckoning of Kolob (3:4). Mortal time ("Adam's reckoning") wasn't appointed until later (after the Fall). A small, strange, lovely detail the Book of Abraham alone gives: paradise kept God's clock. The mortal stopwatch started when Eden ended.
- *The book ends in Eden, before the Fall (5:21).* Where Moses 8 broke off on the eve of the flood, the Book of Abraham breaks off on the far side of nothing — at the completion of Eden, "there was found an help meet for him," with the two trees standing and no serpent yet. Joseph's translation of the Book of Abraham stopped here (it was published incomplete; he never translated more). So the last words of the Book of Abraham leave Adam and Eve whole in the garden, the command given but unbroken. The book that opened with Abraham bound on a pagan altar (1:12) closes with the first man and woman safe in paradise. An accident of an unfinished translation — and yet the book ends on the one moment in the human story when everything was still good. (Calibrated: this is the providence-of-the-fragment, not a claim about authorial design — Joseph simply stopped. But where it stopped is worth noticing.)

**Critical analysis / tensions named:**
- *The book is incomplete, and that's a fact to hold honestly.* The Book of Abraham ends mid-narrative at 5:21 — no Fall, no Abraham-in-Egypt resolution, no later patriarchal story (Genesis carries all of that). The PoGP's Book of Abraham is a *fragment* of a translation Joseph never finished. The walk reads what exists and notes the boundary, rather than treating the abrupt ending as a designed conclusion.
- *"The Gods" through the embodiment (5:7).* The plural carries all the way into the forming of man's body and the placing of his spirit. Same monotheism-within-plurality seam named at Abr 4:1 — the council of Gods acting as one; not relevant to re-litigate here, but consistent.
- *Facsimile 3 and the historicity question.* Fac 3 (Abraham on Pharaoh's throne teaching astronomy) carries the same translation-provenance question flagged at Abr 1 (the 2014 essay). The walk notes the facsimile as part of the book's witness without resting on a verified Egyptological reading of it.
