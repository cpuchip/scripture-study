# Abraham 1 — the covenant begins with desire, and Abraham himself on the altar

**Binding idea:** Genesis starts Abraham's story at the call (Gen 12); the Book of Abraham starts it earlier and from the inside — with *desire*. "I sought for the blessings of the fathers… desiring also to be one who possessed great knowledge… I became a rightful heir, a High Priest" (1:2). The Abrahamic covenant doesn't begin with God selecting a passive man; it begins with a man who *wanted* righteousness badly enough to seek it under threat of death — bound on a pagan altar (Facsimile 1), delivered by Jehovah Himself.

**Read:** Writing "by his own hand, upon papyrus," Abraham opens in "the land of the Chaldeans," needing "another place of residence" (1:1). He recounts *seeking*: "I sought for the blessings of the fathers, and the right whereunto I should be ordained… desiring also to be one who possessed great knowledge, and to be a greater follower of righteousness… and desiring to receive instructions, and to keep the commandments of God, I became a rightful heir, a High Priest, holding the right belonging to the fathers" (1:2) — a right that "came down from the fathers, from the beginning… even the right of the firstborn… who is Adam" (1:3). But his own fathers had "turned from their righteousness… unto the worshiping of the gods of the heathen" (1:5) and "endeavored to take away my life by the hand of the priest of Elkenah" (1:7). The priest had already offered three virgins, daughters of Onitah, "because of their virtue; they would not bow down to worship gods of wood or of stone" (1:11). As the priest moves to slay Abraham on "the altar… made after the form of a bedstead" (1:12-13), "I lifted up my voice unto the Lord my God, and the Lord hearkened… and the angel of his presence stood by me, and immediately unloosed my bands" (1:15). The voice declares: "Abraham, Abraham, behold, my name is Jehovah, and I have heard thee, and have come down to deliver thee… Behold, I will lead thee by my hand… to put upon thee my name, even the Priesthood of thy father… through thy ministry my name shall be known in the earth forever, for I am thy God" (1:16-19). The Lord destroys the altar and smites the priest (1:20). The chapter then reviews Egypt's origins — Pharaoh "a descendant from the loins of Ham… a partaker of the blood of the Canaanites" (1:21), "of that lineage by which he could not have the right of Priesthood" though "a righteous man" (1:26-27) — and closes with Abraham preserving "the records of the fathers… concerning the right of Priesthood… a knowledge of the beginning of the creation, and also of the planets, and of the stars" (1:31).

**Thoughts:**
- *The covenant starts with holy desire (1:2-4).* The verbs are the point: *sought… desiring… desiring… desiring… sought.* Five times in three verses Abraham names his wanting — for the blessings of the fathers, for great knowledge, to be a greater follower of righteousness, to receive instruction, for the priesthood. The Abrahamic covenant — the spine of the whole Old Testament — is presented not as God reaching down to a random Chaldean but as God answering a man who was already reaching up. "I became a rightful heir, a High Priest" reads almost as consequence of the seeking. This reframes election: chosenness here is the meeting of God's offer and Abraham's hunger. (Compare Moses 5:6 — Adam obeyed before he understood; Abraham *desired* before he received. Two postures, same direction: the heart turned toward God before the gift arrives.)
- *The priesthood is the unbroken line back to Adam (1:3).* "It came down from the fathers, from the beginning of time… even the right of the firstborn… who is Adam." This is the same claim Moses 6:7 made ("this same Priesthood, which was in the beginning, shall be in the end of the world also"). The Book of Abraham and the Book of Moses agree: one priesthood, one covenant, one line — Adam to Enoch to Noah to Abraham — not a series of new beginnings. Abraham receives what Adam held.
- *Abraham is the bound victim before he is the binding father — the Akedah inversion.* The most striking thing the Book of Abraham gives that Genesis withholds: Abraham's *first* recorded experience is lying bound on an altar as the intended human sacrifice (1:12-15). Years later (Gen 22) he will be asked to bind his own son Isaac on an altar. So the man commanded to offer his son had *himself been the offering* — he knew the altar from the victim's side first. Genesis 22 reads differently once you know Abraham had already felt the cords. The near-sacrifice of Abraham frames the near-sacrifice of Isaac: a father asked to do to his son what was once nearly done to him, and a God who delivered both. (Calibrated: the text doesn't draw this line explicitly — it's my reading of the two altars — but the parallel is structural, not imposed.)
- *The virgins of Onitah are martyrs of conscience (1:11).* "Offered up because of their virtue; they would not bow down to worship gods of wood or of stone." The footnote sends to Daniel 3 (Shadrach, Meshach, Abednego — "we will not… worship the golden image"). Before Daniel, before the Maccabees, the Book of Abraham gives us three women who chose death over idolatry. They die; Abraham, in the same danger for the same reason, is delivered — the text doesn't pretend faith always ends in rescue. Sometimes the faithful are delivered *from* the fire (Abraham, the three Hebrews) and sometimes *through* death (the daughters of Onitah). Both are in the chapter, side by side, unreconciled — which is honest.
- *Jehovah delivers in person, and names Himself (1:16).* "My name is Jehovah… I have come down to deliver thee." The deliverer is the premortal Christ (footnote → TG "Jesus Christ, Jehovah"), and He comes *down* to do it personally — the same "come down" of Moses 1 and the council. The covenant's first act toward Abraham is a personal rescue by Christ, who then "put[s] upon thee my name, even the Priesthood" (1:18). Name received, bands unloosed, priesthood conferred — deliverance and covenant in one encounter.

**Critical analysis / tensions named — including the hard one (bin-4):**
- *★ The priesthood-lineage passage (1:21-27) — flagged, not resolved.* This is the hardest passage in the Book of Abraham. The text says Pharaoh was "a descendant from the loins of Ham… a partaker of the blood of the Canaanites" (1:21), that "from Ham, sprang that race which preserved the curse in the land" (1:24), and that Pharaoh was "of that lineage by which he could not have the right of Priesthood" (1:27). **Historically, these verses were used to justify the priesthood and temple restriction on members of Black African descent (lifted 1978), and the lineage-based reading was officially disavowed by the Church in the 2013 Gospel Topics essay "Race and the Priesthood."** Two things the text *itself* says, which honesty requires surfacing: (1) the limit is on the **priesthood office/right**, explicitly *not* on righteousness or divine favor — the very same passage calls Pharaoh "**a righteous man**" who "judged his people wisely and justly," "blessed with the blessings of the earth, and with the blessings of wisdom" (1:26); so the text distinguishes priesthood-lineage-right from worth, blessing, and righteousness; (2) it names "unrighteous dominion" and a *claim* to priesthood ("the Pharaohs would fain claim it," 1:27, footnote → TG "Unrighteous Dominion"). But the connection of priesthood-*right* to blood/lineage is exactly what the modern Church has stepped away from. **bin-4: this stays for Michael and the Spirit, not the agent.** I record what the text says, note the text's own righteous-Pharaoh distinction, note the official disavowal of the race-based reading, and do not attempt to settle it. (Same discipline as Moses 7:8/7:22 — logged for the with-Michael 2 Ne 5:21 study, which now spans both walks.)
- *Translation provenance.* The heading claims "Translated from the Papyrus, by Joseph Smith." The relationship between the surviving Joseph Smith Papyri and the Book of Abraham text is contested (the recovered fragments are funerary texts, not the Abraham narrative); the Church's position (2014 Gospel Topics essay "Translation and Historicity of the Book of Abraham") treats "translation" broadly — revelation catalyzed by the papyri rather than a literal decoding. Honest to name: the Book of Abraham's status as scripture in the Restoration does not rest on a verified Egyptological translation, and the walk reads it as revealed scripture without claiming the papyrus-to-text mechanism.
- *Egypt "signifies that which is forbidden" (1:23).* The folk-etymology of "Egyptus" given here ("which in the Chaldean signifies Egypt, which signifies that which is forbidden") is not standard Egyptology. The walk notes it as the text's own internal etymology, not a linguistic claim to verify.
