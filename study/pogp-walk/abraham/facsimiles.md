## Facsimile 1 — the altar (Abraham 1)

**What I see (first-hand):** A human figure lies on a couch shaped like a **lion** — the couch has a carved lion's head at one end and lion's legs and tail. A standing figure on the left, bald/shaven, leans over the reclining man with a **blade raised** in his hand. The reclining figure has **one arm lifted upward**. Above, to the upper right, a **bird with outstretched wings** hovers. Beneath the couch sit **four jars**, each with a different head — a human/bearded head, a jackal or dog head, a falcon head, and another. Below that, a lower band shows a **crocodile**, and beneath it a row of **upright pillars**. A small offering-stand with lotus stands at the right.

**The Explanation's identifications** *(paraphrased — Fig. numbers as the canonical text gives them; I did not verify exact wording this session):* Fig. 1 = the Angel of the Lord; Fig. 2 = Abraham fastened upon an altar; Fig. 3 = the idolatrous priest of Elkenah attempting to offer Abraham; Fig. 4 = the altar for sacrifice, made after the form of a bedstead; Figs. 5–8 = the idolatrous gods of Elkenah, Libnah, Mahmackrah, and Korash (the four jars); Fig. 9 = the idolatrous god of Pharaoh (the crocodile); Fig. 10 = Abraham in Egypt; Fig. 11 = the pillars of heaven; Fig. 12 = Raukeeyang, the firmament.

**Relation to the text — and it lines up, by eye:**
- **"The altar… made after the form of a bedstead" (Abr 1:13)** — I can *see* it: the couch is a bedstead, with the lion-form the text doesn't mention but the image shows. The reclining man *is* on a bedstead-altar.
- **The priest of Elkenah "endeavored to take away my life" (1:7)** — the standing figure with the raised blade, leaning over the bound man. The blade is the chapter's knife.
- **"I lifted up my voice unto the Lord… and the angel of his presence stood by me, and immediately unloosed my bands" (1:15)** — the reclining figure's *raised arm* is the lifted voice; the *bird above* is the angel/deliverance. The image captures the exact moment the text turns: the victim's cry and the heavenly answer, together in one frame.
- **The gods "Elkenah… Libnah… Mahmackrah… Korash, and also a god like unto that of Pharaoh" (1:6, 13)** — the four canopic jars under the couch, four different heads for four named gods.
- **"The god of Pharaoh" / "the god of Shagreel was the sun" (1:9)** — the crocodile in the lower band (Sobek, the Egyptian crocodile-god, here the god of Pharaoh).
- **★ The Akedah inversion I flagged in the chapter note is *visible*** — a man bound on an altar with a knife held over him. Genesis 22 will put Isaac in that position; Facsimile 1 shows Abraham *already there*, as the victim, before he is ever asked to be the father with the knife.

**Egyptological note:** a lion-couch scene is a standard Egyptian embalming/resurrection vignette — typically Anubis (the jackal-headed embalmer) tending the mummy of Osiris on a bier, with the ba-soul or Isis-as-hawk above and the four sons of Horus (the canopic jars) below. So the conventional reading is a funerary scene, not a human sacrifice; Joseph's Explanation reads it as the altar of Abraham 1. That gap is the translation-historicity seam (Abr 1 flag). What's *not* in dispute by eye: a figure on a lion-bedstead, a standing figure over him, four animal-headed jars, a bird, a crocodile — every element the chapter's Explanation engages is physically there.

## Facsimile 2 — the hypocephalus (Abraham 3)

**What I see (first-hand):** A **circular disk**, ringed at the outer edge by a continuous band of hieroglyphic characters running all the way around. Inside, the disk is divided into registers. At the **center** sits a figure, flanked by two **upright standing figures** (numbered 22 and 23) who appear to hold up sun-disks. Across the **top register**, a standing **crowned figure** (center), a **winged hawk** with spread wings (left), and a **seated figure in a boat with a standing attendant** (right). In the **lower register**, a **reclining cow** at center, and to the left **four standing mummiform figures** in a row; to the right, more seated and standing figures. Numbered panels of dense hieroglyphs fill the remaining spaces.

**The Explanation (verbatim, verified this session):**
- **Fig. 1:** "Kolob, signifying the first creation, nearest to the celestial, or the residence of God. First in government, the last pertaining to the measurement of time… One day in Kolob is equal to a thousand years according to the measurement of this earth, which is called by the Egyptians Jah-oh-eh."
- **Fig. 2:** "Stands next to Kolob, called by the Egyptians Oliblish, which is the next grand governing creation near to the celestial or the place where God resides; holding the key of power also, pertaining to other planets; as revealed from God to Abraham, as he offered sacrifice upon an altar, which he had built unto the Lord."
- **Fig. 5:** "Is called in Egyptian Enish-go-on-dosh; this is one of the governing planets also, and is said by the Egyptians to be the Sun… the governing power, which governs fifteen other fixed planets or stars, as also Floeese or the Moon, the Earth and the Sun in their annual revolutions."
- **Fig. 6:** "Represents this earth in its four quarters."

**Relation to the text — this *is* Abraham 3, drawn:**
- The chapter's whole astronomy is a **graded governing hierarchy** — "one planet above another, until thou come nigh unto Kolob… set to govern all those planets" (Abr 3:9). Facsimile 2 is that hierarchy as a **diagram**: Kolob (Fig. 1) at the center/nearest, Oliblish (Fig. 2) "next… grand governing creation," Enish-go-on-dosh (Fig. 5) a governing planet that "governs fifteen other fixed planets," and the earth (Fig. 6) "in its four quarters." The disk renders the order of governing bodies the text describes in words.
- **"One revolution was a day unto the Lord… one thousand years according to the time appointed unto that whereon thou standest" (Abr 3:4)** = Fig. 1's "one day in Kolob is equal to a thousand years." The chapter's verse and the figure's Explanation say the same thing — and I called Abr 3 the densest chapter without ever looking at the diagram that depicts its core claim.
- **My chapter-note read — "the astronomy is a parable of the order of souls" (the "as, also" of Abr 3:18)** — gains a layer here: the hypocephalus is the *cosmological* half (Kolob governing planets), and the text turns it into the *spiritual* half (God governing intelligences). The disk is the diagram; Abr 3:18-19 is the theorem drawn from it.
- **★ Loop closed back to Moses 2:** way back at Moses 2:6, the footnote on "firmament" pointed to "Abr., fac. 2, fig. 4 concerning the firmament or expanse." Fig. 4 here is in the top register (associated with the winged-hawk/expanse imagery). The cross-reference I logged at Moses 2 lands on *this* image.

**Egyptological note:** a hypocephalus is a real Egyptian object — a disk of linen or papyrus placed *under the head* of a mummy, meant to surround the deceased with light/heat and divine protection; the figures are conventional (the four-ram-headed creator-god at center, the cow-goddess Ihet, the sons of Horus). Joseph's reading is astronomical/cosmological; the Egyptological function is funerary. Also honest to note: portions of this hypocephalus were **damaged on the original and filled in by the printer** when the woodcut was made (scholars dispute some of the restored figures) — so the published image is partly a reconstruction. The translation-historicity seam, again. By eye, though: it *is* a concentric cosmological-looking disk with a central governing figure and a graded order radiating from it — which is exactly what Abraham 3 describes.

## Facsimile 3 — the court (Abraham 2–3)

**What I see (first-hand):** A **seated, crowned figure** on a throne (left), holding a staff/scepter, with a standing figure behind wearing the **cow-horns-and-sun-disk crown**. At center, a small **offering stand** topped with lotus. Three **standing figures** face the throne — one with an elaborate headdress, one in a long gown, and at the far right a **darker-skinned standing figure**. Across the very **top runs a band of stars**; across the bottom, a line of hieroglyphs.

**The Explanation (verbatim, verified this session):**
- **Fig. 1:** "Abraham sitting upon Pharaoh's throne, by the politeness of the king, with a crown upon his head, representing the Priesthood, as emblematical of the grand Presidency in Heaven; with the scepter of justice and judgment in his hand."
- **Fig. 2:** "King Pharaoh, whose name is given in the characters above his head."
- **Fig. 3:** "Signifies Abraham in Egypt as given also in Figure 10 of Facsimile No. 1."
- **Fig. 5:** "Shulem, one of the king's principal waiters."
- **Fig. 6:** "Olimlah, a slave belonging to the prince."
- (Context line, verified): Abraham is "reasoning upon the principles of astronomy, in the king's court."

**Relation to the text:**
- **"I show these things unto thee before ye go into Egypt, that ye may declare all these words" (Abr 3:15)** — Facsimile 3 *is* the declaring: Abraham in Pharaoh's court reasoning the astronomy he was shown in Abraham 3. The **band of stars across the top** is the astronomy theme made visible — Abraham teaching the heavens, with the heavens drawn overhead.
- It closes the Egypt arc of **Abraham 2** (the famine drives him to Egypt, 2:21-25) and **Abraham 1:31** (the records of "the planets, and of the stars… for the benefit of my posterity"). The scene shows the records being *taught*, at the highest table in Egypt.
- **★ A quiet reversal worth Michael's eye:** Fig. 1 reads Abraham "upon Pharaoh's throne… with a crown… representing the Priesthood." Set that against **Abraham 1:27** — Pharaoh's *own lineage* "could not have the right of Priesthood." So the facsimile puts the man who *holds* the priesthood on the throne of the dynasty that *couldn't* hold it, teaching them. The image stages the same point Abr 2:10 made doctrinally (the covenant/priesthood is by ordination and reception, not by the throne's blood) — Abraham, the rightful priesthood-holder, instructing Pharaoh's court from Pharaoh's seat.

**Egyptological note:** a standard Egyptian presentation/judgment scene — the deceased (or a god) enthroned, a goddess behind (the cow-horns-and-disk crown of Fig. 2 is Isis or Hathor, not "King Pharaoh"), attendants approaching, an offering stand between. Joseph reads the enthroned figure as Abraham and the standing crowned figure as Pharaoh; the conventional reading makes them Osiris and Isis. The seam. By eye: an enthroned crowned figure, a court of attendants, an offering stand, and a sky of stars — a throne-room-under-the-heavens, which fits "Abraham reasoning astronomy in the king's court."

## What looking actually changed

Three things I could only get by downloading and looking:
1. **Facsimile 1 shows the Akedah inversion physically** — a man bound on an altar with a blade over him and a deliverer above. I argued that reading from the chapter text; the image *is* it.
2. **Facsimile 2 is Abraham 3 as a diagram** — Kolob at center, the governing order radiating out, "one day in Kolob = a thousand years" written into Fig. 1. The chapter I called the densest has a picture, and I'd been writing about it blind.
3. **Facsimile 3 stages the Abr 1:27 ↔ Abr 2:10 tension** — the rightful priesthood-holder enthroned in Pharaoh's seat, teaching the court that couldn't hold the priesthood by blood. The covenant-by-reception point, drawn.

And the honest part: the Egyptological readings diverge from Joseph's Explanations at every facsimile, and the published Fac 2 is partly a reconstruction. That seam stays exactly where it was — flagged, not resolved, the translation-historicity question that's Michael's and the scholarship's to weigh, not the walk's. What the walk can now say with its own eyes: the images depict what Abraham's text describes, at the level the text engages them.
