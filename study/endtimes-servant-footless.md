# The Endtimes Servant — Without Footnotes

*Study date: March 2, 2026*
*Companion study to: [endtimes-servant.md](endtimes-servant.md)*
*Premise: What do the scriptures say about the "endtimes servant" when we deliberately set aside the LDS footnotes and chapter headings (added by Bruce R. McConkie's committee in the 1970s)? Do we reach the same conclusions? Includes external perspectives (Avraham Gileadi, Isaiah Institute).*
*JST references retained — those are textual, not editorial.*
*Study mode: study-exp1 (phased writing with externalized memory)*

---

## The Experiment

The original [endtimes-servant study](endtimes-servant.md) leaned heavily on the LDS scripture apparatus — footnotes, chapter headings, Topical Guide links, and cross-references. Those tools pointed us toward Christ and Joseph Smith as the primary servant referents.

But that apparatus was added in 1979 by a committee led by Elder Bruce R. McConkie. While I personally trust the footnotes, not everyone does. Some argue the committee brought interpretive assumptions — particularly McConkie's own theological framework — into what are supposed to be neutral study aids. Fair or not, the challenge is legitimate: *what happens when you take them away?*

**The rules:**
1. No footnotes (the superscript letters and their targets)
2. No chapter headings or chapter summaries
3. No Topical Guide references
4. JST readings ARE included — they're part of the text tradition, not editorial additions
5. D&C 113 is fully in play — it's revelation, not editorial commentary
6. External scholarship welcome — but clearly labeled as "external perspective"

**The question:** Does the text alone lead to the same multi-referent, Christ-and-Joseph-Smith-and-collective reading? Or does it open other doors?

## I. Isaiah's Servants — The Text Alone

### The Ideal Servant (Isaiah 42:1-7)

> "Behold my servant, whom I uphold; mine elect, in whom my soul delighteth; I have put my spirit upon him: he shall bring forth judgment to the Gentiles." ([Isaiah 42:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/42.md))

Without the heading ("the Messiah"), what does the text say? This servant is:
- Spirit-endowed (v.1)
- Gentle — won't break a bruised reed, won't quench smoking flax (vv.2-3)
- Patient — "He shall not fail nor be discouraged, till he have set judgment in the earth" (v.4)
- Given as "a covenant of the people, for a light of the Gentiles" (v.6)
- A liberator — "To open the blind eyes, to bring out the prisoners" (v.7)

On the text alone, this reads as either Christ or as a prophetic figure empowered by God's Spirit. The "light of the Gentiles" language recurs across Isaiah (49:6) and D&C (86:11), applied to both Christ and his servants. Without the heading anchoring this as Messianic, a reader encounters a *type* — a pattern that can have multiple fulfillments.

### The Blind Servant (Isaiah 42:19)

> "Who is blind, but my servant? or deaf, as my messenger that I sent? who is blind as he that is perfect, and blind as the Lord's servant?" ([Isaiah 42:19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/42.md))

Same chapter, different servant entirely. This one is blind, deaf, failing. The text itself provides the only heading we need: the servant called and then failing to see. This is Israel — the collective, blind to her own calling. A heading isn't needed to recognize this contrast; the chapter does the work itself.

**What the text alone shows:** Within a single chapter, "my servant" refers to at least two distinct figures — ideal and broken, individual and collective. The multi-referent reading isn't imposed by footnotes. It's demanded by the text.

### The Named-Yet-Distinct Servant (Isaiah 49:1-7)

> "Thou art my servant, O Israel, in whom I will be glorified." ([Isaiah 49:3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/49.md))

> "And now, saith the Lord that formed me from the womb to be his servant, to bring Jacob again to him" ([Isaiah 49:5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/49.md))

> "I will also give thee for a light to the Gentiles, that thou mayest be my salvation unto the end of the earth." ([Isaiah 49:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/49.md))

This is where it gets strange. The servant is *named* "O Israel" yet tasked with gathering Israel. He simultaneously IS Israel and works to restore Israel. He is despised, "abhorred by the nation," a "servant of rulers" (v.7) — yet kings will "arise" and "worship."

Without footnotes, this passage holds at least three readings open simultaneously: Christ (the ultimate Israel who gathers Israel), a prophetic leader (like Moses/Joseph Smith, called from the womb, sent to gather), or a collective personification of covenant Israel. The text does not close any of these doors. It's rich precisely because it's layered.

### The Suffering Servant (Isaiah 52:13 – 53:12)

> "Behold, my servant shall deal prudently, he shall be exalted and extolled, and be very high." ([Isaiah 52:13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/52.md))

> "He was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities; the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed." ([Isaiah 53:5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/53.md))

> "Yet it pleased the Lord to bruise him...when thou shalt make his soul an offering for sin he shall see his seed" ([Isaiah 53:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/53.md))

> "By his knowledge shall my righteous servant justify many; for he shall bear their iniquities." ([Isaiah 53:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/53.md))

Even without headings, this is the most clearly Christological passage. "Offering for sin," "bear their iniquities," "intercession for the transgressors" — this is atonement language. We don't need a heading to tell us that; Abinadi tells us in Mosiah 15 (see below). But the question lingers: does "he shall see his seed" (v.10) have a broader meaning? Abinadi answers that too.

### The Willing Sufferer (Isaiah 50:4-10)

> "The Lord God hath opened mine ear, and I was not rebellious, neither turned away back. I gave my back to the smiters, and my cheeks to them that plucked off the hair" ([Isaiah 50:5-6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/50.md))

> "Who is among you that feareth the Lord, that obeyeth the voice of his servant?" ([Isaiah 50:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/50.md))

Without the heading ("Isaiah speaks as the Messiah"), this is another voice in the servant chorus: someone who suffers willingly, speaks with learned tongue, and is not confounded. The call to "obey the voice of his servant" (v.10) implies an active, prophetic figure who speaks — not merely one who suffers silently. Gileadi reads this as the end-time servant (see Section VIII). The text alone leaves it open.

## II. The Stem, Rod, and Root — D&C 113 (Revelation, Not Footnotes)

This section stays fully in play. D&C 113 is canonized revelation — Joseph Smith answering questions about Isaiah 11 with "thus saith the Lord." It's not editorial. It IS scripture.

> **v.1-2** "Who is the Stem of Jesse?" → "It is Christ."

> **v.3-4** "What is the rod?" → "It is a servant in the hands of Christ, who is partly a descendant of Jesse as well as of Ephraim, or of the house of Joseph, on whom there is laid much power." ([D&C 113:4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/113.md))

> **v.5-6** "What is the root of Jesse?" → "It is a descendant of Jesse, as well as of Joseph, unto whom rightly belongs the priesthood, and the keys of the kingdom, for an ensign, and for the gathering of my people in the last days." ([D&C 113:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/113.md))

Three figures from [Isaiah 11:1, 10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/11.md):
- **Stem** = Christ (clear, definitive)
- **Rod** = a servant of dual lineage (Jesse/David + Ephraim/Joseph), "much power" — NOT Christ
- **Root** = another descendant of dual lineage, holds priesthood and keys, gathers Israel in the last days — NOT Christ

D&C 113 *by itself* establishes the multi-referent framework. Without any footnote, without any heading, the revelation insists: Isaiah 11 is about more than one figure. The rod and the root are separate servants, both from Davidic AND Josephite lineage, operating in the last days.

Further, vv.7-10 interpret [Isaiah 52:1-2](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/52.md) — "Put on thy strength, O Zion" — as a reference to priesthood authority held "by lineage." This connects directly to D&C 86.

## III. The Davidic Servant — Ezekiel and Jeremiah (Without Headings)

### "My Servant David" (Ezekiel 34:23-24)

> "And I will set up one shepherd over them, and he shall feed them, even my servant David; he shall feed them, and he shall be their shepherd. And I the Lord will be their God, and my servant David a prince among them" ([Ezekiel 34:23-24](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ezek/34.md))

The chapter heading states: "The Messiah will be their Shepherd." Remove it.

What does the text say? "My servant David" — as a *prince*, not king. "I the Lord will be their God" — God is the supreme ruler; David serves *under* God. David had been dead for 400 years when Ezekiel wrote. So this is either: (a) the Messiah as David's ultimate heir, (b) a future Davidic descendant who will lead Israel, or (c) the literal David, resurrected. The text alone does not resolve this.

### David as King (Ezekiel 37:24-25)

> "And David my servant shall be king over them; and they all shall have one shepherd... and my servant David shall be their prince for ever." ([Ezekiel 37:24-25](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ezek/37.md))

Same pattern, stronger language: "king" and "prince for ever" and "everlasting covenant" (v.26). Without the heading ("David (the Messiah)"), the "forever" language pushes toward a messianic reading, but the insistence on the name "David" — not "the Lord" or "the Holy One" — keeps the door open for a literal Davidic figure.

### The Righteous Branch (Jeremiah 23:5-6)

> "Behold, the days come, saith the Lord, that I will raise unto David a righteous Branch, and a King shall reign and prosper... and this is his name whereby he shall be called, The Lord Our Righteousness." ([Jeremiah 23:5-6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/jer/23.md))

Without the heading ("The Branch, who is the King (the Messiah)"), the text calls this figure "The Lord Our Righteousness" (Hebrew: *Yahweh Tsidkenu*). That divine title strongly suggests the Messiah. But note: Isaiah 11:1 also speaks of a "Branch" — and D&C 113 says the rod/branch in that passage is NOT Christ. So "Branch" in prophetic vocabulary isn't a single-use designation.

**Summary of this section without headings:** Ezekiel and Jeremiah present a "servant David" who shepherds Israel in the last days, rules with justice, and establishes an everlasting covenant. The messianic reading is the strongest, but without headings pre-answering the question, the text allows a genuine question: is this Christ, or a future Davidic leader who prepares Israel for Christ's coming?

## IV. Jesus Speaks of "My Servant" — 3 Nephi 20-21 (Without Footnotes)

This is the most critical test.

### 3 Nephi 20:43-46 — Quoting Isaiah

> "Behold, my servant shall deal prudently; he shall be exalted and extolled and be very high." ([3 Nephi 20:43](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/20.md))

Jesus himself quotes [Isaiah 52:13-15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/52.md) — and then says, "all these things shall surely come, even as the Father hath commanded me" (v.46). He frames this eschatologically. He doesn't say "this was about me when I suffered." He says it *shall come*. Future tense. In a chapter about the gathering of Israel, the coming forth of the New Jerusalem, and the fulfillment of covenant. The servant here operates in a future context.

### 3 Nephi 21:9-11 — The Servant Named

> "For in that day, for my sake shall the Father work a work, which shall be a great and a marvelous work among them" ([3 Nephi 21:9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21.md))

> "But behold, the life of my servant shall be in my hands; therefore they shall not hurt him, although he shall be marred because of them. Yet I will heal him, for I will show unto them that my wisdom is greater than the cunning of the devil." ([3 Nephi 21:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21.md))

> "Therefore it shall come to pass that whosoever will not believe in my words, who am Jesus Christ, which the Father shall cause *him* to bring forth unto the Gentiles, and shall give unto him power that he shall bring them forth unto the Gentiles, (it shall be done even as Moses said) they shall be cut off from among my people who are of the covenant." ([3 Nephi 21:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21.md))

**The footnotes we're setting aside:**
- 10a → [D&C 135:1-3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/135.md) (Joseph Smith's martyrdom)
- 11a → [2 Nephi 3:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3.md) (choice seer)

**What the text alone tells us:**
1. "My servant" is distinct from Jesus — Jesus speaks of him in third person
2. His life is divinely protected ("in my hands")
3. He WILL be "marred" — but healed
4. He brings forth Christ's words to the Gentiles
5. "Even as Moses said" — this is a cross-reference *within* the text itself, pointing to Moses's prophecy of a prophet like him ([Deuteronomy 18:15-19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/deut/18.md)) and to Lehi's transmission of that tradition in [2 Nephi 3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3.md)

Without the footnotes, an alert reader of the Book of Mormon would still link "even as Moses said" back to 2 Nephi 3, where a choice seer is described — named Joseph, son of Joseph, compared to Moses. The connection exists in the text. The footnotes are hyperlinks to something the text itself encodes.

**But here's what opens up without the footnotes:** If you don't immediately map 3 Nephi 21:10 onto Joseph Smith's martyrdom, you're left with a *pattern* rather than a *portrait*. The servant is marred and healed. Joseph was martyred — but was he "healed"? If "healed" means vindicated by history and the continuation of the Church, perhaps. But the text could also describe a future servant who survives the marring. The footnotes close this door. The text leaves it ajar.

## V. The Choice Seer — 2 Nephi 3 (Text Alone)

This is the strongest text-only case for Joseph Smith as a named servant:

> "And his name shall be called after me; and it shall be after the name of his father. And he shall be like unto me" ([2 Nephi 3:15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3.md))

> "A seer shall the Lord my God raise up, who shall be a choice seer unto the fruit of my loins." ([2 Nephi 3:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3.md))

> "And he shall be great like unto Moses" ([2 Nephi 3:9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3.md))

> "the fruit of thy loins shall write; and the fruit of the loins of Judah shall write...and that which shall be written...shall grow together" ([2 Nephi 3:12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3.md))

No footnotes needed. The text gives us:
- **Name:** Joseph, son of Joseph (v.15) — the name match is unambiguous
- **Lineage:** of Joseph of Egypt's seed (fruit of his loins)
- **Mosaic comparison:** "great like unto Moses" (v.9) — same comparison as 3 Nephi 21:11
- **Scripture-bringer:** brings forth God's word, writing of Joseph + writing of Judah growing together (v.12) — Bible + Book of Mormon
- **Protected:** "they that seek to destroy him shall be confounded" (v.14)

The BYU Scripture Citation Index confirms what we'd expect: 2 Nephi 3:15 has been cited [18 times in General Conference and Journal of Discourses, always referring to Joseph Smith](https://scriptures.byu.edu/) — by everyone from Parley P. Pratt (JD 1:262) to M. Russell Ballard (1991-O:6).

**Bottom line:** The footnotes didn't create the Joseph Smith identification. The text shouts it. The name, the lineage, the Moses comparison, the scripture-bringing — it's all there. This is one place where the apparatus merely points to what any careful reader would find.

Note: v.24 introduces "one mighty among them, who shall do much good, both in word and in deed, being an instrument in the hands of God." This *might* be the same seer — or it might be a separate figure. The text doesn't clearly equate them.

## VI. One Mighty and Strong — D&C 85 and 103

### D&C 85:7 — The Unnamed Figure

> "I, the Lord God, will send one mighty and strong, holding the scepter of power in his hand, clothed with light for a covering, whose mouth shall utter words, eternal words; while his bowels shall be a fountain of truth, to set in order the house of God" ([D&C 85:7](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/85.md))

This is NOT identified as Christ in the text. The function is administrative — setting in order God's house, arranging inheritances. "Clothed with light," "scepter of power," "eternal words" — the language is magnificent but the identity is unresolved. In 1905, the First Presidency (Joseph F. Smith) stated this likely referred to the presiding bishop's role in administering Zion's temporal affairs. But the text itself leaves the identity open.

### D&C 103:16,21 — Joseph Smith Explicitly Named

> "Therefore, I will raise up unto my people a man, who shall lead them like as Moses led the children of Israel." ([D&C 103:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/103.md))

> "Verily, verily I say unto you, that my servant Joseph Smith, Jun., is the man to whom I likened the servant to whom the Lord of the vineyard spake in the parable which I have given unto you." ([D&C 103:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/103.md))

This is the most explicit text in the entire canon. No footnotes needed. No headings required. The Lord says plainly: "my servant Joseph Smith, Jun." is the Moses-like leader. The Moses comparison links to [2 Nephi 3:9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/3.md) and [3 Nephi 21:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/21.md). This single revelation demolishes any claim that the Joseph Smith identification only comes from editorial apparatus.

## VII. The Collective Servant — D&C 86

> "Therefore, thus saith the Lord unto you, with whom the priesthood hath continued through the lineage of your fathers—For ye are lawful heirs, according to the flesh, and have been hid from the world with Christ in God—" ([D&C 86:8-9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/86.md))

> "Therefore, blessed are ye if ye continue in my goodness, a light unto the Gentiles, and through this priesthood, a savior unto my people Israel." ([D&C 86:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/86.md))

Here Isaiah's servant language — "a light unto the Gentiles" ([Isaiah 49:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/49.md)) — is applied to a *collective*: the priesthood holders who are "lawful heirs" by lineage. They've been "hid from the world" and their priesthood must continue "until the restoration of all things" (v.10).

D&C 113:8 connects: Zion putting on "her strength" means putting on "the authority of the priesthood, which she, Zion, has a right to by lineage."

The collective-servant reading isn't footnote-generated. It's in the Doctrine and Covenants itself, using Isaiah's own words, telling a group of people: *you are the light to the Gentiles. You are the saviors of Israel.* The servant is plural.

## VIII. External Perspectives — Avraham Gileadi and the Isaiah Institute

*The following represents external scholarship, not scripture. Presented for engagement, not endorsement. Clearly labeled as an outside perspective.*

Avraham Gileadi (Ph.D., Hebrew Bible and Ancient Near Eastern Studies, BYU; studied under Hugh Nibley) offers a reading of Isaiah that partially overlaps with and partially diverges from standard Latter-day Saint interpretation. His framework, published through the [Isaiah Institute](https://isaiahinstitute.com), proposes:

**Two primary servants in Isaiah:**
1. A **collective servant** — Jacob/Israel, blind and deaf ([Isaiah 42:18-20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/42.md)), called to repentance and covenant faithfulness.
2. An **individual end-time servant** — a Davidic figure who gathers all twelve tribes, serves as "a light to the Gentiles," and prepares for Jehovah's coming. This figure is "distinct from that of Jesus, which was spiritual and distinct from that of any latter-day prophet."

**Key Gileadi claims (external perspective):**
- The rod, root, and branch of Isaiah 11 are all separate persons — this matches D&C 113
- The "root of Jesse" is NOT Joseph Smith — Gileadi explicitly pushes back: "Someone who has read these things but hasn't searched them might say, 'That sounds like the prophet Joseph Smith!' and take the high moral ground... though they themselves haven't searched the topic but simply jumped to conclusions about it."
- The end-time servant is still *future* — "hidden from the world for the purpose of testing God's people"
- Isaiah 52:14 ("marred beyond human likeness") describes this end-time servant, not only Christ
- Parts of Isaiah 53 (bearing sins, intercession) may apply to this servant in a non-atoning, representative capacity
- The servant is identified by keyword links across Isaiah: "appoint" ([41:27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/41.md); [42:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/42.md); [49:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/49.md); [55:4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/55.md)), "ensign," "light," "hand," "voice"
- Joshua and Gideon serve as historical types of this servant

**Where Gileadi aligns with our text-only reading:**
- Multi-referent — absolutely. This is consistent with everything we've found.
- Rod/root/stem as distinct figures — D&C 113 confirms this reading.
- Ezekiel's "my servant David" as potentially a literal Davidic figure, not merely a title for Christ.

**Where Gileadi diverges:**
- He argues the servant *hasn't come yet* — a future individual still to be revealed. No LDS prophet has taught this.
- He applies portions of Isaiah 53 (suffering servant) to this non-divine end-time figure. This directly contradicts Abinadi's reading in Mosiah 15, where "God himself shall come down" is the explicit interpretation of Isaiah 53.
- He identifies the servant as "named David" based on Ezekiel and Jeremiah — but D&C 113 says the rod/root figures are descendants of Jesse AND Joseph (Ephraim), not "David" as a personal name.

**An honest assessment:** Gileadi is a serious Isaiah scholar with legitimate credentials. His method — typological reading, keyword analysis, literary structure — produces insights that footnotes obscure. But his insistence that the servant is future and that Isaiah 53 partially describes a non-Christ figure strains against the canonical evidence, especially Mosiah 15. His work is valuable as a lens, not as a conclusion.

## IX. Comparison — Footnoted vs. Footless

| Element | WITH Footnotes (Original Study) | WITHOUT Footnotes (This Study) |
|---|---|---|
| **Isaiah 42:1 = Christ** | Yes — heading + Matt 12:17 footnote | Open — could be Christ or broad servant type; heading removed |
| **Isaiah 53 = Christ** | Yes — heading + Mosiah 14 footnote | Yes — Abinadi's reading (Mosiah 15) reaches same conclusion from within the text |
| **3 Nephi 21:10 = Joseph Smith** | Yes — footnote 10a → D&C 135 (martyrdom) | Probably — "even as Moses said" (v.11) links to 2 Ne 3:9, but the path is longer without the hyperlink |
| **Isaiah 11 = multi-referent** | Yes — footnote → D&C 113 | Yes — D&C 113 IS in play as revelation; same conclusion |
| **Ezekiel 34 = Christ** | Yes — heading says "The Messiah" | Open — without heading, "my servant David" is genuinely ambiguous |
| **D&C 86 = collective servant** | Yes — footnote → Isa 49:6 | Yes — the D&C text itself uses Isaiah's language ("light unto the Gentiles") |
| **Joseph Smith identified** | Yes — 3 Ne 21 footnotes + TG entry | Yes — D&C 103:21 + 2 Nephi 3:15 are text, not footnotes |
| **Future Davidic servant?** | Not really — footnotes close this door | Open — Gileadi's reading becomes more plausible without heading-imposed closures |

**The verdict:** The footnotes are hyperlinks, not arguments. They point to connections that already exist in the text. Every major conclusion of the original study — multi-referent servants, Christ as the suffering servant, Joseph Smith as a named servant, the collective priesthood as a servant body — survives without the editorial apparatus.

What changes without footnotes is **speed of connection** and **closure of ambiguity.** The footnotes make cross-referencing instant. Without them, a reader must independently discover that "even as Moses said" in 3 Nephi 21 echoes 2 Nephi 3:9, or that "a light unto the Gentiles" in D&C 86 uses Isaiah 49's language. These connections exist in the text — the footnotes just make them visible to a casual reader.

And what *opens up* without footnotes is the question of the Davidic servant. Ezekiel's "my servant David" and the "root of Jesse" in D&C 113:6 — someone who holds priesthood, keys, and gathers in the last days — could describe a figure yet to come. The footnotes route this toward Joseph Smith or the Church collectively. Without them, the text allows an additional reading.

## X. Critical Analysis

### What holds firm
The multi-referent reading is the most textually defensible position, with or without footnotes:
- **Christ** as the suffering servant (Isaiah 53 + Mosiah 15)
- **Joseph Smith** as the choice seer and Moses figure (2 Nephi 3 + D&C 103)
- **The priesthood collectively** as "a light unto the Gentiles" (D&C 86)
- **Distinct rod/root figures** who carry both Davidic and Josephite lineage (D&C 113)

### What's stronger without footnotes
The **literary richness** of Isaiah's servant. Without headings pre-answering the question, each servant passage demands that the reader sit with the ambiguity. Who is the blind servant? Who is the named-yet-distinct servant? Who gathers Israel while being called Israel? These are profound questions that deserve contemplation, not instant resolution via a footnote.

### What's weaker without footnotes
The **speed of connection.** A reader who doesn't independently cross-reference 3 Nephi 21:11 ↔ 2 Nephi 3:9 ↔ D&C 103:21 might miss the Joseph Smith identification in 3 Nephi 21. The chain is there — but it's a chain, not a label.

### What's genuinely new
Gileadi's **future end-time Davidic servant** — an individual who fulfills the "root of Jesse" role, gathers the twelve tribes, and prepares for Jehovah's coming. This reading takes D&C 113:6 more literally than standard LDS interpretation: someone who holds priesthood, keys, serves as an ensign, and gathers in the last days. Is this Joseph Smith? A future prophet? The living prophet at the time these events unfold? D&C 113 is less definitive than footnotes make it appear.

### What I don't buy
Gileadi's application of Isaiah 53 to a non-Christ figure. Abinadi is too clear: "God himself shall come down among the children of men, and shall redeem his people" ([Mosiah 15:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15.md)). The suffering servant of Isaiah 53 is Christ. Period. Dual fulfillment is a legitimate hermeneutic, but the primary referent here is unmistakable — and it doesn't require footnotes to see it.

## XI. Becoming

So what changes for me?

The footnotes didn't lie to me. They pointed to real connections. But studying without them taught me something I wouldn't have learned otherwise: **the scriptures are more interconnected than any apparatus can capture, and more ambiguous than any heading wants to admit.**

When Ezekiel says "my servant David," the heading says "the Messiah" and I move on. Without the heading, I have to sit with the question. Who is this? What does it mean that David — dead for centuries — is named as a future shepherd? Is this a title? A type? A literal person? The question is more useful than the answer the heading provides.

When 3 Nephi 21:10 says the servant will be "marred...yet I will heal him," the footnote says *Joseph Smith's martyrdom* and the case feels closed. Without it, I sit with "yet I will heal him" and wonder: healed how? In what sense? The answer may still be Joseph Smith — but the question enriches the reading even if the answer doesn't change.

I think the McConkie committee did good work. The footnotes are faithful study aids. But they're *aids*, not scripture. The text doesn't need them. It speaks for itself — sometimes more powerfully when it's allowed to.

And Gileadi's provocative reading — a future servant who hasn't yet come — is worth holding lightly. Not because it's right (I don't think it is, at least not in the way Gileadi frames it). But because it reminds me: the prophets saw things we haven't seen yet. The servant passages may have fulfillments we can't yet comprehend. The last days aren't finished. The gathering isn't complete. The rod and root of Jesse may yet surprise us.

For now, here's what I take from this study:
1. **Read the text first, apparatus second.** The footnotes accelerate connections but can also shortcut contemplation.
2. **Multi-referent reading is textually demanded**, not just editorially suggested.
3. **Joseph Smith's role as a named servant is IN the text** — 2 Nephi 3, D&C 103. Footnotes amplify; they don't create.
4. **The collective servant includes me.** D&C 86:11 — "a light unto the Gentiles, and through this priesthood, a savior unto my people Israel." That's not metaphor. That's calling.
5. **Hold ambiguity as gift, not problem.** When the text says "my servant David" and I want to know exactly who — the discomfort of not-knowing is an invitation to keep seeking.

> "Whatever principle of intelligence we attain unto in this life, it will rise with us in the resurrection." ([D&C 130:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/130.md))

The intelligence we gain isn't just the answers. It's the quality of the questions. And sometimes the best questions come when you take off the footnotes and let the text speak for itself.
