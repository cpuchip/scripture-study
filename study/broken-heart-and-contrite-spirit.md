# Broken Heart and Contrite Spirit

> *"Ye shall offer for a sacrifice unto me a broken heart and a contrite spirit."*
> — [3 Nephi 9:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/9.md)

**Catalyst:** Reading Alma 34 — Amulek's progression from mercy and justice, through praying directives, to charity in verses 27–30. The same arc appears in Enos's prayer. The question: what is it about a broken heart and a contrite spirit that makes this the required sacrifice, and how does this posture bring mercy?

**Binding question:** What do "broken heart" and "contrite spirit" actually mean — and how does this specific posture bring about mercy from the Savior?

---

## I. The Doublet — Where It Appears and What It Replaces

The phrase shows up across the standard works like a recurring chord. David wrote it first: "The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit: a broken and a contrite heart, O God, thou wilt not despise" ([Psalm 51:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/51.md)). Christ spoke it from the darkness over ruined cities: "Ye shall offer for a sacrifice unto me a broken heart and a contrite spirit" ([3 Nephi 9:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/9.md)). Joseph received it in Zion: "Thou shalt offer a sacrifice unto the Lord thy God in righteousness, even that of a broken heart and a contrite spirit" ([D&C 59:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/59.md)). Moroni sealed it as the gate to the Church: "They came forth with a broken heart and a contrite spirit, and witnessed unto the church that they truly repented" ([Moroni 6:2](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/moro/6.md)).

In every case, it replaces something. Psalm 51:16 precedes it: "thou desirest not sacrifice; else would I give it: thou delightest not in burnt offering." [3 Nephi 9:19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/9.md): "your sacrifices and your burnt offerings shall be done away." The new sacrifice is not easier. It is deeper. A sheep costs property. A broken heart costs the self.

And the condition is absolute. Lehi taught Jacob: "He offereth himself a sacrifice for sin, to answer the ends of the law, unto all those who have a broken heart and a contrite spirit; and unto none else can the ends of the law be answered" ([2 Nephi 2:7](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)). This is not God being stingy. It is God recognizing that only a broken container can hold what He pours.

---

## II. "Broken" — What the Word Actually Means

We say "broken heart" like we say "heartbroken" — a feeling, a sadness, something that time heals. But the word carries more weight than that.

Webster 1828 gives five senses of "broken" before he reaches the one that matters: "Subdued; humbled; contrite." Then a seventh: "Crushed and ruined as by something that destroys hope; blighted." The Hebrew behind the English is sharper still. In Psalm 51:17, "broken" translates **shabar** — to burst, break in pieces, crush, destroy, tear (Strong's 7665). This is the word used for a reed snapped underfoot, for a pot shattered against stone. It is not a gentle sadness. It is structural collapse.

But notice what Christ does with the word. In [3 Nephi 9:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/9.md), He pairs the broken heart not with despair but with promise: "whoso cometh unto me with a broken heart and a contrite spirit, him will I baptize with fire and with the Holy Ghost." The breaking is not the end. It is the preparation. A whole pot holds what it was made to hold and nothing more. A shattered pot, remade, can hold living water.

[Psalm 34:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/34.md) draws the connection even tighter: "The Lord is nigh unto them that are of a broken heart; and saveth such as be of a contrite spirit." Two different verbs. The Lord draws near to the brokenhearted. He saves the contrite. The broken heart attracts proximity. The contrite spirit receives salvation.

---

## III. "Contrite" — A Word We No Longer Use

We do not say "contrite" in 2026 English. The closest we come is "remorseful," which is too small. Remorse is feeling bad about what you did. Contrition is something else.

Webster 1828: "Broken down with grief and penitence; deeply sorrowful for sin because it is displeasing to God; humbly and thoroughly penitent." The grief is not generic. It is sorrow because the sin is displeasing to God. The orientation is Godward.

The Hebrew root is **dakah** — to collapse physically or mentally, to be pressed down until shattered or pulverized (Strong's 1794). This is the same root used in [Psalm 51:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/51.md): "Let the bones which thou hast broken rejoice." The crushing is real. It is felt in the body. And yet the psalmist does not ask to be spared the crushing. He asks that the crushed bones rejoice.

Elder Neil L. Andersen, in October 2025, offered a distinction that helps: "Having a contrite spirit is humbly putting yourself in the hands of God; having a broken heart brings what the Apostle Paul described as 'godly sorrow,' a deep longing of the soul to return to Him whatever the cost." The contrite spirit is the will submitted. The broken heart is the soul grieving. Together they cover the whole person.

But the distinction should not be pressed too hard. Scripture itself swaps the terms. David says "a broken spirit" and "a broken and contrite heart" ([Psalm 51:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/51.md)). Christ says "broken heart and a contrite spirit" ([3 Nephi 9:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/9.md)). The words are braided. To be broken is to be contrite. To be contrite is to be broken. The distinction is a flashlight, not a fence.

## IV. The Alma 34 Progression — Justice, Mercy, Prayer, Charity

Amulek's sermon in [Alma 34](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/34.md) is one of the most carefully constructed progressions in the Book of Mormon. He begins where Alma left off — with justice and mercy. "Mercy can satisfy the demands of justice, and encircle them in the arms of safety" (v16). Then he introduces a phrase that appears nowhere else in scripture: "faith unto repentance" (v15, 16). Not faith and repentance. Faith *unto* repentance — directional, aimed, moving toward.

From there he moves to prayer: "cry unto him for mercy... humble yourselves, and continue in prayer... cry unto him in your houses... pour out your souls in your closets" (v18-26). Prayer is not a single event. It is a posture maintained across every domain of life — fields, flocks, houses, closets.

Then the pivot. Verse 27: "when you do not cry unto the Lord, let your hearts be full, drawn out in prayer unto him continually for your welfare, and also for the welfare of those who are around you." The prayer turns outward. And verse 28 makes the consequence plain: "if ye turn away the needy, and the naked, and visit not the sick and afflicted... behold, your prayer is vain." Charity is not an add-on. It is the proof that the prayer was real.

Verse 29: "if ye do not remember to be charitable, ye are as dross." And verse 30: "bring fruit unto repentance." Another unique phrase. "Faith unto repentance" at the beginning; "fruit unto repentance" at the end. The arc moves from internal assent through vocal petition to embodied love. Faith → cry → charity → fruit. The broken heart and contrite spirit are the posture that makes this arc possible. Without them, the prayer is words. With them, the prayer becomes transformation.

## V. Enos — The Same Arc in a Single Prayer

Enos never uses the phrase "broken heart and contrite spirit." But his prayer traces the same arc Amulek describes. "The words which I had often heard my father speak concerning eternal life, and the joy of the saints, sunk deep into my heart" ([Enos 1:3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/enos/1.md)) — faith, sinking in. "My soul hungered; and I kneeled down before my Maker, and I cried unto him in mighty prayer and supplication for mine own soul" (v4) — the cry for mercy. "Enos, thy sins are forgiven thee" (v5) — mercy received. "I began to feel a desire for the welfare of my brethren, the Nephites; wherefore, I did pour out my whole soul unto God for them" (v9) — charity.

The shift from "my own soul" to "the welfare of my brethren" is the same shift Amulek describes from prayer for self to hearts drawn out for others. Enos does not name it charity, but the geometry is identical. The brokenness that receives mercy becomes the channel through which mercy flows to others.

## VI. Humble, Meek, Submissive — The Vocabulary of Posture

Broken heart and contrite spirit do not stand alone. They belong to a family of words that describe the same posture from different angles. Isaiah pairs "contrite and humble spirit" ([Isaiah 57:15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/57.md)). The Book of Mormon adds "poor in spirit who come unto me" ([3 Nephi 12:3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/12.md)) — Matthew's beatitude with the crucial addition. Christ's blessing is not for poverty of spirit in isolation. It is for poverty of spirit that moves toward Him.

Webster 1828 on humble: "Thinking lowly of one's self... thinking one's self ill-deserving or unworthy, when judged by the demands of God." This is the soil. Humility is the recognition that God's standard reveals our unworthiness. From that soil grows brokenness. From brokenness grows contrition. From contrition grows the capacity to receive.

[D&C 20:37](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/20.md) lays out the full sequence: "All those who humble themselves before God, and desire to be baptized, and come forth with broken hearts and contrite spirits... shall be received by baptism into his church." Humility first. Then the brokenness. Then the reception.

## VII. How This Posture Brings Mercy

The binding question asks how this posture brings mercy. The answer is not that it earns mercy. It never earns. It receives.

Consider the physics of the thing. A whole heart is full — of self, of pride, of its own assessments. A broken heart has space. The crushing that produces contrition is not punishment added to sin. It is the removal of what blocks the gift. Elder Walker, in April 2026, quoted [D&C 97:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/97.md): "All among them who know their hearts are honest, and are broken, and their spirits contrite... they are accepted of me." The Lord does not ask for perfection. He asks for honest and broken hearts, humble spirits, and willingness.

Therefore the mechanism is not transactional. It is architectural. The Atonement is infinite, ever-extended, always offered. "Mine arm of mercy is extended towards you" ([3 Nephi 9:14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/9.md)). But the arm cannot lift what will not be lifted. The broken heart is the posture of being liftable. The contrite spirit is the posture of being shaped. Together they are the clay, not the potter — but clay that has finally stopped trying to fire itself.

[Helaman 8:15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/hel/8.md) makes the condition explicit: "as many as should look upon the Son of God with faith, having a contrite spirit, might live, even unto that life which is eternal." The looking is with faith. The living is through contrition. The contrite spirit is not a feeling to wait for. It is the condition of the gaze.

And the result? "Him will I baptize with fire and with the Holy Ghost" ([3 Nephi 9:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/3-ne/9.md)). Fire purifies. The Holy Ghost dwells. Isaiah saw this: "I dwell in the high and holy place, with him also that is of a contrite and humble spirit, to revive the spirit of the humble, and to revive the heart of the contrite ones" ([Isaiah 57:15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/57.md)). God dwells in the high place — and with the broken. The same verb. The broken heart does not just receive mercy. It becomes a habitation for mercy itself.

---

## VIII. Becoming

The offering of a broken heart and a contrite spirit is not a one-time event at baptism. It is the recurring sacrifice of a life. The sacrament is the repeated invitation to come with this posture, to renew the remission that the posture makes possible.

The question is not whether my heart has been broken. Life breaks hearts — loss, failure, betrayal, sin, the sheer weight of being mortal. The question is whether I offer the breakage to God, or whether I try to hide it, harden it, or heal it myself. The broken heart that stays closed becomes bitter. The broken heart offered becomes receptive.

And the contrite spirit — that is the daily choice to put myself in God's hands rather than manage my own spiritual portfolio. It is the willingness to be wrong, to be shaped, to be corrected. It is the acknowledgment that when judged by the demands of God, I am ill-deserving — and that this is not a cause for despair but for dependence.

The mercy does not come because I broke well. The mercy comes because the breaking made room for what was always being offered. The channel is open. The posture determines whether the water enters. This is the architecture of grace.
