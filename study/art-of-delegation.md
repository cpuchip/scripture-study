# The Art of Delegation — Burden-Bearing as the Pattern of the Atonement

*Study date: March 31, 2026*
*Born from: the stewardship pattern study's three sabbath seeds (March 22), the experience of asking someone for help and feeling the relief of a willingly shared burden*
*Study mode: study (phased writing with externalized memory)*
*Predecessor: [art-of-presidency.md](art-of-presidency.md), [stewardship-pattern.md](stewardship-pattern.md)*

---

## The Binding Question

When I ask someone to carry a burden for me, and they willingly agree, something happens — relief, trust, gratitude. Christ did this infinitely: He took upon Himself our sins and sorrows. Is delegation in the Church a shadow of the Atonement's burden-bearing pattern, and if so, what does that teach about how to delegate, how to receive delegation, and why the whole system depends on willingness?

---

## I. The Felt Experience — What Happens When Burden Transfers

I asked someone to do something for me. They agreed. And I felt it — relief. Not just organizational relief, not "one less item on the list," but the felt sense that a burden on my mind was taken willfully. The weight didn't disappear. It redistributed. Someone chose to carry part of it, and the choosing changed both of us.

Webster 1828 draws a distinction between *burden* and *load* that the scripture writers seem to already know:

> "A burden is a weight to be borne; a load is something laid upon us to be carried. Our burdens may be of such a nature that we feel bound to bear them cheerfully or without complaint. They may arise from the nature of our situation; they may be allotments of Providence. ... What is upon us, as a load, we commonly carry with greater reluctance or sense of oppression."

A burden is borne willingly. A load is carried reluctantly. A calling is a burden in the best sense of the word: it arises from our covenant situation, from the allotments of Providence. When someone accepts a delegated responsibility willingly, they are bearing a burden, not hauling a load. And that willing bearing is exactly what the baptismal covenant describes:

> "As ye are desirous to come into the fold of God, and to be called his people, and are *willing* to bear one another's burdens, that they may be light; yea, and are *willing* to mourn with those that mourn; yea, and comfort those that stand in need of comfort" ([Mosiah 18:8–9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18.md))

"Willing" appears twice. Willingness is the hinge. And the people's response, when Alma finishes describing this covenant of burden-bearing? "They clapped their hands for joy, and exclaimed: This is the desire of our hearts" ([Mosiah 18:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18.md)). Joy. At the prospect of carrying other people's weight. That's counterintuitive unless burden-bearing is itself a gift — a chance to fulfill something we already desire.

Therefore this study isn't about delegation as administrative technique. The [stewardship pattern](stewardship-pattern.md) covered the mechanics; the [art of presidency](art-of-presidency.md) covered the terms. This is about what actually happens in the moment someone says "yes, I'll carry that with you," and why that moment echoes something far older and deeper.

---

## II. The Atonement as Archetype — The Vertical Pattern

The vocabulary of delegation is the vocabulary of the Atonement. This is not a metaphor I'm imposing on the text. The text imposes it on us.

Isaiah 53 reads like a delegation document — every verb is burden-transfer:

> "Surely he hath *borne* our griefs, and *carried* our sorrows" (v. 4)

> "He was *wounded for* our transgressions, he was *bruised for* our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was *upon him*" (v. 5)

> "The Lord hath *laid on him* the iniquity of us all" (v. 6)

> "He shall *bear* their iniquities" (v. 11) ([Isaiah 53:4–6, 11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/53.md))

Borne. Carried. Wounded *for*. Bruised *for*. Laid *on* him. Bear. Webster 1828 defines "bear" as both "to support or sustain" and "to sustain, or be answerable for, as blame, expense, responsibility" — then cites Isaiah 53:11 as the example. To bear is not just to carry. It is to become answerable. Christ didn't merely transport our sins from one place to another. He became answerable for them.

But Alma adds something Isaiah doesn't make explicit. The Atonement's burden-bearing has a *purpose* beyond substitution:

> "He will take upon him their infirmities, that his bowels may be filled with mercy, according to the flesh, that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people according to their infirmities." ([Alma 7:12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/7.md))

He takes burdens so He can *help*. Not merely so the burden is gone, but so the Bearer comprehends the burden from the inside. Five categories: pains, afflictions, temptations, sicknesses, death ([Alma 7:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/7.md)). Four repetitions of "take upon" in three verses. The Atonement isn't just substitution. It's comprehension-through-experience, which becomes the foundation for mercy.

This is the vertical pattern: all burdens gathered *to* one person. Christ as the center, drawing all weight toward Himself. The Atonement is centripetal.

## III. The Jethro Principle — The Horizontal Pattern

If the Atonement gathers burdens to one, delegation spreads burdens from one. It's the opposite motion, but it serves the same end: survival and flourishing for both the burden-bearer and those being served.

Moses learned this the hard way. "Moses sat to judge the people: and the people stood by Moses from the morning unto the evening" ([Exodus 18:13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)). Jethro watched and delivered the plainest administrative advice in scripture: "The thing that thou doest is not good. Thou wilt surely wear away, both thou, and this people that is with thee: for this thing is too heavy for thee; thou art not able to perform it thyself alone" ([Exodus 18:17–18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)).

The consequence of not delegating is dual. The leader wears out — *and the people do too*. Refusal to delegate isn't heroic self-sacrifice. It's mutual destruction.

Jethro's prescription: "Moreover thou shalt provide out of all the people able men, such as fear God, men of truth, hating covetousness; and place such over them, to be rulers of thousands, and rulers of hundreds, rulers of fifties, and rulers of tens. ... They shall bear the burden *with* thee" ([Exodus 18:21–22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)). Not *for* thee. *With.* Shared.

Numbers 11 tells the same story in darker tones. Moses breaks. "I am not able to bear all this people alone, because it is too heavy for me. And if thou deal thus with me, kill me, I pray thee" ([Numbers 11:14–15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/11.md)). God's response is not "be stronger." It's "share the burden." And the mechanism reveals something: "I will take of the spirit which is upon thee, and will put it upon them; and they shall bear the burden of the people with thee, that thou bear it not thyself alone" ([Numbers 11:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/11.md)).

God doesn't just assign helpers. He transfers His Spirit to equip them. Webster 1828: a delegate is "any one sent *and empowered* to act for another." Sent AND empowered. Delegation without empowerment is merely assignment. Numbers 11:17 is empowerment — the Spirit moves from Moses to the seventy, and they prophesy ([Numbers 11:25](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/11.md)).

The Jethro principle is practical, not theological. I should name that honestly. Exodus 18 doesn't use Atonement vocabulary. It's an organizational intervention from a father-in-law who saw his son-in-law burning out. But the *pattern* rhymes with the Atonement: one person carrying too much, a wise voice saying "share the weight," and the discovery that shared weight serves everyone better than concentrated weight.

Harold B. Lee captured the bilateral benefit in a single sentence: "Lighten your individual burdens, you leaders, by increasing the activities of others, that all may be benefitted thereby" ("Watch, That Ye May Be Ready," [October 1971](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1971/10/watch-that-ye-may-be-ready.md)). The leader's burden is lightened *and* the members are benefited. Delegation isn't zero-sum. It multiplies capacity while distributing growth.

Therefore the Church operates on two axes simultaneously. The vertical: Christ bears what we can't — sin, death, the infinite weight. The horizontal: we bear what we can share — callings, service, mourning, comfort. Delegation is the horizontal expression of the same principle the Atonement expresses vertically. Both depend on willingness. Both create something in the bearer that wasn't there before.

## IV. The Willing Receiver — A Theology of Accepting Stewardship

Most delegation advice focuses on the delegator: how to assign well, how to let go, how to follow up. But the scriptures give equal weight to the receiver.

Christ's yoke-invitation is the clearest example. "Come unto me, all ye that labour and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you, and learn of me" ([Matthew 11:28–29](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/11.md)). A yoke joins two animals to pull together. Webster 1828: "Two animals yoked together; a couple; a pair that work together." Christ doesn't say "give me your burden and rest." He says "take my yoke upon you" — enter into shared labor with me. The delegation is bidirectional. He bears our sins, then yokes us to His work. The burden becomes light not because it disappears but because two pull.

"Learn of me" — the purpose of the shared yoke is education. Becoming. Not just load relief.

This is what happens to the delegate. Alma's priests at the waters of Mormon received their stewardship and the compensation was not temporal:

> "The priests were not to depend upon the people for their support; but for their labor they were to receive the grace of God, that they might wax strong in the Spirit, having the knowledge of God, that they might teach with power and authority from God." ([Mosiah 18:26](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18.md))

You give labor; you receive grace. The delegation pipeline runs both directions — burden flows down, grace flows up.

The Lord's own statement on receiving delegation is D&C 58:

> "It is not meet that I should command in all things; for he that is compelled in all things, the same is a slothful and not a wise servant; wherefore he receiveth no reward. ... Men should be anxiously engaged in a good cause, and do many things of their own free will, and bring to pass much righteousness; for the power is in them, wherein they are agents unto themselves." ([D&C 58:26–28](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/58.md))

"The power is in them." God affirms that the delegate has capacity. The delegate's obligation is not passive obedience but anxious engagement — initiative, agency, creative action within the stewardship. D&C 84:33 calls this "magnifying their calling." Not just filling the role, but expanding it, making it greater.

And D&C 84 frames the entire system as covenant:

> "He that receiveth my servants receiveth me; and he that receiveth me receiveth my Father; and he that receiveth my Father receiveth my Father's kingdom; therefore all that my Father hath shall be given unto him." ([D&C 84:36–38](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/84.md))

The delegation chain: Father → Christ → servants → those who receive servants. Receiving a delegated servant IS receiving the Lord. Delegation doesn't dilute authority; it extends it. And the whole chain is covenantal, not administrative (v. 39: "this is according to the oath and covenant which belongeth to the priesthood").

Elder Ballard confirmed the delegate's growth: "Assignments should be made, responsibilities should be delegated, and members should be allowed to fulfill their stewardship as best they can. Counsel, advise, persuade, motivate — but don't do the work for them. Allow others to progress and grow, even if it means sometimes getting less-than-perfect results" ("O Be Wise," [October 2006](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2006/10/o-be-wise.md)). "Allow others to progress and grow" — delegation serves the delegate's becoming, not just the delegator's relief. Less-than-perfect results are the cost of trust.

And Elder Edgley observed the same pattern in organic, non-directed burden-bearing: "Members who suffer tragedy firsthand often experience an increased capacity for love, compassion, and understanding. They become the first, last, and often the most effective responders in giving comfort and showing compassion to others" ("Enduring Together," [October 2007](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2007/10/enduring-together.md)). Burden-bearing creates comprehension in the bearer — the same principle Alma describes in Christ at [Alma 7:12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/7.md). What is true of the infinite Bearer is also true, at human scale, of anyone who carries another's weight.

## V. Trust as Prerequisite — Why Delegation Requires Relationship

Delegation without trust is command. Delegation with trust is covenant. The difference determines everything.

The Lord's instruction on how priesthood authority operates is also an instruction on how to delegate:

> "No power or influence can or ought to be maintained by virtue of the priesthood, only by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned; by kindness, and pure knowledge, which shall greatly enlarge the soul without hypocrisy, and without guile" ([D&C 121:41–42](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md))

Every tool listed is relational, not positional. Persuasion. Long-suffering. Gentleness. Love unfeigned. Kindness. Pure knowledge. These are the conditions under which delegation becomes covenantal rather than transactional. A leader who delegates through these means isn't merely assigning tasks. They're extending trust, inviting capability, and staying relationally present.

Jethro's selection criteria describe the same principle from the organizational side: "able men, such as fear God, men of truth, hating covetousness" ([Exodus 18:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)). Trust isn't blind. It requires discernment about character. But the standard is moral fitness (fear God, love truth, hate corruption), not technical skill. God delegates the same way. "We trust you, brothers and sisters, to use inspiration," Ballard said in "O Be Wise." That trust is real, not rhetorical.

Peter captures both sides of the trust equation: leaders are to "feed the flock of God which is among you, taking the oversight thereof, not by constraint, but willingly; not for filthy lucre, but of a ready mind; neither as being lords over God's heritage, but being ensamples to the flock" ([1 Peter 5:2–3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-pet/5.md)). Then, to everyone: "Casting all your care upon him; for he careth for you" ([1 Peter 5:7](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/1-pet/5.md)). The chain runs: God → leaders → flock, but the posture at every level is willingness and humility, not compulsion.

One tension I should name: the Atonement's offer is universal — "Come unto me, *all* ye that labour and are heavy laden" ([Matthew 11:28](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/11.md)). Delegation is selective. You pick qualified people. Christ doesn't screen applicants. There is a gap between the Atonement's radical inclusivity and delegation's necessary discernment. The covenant of burden-bearing (Mosiah 18) applies to everyone in the fold. Specific stewardship delegation requires trust built on relationship. Both are needed. Neither replaces the other.

## VI. The Failure Modes — When Delegation Breaks

If delegation depends on willingness, trust, and relationship, it breaks when any of those fail. The scriptures describe three failure modes with precision.

**Hoarding: the leader who won't delegate.** Jethro's warning: "Thou wilt surely wear away, both thou, and this people that is with thee" ([Exodus 18:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)). Ballard: "They refuse to delegate or to allow others to grow in their respective responsibilities" ("O Be Wise"). The hoarder protects themselves from the vulnerability of trust. The cost: burnout for the leader, stunted growth for the members. Robert L. Simpson named what delegation frees: "Bishops, learn the great principle of delegation so that your heart and your mind can be free to counsel with the Saints" ("Courts of Love," [April 1972](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1972/04/courts-of-love.md)). The bishop who does everything cannot do the one thing only he can do: counsel.

**Controlling: the leader who delegates the task but not the autonomy.** D&C 121 describes this with devastating clarity: "When we undertake to ... exercise control or dominion or compulsion upon the souls of the children of men, in any degree of unrighteousness, behold, the heavens withdraw themselves; the Spirit of the Lord is grieved" ([D&C 121:37](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)). "It is the nature and disposition of almost all men, as soon as they get a little authority, as they suppose, they will immediately begin to exercise unrighteous dominion" ([D&C 121:39](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md)). The micromanager delegates the labor but retains all control. This isn't delegation. It's subcontracting. And the consequence isn't just organizational dysfunction — the heavens withdraw. Unrighteous dominion severs the controller from God.

**Abandoning: the leader who delegates and disappears.** D&C 121:43–44 addresses this obliquely: "Reproving betimes with sharpness, when moved upon by the Holy Ghost; and then showing forth afterwards an increase of love toward him whom thou hast reproved, lest he esteem thee to be his enemy; that he may know that thy faithfulness is stronger than the cords of death." The leader who corrects and stays present, who gives feedback but doesn't rescind the delegation — that's the model. The opposite is the leader who assigns and vanishes, leaving the delegate without support, accountability, or relationship. Ballard drew the line: "There is a difference between being responsible for getting the work done and doing the work yourself." Delegation transfers labor, not accountability. The delegator remains responsible.

The delegate can fail too. "He that is compelled in all things, the same is a slothful and not a wise servant" ([D&C 58:26](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/58.md)). "He that doeth not anything until he is commanded, and receiveth a commandment with doubtful heart, and keepeth it with slothfulness, the same is damned" ([D&C 58:29](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/58.md)). Not punished — *damned*. Stopped. Unable to progress. The delegate who waits to be commanded, who receives the burden with a doubtful heart, who serves with slothfulness — they've accepted the form of delegation but rejected its substance. The yoke is on, but only one is pulling.

## VII. Becoming — What This Means for How I Live

The Galatians distinction between *baros* (crushing burdens too heavy to carry alone) and *phortion* (personal daily loads) clarifies what delegation is for: "Bear ye one another's burdens, and so fulfil the law of Christ. ... For every man shall bear his own burden" ([Galatians 6:2, 5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/gal/6.md)). Share the crushing weights. Carry your own daily pack. The law of Christ is fulfilled in the sharing.

What I felt when my counselor said "yes" — that relief, that gratitude, that sense of weight redistributing — was not weakness. It was the baptismal covenant working. "Willing to bear one another's burdens, that they may be light." The mechanism is willingness. The result is lightness. And the promise at the end of D&C 121 describes what happens when you do this consistently through persuasion and trust instead of control:

> "Thy dominion shall be an everlasting dominion, and without compulsory means it shall flow unto thee forever and ever." ([D&C 121:46](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md))

Dominion that flows without compulsion IS delegation working perfectly. People follow because they choose to, not because they must.

So here is what I carry forward from this study into my Sunday School presidency and beyond:

1. **Delegate as covenant, not transaction.** When I ask someone to take on a responsibility, I'm inviting them into the pattern of Mosiah 18 — willing burden-bearing that makes burdens light. I should treat the asking with the weight it deserves.

2. **Empower, not just assign.** Numbers 11:17 — God transferred His Spirit, not just a task list. When I delegate, I should share context, vision, and supporting resources. "Sent and empowered," as Webster says. Not just sent.

3. **Let go of perfection.** Ballard says to "allow others to progress and grow, even if it means sometimes getting less-than-perfect results." The delegate's becoming matters more than flawless execution.

4. **Stay present.** D&C 121:44 — "that he may know that thy faithfulness is stronger than the cords of death." Don't delegate and disappear. Don't delegate and hover. Be available, give feedback, show increased trust as competence grows.

5. **Accept delegation willingly.** When someone delegates to me, they're trusting me. Receiving that with a doubtful heart is a form of covenant breaking. D&C 58 says the power is in me. I should act like I believe that.

6. **Name the Atonement when I see it.** When a burden transfers willingly and relief comes, that is the horizontal expression of what Christ does vertically. Name it. Let people know they're participating in something sacred, not just filling an organizational gap.
