# The Only Begotten of the Father

> **Central Question:** Jesus is called the "Only Begotten Son of God" — yet we are also children of God. How are both true? What makes Christ's sonship unique, and what does it mean for ours?

---

## 1. What "Only Begotten" Actually Means

The phrase appears across all five standard works — Moses, D&C, the Book of Mormon, and the New Testament — often in quick succession. It's clearly doing something specific. The question is what.

The Greek behind John's usage is *monogenēs* — *mono* (only, single) + *genēs* (born, begotten). Not "only beloved" or "only significant" but literally: the only one who was begotten this way. The English "begotten" carries that same weight — it refers to being generated, sired, fathered. So "Only Begotten" declares that Christ alone was fathered by God the Eternal Father in the flesh.

Moses 6:57 gives the key from the language of Adam:

> "in the language of Adam, Man of Holiness is his name, and the name of his Only Begotten is the Son of Man, even Jesus Christ, a righteous Judge, who shall come in the meridian of time." — [Moses 6:57](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md)

The Father's name in that language: Man of Holiness. His Son's name: Son of Man — the Son of the Holy Man. This is not metaphor. It is a declaration of literal parentage. The titles are genitive — they identify whose son he is.

When Gabriel spoke to Mary, he did not soften it:

> "The Holy Ghost shall come upon thee, and the power of the Highest shall overshadow thee: therefore also that holy thing which shall be born of thee shall be called the Son of God." — [Luke 1:35](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/1.md)

Alma, prophesying from centuries away, said the same:

> "he shall be born of Mary, at Jerusalem which is the land of our forefathers, she being a virgin, a precious and chosen vessel, who shall be overshadowed and conceive by the power of the Holy Ghost, and bring forth a son, yea, even the Son of God." — [Alma 7:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/7.md)

Note that Alma doesn't say the Holy Ghost is the father — he says Mary conceived *by the power of* the Holy Ghost. The Holy Ghost was the means; the Father was the source. Mosiah 3:5 describes what this made Jesus in mortality: "the Lord Omnipotent who reigneth, who was, and is from all eternity to all eternity, shall come down from heaven among the children of men, and shall dwell in a tabernacle of clay." The immortal One entering a mortal house — a body formed of dust and divinity.

This is what makes Jesus the Only Begotten. Mary was his mortal mother. Elohim, the Eternal Father, was his physical father. No other person in the history of this earth has had that parentage. Every other mortal was fathered by mortal men. Christ's biology was singular: one immortal parent, one mortal parent. That combination gave him the capacity to take upon himself all death and all sin — and also the power to lay down his life and take it up again ([John 10:17–18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/10.md)).

John bears record of this at the baptism of Christ:

> "I, John, bear record that I beheld his glory, as the glory of the Only Begotten of the Father, full of grace and truth, even the Spirit of truth, which came and dwelt in the flesh, and dwelt among us." — [D&C 93:11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93.md)

And again in the great 1832 vision:

> "we heard the voice bearing record that he is the Only Begotten of the Father—That by him, and through him, and of him, the worlds are and were created." — [D&C 76:23–24](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/76.md)

Worlds — plural, without number — were created by the Only Begotten. And yet he condescended into one of them, into a small province in the Middle East, to be born of a village girl. That contrast is the whole sermon.

## 2. We Are Spirit Children — A Different Kind of Sonship

If Jesus is the "only begotten," how can we also be children of God? The answer is that we are children in a different sense: spirit children, born to Heavenly Father in the premortal world.

Paul addresses this directly:

> "Furthermore we have had fathers of our flesh which corrected us, and we gave them reverence: shall we not much rather be in subjection unto the Father of spirits, and live?" — [Hebrews 12:9](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/heb/12.md)

He draws the distinction himself: fathers of our flesh (mortal) vs. the Father of spirits (God). Heavenly Father is our father in a different mode — he is the father of the spirits that inhabit our bodies.

Abraham 3 shows what this looked like before the world was:

> "Now the Lord had shown unto me, Abraham, the intelligences that were organized before the world was; and among all these there were many of the noble and great ones; And God saw these souls that they were good, and he stood in the midst of them." — [Abraham 3:22–23](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/3.md)

We were organized intelligences before this world. We had a premortal existence as spirit children of God. D&C 76:24 says the inhabitants of all the worlds "are begotten sons and daughters unto God." D&C 93:23 confirms it: "Ye were also in the beginning with the Father; that which is Spirit, even the Spirit of truth."

This premortal parentage is why Paul can say in Romans 8: "The Spirit itself beareth witness with our spirit, that we are the children of God" ([Romans 8:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rom/8.md)). It's not metaphor or honorary language. It is literal. We were there. We were born there. We left a Father to come here.

But there is a real distinction. Being born spiritually to God in the premortal life is not the same as being "begotten" of God in the flesh. Jesus is the Only Begotten because in mortality he had a divine Father in the physical sense. The rest of us in mortality had human fathers. Our divine parentage is spiritual, not physical — real, but of a different order. Christ's was both.

Moses 1 captures this difference with precision. God tells Moses: "thou art in the similitude of mine Only Begotten" ([Moses 1:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/1.md)). Moses is like the Only Begotten — in the similitude. He is a type, not the original. And Moses later uses that knowledge as his defense against Satan: "I am a son of God, in the similitude of his Only Begotten" ([Moses 1:13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/1.md)). He knows he is a son of God — that is real — but he also knows the Only Begotten is distinct.

D&C 93:21 draws the hierarchy clearly: "I was in the beginning with the Father, and am the Firstborn." Christ is the Firstborn Spirit. We are spirit children too, but we came after, and we came to mortality differently.

## 3. The Condescension of God: 1 Nephi 11

Nephi's vision is structured around a question the angel asks him twice. Both times, the word is the same: *condescension*.

The first time:

> "And he said unto me: Knowest thou the condescension of God?" — [1 Nephi 11:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11.md)

And Nephi answers honestly:

> "I know that he loveth his children; nevertheless, I do not know the meaning of all things." — [1 Nephi 11:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11.md)

This is one of the most honest answers in scripture. He knows the center — God's love for his children — but admits the full meaning is beyond him. Then the angel shows him a vision: a virgin in Nazareth, exceedingly fair, carried away in the spirit. When she appears again, she is bearing a child in her arms. The angel declares:

> "Behold the Lamb of God, yea, even the Son of the Eternal Father! Knowest thou the meaning of the tree which thy father saw?" — [1 Nephi 11:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11.md)

And Nephi answers: the tree is the love of God. The child in Mary's arms and the tree in Lehi's dream are the same thing — both are representations of the love of God ([1 Nephi 11:22–25](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11.md)). The condescension of God is not simply a theological fact. It is an act of love with a name and a face.

The second question comes later, after Nephi sees the baptism:

> "And the angel said unto me again: Look and behold the condescension of God!" — [1 Nephi 11:26](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/11.md)

The first condescension was the Father's: that the Eternal Father would stoop to father a child through a mortal woman, entering his own creation through ordinary birth. The second condescension is the Son's: that the Firstborn Spirit, creator of worlds without number, full of grace and truth, would be baptized by a mortal man, minister among the sick and outcast, be rejected, and be lifted up on a cross.

The verb *condescend* meant, in older usage, to descend voluntarily to the level of an inferior — to stoop. What the Father did, he did by choice. What the Son did, he did by choice. Neither was compelled. The love that drove both was not abstract. It produced an infant in a manger and a man on a cross.

Mosiah 3:5 sees both sides in a single verse: "the Lord Omnipotent who reigneth, who was, and is from all eternity to all eternity, shall come down from heaven among the children of men, and shall dwell in a tabernacle of clay." The verb is *come down*. Not *be assigned* or *be sent* — though the sending is real — but come down. Voluntary descent. That is condescension.

Jacob 4:5 says Abraham offering Isaac was "a similitude of God and his Only Begotten Son." The father-son relationship was not just an illustration. It was a type embedded in Abraham's flesh. He was asked to live what God would actually do — to understand from the inside what it costs to give a son.

## 4. D&C 93: Firstborn and Church of the Firstborn

D&C 93 reorganizes everything around a concept: Christ went from grace to grace until he received a fulness ([D&C 93:12–13](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93.md)), and we can do the same. The Lord prefaces this with his own identity statement:

> "And now, verily I say unto you, I was in the beginning with the Father, and am the Firstborn; And all those who are begotten through me are partakers of the glory of the same, and are the church of the Firstborn." — [D&C 93:21–22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93.md)

Christ is the Firstborn — first among all spirit children in the premortal world, and the one through whom we are born again in the covenant. Then immediately:

> "Ye were also in the beginning with the Father; that which is Spirit, even the Spirit of truth." — [D&C 93:23](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93.md)

The pattern in these three verses is tight. Christ was in the beginning, is the Firstborn, and is the means by which others are begotten into the church of the Firstborn. We were also in the beginning — as spirit children. And we can be born again through him into that same church.

D&C 76 describes what membership in that church looks like:

> "They are they who are the church of the Firstborn... And are priests of the Most High, after the order of Melchizedek, which was after the order of Enoch, which was after the order of the Only Begotten Son." — [D&C 76:54, 57](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/76.md)

All priesthood traces back to the order of the Only Begotten. The path from our current state to exaltation is the path Christ blazed — and it is the order that bears his name as "the Only Begotten Son."

## 5. Romans 8: Joint-Heirs with Christ

This is where all the threads converge. Paul in Romans 8 lays out the logic of the relationship between our spiritual sonship and our potential inheritance:

> "The Spirit itself beareth witness with our spirit, that we are the children of God: And if children, then heirs; heirs of God, and joint-heirs with Christ; if so be that we suffer with him, that we may be also glorified together." — [Romans 8:16–17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rom/8.md)

The Spirit witnesses that we are children. Not that we will be, if we perform well — that we *are*, because we were born to God before this world was. That birthright is confirmed by the Spirit in this life.

Then Paul makes the legal move: if children, then heirs. In the ancient world, heirs inherited the property and position of their father. Paul says we are heirs of God — which means we are in line to inherit what God has. But then he adds *joint-heirs with Christ*. We don't inherit separately from Christ; we inherit with him, through him, because of what he did. What he earned by right of being the Only Begotten and by his perfect obedience, we receive by adoption through the covenant.

The footnote to verse 16c in the scriptures points to the TG entry "Man, a Spirit Child of Heavenly Father." The footnote to verse 17a points to: "Birthright; Election; Eternal Life; Exaltation; Man, Potential to Become like Heavenly Father." The church has mapped this verse to the deepest concepts of Restoration theology.

The condition Paul names is not perfection but suffering: "if so be that we suffer with him." This isn't punitive — it's participatory. To receive the same inheritance, we walk the same road. Christ suffered for us; we suffer with him. Not in the same measure or manner, but in the same direction. The way into the inheritance passes through the cross, not around it.

Paul frames the same idea in verse 29: God "did predestinate" us "to be conformed to the image of his Son, that he might be the firstborn among many brethren." Christ is the firstborn — D&C 93 confirms this. And the whole plan is to conform us to that image, until we too can be called sons and daughters of God in the fullest sense.

## 6. John 3:16 and the Weight of the Gift

The most recognizable verse in scripture still carries more weight than familiarity suggests:

> "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life." — [John 3:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/3.md)

The footnote to "only" in verse 16e points back to Genesis 22:2 and Jacob 4:5 — to Abraham and Isaac. This is intentional. God was not asking Abraham to do something God himself would not do. He was asking Abraham to do what he already knew he would do. The asking was a preparation, not a parallel. The similitude goes all one direction: Abraham is the type; God is the original.

The word "gave" matters. Not "sent" (though John 3:17 uses that word) — gave. The verb in the Greek is *edōken*, from *didōmi*, to give freely, to grant as a gift. The Father gave his Son as a gift to the world. And what is given as a gift does not carry a demand of return. John 3:36 in the JST adds: "he that believeth on the Son hath everlasting life; and shall receive of his fullness." The gift, fully received, is a fullness.

## What Holds It Together

These passages point to a single architecture:

God the Eternal Father is the Father of spirits — of all humanity in the premortal world, including Jesus Christ, who was the Firstborn among those spirits. But Christ alone was also fathered by God in the flesh, through a mortal mother. That dual nature — infinite Father, mortal mother — gave him power over death that no other mortal has. He could die because he had mortality. He could rise because he had immortality. We couldn't do what he did because we don't have what he had.

But we have what he has as spirit children. We share the same premortal Father. And through the Atonement and covenant, we can become joint-heirs with him — inheriting what he earned, through the adoption of the Spirit, by walking the same road he walked, even if a much shorter distance of it.

The Only Begotten came down so that the many children could come home.

---

## Becoming

Nephi's answer in 1 Nephi 11 is worth staying with: "I know that he loveth his children; nevertheless, I do not know the meaning of all things." He doesn't know everything the condescension means — but he knows the center of it. That's the posture.

What changes in how you live when you actually believe you are a spirit child of God — not metaphorically, but literally? That the same Father who created worlds without number knew your name and face before this world was made?

And what changes when you believe that Christ's suffering opened an inheritance for you that you didn't earn — that you are a joint-heir not because of what you've done but because of what he did and what you're choosing?

The "joint-heirs" passage has a condition: suffering with him. Not someday, as an abstract — now, in whatever road you're on. The glory promised in verse 17 is conditioned on the participation of verse 17. That's not punitive. It's the same road he walked, at a scale proportionate to what we can bear.

The question worth asking this week: Am I actually living as someone who believes they are a child of God — or am I living as though that were a comforting phrase rather than a cosmological fact?
