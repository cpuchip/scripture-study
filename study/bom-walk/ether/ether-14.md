# Ether 14 — the curse of slippery things; the war become an environment of death; "their wickedness had prepared a way"

**Binding idea:** "A great curse upon all the land because of the iniquity of the people" — tools and swords laid down could not be found "upon the morrow" (14:1), so "every man did cleave unto that which was his own, with his hands, and would not borrow neither would he lend; and every man kept the hilt of his sword in his right hand" (14:2). The secret combination devours its leaders — Gilead murdered by his own high priest, then a combination-assassin (Lib) takes the throne (14:9-10). The war rages without end through Lib and his brother Shiz — "Shiz... did slay both women and children, and he did burn the cities" (14:17); "he sweepeth the earth before him!" (14:18) — until "the whole face of the land was covered with the bodies of the dead... there was none left to bury the dead... and the scent thereof went forth upon the face of the land... wherefore the people became troubled by day and by night, because of the scent thereof" (14:21-23). And Moroni's frame: "the Lord did visit them in the fulness of his wrath, and *their wickedness and abominations had prepared a way for their everlasting destruction*" (14:25).

**Read:** Ether 14 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 31 verses. The deepening curse, the all-consuming war, and the self-prepared destruction.

## Thoughts

The chapter opens on a curse that is, at first, almost mundane — things will not stay where you put them: "if a man should lay his tool or his sword upon his shelf... upon the morrow, he could not find it" (14:1). This is the same "slippery" curse Samuel the Lamanite prophesied (Hel 13:18-37) and that fell on the Nephites in their last days (Morm 1:18). But what Ether 14 shows is what such a curse *does to a society*, and it is devastating: "every man did cleave unto that which was his own, with his hands, and would not borrow neither would he lend; and every man kept the hilt of his sword in his right hand" (14:2). The curse on possessions is, at its root, the *death of trust*. When nothing can be securely held, no one will lend, no one will share, every man clutches his own with one hand and grips a sword with the other. This is the exact inversion of Zion. In 4 Nephi, the happiest people "had all things common among them" (4 Ne 1:3) — they could share because the love of God filled their hearts and trust was total. Here, at the bottom of the fall, no one can hold anything, no one will lend, every hand is on a weapon. The slippery curse is the material symptom of a deeper spiritual collapse: a people who have abandoned God lose the very capacity for community, because community runs on trust, and trust has died. The opposite of "all things common" is not "everyone keeps their own" — it is "no one can keep anything, and everyone grips a sword." A society without God does not merely become selfish; it becomes a war of all against all in which even your possessions betray you.

The secret combination, meanwhile, does to its leaders exactly what chapter 9 showed: it devours them. Gilead "received great strength to his army, because of secret combinations" — and then "his high priest murdered him as he sat upon his throne," and a combination-assassin (Lib) murdered *that* man "in a secret pass" and took the kingdom (14:8-10). The combination promises power and delivers assassination; every man who rises by the oath of murder makes himself the next target of it. The throne gained by treachery is held only until the next traitor is ready. This is the internal logic of organized evil playing out to its end — it cannot produce stable rule, only an accelerating cycle of murder, because the very thing it offers (power seized by killing) is available to everyone within it. The Jaredite leadership in its final phase is a revolving door of assassins, each one murdered by the next.

And the war itself becomes something beyond ordinary warfare — it becomes an *environment*, a condition of the whole land (14:21-23). "So great and lasting had been the war, and so long had been the scene of bloodshed and carnage, that the whole face of the land was covered with the bodies of the dead. And so swift and speedy was the war that there was none left to bury the dead, but they did march forth from the shedding of blood to the shedding of blood, leaving the bodies of both men, women, and children strewed upon the face of the land, to become a prey to the worms of the flesh. And the scent thereof went forth upon the face of the land... wherefore the people became troubled by day and by night, because of the scent thereof." This is the most visceral depiction of war's horror in the Book of Mormon. The dead are too many to bury; the living march "from the shedding of blood to the shedding of blood" without pause; the corpses of men, women, and children rot in the open; and the *smell* of the dead becomes the air the survivors breathe, troubling them "by day and by night." War has stopped being a series of battles and become the very atmosphere — a people living inside the stench of their own dead, no longer even pausing to bury them. There is no romance here, no glory; only the obscene physical reality of a civilization rotting where it fell. Mormon, who knew this horror firsthand at Cumorah, abridges it without flinching, because the reader needs to see what the refusal to repent actually produces: not a noble tragedy but a land that stinks of corpses.

The theological frame Moroni gives in verse 25 is the chapter's center: "And thus we see that the Lord did visit them in the fulness of his wrath, and *their wickedness and abominations had prepared a way for their everlasting destruction*." Two things hold together here. This is "the fulness of his wrath" — the choice-land decree fulfilled (Ether 2:8-10): a people ripened in iniquity is swept off. But the second clause is crucial: "*their* wickedness and abominations had *prepared a way*" for it. The destruction is *self-prepared*. God's wrath is not an arbitrary intervention; it is the people being given over to the consequences they themselves built. The slippery curse, the secret combinations, the unrestrained war, the corpses in the field — none of this is God reaching down to smite; it is the natural, inevitable fruit of a society that chose murder, secrecy, and the rejection of every prophet. "The fulness of his wrath" and "their wickedness prepared the way" are the same event described from two sides: God's justice is precisely His allowing the self-consuming evil to run to its end (Morm 4:5, "by the wicked are the wicked punished"). And the engine driving the final phase is named — Shiz "had sworn to avenge himself upon Coriantumr of the blood of his brother" (14:24). The vengeance-oath. The same usurpation of God's prerogative ("vengeance is mine," Morm 3:15) that destroyed the Nephites now drives the Jaredites to their end. Shiz cannot stop, because he has sworn to avenge; and the oath of vengeance, once sworn, becomes a chain that drags both the avenger and his enemy down to mutual destruction. The Jaredites are destroyed not by a thunderbolt but by their own refusal to repent, their own secret combinations, and their own sworn vengeance — the wrath of God being the simple removal of the restraint that had held the consequences back. "There was none to restrain them" (13:31); and so the way they had prepared for their own destruction opened beneath them.

## Notable

Yes — the deepening curse and the all-consuming war. **The curse of slippery things is the death of trust and community** (14:1-2, the inversion of Zion; no one can hold anything, every hand grips a sword); **the secret combination devours its own leaders in an accelerating cycle of murder** (14:8-10, the throne held only until the next traitor); **war becomes an environment of death — the living breathe the stench of their own dead** (14:21-23, the most visceral depiction of war's horror in the book); **the fulness of his wrath is the self-prepared destruction — God removing the restraint** (14:25, "their wickedness had prepared a way"); and **the vengeance-oath is the engine of the end** (14:24, Shiz sworn to avenge, dragging both down to mutual destruction).
