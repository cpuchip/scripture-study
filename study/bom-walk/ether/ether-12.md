# Ether 12 — the witness after the trial of faith; "I give unto men weakness"; faith, hope, and charity

**Binding idea:** Ether "could not be restrained because of the Spirit of the Lord which was in him" and "did cry from the morning, even until the going down of the sun... that by faith all things are fulfilled" (12:2-3). Moroni takes it up: "faith is things which are hoped for and not seen; wherefore, dispute not because ye see not, for ye receive no witness until after the trial of your faith" (12:6) — illustrated by a roll of faith (Christ shown to the fathers, Alma and Amulek, Nephi and Lehi, Ammon, the three disciples), climaxing in the brother of Jared, who "could not be kept from within the veil" (12:19-21). When Moroni fears "the Gentiles will mock... because of our weakness in writing" (12:23), the Lord answers: "Fools mock, but they shall mourn; and my grace is sufficient for the meek... *I give unto men weakness that they may be humble*; and my grace is sufficient for all men that humble themselves before me; for if they humble themselves before me, and have faith in me, *then will I make weak things become strong unto them*" (12:26-27). And the triad: "faith, hope and charity bringeth unto me—the fountain of all righteousness" (12:28). Moroni's witness: "I have seen Jesus, and... he hath talked with me face to face" (12:39).

**Read:** Ether 12 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 41 verses. Moroni's great meditation on faith, weakness, grace, and charity.

## Thoughts

The chapter opens on Ether himself, and the one detail Moroni gives of his preaching is the model of a prophet under compulsion of the Spirit: he "could not be restrained because of the Spirit of the Lord which was in him" (12:2) — the same fire in the bones Jeremiah described, "as a burning fire shut up in my bones, and I was weary with forbearing, and I could not stay" (Jer 20:9). And so he "did cry from the morning, even until the going down of the sun" — all day, every day, to a people who would not hear, because the prophet does not preach on a cost-benefit analysis of his audience's receptivity; he preaches because he cannot not preach. His message is the chapter's seed: "by faith all things are fulfilled." And the corollary, the hope that comes of faith, "maketh an anchor to the souls of men" (12:4; Heb 6:19) — the image of faith-born hope as the thing that holds the soul "sure and steadfast" in the storm. The people rejected it "because they saw them not" (12:5) — which is precisely the misunderstanding of faith that Moroni then sets out to correct.

The hinge of the whole chapter — and one of the most important sentences in scripture about how God works — is verse 6: "faith is things which are hoped for and not seen; wherefore, dispute not because ye see not, *for ye receive no witness until after the trial of your faith*." This is the law that the entire Book of Mormon has been demonstrating, stated as a principle: the witness comes *after* the trial of faith, never before. The sign-seeker has it backwards — he demands to see first and then he will believe. But faith, by definition, acts before it sees; the seeing is the reward of the acting, not its precondition. "Ye receive no witness until after the trial of your faith." You must walk into the dark before the light comes; you must plant the seed before you taste the fruit (Alma 32); you must make the sixteen stones and carry them up the mountain before God touches them (Ether 3). The order is fixed and it is the order of faith: trust, then act, then — after the trial — the witness. To wait for the witness before exercising the faith is to wait forever, because the witness is on the far side of the trial, not the near side.

Moroni then proves it with a roll of faith that functions as the Book of Mormon's own Hebrews 11 (12:7-22). "By faith Christ showed himself unto our fathers... he showed not himself unto them until after they had faith in him" (12:7). "If there be no faith among the children of men God can do no miracle among them" (12:12) — the same principle as Mormon 9:20 (miracles cease through unbelief), now stated as the positive law: faith is the *condition* of the miracle, and God will not (perhaps cannot) work the miracle in the absence of it. Then the examples, each a deliberate echo of a chapter walked: Alma and Amulek and the prison walls (Alma 14:26-29); Nephi and Lehi and the change on the Lamanites, baptized with fire (Hel 5:45-52); Ammon and his brethren among the Lamanites (Alma 17-26); the three disciples' promise. "All they who wrought miracles wrought them by faith... neither at any time hath any wrought miracles until after their faith; wherefore they first believed in the Son of God" (12:16, 18). And the climax is the brother of Jared (12:19-21) — Moroni's own commentary on Ether 3: "so great was his faith in God, that when God put forth his finger he could not hide it from the sight of the brother of Jared... the Lord could not withhold anything from his sight... he could no longer be kept without the veil." Faith perfected breaks the veil. The whole roll is one argument: every manifestation of God's power, from the parting of a prison wall to the parting of the veil, came *after* faith, on the far side of the trial.

But the chapter's most beloved and most personal turn is Moroni's confession of weakness (12:23-27), and it is deeply human. Moroni, holding the plates of the brother of Jared whose writing was "mighty even as thou art, unto the overpowering of man to read them" (12:24), looks at his own work and despairs: "thou hast made us mighty in word... but thou hast not made us mighty in writing... when we write we behold our weakness, and stumble because of the placing of our words; and I fear lest the Gentiles shall mock at our words" (12:23-25). The man finishing the most important record in the world is afraid he is not a good enough writer. And the Lord's answer is the passage that has comforted every person who ever felt inadequate to a sacred task (12:26-27): "Fools mock, but they shall mourn; and my grace is sufficient for the meek... if men come unto me I will show unto them their weakness. *I give unto men weakness that they may be humble*; and my grace is sufficient for all men that humble themselves before me; for if they humble themselves before me, and have faith in me, then will I make weak things become strong unto them." Three things land with full weight here. First, God *gives* weakness — it is not merely permitted or accidental but *given*, on purpose, "that they may be humble." Our weaknesses are not flaws in the design; they are part of the design, the very means by which God keeps us humble enough to need Him. Second, "my grace is sufficient" — not partial, not conditional on first becoming strong, but *sufficient*, complete, for "all men that humble themselves." (Paul heard the same words about his own thorn: "my grace is sufficient for thee: for my strength is made perfect in weakness," 2 Cor 12:9.) Third, the transformation is real and active: "I will make weak things become strong unto them." Not "I will overlook your weakness" but "I will *make* the weak thing strong." The weakness, brought to Christ in humility and faith, becomes the site of His strength. This is the whole gospel of the inadequate: your weakness is given to humble you, His grace is sufficient for you when you humble yourself, and the very weakness you bring to Him He will turn into strength. Moroni feared his clumsy writing would be mocked; the Lord told him the clumsiness was the point, the grace was enough, and the weak thing would be made strong — and so it has been, for the millions who have read Moroni's "weak" words and felt their power.

The chapter closes on the triad and on Moroni's own witness. "Faith, hope and charity bringeth unto me—the fountain of all righteousness" (12:28) — the three that lead to Christ (1 Cor 13:13; Moroni 7). Moroni meditates on each: faith (the brother of Jared moved Mount Zerin, and "if he had not had faith it would not have moved; wherefore thou workest after men have faith," 12:30 — God works *after* faith, the law restated); hope (the mansions of the Father, "man must hope, or he cannot receive an inheritance," 12:32); and charity (the love Christ had "even unto the laying down of thy life," and "except men shall have charity they cannot inherit that place," 12:34). And the Lord's final word to Moroni about his own weakness ties it all together (12:37): "because thou hast seen thy weakness thou shalt be made strong, even unto the sitting down in the place which I have prepared." Seeing one's weakness is not the disqualification Moroni feared; it is the *qualification* — the humility that grace requires. And Moroni bids his first farewell (12:38-41), with the watchman's discharge ("my garments are not spotted with your blood") and his personal testimony: "ye shall know that I have seen Jesus, and that he hath talked with me face to face... even as a man telleth another in mine own language." Moroni — like the brother of Jared, like Emer — saw Jesus, face to face, and spoke with Him as one man to another. (Another witness for the *is-jesus-christ-god* study: Moroni's own face-to-face encounter with the Lord.) And he commends the reader to "seek this Jesus... that the grace of God the Father, and also the Lord Jesus Christ, and the Holy Ghost, which beareth record of them, may be and abide in you forever." The chapter that began with a prophet who could not be restrained from preaching ends with a prophet who has seen the Lord he preached, commending us to seek that same Jesus — the whole arc of faith, from the trial to the witness to the face-to-face.
