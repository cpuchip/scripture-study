# Ether 11 — the prophets rejected and withdrawn; "utterly destroy"; the combination that makes a people deaf

**Binding idea:** Prophets come "in the days of Com... and prophesied of the destruction of that great people except they should repent" (11:1), but "the prophets were rejected by the people" who "sought to destroy them" (11:2). Shiblom's brother "caused that all the prophets who prophesied of the destruction of the people should be put to death" (11:5); then "wars and contentions in all the land, and also many famines and pestilences... a great destruction, such an one as never had been known upon the face of the earth" (11:7) — followed by brief repentance and the Lord's mercy (11:8). The cycle accelerates: in Ethem's days the warning escalates to "the Lord would *utterly destroy* them from off the face of the earth except they repented" (11:12), but "the people hardened their hearts... and the prophets mourned and withdrew from among the people" (11:13). The cause is named: "they did reject all the words of the prophets, *because of their secret society and wicked abominations*" (11:22). And the line runs down: "Coriantor begat Ether, and he died, having dwelt in captivity all his days" (11:23).

## Thoughts

This chapter is the long slide to the brink, and its dominant motion is the *rejection of the prophets* — repeated, escalating, and finally fatal. The prophets come "again," and "again," and "again" (the word recurs across 7:23, 9:28, 11:1, 11:12, 11:20) — God never stops sending the warning. The mercy is relentless: across generations of wickedness, the Lord keeps raising up prophets to cry repentance. But the people's response hardens by degrees. First they merely *reject* the prophets (11:2). Then they *seek to destroy* them, and the prophets flee to the righteous king Com for protection (11:2). Then Shiblom's brother *kills* them all (11:5). And finally, in Ethem's day, the people simply *will not hear* — "the people hardened their hearts, and would not hearken unto their words; and the prophets mourned and withdrew from among the people" (11:13). That last line is the most ominous in the chapter. It is the same withdrawal that befell Mormon (forbidden to preach, Morm 1:16-17), the same famine of the word Amos prophesied (Amos 8:11-12): the prophets, having been rejected, killed, and refused, *withdraw*. The people did not merely reject the prophetic voice; they drove it away, until the prophets themselves mourned and left. A people can push out the very voice that could save them — and when they do, the silence that follows is not God's abandonment but the natural consequence of their own deafness. The most frightening thing is not that God stops sending prophets but that a people can make themselves unable to hear the ones He sends, until the prophets, grieving, go.

And the warning *escalates* precisely as the people harden — a pattern worth marking. Early prophets warn of "the destruction of that great people except they should repent" (11:1); by Ethem's day the warning is "the Lord would *utterly* destroy them from off the face of the earth" (11:12); and by Coriantor's day it is "execute judgment against them to their *utter* destruction" (11:20). The threat does not soften as time runs out; it sharpens. This is mercy intensifying, not diminishing — as the danger grows nearer, God makes the warning more total, more unmistakable, because He is trying harder to reach a people who are hearing less. The escalation of the warning tracks the urgency of the love behind it. And the prophets add a new note (11:21): the Lord "would send or bring forth *another people* to possess the land, by his power, after the manner by which he brought their fathers." The choice-land covenant means the land does not stand empty when a people is swept off — it passes to the next people God brings, exactly as the Jaredites themselves received it. This is the prophecy of the handoff: the Jaredites would be replaced by the Lehites (the Nephites and Lamanites), whom God would bring "after the manner by which he brought their fathers" (the same ocean crossing, the same covenant). The land is the Lord's, and He gives it to whom He will, in succession, on the same terms — and the people who ripen in iniquity simply make room for the next.

The chapter is also precise about *why* the people finally became deaf to the prophets, and it ties the whole book together: "they did reject all the words of the prophets, *because of their secret society and wicked abominations*" (11:22; cf. 11:15). The rejection of the prophetic voice is rooted in the secret combination. This is the link the book has been building: the secret combination, entrenched beyond a king's power (10:34), now drives the people to reject every prophet, because the combination *cannot survive the light the prophets bring*. A society organized around oath-bound secrecy, power, and gain has a structural interest in silencing the voice that exposes it and calls it to repentance. The prophets threaten the combination's existence; so the combination, having captured the people's hearts, turns them against the prophets. Organized hidden evil and deafness to the prophetic word are not two separate problems but one: the combination makes the people unable to hear, because hearing would mean its undoing. This is why the silencing of prophets recurs at every stage of both the Jaredite and Nephite collapse — it is the secret combination defending itself.

And the chapter ends on the line of captivity running down to its final name (11:9, 18-19, 23). Seth, Moron, Coriantor — each "dwelt in captivity all his days." The genealogy that opened the book of Ether (1:6-32), listed in reverse from Ether back to Jared, is now revealed in its forward motion as a chain of imprisoned kings, the once-glorious line ending in bondage. And the last name: "Coriantor begat Ether, and he died, having dwelt in captivity all his days" (11:23). Ether — the last prophet, the man whose record this is — is born into a people on the brink: the kingdom fragmented, the combination entrenched, the prophets withdrawn, the warning escalated to "utter destruction," and his own father dying in captivity. He is born at the end of the world, into the wreckage of a civilization that has rejected every prophet sent to it. And yet, as the next chapter will show, Ether will prophesy anyway — "from the morning, even until the going down of the sun" (12:3) — crying repentance to a people who will not hear, because that is what a prophet does even at the end. The line that began in the founding faith of the brother of Jared ends in the lonely faith of Ether, prophesying in the ruins. The book has come full circle: it began with one man crying unto the Lord and being given a choice land; it will end with one man crying repentance over that land as it destroys itself.

## Notable

Yes — the slide to the brink. **God never stops sending prophets, but a people can make themselves unable to hear** (11:1-13, the prophets come "again" across generations, rejected by degrees until they mourn and withdraw); **the warning escalates as the people harden — mercy intensifying, not diminishing** (11:12, 20, "utterly destroy" sharpening as time runs out); **the choice land passes to another people God brings after the same manner** (11:21, the Jaredite-to-Lehite handoff); **the secret combination makes a people deaf to the prophets** (11:15, 22, the combination silences the voice that would expose it — organized hidden evil and rejection of the prophetic word are one problem); and **the book comes full circle — from the founding faith of the brother of Jared to the lonely faith of Ether** (11:23, the last prophet born into the wreckage).
