# Ether 2 — the choice-land decree; the three-hour chastening; "what will ye that I should do?"

**Binding idea:** The Jaredites journey "into that quarter where there never had man been" (2:5), led by the Lord in a cloud, to "the land of promise, which was choice above all other lands, which the Lord God had preserved for a righteous people" (2:7). And the everlasting decree: "whoso should possess this land of promise, from that time henceforth and forever, should serve him, the true and only God, or they should be swept off when the fulness of his wrath should come upon them" (2:8) — applied straight to the latter-day reader: "this cometh unto you, O ye Gentiles... that ye may repent" (2:11), for "whatsoever nation shall possess it shall be free... if they will but serve the God of the land, who is Jesus Christ" (2:12). At the seashore, after four years, "the Lord... chastened him because he remembered not to call upon the name of the Lord" (2:14). Then the sealed barges "tight like unto a dish" (2:17), with no light and no air — and the Lord solves the air ("make a hole in the top, and also in the bottom," 2:20) but turns the light back to the man: "What will ye that I should do that ye may have light in your vessels?" (2:23, 25).

**Read:** Ether 2 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 25 verses. The journey, the decree of the land, the chastening, and the question that sets up the miracle.

## Thoughts

The journey itself is rendered with a few vivid details — the valley of Nimrod, the flocks "male and female, of every kind," the fish carried in a vessel of water, the seeds, and "deseret, which, by interpretation, is a honey bee" (2:3), the word the Utah pioneers would later take for their own. They are carrying the makings of a whole world — animals, fish, fowl, bees, seeds — into "that quarter where there never had man been." And the mode of their travel is the same as Israel's: "the Lord did go before them, and did talk with them as he stood in a cloud, and gave directions whither they should travel" (2:5; Ex 13:21). The Jaredite exodus prefigures Israel's exodus prefigures Lehi's — God going before His people in a cloud, leading them to a promised land. The pattern is older than Moses.

But the doctrinal center of the chapter is the decree of the land (2:7-12), which states explicitly the law that the whole book of Ether exists to demonstrate. "Whoso should possess this land of promise, from that time henceforth and forever, should serve him, the true and only God, or they should be swept off when the fulness of his wrath should come upon them." This is the consecration of the land, sworn to the brother of Jared, binding "from that time henceforth and forever" — not just on the Jaredites but on *every* nation that would ever possess it. And the condition is absolute and simple: serve God, or be swept off. There is no third option, no neutral tenancy. The land is holy ground, and it tolerates only one kind of occupant — those who serve the God of the land. But note the mercy folded into the severity: the sweeping-off comes only "when they are ripened in iniquity," only "when the fulness of his wrath shall come upon them" — and "it is not until the fulness of iniquity among the children of the land, that they are swept off" (2:10). God does not destroy at the first sin, or the hundredth; He waits until the iniquity is *full* (Gen 15:16, the Amorites' iniquity "not yet full"). The long-suffering is built into the decree. The Jaredites and the Nephites both proved how much wickedness God will bear before the fulness comes. The judgment is real and certain, but it is never hasty.

And then Moroni does what he keeps doing — he turns the decree on *us*: "this cometh unto you, O ye Gentiles, that ye may know the decrees of God—that ye may repent, and not continue in your iniquities until the fulness come, that ye may not bring down the fulness of the wrath of God upon you as the inhabitants of the land have hitherto done" (2:11). The decree is not Jaredite history; it is a live warning to whatever nation holds the land now. Two civilizations have already been swept off it for ripening in iniquity; the latter-day Gentiles who possess it are under the identical decree. And the positive promise is just as live: "whatsoever nation shall possess it shall be free from bondage, and from captivity, and from all other nations under heaven, *if they will but serve the God of the land, who is Jesus Christ*" (2:12). Freedom — national liberty, freedom from captivity and from other nations — is conditioned on serving Christ. This is a startling claim about the relationship between a nation's righteousness and its liberty, and it identifies "the God of the land" by name: Jesus Christ. (Another datapoint for the *is-jesus-christ-god* study — the God who swore the decree, the God of the land, is named as Jesus Christ. The God of the Old Testament land-covenant is Christ.)

The three-hour chastening (2:14-15) is one of the most humanizing moments in the book, and its lesson cuts against a natural assumption. The brother of Jared — "a large and mighty man, and a man highly favored of the Lord," the great intercessor whose prayers preserved the language — had stopped praying. For *four years* at the seashore, apparently, "he remembered not to call upon the name of the Lord," and the Lord "chastened him... for the space of three hours." The most favored man of his generation neglected prayer for years. The lesson is sobering: spiritual favor is not immunity from spiritual drift. The man who could move mountains by prayer (Ether 12:30) let his prayers lapse, and needed a three-hour rebuke to call him back. No one is past the danger of neglecting the very practice that made them favored. But there is mercy even in the chastening — note *how* the Lord chastens: He "talked with the brother of Jared" for three hours. The rebuke comes inside a three-hour *conversation* with the Lord. Even God's correction is communion; the chastening itself is a return to the very thing he had neglected — the Lord's voice. And the warning attached is the one that has run through this whole closing section: "my Spirit will not always strive with man... if ye will sin until ye are fully ripe ye shall be cut off from the presence of the Lord" (2:15; Gen 6:3). The same principle as the day of grace passing (Morm 2:15) and the gifts ceasing (Morm 9:20) — the Spirit's striving is patient but not infinite. The chastening was a mercy precisely because it came while the Spirit was still striving.

The barges are described with an almost obsessive repetition — "tight like unto a dish" five times in one verse (2:17): bottom, sides, top, ends peaked, door. They are completely sealed vessels, submarine-like, which creates the two problems that drive the rest of the story: no light, no air. And the way the Lord handles the two problems is the heart of the chapter and the setup for chapter 3. The air problem He solves *directly* — "thou shalt make a hole in the top, and also in the bottom; and when thou shalt suffer for air thou shalt unstop the hole" (2:20). Simple, practical, handed over. But the *light* problem He does not solve. Instead, He turns the question back to the man: "What will ye that I should do that ye may have light in your vessels?" (2:23). And He doesn't merely ask — He first rules out the obvious answers: "ye cannot have windows, for they will be dashed in pieces; neither shall ye take fire with you, for ye shall not go by the light of fire" (2:23). Windows would shatter in the mountain waves; fire is impossible in a sealed, tossing vessel. Having eliminated every conventional solution, the Lord puts it back: "what will ye that I should prepare for you that ye may have light when ye are swallowed up in the depths of the sea?" (2:25).

This is one of the most instructive moments in all of scripture about how God answers prayer. The brother of Jared came with a problem and an implicit request: *solve this for me*. And God's response was a *question* — not because He lacked an answer, but because He wanted the man to bring one. He will not simply hand over the solution; He requires the brother of Jared to think, to use his agency and his reason, to *propose* something, and then bring it to God to be sanctified. This is the pattern Joseph Smith would later be taught explicitly: "you must study it out in your mind; then you must ask me if it be right" (D&C 9:8). God often refuses to do *for* us what He has prepared us to do *with* Him. The light problem is left open precisely so that the brother of Jared will have to exercise faith of his own devising — and chapter 3 will show what he does with it: he goes and makes sixteen small clear stones and asks God to touch them. The miracle of the shining stones is possible only because God first declined to solve the problem Himself. The unanswered half of the prayer was the invitation to a greater faith. Sometimes God's "what will ye that I should do?" is not a withholding but a summons — to stop waiting passively and bring Him something to work with.
