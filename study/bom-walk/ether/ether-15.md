# Ether 15 — the end: too-late repentance, the Spirit ceased to strive, and Ether's peace

**Binding idea:** Coriantumr, recovering, "began to remember the words which Ether had spoken" — "there had been slain by the sword already nearly two millions of his people," and "he began to repent... and his soul mourned and refused to be comforted" (15:1-3). He offers Shiz the kingdom to spare the people; Shiz answers, give yourself up to be slain (15:4-5). But "the people repented not of their iniquity" (15:6), and the whole population gathers to the hill Ramah — "that same hill where my father Mormon did hide up the records" (15:11) — over four years, "both men, women and children being armed with weapons of war" (15:15). They fight by day and "rend the air" with howling for their dead by night (15:16-17). When Coriantumr offers peace again, "the Spirit of the Lord had ceased striving with them, and Satan had full power over the hearts of the people; for they were given up unto the hardness of their hearts... that they might be destroyed" (15:19). "Drunken with anger" (15:22), they dwindle day by day until only Coriantumr and Shiz remain, and "Coriantumr... smote off the head of Shiz" (15:30) and fell. Ether finishes his record: "Whether the Lord will that I be translated, or that I suffer the will of the Lord in the flesh, it mattereth not, if it so be that I am saved in the kingdom of God. Amen" (15:34).

**Read:** Ether 15 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 34 verses. The annihilation of the Jaredite nation, and the last words of the last prophet.

## Thoughts

The chapter opens on Coriantumr's repentance, and the tragedy of it is its *timing*. After two million of his people lie dead — "two millions of mighty men, and also their wives and their children" — Coriantumr finally "began to repent of the evil which he had done," remembering the words of all the prophets and seeing "that they were fulfilled thus far, every whit; and his soul mourned and refused to be comforted" (15:2-3). And his repentance is real: he writes Shiz offering to *give up the kingdom* — the very thing he had clung to through all the bloodshed — "for the sake of the lives of the people" (15:4). This is exactly what the Lord had asked of him in 13:20: repent, give up the kingdom, and the people would be spared. But the offer that would have saved everyone *then* cannot save anyone *now*, and the reason is devastating: "the people repented not of their iniquity" (15:6). Coriantumr's repentance, sincere as it finally is, cannot save a people who will not themselves repent. A leader's late turning cannot undo a nation's hardness. There is a hard lesson in this for anyone who thinks repentance can always be deferred: there is a point past which even genuine repentance cannot reverse the consequences already set in motion, because the consequences no longer depend on you alone. Coriantumr could repent of his own evil, but he could not repent for two million dead, and he could not repent for a people who would not. The window in which his repentance could have saved his nation had closed; what remained was only the saving of his own soul, and even that came at the cost of watching everyone else die. (And the vengeance-oath blocks even the peace he now offers — Shiz will accept only Coriantumr's death, because Shiz has sworn to avenge his brother. The two oaths, Coriantumr's clinging and Shiz's vengeance, lock the people into mutual destruction even after one of them is willing to yield.)

The gathering to the hill Ramah carries a detail that should make any reader of the whole Book of Mormon shudder: "the army of Coriantumr did pitch their tents by the hill Ramah; and it was that same hill where my father Mormon did hide up the records unto the Lord, which were sacred" (15:11). Ramah is Cumorah. The two civilizations — the Jaredites and the Nephites, separated by more than a thousand years — make their final end *at the same hill*. The same ground that received the last battle of the Jaredites would receive the last battle of the Nephites. This is no coincidence in Moroni's telling; it is the law of the land made geographic. The hill where Moroni's own father would hide the records and command the last Nephite ten thousand is the hill where the last Jaredite armies gathered to annihilate each other. Two peoples, the same covenant, the same ripening in iniquity, the same hill, the same end. Moroni, abridging the Jaredite record, is standing (in memory) on the very ground where he watched his own people die. The repetition is the warning: this is what the land does to a people who fill it with wickedness, and it has done it twice.

The four-year mustering is its own horror (15:12-15): "they were for the space of four years gathering together the people, that they might get all who were upon the face of the land... every one to the army which he would, with their wives and their children—both men, women and children being armed with weapons of war." This is total mobilization carried to its absolute end: not soldiers but the *entire population* — every man, woman, and child — armed and arrayed for mutual annihilation. There are no civilians left, no one outside the war, no remnant kept back. The whole people has been drawn into the machine of its own destruction. And the nights are filled with grief that changes nothing: they fight all day, retire, and "took up a howling and a lamentation for the loss of the slain of their people; and so great were their cries, their howlings and lamentations, that they did rend the air exceedingly" (15:16). Night after night the air is torn with mourning — and every morning they march back to kill and die again. This is the most terrible thing in the chapter: a people who *grieve their dead* and yet *cannot stop killing*. The grief is real; it does not turn them. They weep all night and slaughter all day, locked in a cycle that even their own anguish cannot break.

And verse 19 names why, and it is the theological climax of the whole book: "the Spirit of the Lord had ceased striving with them, and Satan had full power over the hearts of the people; for they were given up unto the hardness of their hearts, and the blindness of their minds that they might be destroyed." Here is the end-state of a people who reject every prophet, embrace every secret combination, and refuse every offered repentance: the Spirit *ceases to strive* (Gen 6:3, the same withdrawal before the Flood; Morm 5:16), and they are "given up" — given over (Rom 1:24-28) to the hardness they chose, with Satan having "full power over the hearts." This is the most frightening doctrine in scripture, and the chapter dramatizes it with unbearable clarity: a people so given up that even Coriantumr's offered peace cannot reach them, even their own nightly grief cannot turn them, even the obvious certainty of mutual destruction cannot stop them. They are "drunken with anger, even as a man who is drunken with wine" (15:22) — anger has become an intoxication that overrides self-preservation, reason, and grief alike. A drunk man cannot make himself sober by willing it; a people drunken with anger cannot stop the war by reasoning about it. The wrath has taken them over. This is what it looks like to be "given up": not God abandoning a people in cruelty, but God finally honoring the choice they have made over and over, withdrawing the Spirit they have spent generations rejecting, and letting the hardness they cultivated run to its end. The terror is that the people no longer *want* to be saved; they want only to kill, and grieve, and kill again.

The dwindling to two men is rendered with a relentless, numbing arithmetic (15:23-31): fifty-two and sixty-nine; thirty-two and twenty-seven; three hours of fighting until all faint with loss of blood; and finally "when they had all fallen by the sword, save it were Coriantumr and Shiz." Two men, the last of millions. Coriantumr, having rested on his sword, "smote off the head of Shiz" — and then the macabre, unforgettable final image: "after he had smitten off the head of Shiz, that Shiz raised up on his hands and fell; and after that he had struggled for breath, he died" (15:31). The decapitated body rising on its hands, struggling for breath, then falling — the last convulsion of a dead civilization. And "Coriantumr fell to the earth, and became as if he had no life" (15:32). The lone survivor, who would live only to be found by another people and buried among strangers (Omni 1:21), collapses beside the man he has just beheaded. There is no victory here, no triumph — only one man left alive among the corpses of his entire world, and even he as good as dead. This is the end the brother of Jared's warning foresaw, the end the prophets cried against for generations, the end the secret combinations and the vengeance-oaths and the rejected mercy all prepared: a civilization reduced to one half-dead man among millions of the slain.

And then, against all of it, Ether's final words — and they are the chapter's, and the book's, redemption (15:32-34). The Lord says to Ether, "Go forth," and he goes forth and beholds "that the words of the Lord had all been fulfilled," finishes his record, and hides it for the people of Limhi to find. And his last written words are these: "Whether the Lord will that I be translated, or that I suffer the will of the Lord in the flesh, it mattereth not, if it so be that I am saved in the kingdom of God. Amen." Set this serenity against everything that has just happened. Ether has watched his *entire civilization* annihilate itself — every soul he ever knew, his whole world, two million dead and then the slow dwindling to nothing. He alone is left of his people. And his last word is not despair, not rage, not even grief, but *peace*: it does not matter what becomes of me — translated or dead — only that I am saved in the kingdom of God. This is the faith that has nothing left but God, and finds that God is enough. The raging people who were "drunken with anger" are all dead; the lone faithful witness, who has lost everything they had and more, is at peace. The contrast is the whole point of the book. A man can lose his entire world and remain serene, if his treasure was never his world but his God. Ether's "it mattereth not" is the opposite of the people's "given up": they were given up to their hardness because they wanted only the kingdom and the vengeance; he is given peace because he wanted only the kingdom of God. The last word of the book of Ether is not the annihilation; it is a faithful man, alone in the ruins, saying that as long as he is saved in the kingdom of God, nothing else matters at all.
