# Ether 8 — secret combinations from Cain; "the Lord worketh not in secret"; the warning to all lands

**Binding idea:** A later Jared, having lost the kingdom and "set his heart upon... the glory of the world" (8:7), is given a plan by his daughter — "exceedingly expert" and "exceedingly fair" — drawn from "the record which our fathers brought across the great deep... that they by their secret plans did obtain kingdoms and great glory" (8:9). She dances before Akish, who desires her, and the bride-price is "the head of my father, the king" (8:10-12). Akish binds his kinsfolk by oath, "the oaths which were given by them of old who also sought power, which had been handed down even from Cain, who was a murderer from the beginning" (8:15), "kept up by the power of the devil" (8:16). "The Lord worketh not in secret combinations, neither doth he will that man should shed blood" (8:19). Moroni refuses to record the oaths — "they are had among all people" (8:20) — and turns to warn the reader: these "have caused the destruction of this people... and also the destruction of the people of Nephi" (8:21); "whoso buildeth it up seeketh to overthrow the freedom of all lands, nations, and countries" (8:25); "awake to a sense of your awful situation" (8:24).

**Read:** Ether 8 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 26 verses. The secret combination enters the Jaredite record, and Moroni's direct warning to the latter-day Gentiles.

## Thoughts

The narrative that opens the chapter is dark and almost theatrical — the daughter of Jared dancing before Akish, the head of a king demanded as a bride-price (an echo of Salome and the head of John the Baptist, Mark 6:22-28). But the detail that matters most is *where* the daughter of Jared got her plan: "Hath he not read the record which our fathers brought across the great deep? Behold, is there not an account concerning them of old, that they by their secret plans did obtain kingdoms and great glory?" (8:9). She found the secret combinations *in the record*. This is a sobering counterpoint to everything the closing books have said about the record as a sacred witness. The same kind of record that preserves the words of Christ for the salvation of future generations can also preserve the methods of evil for their destruction — and the wicked will mine it. The daughter of Jared read the account of the ancient oath-bound conspiracies and revived them, deliberately, to win back her father's throne. Knowledge preserved is morally neutral until a heart takes it up; the same archive that can save can, in the wrong hands, supply the blueprint for murder. The record is a mirror: the righteous find Christ in it, and the ambitious find "secret plans" to "obtain kingdoms and great glory."

And the oaths Akish administers are given a genealogy that reaches to the very beginning of human evil (8:15): "the oaths which were given by them of old who also sought power, *which had been handed down even from Cain, who was a murderer from the beginning*." This is the deep root the Book of Mormon traces under every secret combination — Gadianton's band (Hel 6:26-30), the Jaredite conspiracies, all of it — back to Cain's covenant with Satan (Moses 5:29-31): "swear unto me by thy throat... that thou tell it not." The pattern is always the same: an oath of secrecy, a pledge of mutual protection enforced by death, and the goal of power and gain through murder. And the energy behind it is named: "they were kept up by the *power of the devil*... to keep them in darkness, to help such as sought power to gain power, and to murder, and to plunder, and to lie" (8:16). The secret combination is not merely a human political tool; it is satanic infrastructure, the devil's own organization for keeping men "in darkness" and turning the lust for power into murder. "Most abominable and wicked above all, in the sight of God" (8:18) — God reserves His strongest condemnation not for the sins of weakness but for the organized, sworn, secret pursuit of power through bloodshed.

The hinge of the chapter is verse 19: "the Lord worketh not in secret combinations, neither doth he will that man should shed blood, but in all things hath forbidden it, from the beginning of man." This is the clean contrast that exposes the secret combination for what it is — a *counterfeit* of God's work. God works in the open. His covenants are public, His prophets cry repentance in the streets, His word is meant to be proclaimed "upon the house-tops" (Morm 5:8). The secret combination inverts every term: where God works openly, it works in secret; where God forbids the shedding of blood, it is built on murder; where God seeks to free men, it seeks power over them. The secrecy itself is the tell — anything that requires an oath of concealment, that cannot bear the light, that binds men by fear of death not to divulge it, is by that very fact opposed to God, "for the Lord worketh not in secret." This is a usable discernment: the work of God can always stand in the open; the work of the devil requires the dark.

And then Moroni does something I find quietly profound: he *refuses to write the oaths* (8:20). "I, Moroni, do not write the manner of their oaths and combinations, for it hath been made known unto me that they are had among all people." Where the daughter of Jared *revived* the dark knowledge by reading it out of the record, Moroni *breaks the chain* by refusing to record it. He will warn us that secret combinations exist, that they destroyed both his people and the Jaredites, that they are deadly — but he will not transmit the *method*. He has learned the lesson the daughter of Jared's story teaches: some knowledge is dangerous precisely in its preservation, and the faithful record-keeper exercises restraint about what he passes on. This is the inverse of the daughter of Jared. She searched the record for the secret plans and used them to murder a king; Moroni reads the same kind of record and deliberately declines to write the method down, lest the next ambitious soul find the blueprint. The record-keeper's discipline includes *withholding* — knowing that to write certain things is to arm the wicked. (And it is a model worth carrying into any age that preserves and transmits knowledge: not everything true should be written down where the dangerous can find it.)

The heart of the chapter is the warning, and it is among the most pointed Moroni gives to the latter-day reader (8:21-25). First the historical fact: secret combinations "have caused the destruction of this people of whom I am now speaking, and also the destruction of the people of Nephi" (8:21). *Both* civilizations on this land — the Jaredites and the Nephites — were destroyed by the same disease. Then the law: "whatsoever nation shall uphold such secret combinations, to get power and gain, until they shall spread over the nation, behold, they shall be destroyed; for the Lord will not suffer that the blood of his saints... shall always cry unto him from the ground for vengeance... and yet he avenge them not" (8:22). And then the direct address: "O ye Gentiles, it is wisdom in God that these things should be shown unto you, that thereby ye may repent... and suffer not that these murderous combinations shall get above you" (8:23). The warning is specific and urgent: *when* you see these things come among you — and Moroni speaks as one shown our day (Morm 8:35) — "awake to a sense of your awful situation, because of this secret combination which shall be among you" (8:24). The phrase "awake to a sense of your awful situation" is a summons out of complacency: secret combinations work by remaining unseen, and the first defense is simply to *recognize* them, to wake up to the fact that organized, hidden pursuit of power and gain is operating. And the stakes are total: "whoso buildeth it up seeketh to overthrow the freedom of all lands, nations, and countries; and it bringeth to pass the destruction of all people, for it is built up by the devil, who is the father of all lies" (8:25). The secret combination's ultimate aim is not local power but the overthrow of *freedom itself* — "of all lands, nations, and countries." It is the devil's instrument for the destruction of human liberty everywhere, because the devil "who beguiled our first parents" has always sought to control rather than free.

And the chapter ends by naming why Moroni wrote the warning at all (8:26), and it is not fear but hope: "I, Moroni, am commanded to write these things *that evil may be done away*, and that the time may come that Satan may have no power upon the hearts of the children of men, but that they may be persuaded to do good continually, that they may come unto the fountain of all righteousness and be saved." The purpose of exposing the secret combination is its undoing — that evil be done away, that Satan lose his grip, that men be turned toward good. Moroni does not warn to frighten but to free. The same Moroni who refuses to write the oaths writes the warning, so that the reader, awake to the danger, can resist it and "come unto the fountain of all righteousness." The exposure of the darkness is in service of the light: name the secret combination, recognize it, refuse it, and turn to Christ. That is the whole purpose of the chapter — not to make the reader afraid of the conspiracy, but to make the reader free of it.
