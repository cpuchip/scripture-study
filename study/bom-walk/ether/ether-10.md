# Ether 10 — the roll of kings; the tyrant Riplakish; Lib's prosperity; the combinations a king cannot defeat

**Binding idea:** The long succession of Jaredite kings, alternating righteous and wicked, with prosperity tracking the throne. Shez "did remember the destruction of his fathers, and he did build up a righteous kingdom" (10:2). Riplakish "did not do that which was right" — heavy taxes, forced labor, prisons for those who could not pay, "many spacious buildings" and "an exceedingly beautiful throne" built on the people's backs — until "the people did rise up in rebellion against him" and killed him (10:5-8). Morianton "did do justice unto the people, but not unto himself because of his many whoredoms; wherefore he was cut off from the presence of the Lord" (10:11). Under righteous Lib the land reaches its material peak — industry in ore, silks, linen, tools, "all manner of work of exceedingly curious workmanship," and "never could be a people more blessed than were they... in a land that was choice above all lands" (10:23-28). But generations are born and die in captivity (10:30-31), and in the days of Com "there began to be robbers in the land; and they adopted the old plans, and administered oaths after the manner of the ancients... Now Com did fight against them much; nevertheless, he did not prevail against them" (10:33-34).

**Read:** Ether 10 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 34 verses. The roll of kings, the tyrant, the peak of prosperity, and the entrenchment of the secret combinations.

## Thoughts

Moroni's roll of kings is not mere genealogy; it is an argument, and the chapter heading states the thesis: "Some of the kings are righteous; others are wicked—When righteousness prevails, the people are blessed and prospered by the Lord." Read straight through, the chapter is a controlled demonstration of the choice-land decree at the level of the individual reign. Shez remembers what God did and builds "a righteous kingdom"; the people prosper. Riplakish forgets and oppresses; the people are crushed and then rebel. Levi serves long in captivity, then "did that which was right in the sight of the Lord; and the people did prosper." Corom, Kish, Lib — "did that which was good" — and the land flourishes. The lesson repeats with each name: the king's righteousness is the hinge on which the people's blessing turns. A nation under a throne rises and falls with the soul of the man on it, which is precisely the danger the brother of Jared named (6:23) — concentrate the people's fate in one fallible man, and the people's welfare becomes hostage to his character.

The one extended portrait is Riplakish (10:5-8), and it is the Book of Mormon's sharpest study of *tyranny*. He "did lay that upon men's shoulders which was grievous to be borne; yea, he did tax them with heavy taxes; and with the taxes he did build many spacious buildings." He erects "an exceedingly beautiful throne," builds "many prisons," imprisons whoever cannot or will not pay, forces them to labor "continually for their support," and kills those who refuse. "Wherefore he did obtain all his fine work, yea, even his fine gold he did cause to be refined in prison." This is the anatomy of oppression: monumental wealth and beauty extracted from the forced labor of the poor, the throne and the spacious buildings literally built on the backs of imprisoned men. It is the great-and-spacious building (1 Ne 8:26-27) made into a regime — pride that builds its monuments on the suffering of those it crushes. And the Book of Mormon's verdict on it is the people's own: after forty-two years "the people did rise up in rebellion against him; and... Riplakish was killed." There is a moral realism here that the record does not flinch from — a tyrant who grinds his people for his own glory will, in the end, be thrown down by the very people he ground. Oppression is not stable; it manufactures the rebellion that ends it.

A subtler portrait is Morianton (10:11): "he did do justice unto the people, but not unto himself because of his many whoredoms; wherefore he was cut off from the presence of the Lord." Here is a ruler who governed *well* — eased the people's burden, did them justice, was beloved enough that "they did anoint him to be their king" — and yet was "cut off from the presence of the Lord" by his private sin. This is an important distinction the chapter draws quietly: public justice and personal righteousness are not the same thing, and the first does not save a man who lacks the second. Morianton was a good king and a lost man. A person can be genuinely just to others — fair, generous, a relief to those under him — and still destroy his own soul by the sins he keeps private. Doing justice to the people did not exempt Morianton from doing justice to God in his own life. The good he did for others was real; it simply could not stand in for the righteousness he refused himself.

Lib's reign is the peak of Jaredite material prosperity (10:19-28), and it is worth comparing carefully to the other "happiest people" the record names. The Jaredites under Lib are "exceedingly industrious," working all manner of ore, silks, fine-twined linen, tools, weapons, "all manner of work of exceedingly curious workmanship" — and the verdict is "never could be a people more blessed than were they, and more prospered by the hand of the Lord" (10:28). But set that beside 4 Nephi 1:16, the *other* superlative — "surely there could not be a happier people among all the people who had been created by the hand of God." The difference is everything. The Nephite Zion of 4 Nephi was the happiest people because "the love of God did dwell in the hearts of the people" (4 Ne 1:15) — no contention, all things common, no -ites. The Jaredite peak under Lib is the *most prospered* people — industry, ore, fine workmanship, material abundance. One is a peak of the heart; the other is a peak of the economy. The Jaredites at their height had everything the world calls blessing — wealth, industry, craftsmanship, a choice land — and it did not save them, because material prosperity is not the same as the love of God. The most-prospered people are not necessarily the happiest, and prosperity by itself sets a civilization up for exactly the pride that destroys it. Lib's golden age is real, but it is a golden age of *things*, and the record will show it sliding back into captivity and combination within a generation.

And the chapter closes on the ominous note that sets up the end (10:30-34). Generations are born and die in captivity — Hearthom, Heth, Aaron, Amnigaddah, Coriantum, "all his days" in bondage, the genealogy of Ether 1 now revealed as a chain of imprisoned kings. And then: "in the days of Com there began to be robbers in the land; and they adopted the old plans, and administered oaths after the manner of the ancients, and sought again to destroy the kingdom. Now Com did fight against them much; *nevertheless, he did not prevail against them*" (10:33-34). The secret combinations are back — and this time, for the first time, a *righteous* king "did not prevail against them." The disease that earlier kings could fight off is now entrenched beyond a king's power to root out. This is the turning point of the whole Jaredite decline: when the secret combination becomes so woven into the society that even a king fighting "much" cannot defeat it, the civilization's doom is sealed. The combination has metastasized. From here, the book moves toward the final destruction — because once the organized, oath-bound pursuit of power through murder is beyond rooting out, a nation has lost the ability to save itself, and only its complete destruction will end the combination. Com's failure to prevail is the quiet announcement that the Jaredites have passed the point of recovery.
