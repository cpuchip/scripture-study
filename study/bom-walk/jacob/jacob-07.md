# Jacob 7 — Sherem, and "I Could Not Be Shaken"

*(The first named anti-Christ; Jacob's weary farewell — the LAST chapter of Jacob. Mini-study + book synthesis.)*

**Binding idea:** A learned, eloquent man comes to overthrow the doctrine of Christ — and cannot move Jacob. What is Jacob's anchor against rhetoric, and why does the book end not in triumph but in mourning?

**Read:** "There came a man among the people of Nephi, whose name was Sherem" (v1), who preached "**there should be no Christ**" with "many things which were flattering unto the people," to "overthrow the doctrine of Christ" (v2). He was "**learned**... he could use much flattery, and much power of speech, according to the power of the devil" (v4). He hoped "to shake me from the faith," but "**I truly had seen angels, and they had ministered unto me. And also, I had heard the voice of the Lord speaking unto me in very word... wherefore, I could not be shaken**" (v5). Sherem's charge: Jacob perverts the law of Moses into "the worship of a being which ye say shall come many hundred years hence... **no man knoweth of such things; for he cannot tell of things to come**" (v7). But "the Lord God poured in his Spirit into my soul, insomuch that I did confound him" (v8). The exchange: "Deniest thou the Christ?" — "**If there should be a Christ, I would not deny him; but I know that there is no Christ, neither has been, nor ever will be**" (v9). "Believest thou the scriptures?" — "Yea." — "**Then ye do not understand them; for they truly testify of Christ**" (v11). Sherem: "**Show me a sign** by this power of the Holy Ghost" (v13). Jacob: "What am I that I should **tempt God to show unto thee a sign in the thing which thou knowest to be true**?... Nevertheless, not my will be done; but if God shall smite thee, let that be a sign... And thy will, O Lord, be done, and not mine" (v14). Sherem "fell to the earth" (v15), then before the gathered people "**confessed the Christ**, and the power of the Holy Ghost, and the ministering of angels" (v17): "I have been deceived by the power of the devil" (v18); "**I fear lest I have committed the unpardonable sin, for I have lied unto God; for I denied the Christ**" (v19); and "he gave up the ghost" (v20). "Peace and the love of God was restored... and they searched the scriptures" (v23). Jacob's close: "we being a **lonesome and a solemn people, wanderers, cast out from Jerusalem, born in tribulation, in a wilderness, and hated of our brethren... wherefore, we did mourn out our days**" (v26). He gives the plates to his son Enos. "**Brethren, adieu**" (v27).

**Thoughts:**
- *"I could not be shaken" (v5) — witness outlasts rhetoric.* Sherem has every rhetorical advantage: learning, flattery, "much power of speech." What he cannot touch is Jacob's anchor — not an argument but an *experience*: "I truly had seen angels... I had heard the voice of the Lord speaking unto me in very word." You cannot out-talk a man out of what he has actually encountered. Sherem's eloquence works on "many hearts" (v3), but it breaks on the one man who has met God directly. The defense against the persuasive skeptic is not a better debater; it is a real encounter.
- *Sherem the template (vv2-13).* He is the first named anti-Christ, and his pattern recurs (Nehor, Korihor in Alma): *learned*, *flattering*, denies Christ, claims "no man can know the future" (v7), and demands a *sign* (v13). It is a coherent posture — flatter the people, deny what cannot be empirically shown, and dare the believer to prove it. Jacob's rebuttal is the discernment standard (2 Ne 18:20, "to the law and to the testimony"): "Believest thou the scriptures?" — "Then ye do not understand them; for they truly testify of Christ." The skeptic claimed the scriptures; the prophet showed he had not read them as they are.
- *The sign refused, then given (vv14-15).* Jacob will not "tempt God to show... a sign in the thing which thou knowest to be true." His diagnosis is sharp: Sherem already *knew* it was true ("the thing which thou knowest to be true") and denied it anyway — sign-seeking born of dishonest denial, not honest doubt. So Jacob does not perform; he submits — "not my will be done... thy will, O Lord, be done." The sign comes by God's hand, not the prophet's showmanship. (The exact inverse of priestcraft: Jacob refuses to make himself a sign-worker for an audience.)
- *The deceiver becomes the witness (vv17-19).* Struck down, Sherem gathers the people he misled and undoes it — confessing "the Christ, and the power of the Holy Ghost, and the ministering of angels," the very three he denied. His terror is precise: "I have lied unto God... I denied the Christ, and said that I believed the scriptures; and they truly testify of him." His sin was not unbelief but *lying against known truth* — which is why he fears the unpardonable sin. The most honest words in the chapter are the dying confession of the man who built a career on denial.
- *The book ends in mourning (vv26-27).* Jacob does not close in triumph though the truth prevailed. He closes in melancholy: "a lonesome and a solemn people, wanderers, cast out from Jerusalem, born in tribulation... and hated of our brethren... we did mourn out our days." This is the exile's grief — a generation born far from home, hated by kin, who "mourned out" their lives. And the final word, "**adieu**," is the French *à Dieu* — "to God." Whether one reads it as Joseph Smith's 1829 idiom or weighs the curiosity, the sense fits the whole book: a wanderer commending his weary brethren, and his record, *to God*.

**Brief Jacob synthesis (book complete):** Jacob's small book runs from the watchman's burden (1:19) through the temple sermon on pride and chastity (2-3, where the despised Lamanites are "more righteous"), the law-points-to-Christ teaching and "looking beyond the mark" (4), Zenos's olive-tree allegory of the weeping vinedresser (5), its merciful application (6), and the Sherem confrontation (7). Its spine is **plainness against deception** — Jacob glories in plainness, the people lose it by "looking beyond the mark," and Sherem weaponizes eloquence against it; the answer throughout is the unshakeable witness of one who has *seen* and the relentless mercy of the God who "stretches forth his hands all the day long." The book that opened with a steward who answers the people's sins on his own head closes with that steward, old and lonesome, commending them "to God."

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off — **closes Jacob (7/7)**. The chapter introduces **the anti-Christ template** (Sherem: learned, flattering, denies Christ, claims no one can know the future, demands a sign — the pattern Korihor and Nehor follow), and Jacob's anchor against it: **"I could not be shaken"** because "I truly had seen angels... and heard the voice of the Lord" — witness outlasts rhetoric. The sign is **refused-then-given** (Jacob won't perform; God smites), and the deceiver **becomes a witness** on his deathbed, confessing the unpardonable sin of *lying against known truth*. The book ends in the exile's **mourning** ("a lonesome and a solemn people, wanderers... we did mourn out our days") and the tender "**adieu**" (à Dieu, "to God").
