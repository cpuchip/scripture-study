# Jacob 6 — "He Stretches Forth His Hands All the Day Long"

*(Jacob applies Zenos's allegory — mercy, urgency, the strait gate, farewell. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Having recited the allegory of the weeping vinedresser (ch 5), Jacob draws the application. What does the laboring, outstretched hand of the gardener *mean* for a stiffnecked people — and what does Jacob ask of them today?

**Read:** Zenos's prophecy "must surely come to pass" (v1); the day God "set his hand again the second time to recover his people, is... the last time that the servants of the Lord shall go forth in his power, to nourish and prune his vineyard; and after that the end soon cometh" (v2). "How blessed are they who have labored diligently in his vineyard; and how cursed are they who shall be cast out... and the world shall be burned with fire" (v3). Then the application: "**How merciful is our God unto us, for he remembereth the house of Israel, both roots and branches; and he stretches forth his hands unto them all the day long; and they are a stiffnecked and a gainsaying people; but as many as will not harden their hearts shall be saved**" (v4). "**Cleave unto God as he cleaveth unto you. And while his arm of mercy is extended towards you in the light of the day, harden not your hearts**" (v5). "**Yea, today, if ye will hear his voice, harden not your hearts; for why will ye die?**" (v6). "After ye have been nourished by the good word of God all the day long, will ye bring forth evil fruit?" (v7) — "will ye... deny the good word of Christ, and the power of God, and the gift of the Holy Ghost, and quench the Holy Spirit, and **make a mock of the great plan of redemption**?" (v8). Else "ye must go away into that lake of fire and brimstone... endless torment" (v10). "O then, my beloved brethren, repent ye, and **enter in at the strait gate, and continue in the way which is narrow**, until ye shall obtain eternal life" (v11). "O be wise; what can I say more?" (v12). "I bid you farewell, until I shall meet you before the **pleasing bar of God, which bar striketh the wicked with awful dread and fear**" (v13).

**Thoughts:**
- *The allegory's hand, named as mercy (v4).* Chapter 5 ended with the Lord of the vineyard who "stretched forth mine hand almost all the day long" (5:47) — the laboring hand. Jacob now applies it: "he stretches forth his hands unto them all the day long; and they are a stiffnecked and a gainsaying people." The exhausting labor of the gardener *is* mercy toward people who keep refusing. This is the verse where the long Isaiah-block refrain ("his hand is stretched out") and the allegory's labor finally resolve into one plain word: **mercy**. (And it echoes Isaiah 65:2 / Romans 10:21 almost exactly — "all day long I have stretched forth my hands unto a disobedient and gainsaying people.") The outstretched hand was never primarily wrath; it was a God reaching all day toward people who would not take it.
- *Cleave as he cleaves (v5).* "Cleave unto God as he cleaveth unto you." The relationship is not initiated by us — he is *already* cleaving — but it must be answered: cleave back. And the window is "the light of the day," "while his arm of mercy is extended." The mutual cleaving is the response the allegory's labor invites: he has done the digging and pruning; the vine's part is to hold to the One holding it.
- *"Today... why will ye die?" (v6).* Jacob lands on the urgency of Psalm 95 / Hebrews 3-4: "today, if ye will hear his voice, harden not your hearts." The decision is not deferrable; the day is now. And the closing question is tender exasperation, not threat: "why will ye die?" (Ezek 18:31). A God reaching all day long does not understand why anyone would choose the death he is laboring to prevent.
- *The catalog of rejection (vv7-8).* The worst possible response to the weeping gardener: after being "nourished by the good word of God all the day long," to "deny the good word of Christ, and the power of God, and the gift of the Holy Ghost, and quench the Holy Spirit, and make a mock of the great plan of redemption." Each verb escalates — deny, quench, mock. To mock the plan of redemption is to spit at the hand still reaching. That is the evil fruit a nourished vine should be incapable of.
- *The gate restated (v11), and the pleasing bar (v13).* Jacob's altar call is the doctrine of Christ in miniature — "enter in at the strait gate, and continue in the way which is narrow" (2 Ne 31:17-20, the gate and the path). And his farewell names the judgment with the same double edge as the stone of stumbling (2 Ne 18:14): "the **pleasing** bar of God, which bar striketh the **wicked** with awful dread." The very bar that is pleasing to the righteous is terror to the wicked — the same judgment, opposite experience, decided by what you did with the hand that reached all day. (Moroni will take up "the pleasing bar," Moroni 10:34.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off (it's the application of the ch 5 allegory; the deep study covers the allegory itself). The chapter's payoff: **the laboring hand of the allegory (5:47) is named, plainly, as mercy** — "he stretches forth his hands unto them all the day long; and they are a stiffnecked and a gainsaying people" (v4) — resolving the whole Isaiah-block "hand stretched out" refrain into one word. The asks are **cleave-as-he-cleaves** (v5) and **"today, harden not your hearts"** (v6), and the gate is the doctrine-of-Christ gate (v11). The **pleasing-yet-dreadful bar** (v13) is the stone-of-stumbling judgment again — same bar, opposite experience.
