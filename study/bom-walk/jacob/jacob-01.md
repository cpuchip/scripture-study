# Jacob 1 — The Watchman's Burden: "Answering the Sins of the People Upon Our Own Heads"

*(The Book of Jacob begins. Jacob takes the small plates; Nephi dies; wickedness creeps in. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Jacob inherits the plates and the priesthood — and with them a burden most teachers would rather not name: the people's sins, if he fails to teach, fall partly on his own head. What does it mean to "magnify the office" under that weight?

**Read:** Fifty-five years after Lehi left Jerusalem, "Nephi gave me, Jacob, a commandment concerning the small plates" (v1): write "a few of the things which I considered to be **most precious**," touching the history only lightly (the large plates carry that, vv2-3); engrave "the heads" of sacred preaching, great revelation, and prophecy, "as much as it were possible, **for Christ's sake, and for the sake of our people**" (v4). "We knew of Christ and his kingdom, which should come" (v6); "we labored diligently among our people, that we might **persuade them to come unto Christ**... that they might enter into his rest" (v7); "we would to God that we could persuade all men... that all men would believe in Christ, **and view his death, and suffer his cross and bear the shame of the world**" (v8). Nephi, growing old, "anointed a man to be a king" (v9); the people, loving Nephi (who "wielded the sword of Laban in their defence," v10), kept his name — kings were called "**second Nephi, third Nephi, and so forth**" (v11). "Nephi died" (v12). The people split into seven tribal names (v13), but Jacob simplifies: "I shall call them **Lamanites that seek to destroy the people of Nephi, and those who are friendly to Nephi I shall call Nephites**" (v14). Under the second king they "began to grow hard in their hearts, and indulge themselves... like unto David of old desiring many wives and concubines, and also Solomon" (v15), and to seek gold and "be lifted up somewhat in pride" (v16). So Jacob taught in the temple, "having first obtained mine errand from the Lord" (v17), he and Joseph "consecrated priests and teachers" by Nephi (v18). "And we did magnify our office unto the Lord, taking upon us the responsibility, **answering the sins of the people upon our own heads if we did not teach them the word of God with all diligence**; wherefore, by laboring with our might their blood might not come upon our garments" (v19).

**Thoughts:**
- *The watchman's burden (v19).* Jacob names the weight every true steward carries: "answering the sins of the people upon our own heads if we did not teach them the word of God with all diligence." He is not accountable for the people's *choices* — but he is accountable for his *diligence*. If he fails to teach, "their blood would come upon our garments." This is Ezekiel's watchman (Ezek 33:1-9, 34:10) in Nephite form, and it is the exact inverse of the priestcraft of 2 Ne 26:29: the priestcrafter seeks gain and praise and does *not* seek Zion's welfare; the true priest "labors with [his] might" precisely *for* the people's welfare, at the cost of bearing their blood if he slacks. The office is magnified not by status but by responsibility shouldered.
- *Curation for Christ's sake (vv2-4).* Jacob inherits Nephi's small-plates principle (1 Ne 6:4-6): only "the most precious" — the sacred preaching, the great revelation, the prophecy — and all of it "for Christ's sake." The history goes on the large plates; the small plates are reserved for what points to Christ. The selection criterion is Christ-ward worth, not completeness. (Which is why these chapters are so doctrine-dense: they were curated to be.)
- *"View his death, and suffer his cross, and bear the shame" (v8).* Jacob's plea is not merely "believe in Christ" but believe *and* "view his death, and suffer his cross and bear the shame of the world." Discipleship includes the shame — taking up the cross, not just admiring it. The belief Jacob wants is the kind that follows Christ into the contempt of the world.
- *Division by allegiance, not blood (vv13-14).* Seven tribal names exist, but Jacob collapses them to a functional binary: Lamanites = "those who seek to destroy the people of Nephi"; Nephites = "those who are friendly to Nephi." The category is *posture*, not pure lineage — the same division-by-response that ran through 2 Ne 5 and that 2 Ne 26:33 ("all are alike unto God") and 30:2 (covenant by repentance) make explicit. "Lamanite" here is nearly a moral-political term, not a genetic one.
- *The name retained (v11).* The people loved Nephi enough that every successor king was called "second Nephi, third Nephi" — the founder's name became the throne-name. A small note on how a good leader's name outlives him, becoming the title others wear. (And a quiet contrast with the pride creeping in by v16 — the name is kept while the substance erodes.)
