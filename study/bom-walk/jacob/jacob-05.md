# Jacob 5 — The Allegory of the Olive Tree: The Vinedresser Who Wept

*(Zenos's allegory, quoted by Jacob — the longest chapter in the Book of Mormon, 77 verses. Mini-study; **deep spin-off in progress** → `studies/jacob5_allegory-of-the-olive-tree.md`.)*

**Binding idea:** The allegory likens Israel to a decaying olive tree and God to a vinedresser who keeps coming back to save it. At the center, he weeps. What does the weeping Master add to Isaiah's "what could be done more?"

**Read:** Jacob quotes "the prophet Zenos" (v1): "I will liken thee, O house of Israel, like unto a tame olive tree, which a man took and nourished in his vineyard; and it grew, and waxed old, and began to decay" (v3). The master prunes, digs, nourishes (vv4-5); but "the main top thereof began to perish" (v6). "**It grieveth me that I should lose this tree**," he says, and grafts in "branches from a wild olive tree" while the natural branches are scattered — "I will place [them] in the nethermost part of my vineyard... that I may preserve unto myself the natural branches" (vv7-14). Across the long allegory the master returns again and again, grafting, transplanting, nourishing. Then the grief-climax: "**the Lord of the vineyard wept, and said unto the servant: What could I have done more for my vineyard?**" (v41). "But what could I have done more in my vineyard? **Have I slackened mine hand, that I have not nourished it? Nay**... I have nourished it, and I have digged about it, and I have pruned it, and I have dunged it; and I have **stretched forth mine hand almost all the day long**, and the end draweth nigh... Who is it that has corrupted my vineyard?" (v47). Finally, the last labor: the servant brings "other servants; and they were few" (v70); "Go to, and labor in the vineyard, with your might. For behold, **this is the last time** that I shall nourish my vineyard... if ye labor with your might **with me** ye shall have joy" (v71); "the Lord of the vineyard **labored also with them**" (v72); the natural fruit returns, the bad is cast away, "they became like unto one body" (v74); "behold ye shall have **joy with me** because of the fruit of my vineyard" (v75).

**Thoughts:**
- *The Master weeps (v41) — Isaiah 5 with tears.* Back in 2 Ne 15:4 (Isaiah 5:4) the owner of the vineyard asked "What could have been done more to my vineyard that I have not done in it?" — a courtroom question, an indictment of the vine. Zenos's allegory gives the *same line* (v41) but with three words that change everything: "the Lord of the vineyard **wept**." The God who reasoned the case in Isaiah is here shown crying over the result. The question is no longer only "whose fault?" but "how it grieves me." Five times across the chapter he says "it grieveth me that I should lose this tree" (vv7, 11, 13, 46, 47). The allegory's whole emotional register is a gardener's heartbreak.
- *The hand stretched out all the day long (v47) — the third meaning of the refrain.* The Isaiah-block refrain "his hand is stretched out still" was first judgment (2 Ne 15:25; 19:12), then comfort (22:1), then sovereign decree (24:27). Here it gets its warmest sense: "I have stretched forth mine hand almost all the day long" — the *laboring* hand of the vinedresser, digging and pruning and dunging without rest. The stretched-out hand is finally a working hand, callused from saving a tree that keeps decaying. God's relentlessness is not wrath held back; it is labor that will not quit.
- *"With me" — the shared labor and the shared joy (vv71-75).* The last gathering is not the master working alone or the servants working alone: "if ye labor with your might **with me**" (v71), "the Lord of the vineyard labored **also with them**" (v72), "ye shall have **joy with me**" (v75). The Master does not delegate the final labor from a distance; he gets into the dirt alongside the few servants. And the joy is not given *to* them but shared *with* them — the same "with" that runs through Immanuel, "God with us." The redemption of the vineyard ends with God and his laborers shoulder to shoulder, and the harvest-joy held in common.
- *The whole history in one tree.* Zenos compresses the entire arc of scattering and gathering into one gardener's seasons: the tame tree decaying (apostasy), the wild branches grafted (the Gentiles), the natural branches hidden in the nethermost parts (scattered Israel), the returns (the dispensations), and the last labor with few servants (the latter-day gathering). It is the same scatter-and-gather Nephi and Jacob keep teaching (1 Ne 10:12-14; 15:12-18), but rendered as a story you can feel — and the feeling is grief turning to joy.
- *"Who is it that has corrupted my vineyard?" (v47).* The Master's anguished question has no easy answer in the text — the corruption spread despite perfect care. The allegory refuses to let the gardener's diligence be the variable (he "slackened" nothing). The mystery of why a fully-tended vineyard goes wild is left standing, the same unresolved grief as the Song of the Vineyard — but here the gardener does not abandon it; he weeps and digs again.
