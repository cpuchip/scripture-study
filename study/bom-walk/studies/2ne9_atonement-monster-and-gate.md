# 2 Nephi 9 — The Monster and the Gate

**Binding question:** How does Jacob's framing of death and hell as an "awful monster" structure the Atonement as an act of *escape* — and why does he move from cosmic deliverance (vv. 6–13) straight into the woes and "the keeper of the gate" (vv. 25–41), as though the two halves of the sermon belong to the same argument?

They do. That is the discovery this chapter makes.

---

## I. What Jacob Is Doing Here

Jacob opens by telling us why he has been reading Isaiah to his people: "that ye might know concerning the covenants of the Lord that he has covenanted with all the house of Israel" ([2 Ne. 9:1](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/9.md)). He is not giving a theology lecture. He is giving a people who expect to be scattered — whose children will grow up far from Jerusalem — the framework they need to understand that what is coming to them is not abandonment.

He tells them plainly why he is being hard on them: "if ye were holy I would speak unto you of holiness; but as ye are not holy, and ye look upon me as a teacher, it must needs be expedient that I teach you the consequences of sin" (v. 48). Jacob knows his congregation. He is also afraid of his congregation — afraid they will waste the thing they have been given. That pastoral pressure runs under the whole sermon.

The chapter closes where it opened: "behold how great the covenants of the Lord" (v. 53). Same phrase, same subject, both ends of the sermon. Jacob built a ring. The argument in between the two covenant declarations is the substance of why the covenants matter at all.

---

## II. The Monster

The argument begins with a chain. Jacob runs it deliberately:

> "For as death hath passed upon all men, to fulfil the merciful plan of the great Creator, there must needs be a power of resurrection, and the resurrection must needs come unto man by reason of the fall; and the fall came by reason of transgression; and because man became fallen they were cut off from the presence of the Lord." ([2 Ne. 9:6](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/9.md))

Death → Fall → Transgression → Separation from God. The chain is airtight. And the next verse draws the necessary implication:

> "Wherefore, it must needs be an infinite atonement—save it should be an infinite atonement this corruption could not put on incorruption. Wherefore, the first judgment which came upon man must needs have remained to an endless duration. And if so, this flesh must have laid down to rot and to crumble to its mother earth, to rise no more." (v. 7)

Jacob runs the contrafactual. What if there is no atonement? The argument is not sentimental — it is logical. If the first judgment is permanent, the body stays dead. Fine, we might say; the spirit survives. But Jacob will not let us stop there:

> "O the wisdom of God, his mercy and grace! For behold, if the flesh should rise no more our spirits must become subject to that angel who fell from before the presence of the Eternal God, and became the devil, to rise no more." (v. 8)

And then the full consequence:

> "And our spirits must have become like unto him, and we become devils, angels to a devil, to be shut out from the presence of our God, and to remain with the father of lies, in misery, like unto himself." (v. 9)

"Like unto him." This is the deepest claim in the chapter. Jacob is not describing what happens to bad people after judgment. He is describing what would have happened to *everyone*, structurally, without an Atonement. The endpoint of the Fall — left to run without interruption — is not human death. It is a transformation of human nature into the nature of the devil. Not punishment. Identity.

That is the monster.

> "O how great the goodness of our God, who prepareth a way for our escape from the grasp of this awful monster; yea, that monster, death and hell, which I call the death of the body, and also the death of the spirit." (v. 10)

Jacob names what he is naming. The monster is death of body plus death of spirit together — not one or the other but the compound grip of both. And the word he uses for what God provides is not *victory* over the monster. It is *escape* from its grasp. The monster is still real. The escape route is provided. The question for the rest of the chapter is whether the listener will take it.

---

## III. The Two Exclamations

The chapter's argument is punctuated throughout with cries of praise — vv. 8, 13, 17, 19, 20 all break into exclamation. Two of them are paired in a way that repays attention.

The first: "O how great the goodness of our God, who prepareth a way for our escape" (v. 10). The attribute is *goodness*. The act is preparation of a way. God's goodness shows itself in providing the escape route.

The second: "O how great the holiness of our God! For he knoweth all things, and there is not anything save he knows it" (v. 20). The attribute is *holiness*. Then immediately: "he suffereth the pains of all men, yea, the pains of every living creature, both men, women, and children, who belong to the family of Adam" (v. 21).

Holiness grounds omniscience, and omniscience makes the Atonement possible. God can suffer for every person because he knows every person — knows their particular pain, their specific weight. The first exclamation tells us what God does (provides escape). The second tells us *what makes it possible* (holiness that sees and knows everything). They are not repetition. They are two different claims about two different divine attributes working together in the same act.

---

## IV. The Reversal

The monster has two halves — the grave holds the body, hell holds the spirit. Jacob traces the reversal of both:

> "death and hell must deliver up their dead, and hell must deliver up its captive spirits, and the grave must deliver up its captive bodies, and the bodies and the spirits of men will be restored one to the other; and it is by the power of the resurrection of the Holy One of Israel." (v. 12)

Both deaths reverse. The monster has to let go. But then Jacob says something that sets up everything that follows:

> "all men become incorruptible, and immortal, and they are living souls, having a perfect knowledge like unto us in the flesh, save it be that our knowledge shall be perfect." (v. 13)

And: "we shall have a perfect knowledge of all our guilt, and our uncleanness, and our nakedness; and the righteous shall have a perfect knowledge of their enjoyment, and their righteousness, being clothed with purity, yea, even with the robe of righteousness" (v. 14).

Perfect knowledge at the judgment bar. This is not God reviewing a file. This is persons remembering themselves — completely, with nothing suppressed or softened. The guilty stand naked (Genesis 3's nakedness after the Fall, still unresolved). The righteous are reclothed. Restoration brings perfect knowledge, which means the judgment is less a verdict announced from outside than a recognition from within. Whatever we have become, we will know it perfectly.

This is why the woes are not a change of subject.

---

## V. The Pivot

The Atonement covers those who have no law:

> "where there is no law given there is no punishment; and where there is no punishment there is no condemnation; and where there is no condemnation the mercies of the Holy One of Israel have claim upon them, because of the atonement; for they are delivered by the power of him." (v. 25)

> "For the atonement satisfieth the demands of his justice upon all those who have not the law given to them, that they are delivered from that awful monster, death and hell, and the devil, and the lake of fire and brimstone, which is endless torment." (v. 26)

The Atonement covers those without law completely. Which means the person *with* the law — "yea, that has all the commandments of God, like unto us" (v. 27) — is in a different situation. Not a less-covered situation, but a more accountable one. The escape route is available. To waste the probationary days knowing it is available is, Jacob says, to be in an "awful state."

"Probation" in Jacob's vocabulary is not a penal term. It is the testing period — the days given to prove character, to become something. Webster's 1828 defines it as "Moral trial; the state of man in the present life, in which he has the opportunity of proving his character and being qualified for a happier state. Probation will end with the present life." To waste the probation is to use up the proving time without proving anything.

*(Requoted 2026-06-12 — the earlier wording ("proving his character, and becoming qualified for a happier state") was the 1913 edition's; the genuine 1828 reads "proving his character and being qualified" and adds "Probation will end with the present life." The point — probation as the proving time — holds either way.)*

The pivot is the word "but": "But wo unto him that has the law given" (v. 27). The cosmic deliverance is real. Therefore the failure to receive it by someone who knows about it is inexcusable. The two halves of the sermon are the same argument.

---

## VI. The Woes

Jacob pronounces ten woes, running from verse 27 to verse 38. They are not a random list. A pattern moves through them.

The first four woes describe a posture of *closure* toward the escape:

- v. 27: the law-possessor who wastes the probation
- v. 30: the rich, whose hearts are on their treasure ("their treasure is their god")
- v. 31: the deaf who will not hear
- v. 32: the blind who will not see

These are not people who rejected and fought against the gospel. They are people whose attention went elsewhere. Their treasure, their silence, their closed eyes are what foreclosed the escape.

Then Jacob names specific sins — the uncircumcised of heart (v. 33), the liar (v. 34), the murderer (v. 35), those who commit whoredoms (v. 36), idolaters (v. 37). Each receives a one- or two-sentence verdict. The last woe catches everyone the others miss:

> "And, in fine, wo unto all those who die in their sins; for they shall return to God, and behold his face, and remain in their sins." (v. 38)

The final consequence is not fire, though fire appears elsewhere in the chapter. The final consequence is this: they see God. Face-to-face. And they remain in their sins. Whatever the wicked have become — the liar, the idolater, the person whose treasure was their god — they will know it perfectly (v. 14 again) and they will meet the one who could have changed it. Jacob does not need to describe further.

One woe sits apart from the rest and deserves attention:

> "O that cunning plan of the evil one! O the vainness, and the frailties, and the foolishness of men! When they are learned they think they are wise, and they hearken not unto the counsel of God, for they set it aside, supposing they know of themselves, wherefore, their wisdom is foolishness and it profiteth them not. And they shall perish." (v. 28)

> "But to be learned is good if they hearken unto the counsels of God." (v. 29)

The cunning plan is not to make people wicked. It is to make people confident. Learning is the door to counseling God rather than hearkening to him. The corrective is a single clause. The diagnosis takes the whole of verse 28. Jacob does not hate learning — his own sermon is a demonstration of sophisticated argument. He hates learning that closes rather than opens, that sets aside rather than receives.

---

## VII. Carnally-Minded, Spiritually-Minded

Between the woes and the gate, Jacob places a summary:

> "O, my beloved brethren, remember the awfulness in transgressing against that Holy God, and also the awfulness of yielding to the enticings of that cunning one. Remember, to be carnally-minded is death, and to be spiritually-minded is life eternal." (v. 39)

The echo of Paul (Romans 8:6 carries the same construction — Webster's 1828 cites it as the natural gloss for "carnally-minded") is not accidental. Jacob is working in a tradition. But his formulation is precise: "carnally-minded" and "spiritually-minded" are orientation words, not single-act words. They describe a direction of attention. The woes trace what a carnally-minded orientation produces over time. The gate is what awaits a spiritually-minded one.

---

## VIII. The Keeper of the Gate

> "the way for man is narrow, but it lieth in a straight course before him, and the keeper of the gate is the Holy One of Israel; and he employeth no servant there; and there is none other way save it be by the gate; for he cannot be deceived, for the Lord God is his name." (v. 41)

The most striking clause is the middle one. "He employeth no servant there." Think of who would most expect a servant at the gate — the wealthy, the connected, the learned, those with credentials and influence. Jacob's next verse closes the argument:

> "And whoso knocketh, to him will he open; and the wise, and the learned, and they that are rich, who are puffed up because of their learning, and their wisdom, and their riches—yea, they are they whom he despiseth; and save they shall cast these things away, and consider themselves fools before God, and come down in the depths of humility, he will not open unto them." (v. 42)

The gate has one door. The same Christ who provided the escape from the monster stands at its end. He cannot be deceived — the learned cannot out-argue him, the rich cannot buy a servant's admission, the connected have no connection that reaches there. The chapter's argument closes on the same figure it began with: "the Holy One of Israel," who prepared a way of escape (v. 10), who suffers the pains of all men (v. 21), who provides deliverance for those without law (v. 26), and who stands at the end of the narrow path to open it — or not — himself.

---

## IX. "Come, My Brethren"

Jacob does not end with the gate closed. After the woes and the keeper, he turns:

> "Come, my brethren, every one that thirsteth, come ye to the waters; and he that hath no money, come buy and eat; yea, come buy wine and milk without money and without price." (v. 50)

> "Wherefore, do not spend money for that which is of no worth, nor your labor for that which cannot satisfy. Hearken diligently unto me, and remember the words which I have spoken; and come unto the Holy One of Israel, and feast upon that which perisheth not, neither can be corrupted, and let your soul delight in fatness." (v. 51)

[Isaiah 55:1–2](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/55.md) is the source:

> "Ho, every one that thirsteth, come ye to the waters, and he that hath no money; come ye, buy, and eat; yea, come, buy wine and milk without money and without price." (Isa. 55:1)

> "Wherefore do ye spend money for that which is not bread? and your labour for that which satisfieth not? hearken diligently unto me, and eat ye that which is good, and let your soul delight itself in fatness." (Isa. 55:2)

Jacob's version closely mirrors Isaiah's. What he adds is the name: "come unto the Holy One of Israel." After fifty verses in which that name has been repeated across both the cosmic argument and the woes — the one who delivers from the monster, the one who stands at the gate — Jacob inserts it into the invitation. Isaiah's universal "Ho" becomes Jacob's personal "Come, my brethren." The invitation has a face now. And it opens the same gate the woes have been warning about.

The chapter opened with covenants. It closes with covenants: "how great the covenants of the Lord, and how great his condescensions unto the children of men" (v. 53). Jacob has run the whole argument — the Fall's endpoint without Atonement, the Atonement's scope, the judgment that perfect knowledge brings, the woes that trace what a closed posture produces — and arrived back at the covenants. He read Isaiah to explain the covenants. The explanation has done its work.

---

## Becoming

The woe that lands most personally is v. 28 — the cunning plan. Not wickedness. Confidence. The devil's best move against a person who studies scripture is not to make them immoral; it is to make them expert. To make learning itself the reason for not hearkening. The person who has read enough to feel certain they have understood correctly and need no further counsel.

Jacob's single-clause corrective ("to be learned is good if they hearken") is not an argument against learning. It is a structural check: does my knowledge produce more listening, or less? Do I approach the text to discover, or to confirm? Do I bring my conclusions to God, or ask for his?

The keeper of the gate employs no servant there. No credential, no familiarity with the arguments, no years of study gets a person through. What gets a person through is what Jacob said at verse 42: coming down in the depths of humility, considering oneself a fool before God. That is the posture. Not ignorance — Jacob is not celebrating ignorance — but a kind of perpetual receptivity that learning must not close off.

A practical test: in study and in prayer both, notice which mode dominates. Am I bringing conclusions to be ratified, or questions to be answered? The two produce very different conversations.
