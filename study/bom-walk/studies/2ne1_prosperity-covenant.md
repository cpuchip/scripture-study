# The Prosperity Covenant

**Binding question:** What is the prosperity covenant, how does it run through the whole Book of Mormon, and what does it actually promise (and not promise)?

*Bin-1/2 draft — exploratory, verified, for Michael's ratification.*

---

## I. The Fountainhead

The covenant is born in a private conversation. Nephi is alone in his tent somewhere in the wilderness south of Jerusalem, still a teenager, crying to God because his brothers won't listen. The Lord answers — and in that answer, plants the formula that will run for the next thousand pages:

> "And inasmuch as ye shall keep my commandments, ye shall prosper, and shall be led to a land of promise; yea, even a land which I have prepared for you; yea, a land which is choice above all other lands." ([1 Nephi 2:20](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/2.md))

Then, a separate verse addressed to Laman and Lemuel:

> "And inasmuch as thy brethren shall rebel against thee, they shall be cut off from the presence of the Lord." ([1 Nephi 2:21](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/2.md))

Notice what the text does not do: it does not fuse these into a single biconditional. The positive side — prosperity and the promised land — is Nephi's promise. The negative side — cut off from the Lord's presence — is his brothers' warning. Two separate utterances, two separate subjects, two different consequences.

This asymmetry matters. "Prosper and be led to a land" is spatial and material. "Cut off from the presence of the Lord" is relational and spiritual. The Lord is naming two different things, not one thing in positive and negative forms. The mature formulation, arriving in 2 Nephi 1:20, will fuse them into a single conditional — but the fountainhead keeps them distinct, because they are distinct.

---

## II. The Chain

Within a few years, Lehi is dying. He gathers his sons and delivers the formula that will become the canonical form:

> "And he hath said that: Inasmuch as ye shall keep my commandments ye shall prosper in the land; but inasmuch as ye will not keep my commandments ye shall be cut off from my presence." ([2 Nephi 1:20](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/1.md))

Two things have changed. First, Lehi says "he hath said that" — he is quoting. The covenant has already become something other than a live oracle; it is now a received promise, a tradition that can be transmitted. Second, the wording has stabilized: "prosper IN the land" / "cut off FROM my presence." The prepositions preserve the distinction from the fountainhead. Prosperity is spatial, located *in* the land. Removal is relational, separation *from* a person.

Lehi also gives an earlier, broader form in verse 9 — not just to his own family, but to "those whom the Lord God shall bring out of the land of Jerusalem." The covenant's scope is already larger than one bloodline.

Then Lehi turns to Zoram (v.31), the former servant of Laban who joined the family, and speaks words that invert the threat: "nothing, save it shall be iniquity among them, shall harm or disturb their prosperity." The only thing that can break it is internal. External enemies cannot override the covenant if the people are keeping it. The threat is not outside; it comes from within.

The chain continues. When Lehi is on the verge of death and needs to reach even Laman's children, he quotes it again (2 Nephi 4:4) in almost identical words. It has by now become portable — a formula that travels from one generation to the next, from patriarch to child. By the time Alma confronts the hardened people of Ammonihah around 82 B.C., he uses it as a historical fact:

> "Behold, do ye not remember the words which he spake unto Lehi, saying that: Inasmuch as ye shall keep my commandments, ye shall prosper in the land?" ([Alma 9:13](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/9.md))

Alma is not teaching doctrine. He is arguing from verified history: *look at the Lamanites — they were cut off "from the beginning of their transgressions in the land." The word has already been verified.* The pivot that makes it pointed: because the Nephites have received more light than the Lamanites, their violation of the covenant will be worse, not better (Alma 9:15, 23). Greater covenant privilege means greater accountability, not more protection.

King Benjamin's treatment in Mosiah 2 expands the covenant in two directions. Upward: "all that he requires of you is to keep his commandments; and he has promised you that if ye would keep his commandments ye should prosper in the land; and he never doth vary from that which he hath said" (v.22). The reliability of the covenant is grounded in the unchangeability of God. Downward: the "cut off" side becomes phenomenological — the person who rebels "doth cause him to shrink from the presence of the Lord, and doth fill his breast with guilt, and pain, and anguish, which is like an unquenchable fire" (v.38). The cutting off is not only divine action; it is the sinner's own soul recoiling from the presence it has made itself unfit for.

The Alma 50 instance is Mormon's editorial retrospective, the most self-aware use of the formula in the whole book. Standing at a moment of extraordinary Nephite prosperity — "there never was a happier time among the people of Nephi, since the days of Nephi, than in the days of Moroni" (v.23) — Mormon quotes the covenant and then gives its interpretive key: "it has been their quarrelings and their contentions, yea, their murderings, and their plunderings... which were among themselves, which brought upon them their wars and their destructions" (v.21). The source of Nephite calamity is internal, not external. The Lamanites are a scourge, but what summons the scourge is Nephite covenant-breaking, not Lamanite military strength.

The formula shows up from Lehi to Alma to Helaman to the final pages of the war chapters and beyond. It is the hermeneutic lens the whole record uses to explain itself.

## III. The Jaredite Parallel

Ether 2 changes the register. The Jaredites predate Lehi's family by many centuries. They have no connection to the house of Israel. They are not Israelites; they have no Mosaic covenant; they carry no lineage claim. And yet the Lord delivers them to the same land on the same terms:

> "Whatsoever nation shall possess it shall serve God, or they shall be swept off when the fulness of his wrath shall come upon them." ([Ether 2:9](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/2.md))

> "This is a choice land, and whatsoever nation shall possess it shall be free from bondage, and from captivity, and from all other nations under heaven, if they will but serve the God of the land, who is Jesus Christ." ([Ether 2:12](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/2.md))

Three things are new here. First, the language is starker. Not "prosper" and "cut off from my presence" — but "serve" and "swept off." Physical destruction, not relational distance. This is the covenant's terminal form: what happens when the full arc is complete.

Second, the covenant is not ethnic. "Whatsoever nation" — Ether 2:9, 10, 12. The Jaredites are proof. The Canaanites in 1 Nephi 17 are proof. The Nephites will be proof. And then Moroni turns directly to the reader: "And this cometh unto you, O ye Gentiles, that ye may know the decrees of God" (v.11). He is addressing whatever nation will come to hold this land after the Nephites are gone. Moroni is explicitly applying the covenant to nineteenth-century America and beyond.

Third, verse 12 names the God of the land: "who is Jesus Christ." The covenant is not to generic theism. It is covenant allegiance to Christ specifically. The land is his land, held under his terms.

The Jaredite narrative then enacts the covenant perfectly. They flourish. They pride. They fracture. They are swept off entirely — not driven out, not scattered, but annihilated as a people. The covenant's threatened consequence, deferred across centuries, eventually comes full.

## IV. What "Prosper" Means — and the Trap

Webster 1828 gives "prosper" a crucial first sense: "to favor; to render successful." The subject is the Lord, not the recipient. To prosper is to be *made* successful by divine favor — an active grant, not a passive condition. This matters because it means prosperity is not self-generated. The prosperous person is the one whom God has chosen to render successful.

But then comes the trap, and Helaman 12 names it precisely:

> "Yea, and we may see at the very time when he doth prosper his people, yea, in the increase of their fields, their flocks and their herds, and in gold, and in silver... doing all things for the welfare and happiness of his people; yea, then is the time that they do harden their hearts, and do forget the Lord their God, and do trample under their feet the Holy One — yea, and this because of their ease, and their exceedingly great prosperity." ([Helaman 12:2](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/hel/12.md))

"At the very time" — not after many years. Synchronous. The prosperity is still arriving when the forgetting begins. The blessing is, in that same moment, the trigger of ingratitude.

Mormon names the mechanism: ease. Prosperity removes the friction that keeps people remembering. When survival is uncertain, the Lord's provision is visible. When it is not, the provision becomes invisible, then taken for granted, then forgotten. The covenant's reward is its own undoing — not by design, but by the anthropological weakness that prosperity exposes.

Helaman 3 shows the same pattern at a smaller scale, inside the church itself. At a moment of "exceedingly great prosperity in the church" (v.24-25), within two years "there was exceedingly great pride which had gotten into the hearts of the people; and it was because of their exceedingly great riches and their prosperity in the land; and it did grow upon them from day to day" (v.36). Mormon's precision: the pride entered "not into the church of God, but into the hearts of the people who professed to belong to the church of God" (v.33). Membership in the covenant community does not immunize against the pride trap. The covenant is not coextensive with the institution.

The counter-witness in Helaman 3 is the humble who, under persecution (not prosperity), "wax stronger and stronger in their humility, and firmer and firmer in the faith of Christ" (v.35). Adversity does what ease cannot: it keeps them in posture of dependence. The covenant's reward, if received as a gift rather than an entitlement, can sustain that posture. But it is not automatic.

## V. The Two Halves

The covenant has two sides, and they are not parallel forms of the same thing. They are two distinct realities that the covenant holds together.

**"Prosper in the land"** is temporal, communal, and spatial. It means crops, protection from enemies, freedom from captivity, national coherence. King Benjamin expands it to include "blessed in all things, both temporal and spiritual" (Mosiah 2:41), but the baseline meaning is social and material flourishing. The land itself participates — it is "consecrated" to the righteous (2 Nephi 1:7) and "cursed" against the wicked (1 Nephi 17:35). The land is not neutral terrain; it responds to covenant conditions.

**"Cut off from my presence"** is spiritual, personal, and relational. Webster 1828, on the sovereign sense of "presence," gives "Approach face to face or nearness of a great personage" and, plainly, "The person of a superior" (and even "the apartment in which a prince shows himself to his court") — access to the royal person, with all that implies: protection, attention, care, guidance, the whole of the relationship.

The two halves are not always sequential. They operate at different registers simultaneously. A person can be materially prosperous while being spiritually cut off — that is precisely what Helaman 12 describes in the moment of pride. And a person can be in God's presence, spiritually whole, while the national covenant around them collapses. Alma 50:22 holds both realities at once: "those who were faithful in keeping the commandments of the Lord were delivered at all times, whilst thousands of their wicked brethren have been consigned to bondage, or to perish by the sword." Individual covenant faithfulness does not guarantee exemption from communal consequences — but it does guarantee personal deliverance.

## VI. The Conditional-Land Theology

The covenant is not a gift to a people. It is a standing condition for any people on this land. Nephi makes this explicit in his address to Laman and Lemuel in 1 Nephi 17:

> "Do ye suppose that our fathers would have been more choice than they [the Canaanites] if they had been righteous? I say unto you, Nay." ([1 Nephi 17:34](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/17.md))

> "The Lord esteemeth all flesh in one; he that is righteous is favored of God." ([1 Nephi 17:35](../../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/17.md))

This is the anti-triumphalist core of the whole theology. If Lehi's family had been wicked, they would have been swept off like the Canaanites. If the Canaanites had been righteous, Lehi's family would not have displaced them. Righteousness is the only qualifying condition, and it applies to every people equally.

The Jaredites confirm it. Before Nephi, the Jaredites held the same land on the same terms — "whatsoever nation" — and when they fulfilled iniquity, they were swept off to the last person. The land is the constant. The covenanted people change as their righteousness changes.

The Canaanites in Genesis 15:16 (cross-referenced at Ether 2:9) are the type for all of this: God told Abraham he could not give the land yet, because the iniquity of the Amorites was not yet full. The Lord is patient; ripening takes time. But when it is complete, the covenant's judgment falls. The Jaredites, the Nephites, and the population that preceded them in the land all go the same way when they ripen in iniquity. The Canaanites were not uniquely wicked; they were uniquely ripe.

This theology is not triumphalist. It is self-implicating. Every people who reads these pages reads their own potential sentence.

---

## VII. The Hard Edge

The covenant is a *national and communal* covenant tied to a *specific land*. This is not primarily a promise to individuals about their personal financial trajectories. It does not promise that a righteous person will be materially comfortable, or that an unrighteous person will be poor. The unit of the covenant is a people in a place.

What can be said about individuals under the covenant? Alma 50:22 says faithful individuals "were delivered at all times" even when the national covenant was collapsing around them. This is personal deliverance from communal destruction — not prosperity, but preservation. The righteous can live inside a collapsing covenant community and still receive God's care. But they do not escape the communal consequences; they are delivered through them, not around them.

What can be said about modern nations? Moroni answers this directly in Ether 2:11 — addressing "ye Gentiles" — the inhabitants of this land in whatever age reads the record. The covenant's terms apply to them. The decrees are "everlasting" (Ether 2:10). They are not limited to Nephite or Jaredite history; they are constitutional for this land.

What the covenant does NOT promise, and this matters: it does not promise that God will protect a nation that claims his name but does not serve him. The Nephites made this mistake — assuming covenant identity (being called the people of God) provided covenant protection without covenant faithfulness. Alma 9:24 is unambiguous: "the promises of the Lord are extended to the Lamanites, but they are not unto you if ye transgress." The covenant follows faithfulness, not label.

These are the limits that must be named honestly. The covenant is real and its terms are reliable — "he never doth vary from that which he hath said" (Mosiah 2:22). But reliable in both directions. It is as reliable in warning as in promise.

---

## Becoming

The pride cycle in Helaman 12 is not abstract ancient history. Mormon describes "at the very time when he doth prosper his people... then is the time that they do harden their hearts." The trigger is ease — the removal of friction that keeps us remembering who provides.

The question for a prosperous person, in a prosperous nation, in a time of relative ease, is not whether the covenant applies. It is whether the ease has become invisible, whether the provision has stopped registering as provision. The covenant's warning is not addressed to the obviously wicked. It is addressed to the comfortable.

The posture that survives prosperity is not prosperity itself — it is the posture of Helaman 3:35's community, who "wax stronger and stronger in their humility" by deliberately returning to dependence: fasting, praying, yielding. Not because they are poor, but because they choose not to be comfortable in the way that forgets.

Benjamin's two-sided formulation deserves the last word: "blessed in all things, both temporal and spiritual; and if they hold out faithful to the end they are received into heaven, that thereby they may dwell with God in a state of never-ending happiness" (Mosiah 2:41). The covenant points beyond land tenure. It points toward presence — permanent, uninterrupted, the opposite of being cut off. That is what it has been about all along.
