# Abinadi's Christology — Source Log

*Working document. Kept as research provenance — traces how observations were reached.*

**Binding Question:** How does Abinadi move from "you don't even keep the law of Moses" to the full doctrine of a God who comes down in flesh, is both Father and Son, suffers as Isaiah's servant, and gains "his seed" — and how does the whole argument bend back to answer the Isaiah 52:7 trap the priests set in Mosiah 12:20-24?

---

1. The Shield and the Law as Type (Mosiah 13) — shining face, Spirit's protection, Decalogue, law points to Christ
2. Isaiah 53 Quoted Whole (Mosiah 14) — brass-plates version, wording variants vs. KJV
3. The Condescension Christology (Mosiah 15:1-9) — God comes down, Father/Son doctrine
4. "Who Shall Declare His Generation?" — the seed question (Mosiah 15:10-13)

6. First and Second Resurrection (Mosiah 15:19 — 16:15) — stakes of redemption
7. The Hinge of Mosiah — why this is the doctrinal center; Alma as the listener who carries it out

## Mosiah 12 — The Priests' Trap

### gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/12.md — Mosiah 12:20-24

> "How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings; that publisheth peace; that bringeth good tidings of good; that publisheth salvation; that saith unto Zion, Thy God reigneth;" (v. 21)

> "Thy watchmen shall lift up the voice; with the voice together shall they sing; for they shall see eye to eye when the Lord shall bring again Zion;" (v. 22)

> "Break forth into joy; sing together ye waste places of Jerusalem; for the Lord hath comforted his people, he hath redeemed Jerusalem;" (v. 23)

> "The Lord hath made bare his holy arm in the eyes of all the nations, and all the ends of the earth shall see the salvation of our God?" (v. 24)

**Observation:** The priests throw Isaiah 52:7-10 at Abinadi as a trap — implying that if he were a true prophet publishing peace and salvation, they'd be able to see it. Their citation of the text IS the riddle: "who are the beautiful-footed ones?" Abinadi's entire speech is an extended answer. Notice v.20 frames it as text "written" and "taught by our fathers" — they know the letter, not the spirit.
**Connects to:** Section V (ring closure), Section IV (his seed = the prophets who published peace)

## Mosiah 13 — The Shield and the Law

### gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/13.md — Mosiah 13:3-35

> "Touch me not, for God shall smite you if ye lay your hands upon me, for I have not delivered the message which the Lord sent me to deliver; neither have I told you that which ye requested that I should tell; therefore, God will not suffer that I shall be destroyed at this time." (v. 3)

> "Now it came to pass after Abinadi had spoken these words that the people of king Noah durst not lay their hands on him, for the Spirit of the Lord was upon him; and his face shone with exceeding luster, even as Moses' did while in the mount of Sinai, while speaking with the Lord." (v. 5)

> "But I finish my message; and then it matters not whither I go, if it so be that I am saved." (v. 9)

> "But this much I tell you, what you do with me, after this, shall be as a type and a shadow of things which are to come." (v. 10)

> "And now I read unto you the remainder of the commandments of God, for I perceive that they are not written in your hearts; I perceive that ye have studied and taught iniquity the most part of your lives." (v. 11)

[Decalogue: vv. 12-24 — second commandment through covet]

> "And now ye have said that salvation cometh by the law of Moses. I say unto you that it is expedient that ye should keep the law of Moses as yet; but I say unto you, that the time shall come when it shall no more be expedient to keep the law of Moses." (v. 27)

> "And moreover, I say unto you, that salvation doth not come by the law alone; and were it not for the atonement, which God himself shall make for the sins and iniquities of his people, that they must unavoidably perish, notwithstanding the law of Moses." (v. 28)

> "Therefore there was a law given them, yea, a law of performances and of ordinances, a law which they were to observe strictly from day to day, to keep them in remembrance of God and their duty towards him." (v. 30)

> "But behold, I say unto you, that all these things were types of things to come." (v. 31)

> "And now, did they understand the law? I say unto you, Nay, they did not all understand the law; and this because of the hardness of their hearts; for they understood not that there could not any man be saved except it were through the redemption of God." (v. 32)

> "For behold, did not Moses prophesy unto them concerning the coming of the Messiah, and that God should redeem his people? Yea, and even all the prophets who have prophesied ever since the world began—have they not spoken more or less concerning these things?" (v. 33)

> "Have they not said that God himself should come down among the children of men, and take upon him the form of man, and go forth in mighty power upon the face of the earth?" (v. 34)

> "Yea, and have they not said also that he should bring to pass the resurrection of the dead, and that he, himself, should be oppressed and afflicted?" (v. 35)

**Observation 1 — The shining face:** Moses' face shone after speaking with God on Sinai (Exodus 34:29-35). Abinadi's face shining IS the enacted typology — Moses bore the light of a God he hadn't yet seen face to face, Abinadi bears the light as he delivers the message about the God who will come. The sign is the sermon before the sermon begins.

**Observation 2 — v.10 is its own type:** Abinadi says "what you do with me... shall be as a type and a shadow." He is not just describing the law; he identifies himself with the pattern. What will happen to him (condemned, silenced, burned) mirrors what will happen to the servant of Isaiah 53. The martyr-prophet is a type of the atoning Servant.

**Observation 3 — The law pivot (vv. 27-31):** Abinadi does NOT say the law of Moses is wrong or bad. He says (a) it was given for a stiffnecked people as a mnemonic device for remembering God, (b) all its performances were types pointing forward, and (c) salvation doesn't come from it alone — the atonement is the referent that gives the types their meaning. The law without the atonement is a signpost with no destination.

**Observation 4 — The universalizing claim (vv. 33-35):** Every prophet since the world began has spoken "more or less" concerning Christ. This sweeps the entire prophetic canon under one christological heading — setting up the Isaiah 53 quotation as representative of ALL of them.

**Connects to:** Section I (shining face, law as type), Section V (ring close — law points to Christ the way the Isaiah 52:7 prophecy pointed to the prophets who published peace)

## Mosiah 14 vs. Isaiah 53 — Variant Table

| Verse | KJV Isaiah 53 wording | Mosiah 14 wording | Notes |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------|-------|
| 1 | "Who hath believed our report? **and** to whom..." | "Who hath believed our report, **and** to whom..." | No substantive difference; KJV uses "? and" vs Mosiah's ", and" — punctuation only |
| 2 | "...as a root out of **a** dry ground**:**" | "...as a root out of dry ground**;**" | KJV has indefinite article "a"; Mosiah omits it. Semicolon vs colon punctuation. Mosiah: "when we shall see him **there is** no beauty"; KJV: "when we shall see him, **there is** no beauty" — comma placement differs |
| 4 | "**¶ Surely he hath** borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows**:**" | "**Surely he has** borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows**;**" | KJV: "hath" (archaic present perf.); Mosiah: "has" (more modern form). KJV paragraph mark. Colon→semicolon. |
| 6 | "All we **like sheep** have gone astray" | "All we**, like sheep,** have gone astray" | Mosiah adds commas around "like sheep" setting it off as parenthetical. KJV "the **iniquity** of us all"; Mosiah "the **iniquities** of us all" — SIGNIFICANT: plural vs singular |
| 7 | "...so he **openeth** not his mouth." | "...so he **opened** not his mouth." | KJV present tense "openeth"; Mosiah past tense "opened" — shifts the reading toward narrative completion |
| 8 | "for the **transgression** of my people was he stricken." | "for the **transgressions** of my people was he stricken." | Plural vs singular — consistent with v.6 variant |
| 9 | "because he had done no **violence**" | "because he had done no **evil**" | SIGNIFICANT variant: KJV "violence" (Hebrew חָמָס, *chamas*); Mosiah "evil" — broader term. Suggests brass-plates text or translation difference. |
| 11 | "He shall see **of** the travail of his soul" | "He shall see the travail of his soul" | KJV has "of"; Mosiah omits — small but alters sense ("see of the travail" vs "see the travail") |
| 12 | "and he **bare** the sin of many" | "and he **bore** the sins of many" | KJV "bare" (archaic past of "bear") + singular "sin"; Mosiah "bore" + plural "sins" — consistent with the plural pattern |

**Key findings:**
1. **Consistent plural pattern (vv. 6, 8, 12):** Mosiah consistently reads "iniquities/transgressions/sins" (plural) where KJV reads singular. This is not random — it systematically makes the atonement cover specific individual offenses rather than a collective category.
2. **v.9 "evil" vs "violence":** The most substantive single-word variant. "Violence" is closer to the Hebrew *chamas*; "evil" (Mosiah) is broader. Neither is wrong as a translation of the brass plates; they may reflect a distinct Vorlage.
3. **v.7 tense shift:** KJV "openeth" (present/gnomic) vs Mosiah "opened" (past) — the brass-plates text may have read the servant's silence as historical narrative, not proverbial statement.
4. **Structural consistency:** Despite small variants, the theological content is identical. The brass-plates text Abinadi quotes is recognizably the same Isaiah 53 — not a different prophetic text.

**Connects to:** Section II (Isaiah 53 quoted whole); the plurals matter for Section IV (his seed = individual persons whose specific iniquities/transgressions he bore)

## Mosiah 15 — The Condescension Christology + Ring Closure

### gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15.md — Mosiah 15:1-31

**The Condescension Christology (vv. 1-9):**

> "And now Abinadi said unto them: I would that ye should understand that God himself shall come down among the children of men, and shall redeem his people." (v. 1)

> "And because he dwelleth in flesh he shall be called the Son of God, and having subjected the flesh to the will of the Father, being the Father and the Son—" (v. 2)

> "The Father, because he was conceived by the power of God; and the Son, because of the flesh; thus becoming the Father and Son—" (v. 3)

> "And they are one God, yea, the very Eternal Father of heaven and of earth." (v. 4)

> "And thus the flesh becoming subject to the Spirit, or the Son to the Father, being one God, suffereth temptation, and yieldeth not to the temptation, but suffereth himself to be mocked, and scourged, and cast out, and disowned by his people." (v. 5)

> "And after all this, after working many mighty miracles among the children of men, he shall be led, yea, even as Isaiah said, as a sheep before the shearer is dumb, so he opened not his mouth." (v. 6)

> "Yea, even so he shall be led, crucified, and slain, the flesh becoming subject even unto death, the will of the Son being swallowed up in the will of the Father." (v. 7)

> "And thus God breaketh the bands of death, having gained the victory over death; giving the Son power to make intercession for the children of men—" (v. 8)

> "Having ascended into heaven, having the bowels of mercy; being filled with compassion towards the children of men; standing betwixt them and justice; having broken the bands of death, taken upon himself their iniquity and their transgressions, having redeemed them, and satisfied the demands of justice." (v. 9)

**Observation — Father/Son doctrine (vv. 2-4):** Abinadi gives TWO reasons for the dual title, not one:
- Father: because conceived by the power of God (divine origin — not a human father)
- Son: because of the flesh (mortal nature — born of woman)
The titles describe the same person from two angles. "And they are one God" (v.4) — the unity is not two beings fused; it's one being whose nature holds both realities simultaneously. This is NOT a modalist claim (Father = Son = same mode of God); it is a Christological claim that the incarnate Christ participates in both the divine and the human, and that participation in the divine is FATHER-level (he is the Creator, the God of Israel).
NOTE: v.4 calls him "the very Eternal Father of heaven and of earth" — same title as Mosiah 3:8 (Benjamin's angel). This is the BoM's consistent usage: Christ IS the Father in the sense of Creator and divine progenitor, while also being the Son in the flesh. The relationship between Father (Elohim) and Son (Christ) is NOT what's being described here; Abinadi is describing the internal nature of the incarnate Christ.

**Observation — The will-submission arc (vv. 5, 7):** v.5 "the flesh becoming subject to the Spirit"; v.7 "the will of the Son being swallowed up in the will of the Father." The Gethsemane theology is here before the Gethsemane narrative. The flesh does not drag him down; it is the arena in which obedience is enacted. The "becoming subject" is the atonement mechanism — not just substitution, but the flesh voluntarily bowing to the Father's design.

**Observation — v.6 quotes Isaiah 53 mid-exposition:** Abinadi pivots from the doctrinal formula to the Isaiah text he quoted in chapter 14, citing "as Isaiah said" — proving that his entire Father/Son doctrine is his explication of the servant passage. Isaiah 53 is not an appendix; it is the source text Abinadi is parsing.

**The Seed Question (vv. 10-13):**

> "And now I say unto you, who shall declare his generation? Behold, I say unto you, that when his soul has been made an offering for sin he shall see his seed. And now what say ye? And who shall be his seed?" (v. 10)

> "Behold I say unto you, that whosoever has heard the words of the prophets, yea, all the holy prophets who have prophesied concerning the coming of the Lord—I say unto you, that all those who have hearkened unto their words, and believed that the Lord would redeem his people, and have looked forward to that day for a remission of their sins, I say unto you, that these are his seed, or they are the heirs of the kingdom of God." (v. 11)

> "For these are they whose sins he has borne; these are they for whom he has died, to redeem them from their transgressions. And now, are they not his seed?" (v. 12)

> "Yea, and are not the prophets, every one that has opened his mouth to prophesy, that has not fallen into transgression, I mean all the holy prophets ever since the world began? I say unto you that they are his seed." (v. 13)

**Observation — "Who shall declare his generation?" (v. 10):** Abinadi quotes Isa 53:8 ("who shall declare his generation?") and treats it as a genuine unanswered question — the priests threw Isaiah at him and can't answer it. His answer: the question is about spiritual posterity, not biological lineage. The one who dies childless (in the natural sense) GAINS children through his very death. The atoning sacrifice is the act of generation.

**Observation — Three categories of seed (vv. 11-13):** (1) All who heard and believed the prophets and looked forward to the remission of sins. (2) Those for whom he died. (3) The prophets themselves — every holy prophet since the world began. This last move is striking: the prophets who proclaimed him are ALSO his seed. The prophets did not precede Christ; they derive their prophetic identity FROM him. He is the source of the revelatory tradition that pointed to him.

**The Isaiah 52:7 Ring Closure (vv. 14-18):**

> "And these are they who have published peace, who have brought good tidings of good, who have published salvation; and said unto Zion: Thy God reigneth!" (v. 14)

> "And O how beautiful upon the mountains were their feet!" (v. 15)

> "And again, how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of those that are still publishing peace!" (v. 16)

> "And again, how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of those who shall hereafter publish peace, yea, from this time henceforth and forever!" (v. 17)

> "And behold, I say unto you, this is not all. For O how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that is the founder of peace, yea, even the Lord, who has redeemed his people; yea, him who has granted salvation unto his people;" (v. 18)

**Observation — Three-wave ring:** Abinadi quotes the Isaiah 52:7 language back at the priests in three waves: (1) past — the prophets who published peace (v.14-15), (2) present — those still publishing (v.16), (3) future — those who shall (v.17). Then: "this is not all" — the FOURTH application is the Lord himself, the ultimate bringer of good tidings, the founder of peace (v.18). The "beautiful feet" aren't just messengers — the feet belong first to the prophets (his seed), and ultimately to Christ himself (the founder). The priests asked who the "feet" belong to; Abinadi's answer: they belong to everyone who participates in Christ's mission, with Christ himself as the originating source.

**Resurrection / First Resurrection (vv. 19-31):**

> "For were it not for the redemption which he hath made for his people, which was prepared from the foundation of the world, I say unto you, were it not for this, all mankind must have perished." (v. 19)

> "And there cometh a resurrection, even a first resurrection; yea, even a resurrection of those that have been, and who are, and who shall be, even until the resurrection of Christ—for so shall he be called." (v. 21)

> "And now, the resurrection of all the prophets, and all those that have believed in their words, or all those that have kept the commandments of God, shall come forth in the first resurrection; therefore, they are the first resurrection." (v. 22)

> "And little children also have eternal life." (v. 25)

> "But behold, and fear, and tremble before God, for ye ought to tremble; for the Lord redeemeth none such that rebel against him and die in their sins; yea, even all those that have perished in their sins ever since the world began, that have wilfully rebelled against God, that have known the commandments of God, and would not keep them; these are they that have no part in the first resurrection." (v. 26)

> "Therefore ought ye not to tremble? For salvation cometh to none such; for the Lord hath redeemed none such; yea, neither can the Lord redeem such; for he cannot deny himself; for he cannot deny justice when it has its claim." (v. 27)

**Observation — "which was prepared from the foundation of the world" (v.19):** The redemption is not reactive. God didn't improvise the Atonement in response to the Fall; it was prepared before. This connects to the plan-of-salvation doctrine and gives the law of Moses its proper horizon: the law was not itself the plan; it was a pedagogical scaffold pointing toward a plan that preceded it.

**Observation — "he cannot deny justice when it has its claim" (v.27):** Abinadi doesn't soften the warning. The priests, who "have wilfully rebelled... have known the commandments of God, and would not keep them" — v.26 describes their exact situation. Abinadi is preaching directly at them. The "beautiful feet" of the Isaiah trap become, by v.26-27, a self-indictment.

**Connects to:** Sections III, IV, V, VI

## Mosiah 16 — First and Second Resurrection / Closing Warning

### gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/16.md — Mosiah 16:1-15

> "And now, it came to pass that after Abinadi had spoken these words he stretched forth his hand and said: The time shall come when all shall see the salvation of the Lord; when every nation, kindred, tongue, and people shall see eye to eye and shall confess before God that his judgments are just." (v. 1)

> "For they are carnal and devilish, and the devil has power over them; yea, even that old serpent that did beguile our first parents, which was the cause of their fall; which was the cause of all mankind becoming carnal, sensual, devilish, knowing evil from good, subjecting themselves to the devil." (v. 3)

> "Thus all mankind were lost; and behold, they would have been endlessly lost were it not that God redeemed his people from their lost and fallen state." (v. 4)

> "But remember that he that persists in his own carnal nature, and goes on in the ways of sin and rebellion against God, remaineth in his fallen state and the devil hath all power over him. Therefore he is as though there was no redemption made, being an enemy to God; and also is the devil an enemy to God." (v. 5)

> "And now if Christ had not come into the world, speaking of things to come as though they had already come, there could have been no redemption." (v. 6)

> "And if Christ had not risen from the dead, or have broken the bands of death that the grave should have no victory, and that death should have no sting, there could have been no resurrection." (v. 7)

> "But there is a resurrection, therefore the grave hath no victory, and the sting of death is swallowed up in Christ." (v. 8)

> "He is the light and the life of the world; yea, a light that is endless, that can never be darkened; yea, and also a life which is endless, that there can be no more death." (v. 9)

> "And now, ought ye not to tremble and repent of your sins, and remember that only in and through Christ ye can be saved?" (v. 13)

> "Therefore, if ye teach the law of Moses, also teach that it is a shadow of those things which are to come—" (v. 14)

> "Teach them that redemption cometh through Christ the Lord, who is the very Eternal Father. Amen." (v. 15)

**Observation 1 — v.5 is the theological crux of chapter 16:** "he that persists in his own carnal nature... is as though there was no redemption made." The Atonement is universal in scope but conditional in effect. The "natural man" language (cf. Mosiah 3:19 from Benjamin's sermon, Alma 22:14) recurs here. The redemption exists; willful rebellion makes it as if it did not — for that person.

**Observation 2 — The subjunctive construction in v.6:** "speaking of things to come as though they had already come" — Abinadi places himself and his audience in the same temporal position the brass-plates prophets occupied. They all speak of a redemption that is, from God's perspective, already accomplished. The law of Moses operated in this mode: every sacrifice was a forward-acting type, real in its effect though historically before the fact.

**Observation 3 — v.14-15 are the closing command:** Abinadi addresses the priests directly: "if ye teach the law of Moses, also teach that it is a shadow." This is the binding practical claim of the entire argument. He doesn't say "stop teaching the law." He says: teach the shadow AND what it shadows. The failure of Noah's priests is not that they taught Moses; it's that they taught Moses without Christ.

**Observation 4 — "the very Eternal Father" again (v.15):** The study closes with the same title it opened with (15:4). Christ is the Eternal Father. This is Abinadi's christological anchor and final word.

**Connects to:** Section VI (natural man, carnal nature), Section I (law as shadow), Section III (Eternal Father title), ring structure

## Cross-Reference Sources

### Mosiah 3:8 — Benjamin's angel

> "And he shall be called Jesus Christ, the Son of God, the Father of heaven and earth, the Creator of all things from the beginning; and his mother shall be called Mary."

**Observation:** Identical title cluster to Mosiah 15:4 ("the very Eternal Father of heaven and of earth"). Benjamin's angel speech precedes Abinadi by the narrative chronology; the same christological formula appears in both. The BoM's Christology is not Abinadi's invention — he is articulating a consistent tradition.
**Connects to:** Section III (Father/Son doctrine)

### Mosiah 5:7 — Benjamin's covenant people

> "And now, because of the covenant which ye have made ye shall be called the children of Christ, his sons, and his daughters; for behold, this day he hath spiritually begotten you; for ye say that your hearts are changed through faith on his name; therefore, ye are born of him and have become his sons and his daughters."

**Observation:** Benjamin's covenant people become "children of Christ" through a spiritual birth. This is the same "seed" concept Abinadi develops in 15:11-13, but enacted through covenant. Abinadi defines seed backwards from death (he died → he has seed); Benjamin defines it forward from covenant (you covenant → you become his seed). Both arrive at the same reality: Christ has a spiritual posterity that comes through the atonement.
**Connects to:** Section IV (who shall be his seed)

### Mosiah 7:27 — Ammon's testimony (about Abinadi's teaching)

> "And because he said unto them that Christ was the God, the Father of all things, and said that he should take upon him the image of man, and it should be the image after which man was created in the beginning; or in other words, he said that man was created after the image of God, and that God should come down among the children of men, and take upon him flesh and blood, and go forth upon the face of the earth—"

**Observation:** This is a third party (Ammon) summarizing what Abinadi taught. The summary confirms: "Christ was the God, the Father of all things." The man/image language adds an imago Dei dimension: God comes down to wear the image in which he originally made man. The Incarnation is a kind of return to his own handiwork.
**Connects to:** Section III (condescension Christology)

### Alma 7:9-13 — Alma's own condensed Christology

> "And he shall go forth, suffering pains and afflictions and temptations of every kind; and this that the word might be fulfilled which saith he will take upon him the pains and the sicknesses of his people." (v. 11)

> "And he will take upon him death, that he may loose the bands of death which bind his people; and he will take upon him their infirmities, that his bowels may be filled with mercy, according to the flesh, that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people according to their infirmities." (v. 13)

**Observation:** Alma — the young priest who wrote down Abinadi's words and carried them out — here preaches his own condensed version of the same Christology decades later. "He will take upon him their infirmities, that his bowels may be filled with mercy, according to the flesh" adds something Abinadi didn't emphasize: the Incarnation is not only substitutionary (he bears our sins) but also empathic (he learns our experience so he can succor). Alma extends Abinadi's teaching. The line of transmission is direct.
**Connects to:** Section III (condescension), Section VII (Alma as the listener)

### 1 Nephi 11:16-21 — Nephi's condescension vision

> "And he said unto me: Knowest thou the condescension of God?" (v. 16)

> "And I said unto him: I know that he loveth his children; nevertheless, I do not know the meaning of all things." (v. 17)

> "And he said unto me: Behold, the virgin whom thou seest is the mother of the Son of God, after the manner of the flesh." (v. 18)

> "And the angel said unto me: Behold the Lamb of God, yea, even the Son of the Eternal Father! Knowest thou the meaning of the tree which thy father saw?" (v. 21)

**Observation:** The angel names the Incarnation the "condescension of God" — this is where the doctrinal term used in Restoration discourse originates in the BoM. Nephi's answer to "knowest thou?" is telling: "I know that he loveth his children." The condescension is love enacted as descent. Abinadi's "God himself shall come down" (15:1) is the same move, but here Nephi's vision establishes the vocabulary. "The Son of the Eternal Father" (v.21) again: the angel identifies Christ as both Son and offspring of the Eternal Father — the same dual nature Abinadi explicates.
**Connects to:** Section III (condescension Christology), pre-Abinadi witness

## Mosiah 17:2-4 — Alma writes the words

> "But there was one among them whose name was Alma, he also being a descendant of Nephi. And he was a young man, and he believed the words which Abinadi had spoken, for he knew concerning the iniquity which Abinadi had testified against them; therefore he began to plead with the king that he would not be angry with Abinadi, but suffer that he might depart in peace." (v. 2)

> "But the king was more wroth, and caused that Alma should be cast out from among them, and sent his servants after him that they might slay him." (v. 3)

> "But he fled from before them and hid himself that they found him not. And he being concealed for many days did write all the words which Abinadi had spoken." (v. 4)

**Observation:** Alma's conversion is double. He believed — and then he acted on that belief by pleading for Abinadi's life, which put his own life at risk. The writing is done in hiding ("concealed for many days") — a fugitive, not a scribe in a scriptorium. Abinadi died having delivered only one human soul from Noah's court. That one soul wrote everything. The durability of Abinadi's words was not Abinadi's doing; it was Alma's.
