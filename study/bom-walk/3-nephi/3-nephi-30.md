# 3 Nephi 30 — the closing invitation: turn, come, and be numbered with Israel

**Binding idea:** The last word of 3 Nephi is not a warning but an invitation, spoken in Christ's own voice through Mormon: "Hearken, O ye Gentiles, and hear the words of Jesus Christ, the Son of the living God, which he hath commanded me that I should speak concerning you" (30:1) — "Turn, all ye Gentiles, from your wicked ways... and come unto me, and be baptized in my name, that ye may receive a remission of your sins, and be filled with the Holy Ghost, that ye may be numbered with my people who are of the house of Israel" (30:2). After the catalog of Gentile sins — "lyings and deceivings... whoredoms... secret abominations... idolatries... murders... priestcrafts... envyings... strifes" — the door stands open: turn, come, be baptized, and be *numbered with Israel*.

**Read:** 3 Nephi 30 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 2 verses. The closing invitation of the book of 3 Nephi.

## Thoughts

After the woes of chapter 29 — wo to him who spurns, who denies revelation, who mocks the Jews — the book of 3 Nephi does not end on the woe. It ends on the open door. The very last word Christ gives the Gentile reader, through Mormon, is "come unto me." That sequence matters: the warning came first, and then the invitation, and the invitation is where it rests. A God who only warned would be a God to fear; a God who only invited without naming the sin would be a God who didn't take the sin seriously. Chapter 29 names the seriousness; chapter 30 opens the door anyway. The honest catalog of what the Gentile must turn *from* — "lyings and deceivings... whoredoms... secret abominations... idolatries... murders... priestcrafts... envyings... strifes" — is not softened, and it reads like a mirror held up to the latter-day world. But it is followed immediately by what the Gentile is invited *to*: come, be baptized, be cleansed, be filled with the Spirit. The list of sins is long; the invitation is unconditional in its reach ("all ye Gentiles") and complete in its remedy. Turn from *all* of it, and come.

And the destination of the invitation is the thing that ties the whole close of the book together: "that ye may be numbered with my people who are of the house of Israel." This is the grafting-in — the doctrine of Romans 11 and Galatians 3:29 ("if ye be Christ's, then are ye Abraham's seed, and heirs according to the promise"). The Gentile who comes to Christ is not merely forgiven and saved as an outsider; the Gentile is *adopted into Israel*, numbered with the covenant people, made an heir of the Abrahamic promise (Abr 2:10, "as many as receive this Gospel shall be called after thy name, and shall be accounted thy seed"). And here is where chapters 29 and 30 lock together into one teaching. Chapter 29 warned the Gentile: do not mock the Jews, do not despise Israel, for the Lord remembers His covenant with them. Chapter 30 invites the Gentile: come to Christ and *be numbered with* that very Israel. The covenant the Gentile was warned not to despise is the covenant the Gentile is invited to enter. The warning and the invitation are the same covenant seen from two sides — to mock Israel is to spurn the very door you are being invited through. The Gentile cannot, on one hand, "make game of the Jews" and, on the other, hope to be "numbered with my people who are of the house of Israel." To enter the covenant is to honor it; to despise it is to lock yourself out of the only door home.

There's a quiet weight in the title Christ gives Himself in verse 1: "Jesus Christ, the Son of the living God." It is Peter's confession (Matt 16:16), the rock on which the church is built — and it is Christ's self-identification at the close of the book that exists to be a second witness of exactly that. The book of 3 Nephi, which opened with the signs of His birth and moved through His death, His descent, His ministry, His gospel, and His warnings, signs off with the Son of the living God Himself issuing the invitation. The whole record's purpose is gathered into its last two verses: here is who I am (the Son of the living God), here is what you must leave (your wickedness), here is what to do (come and be baptized), and here is what you become (numbered with Israel). It is the gospel of chapter 27 compressed to its smallest seed — and aimed straight at the reader.

That the book ends as an invitation to the reader, rather than as the conclusion of the Nephite story, is itself the point. 3 Nephi is the heart of the Book of Mormon — the account of the resurrected Christ's visit, the reason the whole record exists. And its closing gesture is not to look back at what happened in Bountiful but to look forward, out of the page, at the Gentile holding the book, and say: *you, too, turn and come.* The risen Christ who descended to the Nephites is the same Christ inviting the reader, and the invitation is identical to the one He extended at the temple — come unto me. The book does not close so much as it opens a door and leaves it open. Everything in it has been building to the two-word command at its center: "come unto me."

Yes — the closing invitation of 3 Nephi. **The last word of 3 Nephi is invitation, not condemnation** (30:1-2, after ch 29's woes, the book rests on the open door); **the Gentile who comes to Christ is numbered with Israel** (30:2, the grafting-in, adoption into the Abrahamic covenant); **the warning and the invitation are the same covenant** (29:8 + 30:2 — the covenant the Gentile must not mock is the one he is invited into); and **the book opens a door and leaves it open — "come unto me"** (30:2, 3 Nephi closes by looking out of the page at the reader, signed "Jesus Christ, the Son of the living God").
