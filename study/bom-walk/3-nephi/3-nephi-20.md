# 3 Nephi 20 — bread He provided Himself, the prophet like unto Moses, and "the Father and I are one"

**Binding idea:** Christ administers the sacrament again — but this time "there had been no bread, neither wine, brought by the disciples, neither by the multitude" (20:6); He provides the emblems miraculously, and declares: "He that eateth this bread eateth of my body to his soul... and his soul shall never hunger nor thirst, but shall be filled" (20:8). The people "were filled with the Spirit; and they did cry out with one voice, and gave glory to Jesus" (20:9). He then unfolds the covenant: the scattered remnants "be gathered in from the east and from the west, and from the south and from the north; and they shall be brought to the knowledge of the Lord their God" (20:13); "this people will I establish in this land... and it shall be a New Jerusalem" (20:22); "I am he of whom Moses spake, saying: A prophet shall the Lord your God raise up unto you... like unto me; him shall ye hear" (20:23); "ye are the children of the prophets... and of the covenant... In thy seed shall all the kindreds of the earth be blessed" (20:25). And He quotes Isaiah 52 at length — the watchmen, the waste places singing, "how beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings" (20:40), "Awake, awake... put on thy strength, O Zion" (20:36) — closing one quotation with His own addition: "all the ends of the earth shall see the salvation of the Father; and the Father and I are one" (20:35).

## Thoughts

The miraculous sacrament in verses 3-9 deepens the meaning of the ordinance from the day before. This time "there had been no bread, neither wine, brought by the disciples, neither by the multitude" — and Christ provides them anyway, just as He multiplied the loaves at Galilee (Mark 6). The point is unmistakable: the emblems He gives are *His own provision*, not something the people bring to Him. And He makes the meaning explicit in a way He hadn't the day before: "He that eateth this bread eateth of my body to his soul... and his soul shall never hunger nor thirst, but shall be filled" (20:8). This is the bread-of-life discourse (John 6:35, "he that cometh to me shall never hunger") enacted in the sacrament. The bread is not merely a symbol pointing to Christ's body; partaking of it feeds the *soul*, and the soul that partakes "shall never hunger nor thirst." The sacrament is the means by which Christ Himself becomes the food of the soul — He provides the bread, the bread is His body, and the soul that receives it is filled forever. And the people respond with worship: "filled with the Spirit... gave glory to Jesus, whom they both saw and heard" (20:9). The sacrament rightly received ends in being filled with the Spirit and worshiping Christ — exactly the fruit promised in 18:7, "if ye do always remember me ye shall have my Spirit to be with you."

The most theologically pointed moment is verse 35, where Christ quotes Isaiah 52:10 — "all the ends of the earth shall see the salvation of God" — and *adds to it*: "all the ends of the earth shall see the salvation of the Father; and the Father and I are one." The phrase "the Father and I are one" is not in Isaiah; Christ appends it. He is reading the ancient prophecy and inserting His own commentary into it, affirming His oneness with the Father at the exact point where Isaiah speaks of "the salvation of God." The salvation of the Father that all the earth will see *is* the salvation accomplished by the Son, because "the Father and I are one." For Michael's "Is Jesus Christ God?" study, this is another clear datapoint — Christ explicitly claiming oneness with the Father, woven into His exposition of Isaiah. It's the same oneness He prayed His disciples into the chapter before (19:23, 29). The Son doesn't read Isaiah as describing a salvation separate from Himself; He reads it as describing His own work, because He and the Father are one.

Verse 23 makes the Restoration's reading of Deuteronomy 18 explicit: "I am he of whom Moses spake, saying: A prophet shall the Lord your God raise up unto you of your brethren, like unto me; him shall ye hear in all things... and every soul who will not hear that prophet shall be cut off." This is the prophecy Peter applied to Christ at the temple (Acts 3:22-23), and here Christ applies it to Himself directly: He is the prophet like unto Moses. The parallel runs deep — Moses gave the law, Christ fulfilled and replaced it; Moses led Israel out of Egyptian bondage, Christ leads them out of the bondage of sin and death; Moses spoke with God face to face, Christ *is* God. And the warning is stark: "every soul who will not hear that prophet shall be cut off." To refuse Christ is not a neutral option; it's to be cut off from the covenant people. The prophet-like-Moses is the one Israel was always told to listen for, and He is standing among them. And verse 24 universalizes it: "all the prophets from Samuel and those that follow after, as many as have spoken, have testified of me" — the same claim as Helaman 8, that the entire prophetic line points to Christ. Every prophet was a witness of the One who now stands in their midst.

The Abrahamic covenant in verses 25-27 carries a definition of "blessing" I don't want to miss. The covenant promise is "In thy seed shall all the kindreds of the earth be blessed" — and Christ says how that blessing came: "The Father having raised me up unto you first, and sent me to bless you in turning away every one of you from his iniquities" (20:26). The blessing of Abraham's seed is not primarily prosperity or land or power; it is *being turned away from iniquity*. The greatest blessing the covenant confers is repentance — the turning from sin that Christ accomplishes. This reframes the whole Abrahamic promise. When God said "in thy seed shall all the kindreds of the earth be blessed," the blessing He had in mind, fulfilled in Christ, was the turning of hearts away from sin. And it flows outward in order: Christ came "unto you first" (to the covenant people), to bless them by turning them from iniquity, and then the blessing pours out "through me upon the Gentiles" (20:27). The covenant's purpose was never tribal privilege; it was to be the channel through which the blessing of repentance and the gift of the Holy Ghost would reach "all the kindreds of the earth." Israel was blessed first not to hoard the blessing but to carry it to everyone — exactly the bridge-not-destination point of chapter 16. The deepest blessing of being God's covenant people is being turned from your sins, and the deepest purpose of it is to carry that blessing to all.

And the chapter's long Isaiah 52 quotation (vv32-45) sounds the gathering's triumph one more time, with images worth holding. "How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings... that saith unto Zion: Thy God reigneth!" (20:40) — the messenger of the gospel, his very feet beautiful because of what he carries; the good news that "thy God reigneth" is the most beautiful thing a messenger can bear. "Awake, awake... put on thy strength, O Zion; put on thy beautiful garments" (20:36) — Zion called to wake from her dust and clothe herself in strength and beauty, the redeemed city rising. "Depart ye, depart ye... touch not that which is unclean... be ye clean that bear the vessels of the Lord" (20:41) — the call to come out of Babylon and be clean, especially those who "bear the vessels of the Lord" (those who carry sacred things must be clean). "Ye have sold yourselves for naught, and ye shall be redeemed without money" (20:38) — the people who sold themselves into bondage for nothing are bought back not with money but by grace. And the suffering servant: "his visage was so marred, more than any man... So shall he sprinkle many nations" (20:44-45) — the marred, suffering Christ who yet sprinkles (cleanses) the nations. The whole sweep is the redemption of Zion: the messenger's beautiful feet, the city awaking in strength, the call to come out and be clean, the free redemption, the marred-yet-cleansing servant. After all the warnings to the Gentiles and the long account of scattering, the chapter pours out Isaiah's vision of the gathered, redeemed, singing Zion — and ties it to Christ Himself, "the Father and I are one," the salvation all the earth will see.
