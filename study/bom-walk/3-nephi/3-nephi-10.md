# 3 Nephi 10 — how oft would I have gathered you, and ye would not

**Binding idea:** After the voice, "there was silence in the land for the space of many hours" — the astonishment so great the people ceased their howling (10:1-2). Then the voice comes again with the tenderest image of Christ in all scripture, in three tenses: "how oft have I gathered you as a hen gathereth her chickens under her wings, and have nourished you" (past, 10:4); "how oft would I have gathered you... and ye would not" (the refused offer, 10:5); "how oft will I gather you... if ye will repent and return unto me with full purpose of heart" (the still-open future, 10:6). The three days end, "the darkness dispersed from off the face of the land, and the earth did cease to tremble," "the earth did cleave together again, that it stood," and "their mourning was turned into joy, and their lamentations into the praise and thanksgiving unto the Lord Jesus Christ, their Redeemer" (10:9-10). The more righteous were spared — "it was they who received the prophets and stoned them not" (10:12) — and the chapter ends on the promise: "soon after the ascension of Christ into heaven he did truly manifest himself unto them — Showing his body unto them" (10:18-19).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 10 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 19 verses. The silence; the hen-lament; the light returns; the promise of the appearance.

## Thoughts

The image at the center of this chapter is, I think, the most tender picture of Christ in all of scripture: the mother hen gathering her chicks under her wings. "How oft have I gathered you as a hen gathereth her chickens under her wings, and have nourished you." This is the same lament Christ wept over Jerusalem (Matthew 23:37), here spoken over the fallen cities of the Nephites. And what makes it so devastating is the choice of image. He doesn't compare Himself to an eagle or a lion — symbols of power. He compares Himself to a *hen*, the humblest of protective creatures, who spreads her wings and calls her chicks to run beneath them when the danger comes. The hen offers warmth, shelter, nearness, the covering of her own body against the storm and the predator. It's an image of utter gentleness and total self-giving — the hen who, in a fire, will cover her chicks with her own wings and die rather than leave them exposed. That this is how the Creator of heaven and earth (9:15) chooses to picture His love is staggering: the God who could destroy cities is, toward those who will come, a mother hen wanting nothing but to gather them under the warmth of His wings. The power and the tenderness are the same Person. And the gathering is not abstract — it's *shelter*. To be gathered to Christ is to run under the wing, into the warmth, near to Him, covered by Him, exactly when the danger is greatest.

But the grammar of verses 4-6 is the whole drama of salvation, and I don't want to miss it. The lament moves through three tenses. Past: "how oft have I gathered you... and have nourished you" — He has been gathering them all along, throughout their history, the hen forever spreading her wings. The refused offer: "how oft would I have gathered you... and ye would not" — the heartbreaking middle tense, the gathering He desired and they refused. And the still-open future: "how oft will I gather you... if ye will repent and return unto me with full purpose of heart" — the wings are still spread, even now, even after the destruction, for any who will come. The three tenses hold the entire relationship between Christ and a people: He has always been gathering, they refused to be gathered, and He is still offering to gather. And the hinge is those four words in verse 5: "and ye would not." The destruction was never God's failure to gather; it was the people's refusal to be gathered. The hen spread her wings; the chicks would not run beneath them. This is the most poignant statement of agency in the face of love anywhere in the Book of Mormon. God's desire to save is total and unwearying — "how oft," again and again, the wings always open. The only thing in the universe that can defeat that desire is the human "would not." The chicks scattered in the storm were not chicks the hen wouldn't shelter; they were chicks that would not come under the wing. The whole of human tragedy is in those two words — not that Christ would not gather, but that we would not be gathered.

There's something important in the past tense of verse 4, too — "how oft have I gathered you... and have nourished you." He had been gathering and nourishing them all along, through all the centuries of prophets and deliverances and signs. The Nephites who fell were not a people Christ had neglected; they were a people He had repeatedly sheltered and fed, who kept running back out into the storm. Every deliverance in the whole record — from the Lamanite armies, from the famine, from the robbers — was the hen gathering the chicks. And they kept leaving the wing. That recasts the destruction not as a sudden abandonment but as the final consequence of a long pattern of refusing the shelter that was always offered. He had gathered them "how oft" — many times — and each time they wandered back out. The judgment fell on a people who had been sheltered again and again and would not stay.

The turn in verses 9-10 is the resolution of the whole destruction, and it's gentle: "the darkness dispersed from off the face of the land, and the earth did cease to tremble, and the rocks did cease to rend... And the earth did cleave together again, that it stood." The convulsed creation that recoiled at Christ's death settles back into stillness — and the timing matters, because this is the morning of the third day. The darkness lifts when, on the other side of the world, He rises. The earth that tore apart at His death cleaves together again at His resurrection. And with the returning light, "their mourning was turned into joy, and their lamentations into the praise and thanksgiving unto the Lord Jesus Christ, their Redeemer." The same survivors who had been howling in the dark now praise — and the praise is directed precisely to "the Lord Jesus Christ, their Redeemer," the One whose voice they just heard from the darkness. The mourning into joy isn't generic relief that the storm passed; it's worship of the Redeemer who spoke to them in the worst of it. The arc of the survivors — howling, then stunned silence, then grief again, then praise — is the arc of a soul brought through devastation to its Redeemer. They came out the other side of the three days not just alive but worshiping. And the criterion of who came through is named in verse 12: "it was they who received the prophets and stoned them not... who had not shed the blood of the saints, who were spared." The spared were the ones who hadn't killed the truth-tellers — the whole prophet-rejection thread of the walk resolving in the simplest possible measure of a people's standing: how they treated the ones God sent.

And the chapter ends pointing forward, to the thing the whole book of 3 Nephi has been building toward (vv18-19): "soon after the ascension of Christ into heaven he did truly manifest himself unto them — Showing his body unto them, and ministering unto them." The voice in the darkness was the prelude; now He will come in person, showing His resurrected body, ministering among them. The hen who has been calling from heaven is about to descend and walk among the chicks. Everything from the signs of His birth (ch 1) through the destruction (ch 8) to the voice (ch 9) has been clearing the ground for this — the survivors are "the more righteous part," gathered and spared and now praising, ready for the One who spared them to come down and show them His wounded hands. The next chapter is the descent. The hen comes to the chicks.

## Notable

Yes — the tenderest chapter of the destruction sequence. **The hen gathering her chickens — the tenderest image of Christ** (10:4-6, the Creator picturing His love as the humblest protective creature, the wing against the storm); **the three tenses of Christ's gathering** (10:4-6, have / would have / will gather if ye repent — the wings still spread after the destruction); **"ye would not"** — the willingness of Christ defeated only by the human refusal (10:5, the whole of human tragedy in two words, the most poignant statement of agency in the face of love); **mourning turned into joy on the third-day morning** (10:9-10, the rent earth cleaving together at the resurrection, grief becoming worship of the Redeemer); **the spared are those who received the prophets** (10:12); and **the promise of the descent** (10:18-19, He will show His body — the threshold of ch 11).
