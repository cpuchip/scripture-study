# 3 Nephi 22 — for a small moment forsaken, with everlasting kindness gathered: Christ quotes Isaiah 54

**Binding idea:** Christ gives the Nephites Isaiah 54 entire — the great comfort to forsaken Israel. "Sing, O barren, thou that didst not bear... for more are the children of the desolate than the children of the married wife" (22:1). "Enlarge the place of thy tent... lengthen thy cords and strengthen thy stakes" (22:2). "Thy maker, thy husband, the Lord of Hosts is his name; and thy Redeemer, the Holy One of Israel — the God of the whole earth shall he be called" (22:5). And the heart of it: "For a small moment have I forsaken thee, but with great mercies will I gather thee. In a little wrath I hid my face from thee for a moment, but with everlasting kindness will I have mercy on thee... For the mountains shall depart and the hills be removed, but my kindness shall not depart from thee, neither shall the covenant of my peace be removed" (22:7-10). "All thy children shall be taught of the Lord; and great shall be the peace of thy children" (22:13); "No weapon that is formed against thee shall prosper... This is the heritage of the servants of the Lord, and their righteousness is of me, saith the Lord" (22:17).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 22 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 17 verses = Isaiah 54 essentially verbatim. The comfort to forsaken-and-gathered Israel.

## Thoughts

Christ chose this chapter to comfort a people who had just lived through annihilation — the destruction of 3 Nephi 8, the years of feeling abandoned — and Isaiah 54 is precisely the word for a people who have felt forsaken. The whole chapter speaks to "a woman forsaken and grieved in spirit" (22:6), and its message is that the forsaking was not the end, not even the real story. The placement matters: Christ doesn't give this Isaiah to a triumphant people but to survivors, to a remnant, to those who know what it is to be desolate. And He gives them Isaiah 54 because it is the divine answer to the experience of having been forsaken — not a denial that the forsaking happened, but a reframing of its proportion and meaning.

The center of that reframing, and one of the most comforting passages in all of scripture, is verses 7-8: "For a small moment have I forsaken thee, but with great mercies will I gather thee. In a little wrath I hid my face from thee for a moment, but with everlasting kindness will I have mercy on thee." The whole comfort is in the asymmetry. Look at the words attached to the affliction: "a small moment," "a little wrath," "for a moment." And the words attached to the mercy: "great mercies," "everlasting kindness." The forsaking is small and brief; the kindness is great and everlasting. This is not God minimizing the pain — the forsaking was real, the grief was real ("grieved in spirit," v6). It's God putting the pain in its true proportion against the eternity of His kindness. Whatever season of feeling abandoned by God a person endures — and Israel endured centuries of it, the Nephites endured the destruction — it is, in the scale of God's everlasting kindness, "a small moment." The proportions are wildly weighted toward mercy. The wrath is measured in moments; the kindness in eternities. This is the word for anyone who has felt God's face hidden: the hiding is "for a moment," the everlasting kindness is forever, and the gathering — "with great mercies will I gather thee" — is the certain end. The forsaking is never the last word; the gathering is.

And verse 10 anchors that comfort in the most permanent thing imaginable, only to say God's kindness is more permanent still: "For the mountains shall depart and the hills be removed, but my kindness shall not depart from thee, neither shall the covenant of my peace be removed." Mountains are our image of permanence — "everlasting hills," the things that outlast every human generation, that seem to have always been and will always be. And Isaiah says: even those will depart, the mountains themselves will dissolve, before God's kindness departs from His covenant people. The covenant of peace is more permanent than the mountains. There is no firmer security available in language than this: the most enduring physical thing we can point to is *less* enduring than God's covenant kindness. When I doubt whether God's commitment to me will hold, this verse says the mountains will crumble first. The covenant of peace outlasts the geology of the earth. And the reason it can make that claim is verse 9: God binds it to the oath He swore after the flood — "as I have sworn that the waters of Noah should no more go over the earth, so have I sworn that I would not be wroth with thee." The rainbow promise, which has held since Noah, is the guarantee of the covenant of peace; as surely as no flood has covered the earth since, so surely will God not be permanently wroth with His people. The covenant is as sure as the sky after rain.

The image that opens the chapter is the barren woman who sings (v1): "Sing, O barren, thou that didst not bear; break forth into singing... for more are the children of the desolate than the children of the married wife." Israel, or Zion, is pictured as a barren woman — desolate, childless, the deepest grief in the ancient world, the picture of a future cut off. And she is commanded to *sing*, before the children come, because she will have *more* children than the woman who was never barren. The desolate becomes the most fruitful. This is the gospel's recurring reversal — the last first, the empty filled, the barren made to sing — and it's a word of hope to anyone or any people that feels desolate and fruitless, cut off from a future. The command to sing comes *before* the fulfillment, while she is still barren, because the promise is sure enough to sing about in advance. And the practical instruction follows (vv2-3): "Enlarge the place of thy tent... lengthen thy cords and strengthen thy stakes" — make room *now* for children you don't yet have, because they are coming. The barren woman is told to expand her household in faith. (The Restoration reads "strengthen thy stakes" as the literal stakes of Zion, the gathering places — make room, the gathering is coming.) Hope acts before it sees; the tent is enlarged before the children arrive.

And the relationship the whole chapter rests on is named in verse 5: "thy maker, thy husband, the Lord of Hosts is his name." God is not pictured as Israel's master or king here but as her *husband* — "thy maker, thy husband." The covenant is a marriage. This is why the forsaking grieved so deeply ("a woman forsaken and grieved in spirit, and a wife of youth, when thou wast refused," v6) — it was the grief of a wife who felt abandoned by her husband. And it's why the reconciliation is so tender: the husband returning to the wife of his youth, the marriage restored "with everlasting kindness." God's relationship to His people is not contractual but marital — the intimacy, the grief of estrangement, the joy of reconciliation, all the depth of the deepest human bond, raised to the divine. And the chapter ends with the security that flows from it: "all thy children shall be taught of the Lord; and great shall be the peace of thy children" (v13, the new covenant of Jeremiah 31 — every child taught directly by God), and "No weapon that is formed against thee shall prosper" (v17). The marriage restored produces children taught by God and a peace no weapon can break — "and their righteousness is of me, saith the Lord." Even the righteousness that protects them is His gift, not their achievement. The forsaken wife, gathered with everlasting kindness, married to her Maker, becomes the mother of children taught by the Lord, in a peace more permanent than the mountains, righteous with a righteousness that "is of me." That is the whole arc of the gathering, given by Christ to a people who knew desolation: you were forsaken for a small moment; you will be gathered with everlasting kindness; and the covenant of that peace will outlast the mountains.
