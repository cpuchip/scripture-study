# 3 Nephi 19 — they prayed unto Him as their Lord and their God, and were made white as He is

**Binding idea:** The second day. The twelve disciples teach the gathered multitude (so great they're "separated into twelve bodies," 19:5), and "pray for that which they most desired... that the Holy Ghost should be given unto them" (19:9). Nephi baptizes them, and "the Holy Ghost did fall upon them, and they were filled with the Holy Ghost and with fire... encircled about as if it were by fire... and angels did come down out of heaven and did minister unto them" (19:13-14). Christ comes, has them kneel, and they pray — "they did pray unto Jesus, calling him their Lord and their God" (19:18) — "and they pray unto me because I am with them" (19:22). Christ prays the intercessory prayer: "I pray... for all those who shall believe on their words, that they may believe in me, that I may be in them as thou, Father, art in me, that we may be one" (19:23). As they pray, "his countenance did smile upon them, and the light of his countenance did shine upon them, and behold they were as white as the countenance and also the garments of Jesus" (19:25). He prays again, thanking the Father "that thou hast purified those whom I have chosen, because of their faith" (19:28), and again in words "tongue cannot speak... neither can be written by man" — yet "their hearts were open and they did understand in their hearts the words which he prayed" (19:32-33). And He testifies: "So great faith have I never seen among all the Jews" (19:35).

## Thoughts

The most theologically striking moment in this chapter is verse 18: "they did pray unto Jesus, calling him their Lord and their God." This is unusual — the standard pattern, which Christ Himself just taught (18:19-21), is to pray *to the Father in the name of the Son*, not to the Son directly. But here the disciples pray to Jesus, and call Him "their Lord and their God." The text doesn't leave this unexplained; verse 22 gives the reason in Christ's own prayer: "they pray unto me because I am with them." When the Son is bodily, visibly present, standing in their midst, they pray to Him — because He is right there, and He is God. This is one of the clearest affirmations in the Book of Mormon of Christ's divinity and of the propriety of worshiping Him: the disciples "calling him their Lord and their God," and Christ accepting it without correction, even reporting it approvingly to the Father. For Michael's "Is Jesus Christ God?" study, this is direct: the disciples prayed to Christ and called Him God, in His presence, and He received their worship as right. The ordinary order (pray to the Father through the Son) holds when the Son is not visibly present; but the deeper truth it rests on is that the Son *is* God, worthy of the prayer and the worship, which becomes the natural thing to do when He stands before you. They didn't pray to the Father about Jesus; with Jesus present, they prayed to Jesus, as God.

The intercessory prayer (vv23, 28-29) is the Nephite counterpart to John 17, and its repeated petition is staggering in its ambition for us: "that I may be in them as thou, Father, art in me, that we may be one." The oneness that the believers are being prayed into is *the same oneness* that exists between the Father and the Son. Christ doesn't pray that His disciples might merely be unified with each other, or even merely close to God; He prays that they might be drawn into the very union that He shares with the Father — "I in them, as thou in me." This is the telos of the whole gospel: not just forgiveness, not just salvation from hell, but *union with God*, being brought inside the divine oneness. The Godhead is not a closed circle; it opens to receive the purified believer. "That they may be purified in me... that I may be in them as thou, Father, art in me, that we may be one, that I may be glorified in them" (v29). The endpoint is Christ glorified *in* the believer — the believer becoming the place where Christ's glory dwells and shines. This is the highest possible vision of what a human being is for: to be purified by faith until Christ can dwell in you as the Father dwells in Him, until you are one with God and God is glorified in you. Everything in the chapter — the baptism, the fire, the prayer, the whiteness — is moving toward that union.

And the union has a visible effect: the disciples are transfigured. "His countenance did smile upon them, and the light of his countenance did shine upon them, and behold they were as white as the countenance and also the garments of Jesus; and behold the whiteness thereof did exceed all the whiteness, yea, even there could be nothing upon earth so white as the whiteness thereof" (v25), and again, "they were white, even as Jesus" (v30). This is the whiteness of *glory* — the same transfiguring radiance that shone from Christ's garments on the Mount of Transfiguration (Matthew 17:2), the luminosity of the sanctified. It is emphatically not a matter of complexion or race; it's the light of Christ's own glory reflected in faces and garments made to shine like His. The disciples, purified by faith and drawn into the divine union, begin to *look like* Christ — to radiate His light. This is what purification "in me" produces: not just a cleaned record but a transfigured being, made luminous with the glory of the One they're united to. The becoming-like-Christ is not metaphor here; it's visible, a whiteness exceeding anything on earth. And it came not from their effort but from His countenance shining *on* them — they were made white by His smile, by the light of His face turned toward them (Numbers 6:25, "the Lord make his face shine upon thee"). To be looked upon by Christ with favor is to be made to shine.

The prayer beyond words returns (vv32-34), but with a deepening from chapter 17. There, the prayer "cannot be written"; here, "tongue cannot speak the words which he prayed, neither can be written by man" — *and yet* "the multitude did hear and do bear record; and their hearts were open and they did understand in their hearts the words which he prayed." The words could not be spoken or written, but they were *understood in the heart*. This is a profound point about how the deepest revelation works: it bypasses language altogether and is received directly in the heart by the Spirit. There are communications from God too great for words — too great to speak, too great to write — that nonetheless land with perfect clarity in an open heart. The limit is on language, not on understanding; the Spirit carries what the tongue cannot. This is the difference between knowing something *about* God through words and knowing God directly, heart to heart, in a way no words could capture or convey. The Nephites understood Christ's prayer not because the words were intelligible but because their hearts were open and the Spirit communicated it directly. Some of the most important things I will ever know from God will come this way — not as propositions I could write down, but as understanding poured directly into an open heart, beyond language.

And the chapter's final note (vv35-36) names the whole difference between this people and the Jerusalem fold: "So great faith have I never seen among all the Jews; wherefore I could not show unto them so great miracles, because of their unbelief." The Nephites' faith exceeded the faith Christ found anywhere at Jerusalem — and the consequence is staggering: "I *could not* show unto them so great miracles, because of their unbelief." Not "I chose not to" but "I could not." The miracles required the receiving faith; unbelief actually constrained what the Son of God could do. This is the same principle Mark records of Nazareth — "he could there do no mighty work... because of their unbelief" (Mark 6:5-6, Matthew 13:58). God's power is not arbitrary or stingy; it flows in proportion to the faith that receives it. The greater faith of the Nephite "other sheep" unlocked greater miracles than the chosen people at Jerusalem ever saw — the angels, the fire, the transfiguration, the prayers beyond words. The reversal is complete and instructive: the despised remnant, by faith, received what the first fold could not, because faith, not lineage, is what opens heaven. And it lands as both promise and warning — there is no limit to what Christ can do for a believing people, and a real limit on what He can do for an unbelieving one, and the variable is always the faith, never His willingness.
