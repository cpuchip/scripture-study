# 3 Nephi 1 — "on the morrow come I into the world": the voice of God the night before He is born

**Binding idea:** The book of the Lord's coming opens. Six hundred years from Lehi's departure, the signs Samuel foretold are nearly due — and the unbelievers, mocking that "the time is past... the words of Samuel are not fulfilled" (1:5-6), set a day on which "all those who believed... should be put to death except the sign should come to pass" (1:9). The believers' faith is now literally a death sentence on a clock. Nephi (son of Nephi) "cried mightily unto the Lord all that day" for his people, and the answer is the most astonishing voice in the book: "on this night shall the sign be given, and on the morrow come I into the world, to show unto the world that I will fulfil all that which I have caused to be spoken by the mouth of my holy prophets" (1:13). The premortal Christ speaks from heaven the day before His birth — "I come unto my own... to do the will, both of the Father and of the Son — of the Father because of me, and of the Son because of my flesh" (1:14). The sign comes exactly: "at the going down of the sun there was no darkness... it was as light as though it was mid-day" (1:15-19), a new star arises, and "all things, every whit, according to the words of the prophets" (1:20).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 1 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 30 verses. Opens the book of Nephi the son of Nephi. The sign of Christ's birth; the last-hour deliverance.

## Thoughts

The verse I cannot get past is 13, because of who is speaking and when: "on the morrow come I into the world." This is the voice of the Lord — the premortal Christ, the God of the whole record, the Jehovah whom all the prophets testified of — speaking from heaven *the night before He is born in Bethlehem*. The simultaneity is staggering when you hold it still. The same Being who is about to become a helpless infant, who in hours will be a baby in a manger unable to lift His own head, is at this moment the Lord of heaven answering a prophet's all-day prayer and announcing the most important event in the history of the world. He is fully God on the night before He becomes fully an infant. And He announces it not as a passive thing happening to Him but as an act He is performing: "come I into the world" — He is doing the entering, condescending by His own will into the flesh. The Restoration's whole Christology is compressed into this one moment: the God who speaks is the God who will be born; the voice from heaven and the cry from the manger belong to one Person. There is no other way to read it. The One who said "on the morrow come I into the world" is the One who, on the morrow, came.

And verse 14 makes the Father/Son relationship explicit in the same breath, which is why this chapter belongs alongside Michael's study on whether Jesus Christ is God. "I come... to do the will, both of the Father and of the Son — of the Father because of me, and of the Son because of my flesh." This is the Mosiah 15 Christology stated by Christ Himself, the night before His birth: He is the Father and the Son. "The Father because of me" — He is the Father by virtue of His divinity, His creative and covenantal role, His being the Father of heaven and earth. "The Son because of my flesh" — He is the Son by virtue of the mortal body He is about to take. One Being, both titles, the distinction lying in divinity versus the flesh He's about to assume. This is the answer to the question Michael and I worked: from the Restoration standpoint, is Jesus Christ God? Here He is, speaking as God from heaven, calling Himself both Father and Son, the night before His birth — the divinity established before the manger, not conferred at it. The baby of Bethlehem was God before He was a baby, and said so the night before.

The human drama of the chapter is the death-deadline, and it teaches something hard about the timing of deliverance. The believers are not merely doubted; they are under a death sentence with a date attached — "a day set apart by the unbelievers, that all those who believed... should be put to death except the sign should come to pass" (1:9). Their faith has become a wager with their lives as the stake, and the clock is running out. And the Lord's answer comes — but notice *when*. Not weeks early to spare them the terror. Not when the deadline was first set. It comes the very day Nephi prays "all that day," with the execution apparently imminent: "on this night shall the sign be given." The deliverance arrives at the last possible moment, with the believers having had to hold their faith all the way to the edge of death. This is the pattern of the eleventh-hour rescue that runs through scripture — the Red Sea opening with Pharaoh's chariots in sight, the ram caught in the thicket as Abraham's knife is raised, the angel staying Nephi's hand. God's deliverance is real and certain, but it frequently comes at the last moment, not before — and the waiting-to-the-edge is itself part of what the faith is for. The believers who would be killed tomorrow had to trust today, with no sign yet, the scoffers jeering, the date set. Their faith was tested precisely in the gap between the promise and its fulfillment, which is the only place faith can ever actually be tested. If the sign had come early, there'd have been no faith required to survive the night. It came exactly in time, which means it required everything right up to the last hour.

The vindication in verses 15-20 is total, and verse 16 names the poetic justice: the unbelievers who set the death-deadline "fell to the earth and became as if they were dead, for they knew that the great plan of destruction which they had laid for those who believed in the words of the prophets had been frustrated." The trap they built for the righteous sprang on themselves. They were going to kill the believers for trusting a prophecy; the prophecy's fulfillment left them collapsed in terror, their murderous plan undone by the very sign they'd bet would never come. This is the recurring shape of God's deliverance — not just that the righteous are saved, but that the persecutor's own scheme is turned back on him (Haman's gallows, the lions' den). And the phrase "all things, every whit, according to the words of the prophets" (v20) is the chapter's quiet triumph: Samuel, the Lamanite on the wall whom they shot at and drove away, was right down to the detail — the day, the night with no darkness, the framing sunset and sunrise, the new star. Prophecy verified to the letter. The believers who held on through the deadline were vindicated, and the prophet they'd rejected was proven true by the heavens themselves.

But the chapter doesn't end in triumph, and the realism of that is worth marking. The instant the sign is given, "there began to be lyings sent forth among the people, by Satan, to harden their hearts, to the intent that they might not believe in those signs and wonders which they had seen" (v22). Read that: Satan works to make people disbelieve what they *literally saw with their own eyes*. The night-with-no-darkness happened; everyone witnessed it; and Satan immediately begins manufacturing doubt about an event there could be no honest doubt about. This is the same lesson as Helaman 16 — the miracle doesn't end the battle for the heart; the adversary just shifts to revising the memory of it. A sign, however undeniable in the moment, does not produce permanent faith on its own; the heart can be talked out of even what it saw. The encouraging difference this time is that "the more part of the people did believe, and were converted" — the sign mostly won, the great remission of sins came, peace returned. But the warning stands: even a miracle witnessed by all is not self-sustaining. Faith built only on the sign erodes the moment the lies start; faith that survives is faith that does something with the sign — repents, is baptized, is converted, as the more part here did. And the closing verses (29-30) sound the perpetual note that even this won't last a generation: the rising generation of the righteous Lamanites is "led away by... lyings and flattering words" to join the robbers, and "the Lamanites began to decrease as to their faith." Righteousness is never inherited; every generation must be won again. The book of Christ's coming opens with the heavens vindicating a prophet and a people converted en masse — and already, in the same chapter, the next generation slipping. Even at the threshold of the Lord's own appearing, the human heart remains exactly what Mormon said it was.
