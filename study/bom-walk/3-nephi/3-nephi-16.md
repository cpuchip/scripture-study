# 3 Nephi 16 — the record made for the Gentiles, the fulness that can be taken away, and Zion brought again

**Binding idea:** Christ widens the flock once more — "I have other sheep, which are not of this land, neither of the land of Jerusalem" (16:1), the lost tribes He will yet visit, "that there may be one fold and one shepherd" (16:3). Then He states the purpose of the very record being made: "I command you that ye shall write these sayings after I am gone... that these sayings which ye shall write shall be kept and shall be manifested unto the Gentiles, that through the fulness of the Gentiles, the remnant of their seed... may be brought to a knowledge of me, their Redeemer" (16:4). In the latter days "the truth [shall] come unto the Gentiles" first, because of their belief and Israel's unbelief (16:6-7) — but with a warning: "at that day when the Gentiles shall sin against my gospel, and shall reject the fulness of my gospel, and shall be lifted up in the pride of their hearts above all nations... I will bring the fulness of my gospel from among them" and remember the covenant with Israel (16:10-12). And He closes with Isaiah: "Thy watchmen shall lift up the voice... for they shall see eye to eye when the Lord shall bring again Zion... and all the ends of the earth shall see the salvation of God" (16:18-20).

## Thoughts

Verse 4 is the moment the Book of Mormon names its own purpose in the mouth of the risen Christ, and it places the latter-day reader squarely inside the plan. Christ commands the record to be written, and the reason is a chain that reaches across two millennia: the sayings will be "kept and shall be manifested unto the Gentiles, that through the fulness of the Gentiles, the remnant of their seed... may be brought to a knowledge of me, their Redeemer." Trace the mechanism. The record is made now, by Nephi, by command of Christ. It is kept and brought forth in the latter days to the Gentiles. Through the Gentiles — who receive its fulness — it reaches "the remnant of their seed," the scattered descendants of these very Nephites and Lamanites, and brings them back to Christ. The whole logic of the Book of Mormon's coming forth is here: it was written for a future it would reach by a long, providential route — through the Gentiles, to the remnant. And I am one of the Gentiles in that chain. The book I'm reading came to me precisely as Christ commanded it would, and its purpose in reaching me is not to terminate in me but to flow *through* me — through "the fulness of the Gentiles" — to the remnant of Israel. The record is not a relic to admire; it's a covenant instrument with a destination beyond its reader. Christ wrote it (commanded it written) aiming at the scattered children of the people standing before Him, and the Gentile reader is the bridge.

The order of the gathering is a great reversal, and verses 6-7 name it: in the latter days "the truth [shall] come unto the Gentiles" *first*, "because of their belief in me... and because of the unbelief of you, O house of Israel." In antiquity the order ran Israel-first, Gentiles-after; in the last days it runs Gentiles-first, Israel-after. This is Paul's mystery in Romans 11 — the Gentiles grafted into the olive tree while the natural branches are temporarily broken off, so that the natural branches might be provoked to jealousy and grafted back in. The covenant people, by their unbelief, forfeited the first place; the Gentiles, by their belief, received it. And the purpose of the Gentiles receiving it first is not Gentile supremacy but Israel's eventual restoration — the fulness comes to the Gentiles so that *through* them it returns to Israel. The reversal is temporary and instrumental: the Gentiles are first now so that they can serve the gathering of Israel, who will be first in the end ("the last shall be first"). There's a deep humility built into the Gentile's position: I received the gospel first not because I'm better but because Israel temporarily stumbled, and I hold it not as a possession but as a trust to carry back to its original heirs.

And that's exactly why the warning in verses 8-15 is so sobering, and so contemporary. The Gentiles receive the fulness first — but conditionally. "At that day when the Gentiles shall sin against my gospel, and shall reject the fulness of my gospel, and shall be lifted up in the pride of their hearts above all nations, and above all the people of the whole earth, and shall be filled with all manner of lyings, and of deceits, and of mischiefs, and all manner of hypocrisy, and murders, and priestcrafts, and whoredoms, and of secret abominations... I will bring the fulness of my gospel from among them." Read that litany slowly, because it reads less like ancient prophecy and more like a description of the modern Gentile world — the latter-day nations "lifted up in pride above all nations," filled with lyings and deceits and priestcrafts and secret abominations. The privilege of receiving the fulness first carries the gravest possible exposure: the fulness can be *taken away*. The Gentiles who reject it in pride become exactly what 3 Nephi 12:13 warned the disciples against — "salt that hath lost its savor, which is thenceforth good for nothing but to be cast out, and to be trodden under foot" (v15). The most chilling thing about the warning is that it's aimed at the *believing* Gentiles' descendants — at a people who once had the fulness and then sinned against it. This isn't a warning to outsiders; it's a warning to the heirs. Having the fulness is no guarantee of keeping it; pride forfeits it. And the corrective is gentle and open: "if the Gentiles will repent and return unto me... they shall be numbered among my people, O house of Israel" (v13). The door stays open — repent, and the Gentile is grafted permanently into Israel; refuse in pride, and the fulness departs. The warning is not a threat but a mercy: God tells the Gentiles in advance exactly how they could lose what they were given, so they might not lose it. As a Gentile reader of this very record, I'm meant to feel the weight personally — the fulness I've received is held on the condition that I not become proud and reject it.

The chapter closes with Isaiah, and the Isaiah passage (52:8-10) is pure triumph after the warning — the vision of what the gathering finally accomplishes. "Thy watchmen shall lift up the voice; with the voice together shall they sing, for they shall see eye to eye when the Lord shall bring again Zion." The image of the watchmen — the prophets, the witnesses on the walls (like Samuel the Lamanite) — is transformed: where once watchmen cried warnings into hostile cities, in the day of Zion they "lift up the voice" together and "sing," and they "see eye to eye." The phrase "see eye to eye" means perfect agreement, unity of vision — the watchmen who once contended and were divided now see the same thing and sing it together, "when the Lord shall bring again Zion." The contention that the risen Christ named as "of the devil" (11:29) is finally healed in Zion: not many voices in dispute but one voice singing in agreement. "Break forth into joy, sing together, ye waste places of Jerusalem" — even the ruins, the desolate cities, are commanded to sing, because "the Lord hath comforted his people, he hath redeemed Jerusalem." And the universal climax: "The Lord hath made bare his holy arm in the eyes of all the nations; and all the ends of the earth shall see the salvation of God." The whole long arc — the scattering, the warning, the conditional fulness, the gathering — terminates in a single image of God rolling up His sleeve, baring His holy arm, and acting so visibly that "all the ends of the earth" see His salvation. The gathering is not a hidden, partial thing; it ends in a public, universal revelation of God's saving power. After the destruction of 3 Nephi 8 and the warnings of this chapter, the last word Christ leaves with the Isaiah quotation is *joy* — the waste places singing, the watchmen united, the whole earth seeing the salvation of God. The gathering of Israel ends not in judgment but in a song.
