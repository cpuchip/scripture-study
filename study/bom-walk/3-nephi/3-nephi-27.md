# 3 Nephi 27 — the name and the gospel; "what manner of men ought ye to be? Even as I am"

**Binding idea:** The disciples, united "in mighty prayer and fasting" (27:1), ask Christ the name to call the church, "for there are disputations among the people concerning this matter" (27:3). His answer sets two requirements, not one: "if it be called in my name then it is my church, *if it so be that they are built upon my gospel*" (27:8). The name alone isn't enough — name *and* gospel. Then He gives the fullest, most explicit definition of "the gospel" in all of scripture: "this is the gospel which I have given unto you—that I came into the world to do the will of my Father... that I might be lifted up upon the cross... that I might draw all men unto me... to be judged of their works" (27:13-15), with the human response named exactly — "whoso repenteth and is baptized in my name shall be filled... no unclean thing can enter into his kingdom... save it be those who have washed their garments in my blood, because of their faith, and the repentance of all their sins, and their faithfulness unto the end... be baptized in my name, that ye may be sanctified by the reception of the Holy Ghost, that ye may stand spotless before me at the last day. Verily, verily, I say unto you, this is my gospel" (27:16-21). It climaxes in the question and its answer: "what manner of men ought ye to be? Verily I say unto you, even as I am" (27:27).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 27 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 33 verses. The name, the gospel defined, and the standard of becoming.

## Thoughts

It matters that this chapter opens with the disciples "united in mighty prayer and fasting." They had a real question — what to call the church, with "disputations among the people concerning this matter" — and they did not resolve it by committee or by vote or by the strongest personality. They fasted and prayed until Christ Himself appeared. The pattern is its own lesson: a genuine question about the Lord's work is brought to the Lord, together, in fasting, and answered by revelation. And Christ's first words are the same question He has asked before — "What will ye that I shall give unto you?" — the Lord who, having all power, still asks what His servants desire, and gives according to the asking.

The answer to the name question turns on a distinction that's easy to miss: Christ gives *two* requirements, and the second is the real one. "How be it my church save it be called in my name? For if a church be called in Moses' name then it be Moses' church; or if it be called in the name of a man then it be the church of a man; but if it be called in my name then it is my church, *if it so be that they are built upon my gospel*." The name is necessary — a church named after a man is that man's church, so His church must bear His name. But the name is not *sufficient*. The conditional clause carries the weight: "if it so be that they are built upon my gospel." A church can wear the right name and still not be His, if it is "built upon the works of men, or upon the works of the devil." Such a church "have joy in their works for a season, and by and by the end cometh, and they are hewn down and cast into the fire." So the test of whether a church is Christ's is double: His name *and* His gospel as the foundation. The name without the gospel is a label on the wrong building. This is why the very next thing Christ does is define the gospel — because He has just made the gospel the load-bearing test, and a church needs to know precisely what it must be built upon.

And then comes what I think is the single clearest definition of "the gospel" anywhere in scripture (vv13-21). We use the word constantly, often loosely, to mean the whole of the religion or the general good news. Christ tightens it to something specific and repeatable: "this is the gospel which I have given unto you—that I came into the world to do the will of my Father, because my Father sent me. And my Father sent me that I might be lifted up upon the cross; and after that I had been lifted up upon the cross, that I might draw all men unto me." The gospel, in Christ's own definition, has a precise shape: the Father sent the Son; the Son came to do the Father's will; the Father's will was that the Son be lifted up on the cross; the lifting-up draws all men to Him; and all men, drawn, stand to be judged of their works. That is the divine side — what God did. Then the human side, equally precise: "whoso repenteth and is baptized in my name shall be filled; and if he endureth to the end... him will I hold guiltless." And the fullest single sentence: "Repent, all ye ends of the earth, and come unto me and be baptized in my name, that ye may be sanctified by the reception of the Holy Ghost, that ye may stand spotless before me at the last day. Verily, verily, I say unto you, this is my gospel." Lay it out and the gospel is: faith in Christ → repentance → baptism in His name → sanctification by the Holy Ghost → endure to the end → stand spotless. This is the doctrine of Christ that Nephi taught in 2 Nephi 31, now spoken by Christ Himself, in His own voice, as the definition. It is not vague. It is a doorway with a specific shape, and the shape is the same every time it's named in the Book of Mormon. The gospel is not everything religious; it is *this* — the atoning mission of Christ and the prescribed response that lays hold on it.

There's a phrase in the middle of it I don't want to rush past: "those who have washed their garments in my blood" (v19). It is a deliberate paradox — blood stains; only Christ's blood *cleanses*. To wash a garment in blood and have it come out white (cf. Rev 7:14, "washed their robes, and made them white in the blood of the Lamb") is an image that should not work, and it works only because of who the blood belongs to. "No unclean thing can enter into his kingdom" — that is the absolute requirement, and it would be hopeless, because all of us are unclean, except that the one thing that can make an unclean garment clean is the blood of the Lamb. The impossibility (no unclean thing) and the provision (washed in His blood) are stated in the same breath. And the washing is conditioned on the response: "because of their faith, and the repentance of all their sins, and their faithfulness unto the end." The blood is what cleanses; faith, repentance, and faithfulness are how a person comes to be washed in it.

But the verse that holds the whole chapter is 27. Christ has commanded them to write the record (more on that below), and told them they "shall be judges of this people." And then He asks: "what manner of men ought ye to be?" — and answers His own question with the highest answer there is: "Verily I say unto you, even as I am." Not "be good men." Not "be better than the people you judge." *Be even as I am.* This is the same standard He gave in the Sermon — "be ye therefore perfect, even as I, or your Father who is in heaven is perfect" (3 Ne 12:48) — but here it is sharpened to the first person and made personal: as *I* am. The whole trajectory of the gospel just defined — faith, repentance, baptism, sanctification by the Holy Ghost — has this as its destination: not merely forgiven, not merely admitted to the kingdom, but *made like Him*. "Even as I am" is the answer to what the sanctifying Spirit is *for*. The standard is not a bar set impossibly high to crush us; it is the actual end of the process the gospel begins. We are washed in His blood, sanctified by His Spirit, so that we may become what He is. And He asks the question precisely of men who are about to judge others — the ones who will hold others to a standard had better be becoming like the One whose standard it is. The question "what manner of men ought ye to be?" is, in the end, the most demanding and the most hopeful question in the chapter, because its answer is both impossibly high and, through the gospel just defined, actually attainable.

The record theme that has run through the whole 3 Nephi close returns here too, and now with the judgment attached (vv23-26). "Write the things which ye have seen and heard, save it be those which are forbidden... out of the books which have been written, and which shall be written, shall this people be judged... all things are written by the Father; therefore out of the books which shall be written shall the world be judged." This is the fourth chapter running on the record — Christ audited it (23), supplied it (24-25), explained its purpose for future generations (26), and now states its ultimate function: *judgment*. We will be judged out of the books. And note the two records: the earthly books that the disciples are commanded to write, and the fact that "all things are written by the Father" — the complete heavenly record (the book of remembrance of 24:16, the book of life). The earthly record and the heavenly record converge at the judgment. This is why Christ has been so exacting about the record through these chapters: it is not archival housekeeping; it is the very basis on which the world will be judged. The care taken with the record is the care taken with the standard of judgment itself.

The chapter ends in two notes of intense feeling that I find deeply human in the Savior. First, joy: "my joy is great, even unto fulness, because of you, and also this generation... none of them are lost; and in them I have fulness of joy" (vv30-31). The Christ who wept over the children in chapter 17 here declares His joy *full* because of this generation — none lost. But then, immediately, sorrow: "it sorroweth me because of the fourth generation from this generation, for they are led away captive... they will sell me for silver and for gold" (v32). He sees, with perfect foreknowledge, the apostasy and destruction four generations out — the Nephites who will sell Him for gold, who will become like the son of perdition — and it *sorrows* Him. The same Savior, in two breaths, has fulness of joy and real grief. His foreknowledge does not anesthetize His feeling; He knows exactly what is coming and grieves it anyway, the way God "was grieved" in Genesis 6:6. Joy over those who receive Him, grief over those who will reject Him — both real, both at once. And the chapter closes on the strait gate (v33): "few there be that find it... until the night cometh, wherein no man can work." The narrow way, and the urgency — work while it is day, because night comes. After the fulness of joy and the foreseen sorrow, the closing word is: choose the narrow way now, while there is still light to work in.

## Notable

Yes — a peak doctrinal chapter. **The church must bear Christ's name AND be built on His gospel** (27:5-12, two requirements; the name without the gospel is a label on the wrong building); **the gospel defined by Christ Himself** (27:13-21, the fullest definition in scripture — the Father sent the Son to be lifted up to draw all men; faith, repentance, baptism, Holy Ghost/sanctification, endure → spotless); **"washed their garments in my blood"** (27:19, the paradox by which blood cleanses); **"what manner of men ought ye to be? Even as I am"** (27:27, the doctrine of becoming like Christ — the destination of the sanctifying gospel); **out of the books shall the world be judged** (27:23-26, the record's ultimate function; earthly and heavenly records converge); **Christ has fulness of joy and real sorrow at once** (27:30-32, joy over this generation, grief over the fourth); and **enter the strait gate while it is day** (27:33).
