# 3 Nephi 4 — they did not fear them, but they did fear their God

**Binding idea:** Gidgiddoni's defensive strategy is vindicated. Because the Nephites gathered everything to one place and provisioned for "seven years" (4:4), the robbers find only deserted, foodless land — "the robbers could not exist save it were in the wilderness, for the want of food" (4:3) — and are forced to attack or starve. They come down in terror-paint, "dyed in blood," and the Nephites fall to the earth — but "in this thing they were disappointed, for the Nephites did not fear them; but they did fear their God and did supplicate him for protection... in the strength of the Lord they did receive them" (4:10). Giddianhi is slain fleeing; his successor Zemnarihah lays a siege that backfires (the besiegers, not the besieged, starve), is captured and "hanged upon a tree," and the tree felled with a prayer to keep felling "all who shall seek to slay them because of power and secret combinations" (4:28-29). The people "break forth, all as one, in singing, and praising their God," crying "Hosanna to the Most High God" — and "they knew it was because of their repentance and their humility that they had been delivered from an everlasting destruction" (4:31-33).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 4 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 33 verses. The battle; the siege that backfired; the song of the delivered.

## Thoughts

The strategic genius of Gidgiddoni's plan, now fully visible, is that it starves the parasite. The Gadianton robbers had always been unkillable in the mountains because they lived by plunder — they fed on the society they preyed upon. So the Nephites did the one thing that turns the predator's strength into weakness: they gathered everything — people, flocks, grain, all their substance — into one fortified place and left the rest of the land "desolate." Now there was nothing to plunder. "The robbers could not exist save it were in the wilderness, for the want of food" (4:3). A band that lives by robbery cannot survive when there's nothing left to rob; the parasite dies without a host. This forced the robbers to do the one thing that gave the Nephites the advantage — come out and fight in the open, on the defender's terms, against a provisioned and concentrated people. And when Zemnarihah later tried a siege instead, it backfired completely, because a siege only works against people who will run out of supplies, and the Nephites had laid up seven years' worth (the Joseph-in-Egypt prudence, Genesis 41). The besiegers starved while the besieged ate. There's wisdom here beyond the military: the way to defeat something that feeds on you is not to chase it into its strongholds but to deny it what it feeds on. The robbers' power was entirely parasitic, and the antidote was to remove the host. It's the same logic that defeats a temptation or a vice — not by attacking it head-on in its territory, but by withdrawing what it lives on.

The heart of the chapter, though, is verses 8-10, and it turns on a misread. The robbers come in their terror-display — shorn heads, blood-dyed skin, head-plates, "great and terrible" — designed to make the enemy collapse in fear. And the Nephites *do* fall to the earth. The robbers "shout with a loud voice, because of their joy, for they had supposed that the Nephites had fallen with fear because of the terror of their armies." But they misread it entirely: "in this thing they were disappointed, for the Nephites did not fear them; but they did fear their God and did supplicate him for protection." What looked like terror was prayer. The same posture — bodies on the ground — meant the opposite of what the robbers thought. And the reason the Nephites couldn't be terrorized is stated precisely: they *did* fear, but they feared God, not men. This is one of the deepest answers in scripture to intimidation: the heart that has given its ultimate fear to God cannot be ruled by the fear of men. The robbers' whole strategy — the blood-paint, the threats, Giddianhi's oath to make them "extinct" — depended on the Nephites fearing *them*. But you cannot frighten a people whose fear is already spent on Someone higher. Terror tactics work only on those who have no greater fear to displace the lesser one. The Nephites had the greater fear, the reverence of God, and so the lesser fear had nowhere to land. When the world tries to terrorize me into yielding, the question is whether my fear is already given to God, because a fear rightly placed is the only thing that makes a soul un-terrorizable.

And then the victory, "in the strength of the Lord they did receive them" (4:10), which is the explicit answer to Helaman 4:13. There, the Nephites "boasted in their own strength" and were therefore "left in their own strength" — and lost. Here, they receive the enemy "in the strength of the Lord" — and win. Same nation, opposite outcomes, and the difference is whose strength they trusted. The whole arc from Helaman 4 to 3 Nephi 4 is a controlled experiment in that one doctrine: the people left in their own strength are man-for-man and lose; the people who fight in the Lord's strength prevail against a more numerous and terrifying foe. The strategy mattered, the seven years' provisions mattered, the discipline mattered — but the chapter is emphatic that the deliverance itself came from God, and the people knew it.

Which is why the ending is the most important part. After the victory, the people don't congratulate themselves on their brilliant logistics or Gidgiddoni's tactics. They "break forth, all as one, in singing, and praising their God for the great thing which he had done for them" (4:31). "Hosanna to the Most High God... Blessed be the name of the Lord God Almighty" (4:32). And verse 33 makes the attribution explicit and complete: "they knew it was because of their repentance and their humility that they had been delivered from an everlasting destruction." They credited the deliverance to *their repentance and humility* — that is, to the spiritual posture that let God deliver them — and to *God's goodness*, not to their own military strength. This is the exact reversal of the pride that destroyed them in Helaman 4. A delivered people that gives all the glory to God, that attributes its survival to repentance rather than competence, is a people that can be trusted to keep the victory — because the moment they start crediting themselves is the moment the next fall begins (Helaman 12:2, "at the very time when he doth prosper his people... then is the time that they do harden"). Here, at the peak of triumph, they're on their faces in tears of gratitude, singing. That's the right shape of a delivered heart, and it's the shape that, for once, the Nephites got right. The tragedy the rest of 3 Nephi will tell is how quickly even this curdles — but in this moment, freshly delivered, they sing hosanna and give God everything.

There's a small, sober note in the felling of Zemnarihah (vv28-29). They hang him on a tree, and "when they had hanged him until he was dead they did fell the tree to the earth," crying that God would preserve them to "cause to be felled to the earth all who shall seek to slay them because of power and secret combinations, even as this man hath been felled." The felled tree is a symbol of the felled combination — and the prayer is not for vengeance but for *ongoing vigilance*, that they would keep cutting down the secret works whenever they rise. It's a recognition that the combination, the cancer of the whole book of Helaman, is never permanently destroyed; it has to be felled again each time it grows, because (as Mormon taught in Helaman 6:26-30) it has one ancient author who keeps handing it down. The prayer at the felled tree is the prayer of a people who understand they've won a battle, not the war — that the secret combinations will rise again, and the only safety is to remain "in righteousness and in holiness of heart" so that they can keep felling them. Vigilance, not victory, is the permanent posture against the works of darkness.

## Notable

Yes — the great robber war won, with several sharp doctrines. **Fear of God displaces fear of men** (4:8-10 — what looked like terror was prayer; the un-terrorizable heart); **in the strength of the Lord, not their own** (4:10, 33 — the explicit answer to Hel 4:13, "left in their own strength"); **the delivered give all the glory to God and credit repentance** (4:31-33 — the right shape of victory, the reversal of the pride that destroyed them); **starve the parasite, deny the host** (the robber economy can't survive without plunder); **provisioning as the prudence that makes deliverance possible** (seven years' store, Joseph-in-Egypt); and **vigilance, not victory, is the permanent posture against the combinations** (the felled tree, 4:28-29).
