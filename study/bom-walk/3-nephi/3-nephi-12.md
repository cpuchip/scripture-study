# 3 Nephi 12 — the Sermon at the Temple: the Beatitudes wrapped in the doctrine of Christ, and "be perfect even as I"

**Binding idea:** Christ delivers to the Nephites the Sermon He gave at the Mount (compare Matthew 5) — but reframed for a people who have just touched His wounds, and spoken now *after* the crucifixion. He commissions the twelve, then opens not with the bare Beatitudes but with the doctrine of Christ: "blessed are ye if ye shall believe in me and be baptized... and after that ye are baptized with water, behold, I will baptize you with fire and with the Holy Ghost" (12:1-2). The Beatitudes themselves are Christ-directed — "blessed are the poor in spirit *who come unto me*"; "they shall be filled *with the Holy Ghost*" (12:3, 6). He makes them "the salt of the earth" and "the light of this people" (12:13-14), declares the law "in me it hath all been fulfilled" and "Old things are done away, and all things have become new" (12:18, 47), gives the higher law that governs the heart and not just the act ("whosoever is angry with his brother," "whosoever looketh on a woman to lust," "turn to him the other [cheek]," "love your enemies," 12:22-44), and closes: "I would that ye should be perfect even as I, or your Father who is in heaven is perfect" (12:48).

## Thoughts

What strikes me first is that the Nephite Beatitudes are not the generic blessings of Matthew 5 — they are wrapped, front and back, in the doctrine of Christ from the previous chapter. Before "blessed are the poor in spirit," Christ says "blessed are ye if ye shall believe in me and be baptized... I will baptize you with fire and with the Holy Ghost" (12:1-2). The first beatitude itself is altered: not "blessed are the poor in spirit" but "blessed are the poor in spirit *who come unto me*" (12:3). And "they shall be filled" becomes "they shall be filled *with the Holy Ghost*" (12:6). The whole Sermon is now explicitly Christ-centered — the blessings don't flow from generic virtue but from coming to Him. Poverty of spirit is blessed *when it comes to Christ*; the hungering for righteousness is filled *with the Holy Ghost*. This is the Sermon read through the lens of the doctrine of Christ He just gave (11:31-39): faith, repentance, baptism, the Holy Ghost. The Beatitudes aren't a freestanding ethical code; they're descriptions of what a soul becomes when it comes to Christ and receives His Spirit. The poor in spirit who come to Him inherit the kingdom; the meek who follow Him inherit the earth. Take Christ out of the Beatitudes and they're noble aspirations; leave Him in and they're the fruit of conversion.

Verse 2 contains one of the most personally significant additions in the whole Sermon, and it reaches across two thousand years to me: "more blessed are they who shall believe in your words because that ye shall testify that ye have seen me, and that ye know that I am. Yea, blessed are they who shall believe in your words, and come down into the depths of humility and be baptized." The multitude that day had *seen* Him, *touched* the prints. But Christ pronounces a special blessing — "more blessed" — on those who would believe the *witnesses' words* without having seen for themselves. This is the Nephite parallel to "blessed are they that have not seen, and yet have believed" (John 20:29). And it's pointed directly at every future reader of the record, including me. I did not stand at the temple in Bountiful; I did not put my hand in His side. I have only the words of the witnesses — Nephi's record, Mormon's abridgment, Moroni's promise. And Christ says those who believe on those words are "more blessed" than those who saw. The faith that rests on testimony rather than sight is not a lesser faith; it is, He says, a more blessed one. The whole record was made for exactly this blessing — for the reader who would never touch the wounds but would believe the words of those who did.

The Sermon's pivot from Matthew is the law, and the timing makes all the difference. In Matthew 5:18 Christ says the law stands "till all be fulfilled" — future. Here, spoken after the crucifixion, He says "in me it hath all been fulfilled" (12:18), and then, more sweepingly, "those things which were of old time, which were under the law, in me are all fulfilled. Old things are done away, and all things have become new" (12:46-47). The Nephites are hearing the Sermon on the far side of the cross, so the law of Moses isn't being upheld pending fulfillment — it's *already* fulfilled, done away, replaced. This is the great transition accomplished: the schoolmaster law that pointed to Christ has done its work; "all things have become new" (2 Corinthians 5:17). And what replaces it is exactly the broken heart and contrite spirit of 9:20 — "I have given you the law and the commandments of my Father, that ye shall believe in me, and that ye shall repent of your sins, and come unto me with a broken heart and a contrite spirit" (12:19). The outward law of performances gives way to the inward law of the heart. Which is precisely what the rest of the chapter does: it takes each old commandment and drives it inward.

The higher law (vv21-44) is the internalization of every commandment, and read after chapter 11's teaching on contention, the first one lands with extra weight. The old law said "thou shalt not kill"; the higher law says "whosoever is angry with his brother shall be in danger of his judgment" (12:22). Notably, the Nephite version omits the qualifier "without a cause" that appears in some Matthew manuscripts — making the standard starker: not just *unjustified* anger, but anger itself, is the danger. This comes immediately after Christ named the spirit of contention as "of the devil, the father of contention" (11:29). The pattern is consistent and pointed: the risen Christ keeps returning to anger and contention as the thing to root out. The law of Moses could only forbid the act of murder; the higher law forbids the anger that is murder's seed. And so it goes through the whole catalog — not just no adultery but no lust (the heart, not the act); not just an eye for an eye but turn the other cheek (no retaliation); not just love your neighbor but love your enemy. The law of Moses governed behavior; the higher law governs the heart, because Christ wants not merely clean hands but a clean heart. Every commandment is pushed from the outward deed back to the inward source, because the inward source is where the real sin — and the real righteousness — lives. This is the whole meaning of the new covenant: not a longer list of rules but the transformation of the heart from which all rules flow.

And the chapter ends with the verse that, in the Nephite version, contains its most profound textual difference: "I would that ye should be perfect even as I, or your Father who is in heaven is perfect" (12:48). In the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5:48), Christ said only "even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect" — because He had not yet been perfected through death and resurrection; He could not yet hold Himself up as the completed standard. But here, risen and glorified, He adds "even as I." The resurrected Christ can now include Himself as the exemplar of perfection, because He has *become* it — "having suffered the will of the Father in all things from the beginning" (11:11) and passed through death into the perfected, glorified body they just touched. The "perfect" He calls them to (Greek *teleios* — complete, whole, finished, having reached the intended end) is not flawless behavior but the wholeness He has now Himself attained and offers them. The command is not "never make a mistake"; it's "be made complete, even as I am complete" — and the path to it is the whole Sermon: come to Him, receive His Spirit, let the law be driven inward, become as a little child. The God who descended and let them feel His wounds now sets Himself as the goal: be made whole, even as I am whole. The Sermon that began with the Beatitudes of coming to Christ ends with the destination of becoming like Him.

## Notable

Yes — the Sermon at the Temple, part 1, with distinctive Nephite variants worth marking. **The Beatitudes are wrapped in the doctrine of Christ** (12:1-6 — "poor in spirit who come unto me," "filled with the Holy Ghost"; the blessings flow from conversion, not generic virtue); **"more blessed are they who believe the witnesses' words without seeing"** (12:2 — the John 20:29 blessing reaching the latter-day reader, the faith on testimony the *more* blessed one); **the law now fulfilled — "Old things are done away, and all things have become new"** (12:18, 46-47 — post-resurrection pivot, the broken heart replacing the performances); **the higher law governs the heart, not just the act** (12:21-44 — the internalization of every commandment), with **the higher law on anger right after contention named as of the devil** (12:22, "without a cause" omitted); **salt and light as gift/commission** (12:13-16, "I give unto you to be"); and **"be perfect even as I"** (12:48 — the resurrected Christ adding Himself as the standard the pre-cross Sermon couldn't; teleios = wholeness/completion).
