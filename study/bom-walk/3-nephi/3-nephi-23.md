# 3 Nephi 23 — great are the words of Isaiah, and the Lord audits the record

**Binding idea:** Christ commands diligent study of Isaiah: "ye ought to search these things... a commandment I give unto you that ye search these things diligently; for great are the words of Isaiah" (23:1) — for "he spake as touching all things concerning my people which are of the house of Israel... and all things that he spake have been and shall be" (23:2-3). "Search the prophets, for many there be that testify of these things" (23:5). Then, having "expounded all the scriptures unto them which they had received," He says, "Behold, other scriptures I would that ye should write, that ye have not" (23:6). He asks for the record, examines it, and notes an omission: "I commanded my servant Samuel, the Lamanite, that he should testify... that there were many saints who should arise from the dead, and should appear unto many... Was it not so?" (23:9). The disciples confirm it was fulfilled. "How be it that ye have not written this thing?" (23:11) — Nephi remembers it had not been written, "and Jesus commanded that it should be written" (23:13). Then He "expounded all the scriptures in one... and commanded them that they should teach the things which he had expounded" (23:14).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 23 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 14 verses. Search Isaiah; the record audited and corrected.

## Thoughts

The first thing the chapter does is settle, in Christ's own voice, the question of whether Isaiah is worth the work: "search these things diligently; for great are the words of Isaiah" (23:1). Isaiah is the prophet most readers find hardest — the dense imagery, the layered prophecies, the shifts between Israel and the Gentiles and the past and the future. And Christ's response to that difficulty is not "skip ahead to the plain parts" but "search these things diligently." He commands the labor. And He gives the reason that makes the labor worth it: "he spake as touching all things concerning my people which are of the house of Israel... and all things that he spake have been and shall be" (23:2-3). Isaiah's scope is total — all of Israel, the Gentiles too, the whole sweep of past, present, and future. That's exactly what makes him hard *and* what makes him essential: he's comprehensive, and a comprehensive prophet rewards diligent search the way a shallow one never could. For anyone (including me) tempted to skim past the Isaiah chapters as too difficult, Christ's word is a direct rebuke and invitation: these are the words I command you to search *diligently*, because they are *great*, because they contain all things, and because everything in them "have been and shall be." The difficulty is not a reason to avoid Isaiah; it's the texture of his greatness.

But the most striking thing in the chapter is what Christ does in verses 6-13: He audits the record. Having expounded all the scriptures the Nephites already had, He says, "other scriptures I would that ye should write, that ye have not" — and then He asks Nephi to bring the record, "cast his eyes upon them," and identifies a specific omission. He had commanded Samuel the Lamanite to prophesy that at His resurrection "many saints... should arise from the dead, and should appear unto many, and should minister unto them" (the Nephite parallel to Matthew 27:52-53). He asks, "Was it not so?" The disciples confirm Samuel prophesied it and it was fulfilled. And Christ says, with gentle but real accountability, "How be it that ye have not written this thing?" Nephi remembers it had not been written. And Christ commands the correction: "it was written according as he commanded." The resurrected Lord of heaven and earth examined the scriptural record, found that a *fulfilled prophecy* had been left out, and commanded it be added. This is a stunning moment of divine record-verification. Christ Himself holds the record to a standard of completeness and accuracy — checking it against what actually happened, identifying the gap, requiring the fix. The scripture record is not casually assembled; it is audited by the Lord Himself, and its standard is fidelity to what occurred. (This resonates deeply with the source-verification discipline of this whole project — the read-before-quoting rule, the cite-count rule, the insistence that the record match reality. Even Christ verifies the record against the event, and corrects the omission. If the risen Lord audits His own scripture for accuracy and completeness, the discipline of verifying every claim against the actual source is not pedantry; it's participation in the standard Christ Himself applied to the record.)

What specifically got omitted is worth marking: a *fulfilled* prophecy. Samuel prophesied the saints would rise; they rose; and the fulfillment went unrecorded. Christ doesn't merely want the prophecy preserved — He wants the *fulfillment* preserved. The record must contain not just the word but its vindication, because the fulfilled word is itself a testimony. A prophecy fulfilled is one of the strongest evidences of God's hand (as Nephi argued in Helaman 8:20-21, "we know that Jerusalem was destroyed according to the words of Jeremiah"), and to leave a fulfillment out of the record is to lose a witness. Christ's insistence that the resurrection of the saints be written is His insistence that the evidence of His own resurrection-power be preserved for future readers — the dead rising at His rising is proof of His victory over death, and that proof belongs in the record. The omission wasn't a small clerical miss; it was the loss of a witness to the resurrection, and Christ would not let it stand.

And there's a quiet accountability in "how be it that ye have not written this thing?" that's worth feeling. It's not harsh — Nephi simply "remembered that this thing had not been written," and the correction was made without rebuke. But it is real. The record-keepers, faithful as they were (Nephi the just man whose miracles vouched for the record, 8:1), had nonetheless left something out, and Christ held them to it. Even the most faithful record-keeping is subject to oversight and correction; even good and honest stewards miss things, and the answer is not shame but correction — "it was written according as he commanded." The grace of it is that the gap, once named, was simply filled. There's a model here for any stewardship of records or truth: the standard is high (Christ Himself checks), the accountability is real ("how be it that ye have not written"), and the response to a found gap is not self-justification or shame but immediate correction. Nephi didn't defend the omission; he remembered, and it was written. That's the right posture toward a found error — not "well, it was close enough," but acknowledgment and immediate fix.

The chapter closes with the pattern of Christ's whole ministry to the scriptures (v14): He "expounded all the scriptures in one, which they had written, and commanded them that they should teach the things which he had expounded." Two things stand out. First, "all the scriptures in one" — Christ unifies the scriptures; under His exposition the scattered prophecies become one coherent testimony (the same as Luke 24:27, "in all the scriptures the things concerning himself"). The scriptures are not a collection of disconnected texts but, rightly expounded by Christ, "one" — one testimony of Him. Second, the chain of transmission: Christ expounds, the disciples teach. He doesn't expound and then leave it; He commands them to teach what He expounded. The interpretation is not for private possession but for public teaching — what the Lord opens to the disciples, the disciples are to open to the people. The expounding flows outward. And it's the right exposition that they're to teach — not their own theories but "the things which he had expounded." The whole sequence — search Isaiah diligently, audit the record for accuracy, expound the scriptures as one, teach what Christ expounded — is a complete model of how the word is to be handled: studied hard, kept accurate, unified in Christ, and faithfully passed on.
