# 3 Nephi 17 — He could not leave a weeping people: compassion that changed His plan, and the children encircled with fire

**Binding idea:** Christ announces He must go — "my time is at hand... I perceive that ye are weak, that ye cannot understand all my words" — and tells them to go home, ponder, and prepare for the morrow; He will go to the Father "and also to show myself unto the lost tribes of Israel, for they are not lost unto the Father" (17:1-4). But He sees the multitude "in tears... as if they would ask him to tarry a little longer," and stays: "Behold, my bowels are filled with compassion towards you" (17:5-6). He heals their sick "every one" (17:9), then has them kneel and prays a prayer so great "no tongue can speak, neither can there be written by any man, neither can the hearts of men conceive" it (17:17). He weeps, takes "their little children, one by one, and blessed them, and prayed unto the Father for them" (17:21), weeps again, says "Behold your little ones" — and the heavens open, "angels descending out of heaven as it were in the midst of fire; and they came down and encircled those little ones about, and they were encircled about with fire; and the angels did minister unto them" (17:24).

**Read:** 3 Nephi 17 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 25 verses. The compassion, the healings, the unwritable prayer, the children blessed, the angels of fire.

## Thoughts

The most astonishing thing in this chapter — and maybe in the whole Book of Mormon — is that Christ changed His plan because His people wept. He had announced His departure: "my time is at hand... but now I go unto the Father, and also to show myself unto the lost tribes of Israel" (vv1, 4). He had a schedule, and it was not a small one — He was on His way to minister to the lost tribes of Israel. And then, in verse 5, "he cast his eyes round about again on the multitude, and beheld they were in tears, and did look steadfastly upon him as if they would ask him to tarry a little longer with them." And He stayed. "Behold, my bowels are filled with compassion towards you" (v6). The Lord of heaven and earth, with the gathering of Israel's lost tribes on His itinerary, looked at a weeping people who wanted just a little more time with Him — and could not leave them. He let their tears rewrite His plan. This is the most accessible possible picture of Christ: not a God who keeps to His schedule regardless of our longing, but a God who is moved by our desire for Him, who tarries when we want Him near, who reads the unspoken plea in a tearful face and answers it. The thing that held Him was not their worthiness or their understanding (He had just said they couldn't understand His words) — it was simply that they loved Him and didn't want Him to go. And that was enough. He stays for the ones who don't want Him to leave. The implication for my own prayer is staggering: the longing to have Him stay a little longer is itself something He answers.

The phrase repeated in verses 6-7 — "my bowels are filled with compassion towards you... I have compassion upon you; my bowels are filled with mercy" — is the deepest idiom scripture has for love. In Hebrew thought the "bowels" were the seat of the most visceral, gut-level feeling; to say His bowels are filled with compassion is to say His love for them is not detached benevolence but a felt, overwhelming, physical tenderness that moves in the depths of His being. Christ's love is not a policy; it's a churning compassion, the same word used of the father running to the prodigal, of the Samaritan moved by the wounded man. And what that compassion *does* is immediately practical: "Have ye any that are sick among you? Bring them hither... and I will heal them." The compassion isn't a feeling He merely has; it's a feeling that acts. He heals them "every one as they were brought forth" (v9) — not a selection, not the most deserving, but every single one. The visceral love produces universal healing. And the people respond by bathing His feet with their tears (v10) — the whole scene awash in tears, His and theirs, the tears of a people who didn't want Him to go and the tears of a Lord who couldn't leave them weeping.

The prayer in verses 14-18 is the moment language fails, and the witnesses tell us so directly. Christ kneels, prays to the Father, and "the things which he prayed cannot be written." The multitude bears record: "The eye hath never seen, neither hath the ear heard, before, so great and marvelous things as we saw and heard Jesus speak unto the Father; And no tongue can speak, neither can there be written by any man, neither can the hearts of men conceive so great and marvelous things as we both saw and heard." This is the boundary of the record itself — the abridger who has written everything else stops here and says: this cannot be written. There are heights of communion between the Son and the Father that exceed any human capacity to capture, hear, or even conceive. And the most moving detail is *why* the joy was unspeakable: "no one can conceive of the joy which filled our souls at the time we heard him *pray for us* unto the Father." It wasn't just the grandeur of the prayer; it was that the prayer was *for them*. To hear Christ pray for you to the Father, in words beyond language — that is a joy beyond conceiving. He intercedes for His people in terms no human tongue could form, and the experience of being prayed for by Christ overwhelms the soul. This is what Christ does even now (Hebrews 7:25, "he ever liveth to make intercession for them"); the Nephites simply got to hear it audibly. The joy of being interceded for by the Son is, the record says flatly, beyond conceiving.

And then He wept — twice (vv21-22). After the prayer, after declaring "Blessed are ye because of your faith. And now behold, my joy is full" (v20), "he wept." And after blessing the children, "he wept again." The Lord of heaven, who had just healed every sick person and prayed an unwritable prayer, wept — and the multitude "bare record of it." These are not tears of sorrow (though He had groaned earlier, "I am troubled because of the wickedness of the people of the house of Israel," v14); these, after "my joy is full," are tears of overflowing love and joy. The God who wept at Lazarus's tomb (John 11:35) weeps here too, but now from fullness rather than grief — a love so complete it spills over as tears. There is no stoic, impassive deity here. The risen, glorified Christ is the most emotionally present being imaginable: troubled by wickedness, filled with visceral compassion, overflowing with joy, weeping twice in the space of a few verses. If I ever imagined the resurrected Christ as serene and removed, this chapter destroys it. He feels everything, fully, and He weeps.

The climax of the whole visit is not the leaders, not the twelve, not the doctrine — it's the children (vv21-24). He took "their little children, one by one, and blessed them, and prayed unto the Father for them" — the same patient, individual, one-by-one attention He gave to the wounds in chapter 11, now given to each child. Twenty-five hundred people, and He attended to the children individually. Then "Behold your little ones," and the heavens open, and "angels descending out of heaven as it were in the midst of fire... encircled those little ones about, and they were encircled about with fire; and the angels did minister unto them." The supreme miracle of the entire ministry — angels descending in fire to encircle and minister — is given to the *children*. Not to Nephi, not to the twelve, but to the little ones. This is consistent with everything Christ taught — "of such is the kingdom of God," "except ye become as a little child" — but here it's enacted in glory: the children are honored above all, encircled with the same fire that protected Nephi and Lehi in the prison (Helaman 5), ministered to by angels while their parents watch. The whole arc of the chapter moves toward the children: compassion, healing, prayer, weeping, and then the heavens opening over the little ones. Christ saved the most glorious manifestation for the least, the smallest, the ones the world counts as not yet mattering. And the chapter ends with the careful insistence that this was real: 2,500 souls "did see and hear and bear record; and they know that their record is true for they all of them did see and hear, every man for himself." The tenderest scene in scripture is also one of the most carefully witnessed — 2,500 individual witnesses to the children encircled with fire. The God who could not leave a weeping people ended by opening heaven over their children.

## Notable

Yes — **the tenderest chapter in all of scripture**, and among the most important. **Compassion changed His plan — He stayed for a weeping people** (17:1-7, the accessibility of Christ, who let their tears rewrite His itinerary to the lost tribes); **"my bowels are filled with compassion" — the visceral love that acts** (17:6-9, the deepest idiom for love, healing every one); **Christ paces His teaching to our capacity** (17:2-3, ponder and return); **"they are not lost unto the Father" — nothing is lost to God** (17:4); **the prayer that cannot be written** (17:14-17, the boundary of language, and the joy of being prayed for by Christ); **Christ wept twice from fullness not grief** (17:21-22, the most emotionally present God); and the climax — **the children encircled with fire, angels for the least** (17:21-24, the supreme miracle given to the little ones, blessed one by one). The tenderest scene is also one of the most carefully witnessed — 2,500 souls, "every man for himself" (17:25).
