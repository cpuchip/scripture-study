# Helaman 13 — the Lamanite on the wall, the slippery treasures, and happiness sought where it cannot be

**Binding idea:** The reversal is now total: "the Nephites did still remain in wickedness... while the Lamanites did observe strictly to keep the commandments of God" (13:1). So God sends a Lamanite — Samuel — to call the Nephites to repentance; cast out and forbidden the city, he climbs the wall and prophesies from it (13:2-4). He warns that "four hundred years pass not away save the sword of justice falleth upon this people" (13:5), that the city is spared only "for the righteous' sake" and will be ripe for destruction "when ye shall cast out the righteous from among you" (13:14), and that because "ye have set your hearts upon riches" the riches themselves are cursed to "become slippery, that ye cannot hold them" (13:31). He exposes the deepest self-deception — they honor the dead prophets and kill the living one, welcoming instead the flatterer who says "all is well... do whatsoever your heart desireth" (13:24-28). And he names the futility under all of it: "ye have sought all the days of your lives for that which ye could not obtain; and ye have sought for happiness in doing iniquity, which thing is contrary to the nature of that righteousness which is in our great and Eternal Head" (13:38).

**Read:** Helaman 13 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 39 verses. Opens "The prophecy of Samuel, the Lamanite" (chapters 13-15). The wall sermon; the slippery treasures; the futile chase.

## Thoughts

The image that opens the chapter is the whole reversal compressed into a single picture: a Lamanite standing on the wall of Zarahemla, prophesying to Nephites who wouldn't let him through the gate. Samuel is everything the Nephites had spent their whole history despising — a Lamanite, the cursed race, the hereditary enemy — and he is the prophet God sends them, because by now he's more righteous than they are. They cast him out; the voice of the Lord sends him back; they bar the city; he climbs the wall. There is something pointed in God choosing exactly the messenger they'd be most inclined to reject. The Nephites' whole identity rested on being the chosen lineage and the Lamanites being the fallen one, and God sends their salvation through the mouth of the very people they looked down on. It's the same lesson as the good Samaritan, as the centurion's faith, as the Syrophoenician woman — God's truth keeps arriving from the quarter we're most certain it couldn't. The Nephites couldn't hear it precisely because of who was saying it; their contempt for the messenger sealed their ears to the message. And I have to ask myself the same question: is there a truth I'm refusing because of who's bringing it — a person, a tradition, a source I've decided in advance has nothing for me? The Lamanite on the wall is God's standing rebuke to that reflex.

The slippery-treasures curse (vv17-23, 31-37) is one of the most haunting images in the book, and what makes it precise is the diagnosis underneath it. The curse is not the riches — verse 18 explicitly exempts "a righteous man" who hides his treasure "unto the Lord." The curse is the *heart set on the riches*. "Ye are cursed because of your riches, and also are your riches cursed because ye have set your hearts upon them... Ye do not remember the Lord your God in the things with which he hath blessed you, but ye do always remember your riches" (vv21-22). They remembered the gift and forgot the Giver — exactly Mormon's diagnosis a chapter earlier (12:2). And the curse fits the sin with terrible precision: the thing they set their hearts on becomes the thing they cannot hold. "We lay a tool here and on the morrow it is gone... we have hid up our treasures and they have slipped away from us" (vv34-35). What you trust instead of God will betray you in the exact moment you reach for it — the sword gone "in the day we have sought them for battle." This is "where your treasure is, there will your heart be also" (Luke 12:34) run forward to its end: a heart anchored to slippery things ends up holding nothing. And it was fulfilled literally — Mormon 1:18 records that in the final destruction "their treasures... became slippery." The curse is mercy's mirror image: God lets the idol prove itself worthless, lets the thing you chose over Him slip through your fingers, because only when the false security fails do you finally look for the real one. The tragedy is that by then, in Samuel's telling, it's "everlastingly too late."

Verses 24-28 hold up the sharpest mirror in the chapter, and it cuts straight at me. The Nephites cast out and stone the living prophets while saying "If our days had been in the days of our fathers of old, we would not have slain the prophets" (v25). They build the tombs of the dead prophets and kill the living one, completely certain they'd have done better than their ancestors — and Samuel's verdict is "ye are worse than they" (v26). This is exactly Jesus' indictment of the Pharisees (Matthew 23:29-37): the people most confident they'd have honored the prophets of old are the people rejecting the prophet standing in front of them. It's easy to revere a dead prophet; he makes no demands and his rebukes land on someone else. The living prophet who "testifieth of your sins" is the one you call false, "a sinner, and of the devil." And verses 27-28 name the inverse with devastating accuracy: the man who says "Do this, and there is no iniquity... walk after the pride of your own hearts... do whatsoever your heart desireth" — *that* man they receive, lift up, clothe in costly apparel, "because he speaketh flattering words unto you, and he saith that all is well." This is the eternal market for the comforting lie over the convicting truth — Paul's "itching ears" that "heap to themselves teachers" (2 Tim 4:3). The flatterer who blesses your sins gets the gold; the prophet who names them gets the stones. And the test it leaves me is brutally simple: which voice do I reward? The one that tells me all is well and I should follow my heart, or the one that testifies my deeds are evil? My instinctive irritation at the second and warmth toward the first is the very reflex Samuel is exposing.

The deepest verse, and the one I most want to carry, is 38: "ye have sought all the days of your lives for that which ye could not obtain; and ye have sought for happiness in doing iniquity, which thing is contrary to the nature of that righteousness which is in our great and Eternal Head." This is the companion to Alma 41:10 — "wickedness never was happiness" — but stated as ontology, as the structure of reality. Happiness isn't merely *associated* with righteousness by divine decree; happiness *is the nature of* the righteousness that is in God Himself. And sin is "contrary to the nature" of that righteousness. So seeking happiness in iniquity isn't just forbidden or risky — it's structurally impossible, like seeking dryness in the ocean or darkness in the sun. You cannot find a thing in its own opposite. The Nephites spent "all the days of their lives" chasing happiness in the one place it categorically cannot exist, which is why Samuel calls it "that which ye could not obtain" — not "would not be allowed to keep" but *could not obtain*, ever, by anyone, because the thing they were chasing doesn't reside where they were looking. That reframes every pursuit of happiness through sin I've ever made or witnessed: it was never a matter of getting caught or paying later; it was a category error, looking for the nature of God in the thing most contrary to His nature. The reason "wickedness never was happiness" is that wickedness is the negation of the very substance happiness is made of. And the corollary is hope: happiness *is* obtainable, abundantly, because it's the nature of the righteousness that God is — it's just only found where it actually lives.

The threshold in verse 14 is worth marking too, because it names the moment a society passes the point of no return: "when ye shall cast out the righteous from among you, then shall ye be ripe for destruction." The city stands only "for the righteous' sake" — they are the salt holding back the rot, the reason fire doesn't fall (the Abraham-bargaining-for-Sodom principle, Genesis 18). And the tipping point isn't reached when the wicked become numerous; it's reached when the righteous are driven out, when the leaven of grace is finally expelled. As long as the righteous remain, even a wicked city is spared for their sake. It's a sobering measure of any community's actual condition — not how much wickedness it contains, but whether it still makes room for the righteous or has begun to cast them out. The casting-out of Samuel himself, in this very chapter, is the Nephites enacting their own ripening in real time: they are doing to the Lamanite prophet exactly what makes them ready for the sword.
