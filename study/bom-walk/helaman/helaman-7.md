# Helaman 7 — these are my days: the prophet's grief becomes the sermon

**Binding idea:** Nephi returns from the land northward, where he was wholly rejected, to find Zarahemla worse — "those Gadianton robbers filling the judgment-seats — having usurped the power and authority of the land... condemning the righteous because of their righteousness; letting the guilty and the wicked go unpunished because of their money" (7:4-5). His heart "swollen with sorrow," he climbs the tower in his garden by the highway and pours out his soul to God — not to preach, but to grieve. The passers-by gather a multitude "to know the cause of so great mourning" (7:11), and his grief itself becomes the sermon. He cries: "O repent ye, repent ye! Why will ye die?... Why has he forsaken you? It is because you have hardened your hearts" (7:17-18). And the diagnosis that explains the whole reversal: "it shall be better for the Lamanites than for you except ye shall repent. For behold, they are more righteous than you, for they have not sinned against that great knowledge which ye have received" (7:23-24). His lament begins with the ache of every faithful soul in a dark age: "Oh, that I could have had my days in the days when my father Nephi first came out of the land of Jerusalem... But behold, I am consigned that these are my days" (7:7-9).

**Read:** Helaman 7 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 29 verses. Opens "The Prophecy of Nephi" (chapters 7-16, through Samuel the Lamanite). Nephi's tower lament.

## Thoughts

The verse that stops me is 7, because it's the ache I recognize: "Oh, that I could have had my days in the days when my father Nephi first came out of the land of Jerusalem... then were his people easy to be entreated, firm to keep the commandments of God, and slow to be led to do iniquity." Nephi the prophet wishes he had lived in a more righteous age — the founding age, when (he imagines) the people were faithful and his soul "would have had joy in the righteousness of my brethren." Every disciple in a dark time feels this: the longing for a golden era when faith was easier, when you wouldn't have had to swim against the current. And there's a quiet irony in his particular wish, because the days of "my father Nephi first came out of Jerusalem" were not actually easy — they were full of Laman and Lemuel's murmuring, rebellion, and attempted murder. The "easy to be entreated" memory is partly idealized, the way every age's golden past is. But what redeems the longing is verse 9: "But behold, I am consigned that these are my days." He doesn't stay in the wish. He accepts the assignment. *These* are my days — not the romanticized past, not some better era I'd have chosen, but this one, this wickedness, this hour. And his being "consigned" to it means his sorrow will be the cost of his faithfulness: "my soul shall be filled with sorrow because of the wickedness of my brethren." That's the whole posture I want to learn from this chapter — to stop wishing I'd been given an easier time to be faithful in, and to accept that the time I was actually given is the time I'm called to. The assignment is never the golden age you'd have picked; it's the one you're standing in.

What moves me about the tower scene is that Nephi didn't climb up to preach — he climbed up to grieve. Verse 14 is explicit: "I have got upon my tower that I might pour out my soul unto my God, because of the exceeding sorrow of my heart, which is because of your iniquities." The garden tower was by the highway to the market, a public place, but he wasn't performing; he was praying, in agony, and the agony was real. And then the crowd gathered *because of the grief itself* — "because of my mourning and lamentation ye have gathered yourselves together, and do marvel." The sermon that drew them wasn't an argument; it was a man visibly broken-hearted over their sin. There's a deep principle here about what actually reaches hardened people: not clever denunciation but evident love grieving. Nephi's tears were the first sermon, and they worked where his words in the north had been rejected. This is the weeping prophet — Jeremiah, "oh that my head were waters, and mine eyes a fountain of tears" (the footnote points right to it); Christ weeping over Jerusalem. The prophet who genuinely sorrows over a people he's also rebuking is showing that the rebuke is love, not contempt. I tend to think correction has to be either gentle or sharp; Nephi shows it can be sharp *and* soaked in grief, and that the grief is what makes the sharpness bearable and credible.

The doctrinal key to the whole great reversal lands in verses 23-24: "it shall be better for the Lamanites than for you... For behold, they are more righteous than you, for they have not sinned against that great knowledge which ye have received." This is the principle that explains everything in chapters 4-6. The Nephites are *more* condemned than the Lamanites, not despite their greater light but *because* of it. The Lamanites' lesser knowledge means their wickedness is judged more mercifully; the Nephites' greater knowledge means their wickedness is judged more severely. Light received is not only a privilege — it's a weight of accountability. "To whom much is given, much is required" (Luke 12:48). This is sobering precisely for someone like me who has been given much: covenants, scripture, the Spirit, a lifetime of teaching. The Nephites had "that great knowledge," and it became the measure of their guilt when they sinned against it. The Lamanites, sinning with less, were held to less. So the very thing I'd be tempted to count as my spiritual security — how much I've been given — is the thing that raises the bar I'll be measured against. Greater knowledge doesn't make sin safer; it makes it heavier. The chosen people became the more condemned people because they knew better and did worse.

The structure of God's "forsaking" in verses 17-18 corrects a misreading I'm prone to. Nephi cries "Why has he forsaken you?" and immediately answers his own question: "It is because you have hardened your hearts; yea, ye will not hearken unto the voice of the good shepherd." God doesn't forsake arbitrarily or capriciously. The forsaking is self-caused — it's the natural consequence of hearts that refuse to hearken to the shepherd who is calling. This is the same mechanism as Helaman 4:24 (the Spirit withdraws because it cannot dwell in unholy temples): God's apparent abandonment is never Him walking away from people who wanted Him; it's people walking away from Him and then experiencing the absence they chose. The good shepherd is still calling — that's the whole image — but "ye will not hearken." The sheep aren't lost because the shepherd abandoned them; they're lost because they wouldn't follow his voice. And verse 20 names the speed of it again: "O, how could you have forgotten your God in the very day that he has delivered you?" The forgetting was almost instantaneous — gratitude evaporating the moment after rescue, which is the same disease the whole book keeps diagnosing. Remember was the cure in chapter 5; forget is the death in chapter 7. The distance between deliverance and forgetting was, once more, "the space of not many years" — the footnote says six.
