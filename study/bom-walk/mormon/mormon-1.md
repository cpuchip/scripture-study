# Mormon 1 — the sober child entrusted with the record; the gifts withdrawn; the willing preacher forbidden

**Binding idea:** The man whose name the whole book bears steps onto the stage in his own time. "I, Mormon, make a record of the things which I have both seen and heard, and call it the Book of Mormon" (1:1). Ammaron, hiding up the records, comes to the ten-year-old Mormon: "I perceive that thou art a sober child, and art quick to observe" (1:2) — and commissions him to take the plates of Nephi at twenty-four and "engrave on the plates of Nephi all the things that ye have observed concerning this people" (1:4). But Mormon is born into the collapse 4 Nephi described: war resumes (1:8-11), and "wickedness did prevail... insomuch that the Lord did take away his beloved disciples, and the work of miracles and of healing did cease... and there were no gifts from the Lord, and the Holy Ghost did not come upon any, because of their wickedness and unbelief" (1:13-14). Yet at fifteen Mormon "was visited of the Lord, and tasted and knew of the goodness of Jesus" (1:15) — and when he tries to preach, "my mouth was shut, and I was forbidden... for behold they had wilfully rebelled against their God" (1:16-17).

**Read:** Mormon 1 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 19 verses. The boy, the commission, and the silence that fell on a people past the reach of the word.

## Thoughts

We have been reading "I, Mormon" for the whole back half of the Book of Mormon — the editorial voice that bridged the small plates (Words of Mormon), that abridged Mosiah through 3 Nephi, that interrupted to say "I know not" and then "I have inquired" (3 Ne 28). Now, at last, that voice steps into its own moment and tells us who he was. And the first thing we learn is how he was chosen. Ammaron, the last of the record-keepers of 4 Nephi, "constrained by the Holy Ghost" to hide the records, looks at a ten-year-old boy and sees the two qualities a record-keeper needs: "I perceive that thou art a sober child, and art quick to observe." Sober — serious, weighty, trustworthy, not given to frivolity. Quick to observe — perceptive, attentive, the kind of mind that notices and remembers. These are the qualities of a true witness and a faithful historian, and they were visible in a child. Mormon was not chosen for lineage (though he was a descendant of Nephi) or for office or for learning, though he had begun "to be learned somewhat after the manner of the learning of my people." He was chosen for *character* — for the kind of person he already was at ten. The Lord's record would pass to the man who, as a boy, was sober and saw clearly.

And the commission given is precisely the commission of a witness: *observe, remember, record*. "When ye are about twenty and four years old I would that ye should remember the things that ye have observed concerning this people... and ye shall engrave on the plates of Nephi all the things that ye have observed." Notice the fourteen-year gap built into it — Ammaron tells the ten-year-old to come for the plates at twenty-four. The boy is to *carry* the charge for fourteen years, observing all the while, remembering. The whole intervening time is part of the assignment: Mormon's task from age ten was to watch his people with the eyes of one who would one day have to write them down truthfully. To be told as a child "you will be the one who records this" is to be told "watch carefully, because it will matter what you saw." There is something formative in that — a person entrusted in advance with bearing witness lives differently, attends differently, than one who merely happens upon events. Mormon's whole youth was a long act of observation under commission.

But the world he was commissioned to observe was collapsing, and verses 13-14 record the most chilling reversal in the book. After the war and a brief peace, "wickedness did prevail upon the face of the whole land, insomuch that the Lord did take away his beloved disciples [the three Nephites], and the work of miracles and of healing did cease because of the iniquity of the people. And there were no gifts from the Lord, and the Holy Ghost did not come upon any, because of their wickedness and unbelief." Everything 4 Nephi's golden age had — miracles, healings, the gifts, the constant presence of the Spirit — is withdrawn. And this is the exact counterpart to Mormon's own warning in 3 Nephi 29, which I just walked: there he pronounced a woe on anyone who would say "the Lord no longer worketh by revelation, or by gifts... or by healings." The resolution of the apparent tension is precise and important. God did not stop working by gifts — God changes not. The *people* cut themselves off from the gifts. "There were no gifts from the Lord... *because of their wickedness and unbelief.*" The gifts cease not because the heavens close from above but because hearts close from below. The cessationism Mormon condemned is the error of blaming God for a withdrawal the people caused. Miracles still flow wherever faith opens the channel (Moroni will spell this out exactly in Moroni 7:35-37 and Mormon 9); they dry up only where unbelief stops it. The same God who poured out gifts on the believing Nephites of 4 Nephi withheld them from the unbelieving Nephites of Mormon 1 — and the variable was never God.

And then, against that black backdrop, verse 15: "And I, being fifteen years of age and being somewhat of a sober mind, therefore I was visited of the Lord, and tasted and knew of the goodness of Jesus." In a generation from which the Holy Ghost had withdrawn, from which the gifts had ceased, one fifteen-year-old was visited by the Lord and came to *know* — not believe, know — the goodness of Jesus personally. The word "therefore" is doing real work: he was of a sober mind, *therefore* he was visited. The sobriety Ammaron saw at ten became, at fifteen, the openness that the Lord could visit even when the whole nation was closed. The gifts had not ceased absolutely; they had ceased *generally*, for a people in unbelief — but the individual whose heart stayed sober and open could still taste the goodness of Jesus. This is the hope buried in the doctrine of verse 14: the withdrawal of gifts from a wicked people does not seal off the individual who keeps faith. Mormon, alone in a faithless generation, still knew the Lord. ("Tasted... the goodness of Jesus" — Psalm 34:8, "O taste and see that the Lord is good"; Alma 36:26, the converted who have "tasted as I have tasted." It is the language of personal, experiential knowledge, not secondhand report.)

But the most sobering turn is verses 16-17, and it is a doctrine I had not weighed carefully. This converted, Lord-visited young man, who knew the goodness of Jesus and naturally wanted to share it, "did endeavor to preach unto this people, but my mouth was shut, and I was forbidden that I should preach unto them; for behold they had wilfully rebelled against their God." Mormon *wanted* to preach. He was *forbidden*. Not by men — by the Lord. Because the people "had wilfully rebelled," and "because of the hardness of their hearts." There is a threshold of wilful rebellion past which the Lord withdraws even the offer of the word — not as arbitrary cruelty but as the terrible natural consequence of persistent rejection. This is the "famine... of hearing the words of the Lord" that Amos prophesied (Amos 8:11-12), the silence Micah described falling on those who would not listen (Micah 3:6-7, the footnote). A people can reject the word so thoroughly that the word is taken away — that the very preacher who would speak it is told to be silent. The contrast with 3 Nephi 30 is stark and deliberate: there the risen Christ left the door open with "come unto me"; here, a few generations of wilful rebellion later, the door has closed from the inside, and even a willing missionary is forbidden to knock. The withdrawal of the word is the people's own work, ratified by the Lord. Mercy keeps the door open as long as there is anyone reaching for it; when a people wilfully bar it, the Lord honors even that terrible choice.

The chapter ends on the cursed land and the fulfillment of the prophets (vv18-19). The Gadianton robbers infest; people hide their treasures, which "became slippery... that they could not hold them" — exactly as Samuel the Lamanite had prophesied (Hel 13:18-37). Sorceries, witchcrafts, and magics spread, "even unto the fulfilling of all the words of Abinadi, and also Samuel the Lamanite." The collapse is not random; it is *prophesied*, and its arrival vindicates the prophets who were killed or rejected for saying it would come. Abinadi, burned by Noah's priests; Samuel, shot at on the wall — their words are now coming true on the very people who rejected them. The fulfilled word is the witness (the principle of 3 Ne 23, Hel 8:20-21), and here the witness is terrible: the destruction itself proves the prophets right. Mormon, the sober boy who was told to observe and record, is watching the prophecies he learned come to pass — and he will spend his life writing down a collapse that the prophets had already written down in advance.

## Notable

Yes — the book of Mormon proper opens. **The record passes to character — "a sober child, and quick to observe"** (1:2-5, Mormon chosen as a boy for character, not credentials); **the commission of the witness — observe, remember, record** (1:3-4, a fourteen-year charge to watch with the eyes of one who will write it down); **the gifts cease through the people's unbelief, not God's change** (1:13-14, the inverse of 3 Ne 29's cessationism warning — the variable is never God); **the individual of a sober mind is visited even in a faithless age** (1:15, Mormon at fifteen tastes and knows the goodness of Jesus when the Spirit had withdrawn from everyone else); **the willing preacher forbidden — the word withdrawn from wilful rebellion** (1:16-17, the famine of the word as judgment; the door 3 Ne 30 left open now closed from the inside); and **the collapse fulfills the prophets — Abinadi and Samuel vindicated** (1:18-19, the destruction itself proves the rejected prophets right).
