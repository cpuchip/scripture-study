# Mormon 5 — leading without hope; why the record comes forth; the Spirit ceased to strive; the covenant remembered

**Binding idea:** Mormon "did repent of the oath" and takes command again, "for they looked upon me as though I could deliver them" (5:1) — "but behold, I was without hope, for I knew the judgments of the Lord which should come upon them; for they repented not... but did struggle for their lives without calling upon that Being who created them" (5:2). He explains to the latter-day reader why he writes: "these things must surely be made known... a knowledge of these things must come unto the remnant of these people, and also unto the Gentiles" (5:8-9). The record's purpose: "that they may be persuaded that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of the living God; that the Father may bring about... his great and eternal purpose, in restoring the Jews, or all the house of Israel, to the land of their inheritance... unto the fulfilling of his covenant" (5:14). "The Spirit of the Lord hath already ceased to strive with their fathers... they were once a delightsome people, and they had Christ for their shepherd... but now... they are led about by Satan, even as chaff is driven before the wind" (5:16-18). Yet "after they have been driven and scattered by the Gentiles, behold, then will the Lord remember the covenant which he made unto Abraham" (5:20).

**Read:** Mormon 5 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 24 verses. Leading without hope, the record's purpose, and the covenant that outlasts the fall.

## Thoughts

The chapter opens on one of the most quietly moving acts in Mormon's life: he comes back. Having "utterly refused" to lead the vengeance-war (3:11), he now "did repent of the oath which I had made that I would no more assist them," and takes command again — "for they looked upon me as though I could deliver them from their afflictions." And then the devastating clause: "But behold, I was *without hope*, for I knew the judgments of the Lord which should come upon them." This is a kind of faithfulness most of us never have to test. Mormon returns to lead a people he *knows* are doomed — not because he expects victory, not because he thinks they will repent, but because they look to him, and he loves them, and someone must stand at their head as they die. He leads without hope. There is no expectation of success in it, no possibility of the outcome he would want; there is only the decision to be with his people and bear the burden to the end. The man who refused to lead them into evil will lead them into a death he cannot prevent, because leading them in their last extremity is not evil — it is love without illusion. And the detail that seals their doom is verse 2's other half: "they repented not of their iniquities, but did struggle for their lives without calling upon that Being who created them." They fought desperately to survive and never once turned to God. The will to live without the willingness to repent — frantic effort aimed at everything except the one thing that could have saved them.

The center of the chapter is Mormon explaining, again, why the record is being written and preserved — and it is the clearest statement of the Book of Mormon's purpose. He does not want to write it: "I, Mormon, do not desire to harrow up the souls of men in casting before them such an awful scene of blood and carnage" (5:8). But he writes anyway, "knowing that these things must surely be made known" — and he writes "a small abridgment, daring not to give a full account... that ye might not have too great sorrow because of the wickedness of this people" (5:9). Even here the record-keeper exercises mercy in the editing: he tells the truth but spares the reader the full weight of the horror, the same forbearance he showed in 2:18. And then the purpose, verse 14, stated with total clarity: the record will "go unto the unbelieving of the Jews... that they may be persuaded that *Jesus is the Christ, the Son of the living God*; that the Father may bring about, through his most Beloved, his great and eternal purpose, in restoring the Jews, or all the house of Israel, to the land of their inheritance... unto the fulfilling of his covenant." Two intertwined purposes: to *persuade* — that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of the living God (Peter's confession again, the same doubled divine claim as 3:21) — and to *gather*, to bring about the restoration of Israel that is the fulfillment of the Abrahamic covenant. The book is both a witness of Christ and an instrument of the gathering; conversion and covenant are not separate works but one. (Another clean datapoint for the *is-jesus-christ-god* study: the stated purpose of the record is to persuade that Jesus is "the Christ, the Son of the living God.")

Verse 15 carries the hardest language in the chapter, and it belongs to the same complex as 2 Nephi 5:21 that has run through this whole walk — so I name it and leave it for Michael and the Spirit, as the standing decision requires. Mormon writes that "this people shall be scattered, and shall become *a dark, a filthy, and a loathsome people*, beyond the description of that which ever hath been amongst us, yea, even that which hath been among the Lamanites, and this *because of their unbelief and idolatry*." What I can observe honestly: the verse explicitly names the *cause* — "because of their unbelief and idolatry," a spiritual condition, not an ancestry. The word "dark" sits in a triad with "filthy" and "loathsome," which are unmistakably moral and spiritual descriptors, not complexion. And the comparison is striking: it is said of the *Nephite* seed — the once "delightsome" covenant people (v17) — that they will become *worse* than "that which hath been among the Lamanites." The formerly-favored will fall further than the formerly-cursed. (The cross-reference on "become" chains to 2 Ne 26:33, "all are alike unto God.") All of that points the same direction as the cumulative finding of the walk: the language tracks a spiritual state, caused by unbelief and idolatry, and applied here to the people who had every advantage and threw it away. But the literal "dark" remains the unreconciled residue I have flagged throughout — and per the standing instruction, that residue is for Michael and the Spirit, not for me to pronounce on. I log it; I do not resolve it.

The image of what the loss of the Spirit *does* to a people is among the most vivid in the book (5:16-18). "The Spirit of the Lord hath already ceased to strive with their fathers; and they are without Christ and God in the world; and they are driven about as chaff before the wind." And the contrast that makes it unbearable: "They were once a delightsome people, and they had Christ for their shepherd; yea, they were led even by God the Father. But now... they are led about by Satan, even as chaff is driven before the wind, or as a vessel is tossed about upon the waves, without sail or anchor, or without anything wherewith to steer her." Two images, both about *loss of direction*. Chaff has no will of its own — it goes wherever the wind takes it, the empty husk separated from the grain (Ps 1:4). And the rudderless ship — "without sail or anchor, or without anything wherewith to steer her" — is tossed by every wave, going nowhere it chooses. This is what the Spirit's withdrawal produces: not merely the absence of a feeling but the loss of all bearings. A people led by God the Father had a shepherd, a direction, an anchor; a people led by Satan have none — they are driven, tossed, steered by every passing force, masters of nothing including themselves. The freedom they thought they were seizing by throwing off God is revealed as the opposite of freedom: to be without the Spirit is to be chaff, to be a ship no one is steering. (Ephesians 4:14 names the same thing — "tossed to and fro... carried about with every wind of doctrine" — as the condition the gospel is meant to cure.)

But the chapter does not end in the chaff and the wreck, because the covenant outlasts the fall (5:19-21). "After they have been driven and scattered by the Gentiles, behold, then will the Lord remember the covenant which he made unto Abraham and unto all the house of Israel." The scattering is not the last word. There is a "then" — after the driving, after the scattering, after centuries as chaff, the Lord *remembers* the Abrahamic covenant, and the remnant is gathered. The covenant is not voided by the people's unbelief; it waits, on God's side, to be fulfilled "in his own due time" (5:13). And there is a second thing the Lord remembers: "the prayers of the righteous, which have been put up unto him for them" (5:21). The prayers of the faithful dead — Enos pleading for the Lamanites' preservation (Enos 1:12-18), generations of prophets praying for a people who would not listen — those prayers are *banked*, remembered by God, and answered long after the ones who prayed have died. A prayer offered in faith for a wayward people is not wasted even if that generation never repents; it is held by God and brought to bear on their seed centuries later. The righteous who prayed for the Lamanites did not see the answer; the answer is coming anyway, through this very record, to their distant descendants.

The chapter closes by turning the warning on the *Gentiles* (5:22-24) — the people who would possess the land and into whose hands the record would come. "O ye Gentiles, how can ye stand before the power of God, except ye shall repent and turn from your evil ways? Know ye not that ye are in the hands of God?" The Gentiles are not spectators to this tragedy; they are participants under the same terms. They will scatter the remnant, they will possess the land, they will receive the record — and they too must repent, or "a remnant of the seed of Jacob shall go forth among you as a lion... and tear you in pieces, and there is none to deliver" (5:24; 3 Ne 20:16). The covenant cuts both ways: the same God who will remember Israel will also hold the Gentiles to account. There is no possessing the land of promise on easier terms than the Nephites had. Mormon, the idle witness, ends his explanation of why the record exists by warning the very people who will hold it — because the whole point of preserving the account of a civilization destroyed by unbelief is to spare the next civilization the same fate, if it will hear.
