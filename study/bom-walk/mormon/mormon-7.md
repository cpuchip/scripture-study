# Mormon 7 — the dying prophet's farewell: "know ye that ye are of the house of Israel"

**Binding idea:** Mormon's last words, addressed not to his own annihilated people but to "the remnant of this people who are spared" (7:1) — the descendants of the Lamanites who destroyed his nation. No curse; only invitation, in a series of tender imperatives: "Know ye that ye are of the house of Israel" (7:2); "Know ye that ye must come unto repentance, or ye cannot be saved" (7:3); "Know ye that ye must lay down your weapons of war, and delight no more in the shedding of blood" (7:4); "believe in Jesus Christ, that he is the Son of God, and that he was slain by the Jews, and by the power of the Father he hath risen again, whereby he hath gained the victory over the grave" (7:5). The two records confirm each other: "if ye believe that [the Bible] ye will believe this [the record] also" (7:9). And the closing promise: "if it so be that ye believe in Christ, and are baptized, first with water, then with fire and with the Holy Ghost... it shall be well with you in the day of judgment. Amen" (7:10).

## Thoughts

This is the end of Mormon's own record — after this, his son Moroni takes the plates — and the astonishing thing about how he chooses to close is *who he addresses*. He has just buried (in words) his entire people, hundreds of thousands of them, slaughtered by the Lamanites. He has poured out his anguish over their moldering bodies. And now, for his final words, he turns to *the Lamanites* — to "the remnant of this people who are spared," the descendants of the very people who destroyed his nation — and he blesses them. There is not one syllable of bitterness, not one curse on his enemies, not one word of vengeance. The man whose whole civilization the Lamanites annihilated spends his last breath on the page trying to *save the Lamanites' children*. This is the practical shape of "love your enemies" carried to its furthest possible extreme: a dying man whose people were exterminated, using his final words to plead with the exterminators' descendants to come to Christ and be saved. It mirrors the Savior's own close in 3 Nephi 30 — the open invitation extended to those outside, "come unto me." Mormon, at the end, sounds exactly like the Christ he witnessed: the door held open, even to those who did the killing.

The sermon is built on a fourfold "Know ye," and the order is theologically deliberate. First, *identity*: "Know ye that ye are of the house of Israel" (7:2). Before repentance, before belief, before anything else — know who you *are*. These Lamanites, who have no idea they are covenant people, who have been taught for centuries to hate the Nephites, are in fact the remnant of Jacob, heirs of the Abrahamic covenant. Mormon begins not with their sin but with their belonging. You cannot call a people home until they know they have a home; the first gift is the knowledge of their own identity. Then *repentance*: "ye must come unto repentance, or ye cannot be saved" (7:3) — the unconditional requirement, stated plainly. Then the specific renunciation that their whole history demanded: "ye must lay down your weapons of war, and delight no more in the shedding of blood, and take them not again, save it be that God shall command you" (7:4). This is pointed precisely at the sin that destroyed both peoples — the delight in bloodshed (4:11). Mormon, the lifelong general who came to hate the war he was so good at, tells the survivors: lay it down. Delight in blood no more. The only exception is the one the Nephites had at their best and abandoned at their worst — defensive war only, and only at God's command (Alma 43:47). The renunciation of violence is not incidental to the gospel Mormon preaches; it is near the center of it, because violence is what he watched consume everyone he loved.

And then the gospel itself, compressed into its essentials (7:5-7): "believe in Jesus Christ, that he is the Son of God, and that he was slain by the Jews, and by the power of the Father he hath risen again, whereby he hath gained the victory over the grave; and also in him is the sting of death swallowed up. And he bringeth to pass the resurrection of the dead, whereby man must be raised to stand before his judgment-seat. And he hath brought to pass the redemption of the world." This is the whole gospel in three sentences — Christ's divine sonship, His death, His resurrection, His victory over the grave (the "sting of death swallowed up" is Paul's triumph from 1 Cor 15:54-55, itself drawn from Isaiah 25:8), the universal resurrection, the judgment, the redemption of the world. And the destiny held out to the guiltless is rendered in language of pure joy: "to dwell in the presence of God in his kingdom, to sing ceaseless praises with the choirs above, unto the Father, and unto the Son, and unto the Holy Ghost, *which are one God*, in a state of happiness which hath no end." Note the doubled witness again — "the Father, and the Son, and the Holy Ghost, which are one God" (Deut 6:4; D&C 20:28). Mormon's farewell, like his statement of the record's purpose (3:21, 5:14), affirms the oneness of the Godhead: three named, one God. (Another datapoint for the *is-jesus-christ-god* study — the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost confessed as "one God.") And there is a tenderness in offering *that* vision — ceaseless praise in unending happiness — to people whose lives Mormon knew only as war: he holds out to the children of bloodshed a destiny of song.

The center of the chapter for the Restoration is verses 8-9, the clearest statement anywhere of how the Bible and the Book of Mormon relate. The remnant is told to "lay hold upon the gospel of Christ, which shall be set before you, *not only in this record but also in the record which shall come unto the Gentiles from the Jews*, which record shall come from the Gentiles unto you." Two records: the Bible (from the Jews, through the Gentiles) and the Book of Mormon (this record, also through the Gentiles). And then the reciprocal logic: "this is written for the intent that ye may believe *that*; and if ye believe *that* ye will believe *this* also." Each book is written to lead to the other. The Book of Mormon exists to confirm the Bible ("that ye may believe that"); and belief in the Bible naturally opens to belief in the Book of Mormon ("if ye believe that ye will believe this also"). The two are not rivals; they are mutual witnesses, each establishing the other's truth (2 Ne 29:8, the doctrine of two nations, two witnesses; 1 Ne 13:40, the latter record establishing the truth of the first). This is the answer to the false dichotomy that pits the books against each other: the Book of Mormon does not replace the Bible or compete with it — it was written precisely *so that* people would believe the Bible, and it expects that those who truly believe the Bible will recognize it. The "other witness" of 3:21 is named here as a two-way confirmation.

And the chapter closes — and Mormon's whole record closes — on identity, path, and promise woven together (7:10): "ye will also know that ye are a remnant of the seed of Jacob; therefore ye are numbered among the people of the first covenant; and if it so be that ye believe in Christ, and are baptized, first with water, then with fire and with the Holy Ghost, following the example of our Savior... it shall be well with you in the day of judgment. Amen." It returns to where it began — *you are of Israel, you are of the first covenant* — and lays out the path (believe, be baptized of water then of fire and the Holy Ghost, following Christ's own example, 3 Ne 19:13; 2 Ne 31) and the promise: "it shall be well with you in the day of judgment." That is the last thing Mormon writes. Not a lament, though he had every reason for one; not a curse, though his people lay slaughtered; but a benediction over his enemies' descendants — *it shall be well with you*. The "Amen" seals a life spent watching wickedness, leading a doomed people, and burying a civilization, and the seal is a blessing on the ones who would come after, the ones who might yet believe. The whole arc of Mormon's life — the sober child, the boy general, the idle witness, the grieving father over the slain — resolves into this final posture: hope held out to the future, even from the ashes. He could not save his own people. He gave everything to save the people his record would one day reach.

Yes — the last words of Mormon, a benediction on the descendants of his enemies. **The dying prophet's last words bless the descendants of his enemies** (7:1-2, love of enemies at its furthest extreme; no curse, only invitation); **identity comes first — "know ye that ye are of the house of Israel"** (7:2, 10, you cannot call a people home until they know they have one); **lay down your weapons, delight no more in the shedding of blood** (7:4, the renunciation of the violence that destroyed both peoples); **the compressed gospel — Son of God, slain, risen, victor over the grave** (7:5-7, the whole gospel in three sentences, the sting of death swallowed up); **the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are one God** (7:7, *is-jesus-christ-god* datapoint); **the Bible and the Book of Mormon are mutual witnesses** (7:8-9, "if ye believe that ye will believe this also" — each written to lead to the other); and **"it shall be well with you" — the last word is blessing, not lament** (7:10).
