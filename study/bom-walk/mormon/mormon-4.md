# Mormon 4 — the offensive war turns; "by the wicked the wicked are punished"; the peak of wickedness

**Binding idea:** The offensive war the Nephites swore in chapter 3 turns against them. "It was because the armies of the Nephites went up unto the Lamanites that they began to be smitten; for were it not for that, the Lamanites could have had no power over them" (4:4). Mormon names the theology of the judgment: "the judgments of God will overtake the wicked; and it is by the wicked that the wicked are punished; for it is the wicked that stir up the hearts of the children of men unto bloodshed" (4:5). The violence reaches a peak never before seen: "every heart was hardened, so that they delighted in the shedding of blood continually. And there never had been so great wickedness among all the children of Lehi, nor even among all the house of Israel... as was among this people" (4:11-12). Women and children are "offer[ed] up as sacrifices unto their idol gods" (4:14, 21). And from the 375th year, "the Nephites gain no power over the Lamanites, but began to be swept off by them even as a dew before the sun" (4:18). Mormon goes to the hill Shim and "did take up all the records which Ammaron had hid up unto the Lord" (4:23).

**Read:** Mormon 4 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 23 verses. The offensive war's collapse, the peak of wickedness, and the securing of the record.

## Thoughts

The chapter is mostly the grim mechanics of a civilization's death — cities taken and retaken, thousands slain on both sides, advance and retreat — but two verses carry the doctrinal weight, and they explain the whole collapse. The first is verse 4: "it was because the armies of the Nephites went up unto the Lamanites that they began to be smitten; for were it not for that, the Lamanites could have had no power over them." This is the direct consequence of chapter 3's vengeance-oath. The moment the Nephites abandoned the defensive ground — the only ground on which a covenant people may fight (Alma 48:14) — and went *up* on the offensive, sworn to "cut off" their enemies, they forfeited the protection that had been theirs. Mormon is precise about the counterfactual: "were it not for that, the Lamanites could have had no power over them." Had they stayed defensive, they could not have been beaten. The aggression itself stripped their defense. There is a hard principle here that runs against every instinct of wounded pride: the turn to vengeance does not make a people stronger; it makes them vulnerable, because it severs them from the only strength that was ever really theirs. The Nephites thought going on the offensive would let them avenge their dead; instead it began the process of their being swept away.

The second weight-bearing verse is one of the most penetrating statements about divine judgment in scripture: "the judgments of God will overtake the wicked; and *it is by the wicked that the wicked are punished*; for it is the wicked that stir up the hearts of the children of men unto bloodshed" (4:5). This is Mormon's theology of how judgment actually works, and it is not the popular picture of God hurling thunderbolts. God's judgment, much of the time, is simply *letting sin run its course* — and the course sin runs is self-destruction. The Lamanites (wicked) are the instrument by which the Nephites (wicked) are punished; the Nephites are the instrument by which the Lamanites are punished. The wicked are at once the punishers and the punished, because wickedness is inherently self-consuming: "it is the wicked that stir up the hearts of the children of men unto bloodshed." God does not need to import an external punishment for a people fully given to evil; their evil punishes them through each other. This is sobering precisely because it requires no divine intervention to explain — a world of hardened hearts will tear itself apart by its own logic, and that tearing *is* the judgment of God, executed by the wicked upon the wicked. It also quietly answers a question that haunts these chapters: how can God use the Lamanites (who are themselves wicked, who sacrifice children to idols) to punish the Nephites? The answer is that He is not endorsing the Lamanites; He is permitting wickedness to do what wickedness does. The instrument is as guilty as the target. Both are being punished, by each other, at once.

And then the chapter records a peak of evil it explicitly says cannot be described: "it is impossible for the tongue to describe, or for man to write a perfect description of the horrible scene of the blood and carnage... and every heart was hardened, so that they *delighted in the shedding of blood continually*" (4:11). The final stage of hardening is not merely tolerating bloodshed but *delighting* in it — violence become pleasure, the conscience so dead that the killing of others is enjoyed. And Mormon's assessment is staggering in its scope: "there never had been so great wickedness among all the children of Lehi, nor even among all the house of Israel... as was among this people." Worse than anyone in the entire history of Israel. The cross-reference is to Genesis 6:5 — the wickedness before the Flood, when "every imagination of the thoughts of [man's] heart was only evil continually." This is Noah-level corruption, the kind that precedes total destruction, and Mormon is telling us his people had reached it.

The specific horror he names is the sacrifice of women and children to idols (4:14, 21) — first by the Lamanites against captured Nephites, and the detail recurs, which means it had become routine. This is the deepest pit a people can fall into: the idolatry that demands the murder of innocents, the same abomination that defiled the valley of Hinnom in Jeremiah's day (Jer 19:5) and that nearly took Abraham's own life on the altar (Abr 1:8). And the Nephite response is its own indictment: when the Lamanites sacrificed their women and children, the Nephites went against them "with exceedingly great anger" (4:15) — not with repentance, not with grief that turned them to God, but with rage. The slaughter of their innocents did not soften them toward heaven; it hardened them further toward vengeance. A people who respond to atrocity only with greater fury, never with the question "why has God's protection left us?", are a people whose hearts are past softening. The atrocities feed the war, the war feeds the atrocities, and neither side ever turns to God — the cycle of vengeance the Lord had forbidden (3:15) running now to its end.

The image of the end is unforgettable: from the 375th year, "the Nephites gain no power over the Lamanites, but began to be swept off by them even as a dew before the sun" (4:18). Dew before the sun — the morning moisture that simply *evaporates* as the sun rises, gone without a struggle, leaving no trace. After all the boasting in their own strength, this is what they come to: not a heroic last stand but evaporation, swept away as effortlessly as the sun burns off the dew. The image is almost gentle in its physics and total in its meaning. A people who would not be gathered as Christ longed to gather them (3 Ne 10:4-6, "as a hen gathereth her chickens") are now swept away as dew. The verbs of the Savior's love and the verbs of their destruction are both verbs of being-acted-upon; they would not be gathered, so they are swept off.

And the chapter ends on the one constant through all of it — the record. "Now I, Mormon, seeing that the Lamanites were about to overthrow the land, therefore I did go to the hill Shim, and did take up all the records which Ammaron had hid up unto the Lord" (4:23). As his civilization evaporates, Mormon does the one thing that will outlast it: he secures the full archive. He had taken the plates of Nephi years before (2:17); now, seeing the end, he retrieves *all* the sacred records Ammaron had hidden. This is the "idle witness" of chapter 3 made concrete — while he can no longer save his people, he can save the record of them, and the record of all that came before them, for us. There is something quietly heroic in a man walking to a hill to gather books while the world burns down around him, because he knows the books will reach a people the swords never could. Everything else the Nephites built is being swept off as dew; the record Mormon carries out of the collapse is the one thing that survives — and it is the thing that matters, because it is the witness that Jesus is the Christ. The civilization dies; the testimony walks out alive in Mormon's arms.
