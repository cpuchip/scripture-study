# Mormon 6 — Cumorah: the record hidden, the nation fallen, and "O ye fair ones"

**Binding idea:** The end. The Nephites gather to Cumorah for "the last struggle" (6:6). Mormon, "begin[ning] to be old," secures the record: "I made this record out of the plates of Nephi, and hid up in the hill Cumorah all the records which had been entrusted to me by the hand of the Lord, save it were these few plates which I gave unto my son Moroni" (6:6). Then the slaughter — Mormon's ten thousand "hewn down," and Mormon "fell wounded in the midst" (6:10); the roll call of the fallen commanders, each "with his ten thousand" (6:11-15); "their flesh, and bones, and blood lay upon the face of the earth... to molder upon the land" (6:15). And the lament: "my soul was rent with anguish... and I cried: O ye fair ones, how could ye have departed from the ways of the Lord! O ye fair ones, how could ye have rejected that Jesus, who stood with open arms to receive you! Behold, if ye had not done this, ye would not have fallen" (6:16-18). He commits them to God: "the Eternal Father of heaven, knoweth your state; and he doeth with you according to his justice and mercy" (6:22).

## Thoughts

Before the battle, Mormon does the most consequential thing in the chapter — the thing that means we are reading these words at all. "I made this record out of the plates of Nephi, and hid up in the hill Cumorah all the records which had been entrusted to me by the hand of the Lord, save it were these few plates which I gave unto my son Moroni." This is the hinge of the whole Book of Mormon's survival. Mormon separates the archive: the *full* set of sacred records he hides in Cumorah (where they remain), and the *abridgment* he made — the plates of Mormon, the book that bears his name — he gives to Moroni, who will finish it and bury it, and from whom Joseph Smith will receive it fourteen centuries later (D&C 128:20; JS-H 1:52). At the very moment of national death, the record is secured for the future. There is a profound order of priorities in it: with his civilization hours from annihilation, with his ten thousand about to die under his own command, Mormon's last administrative act is to make sure the *record* survives. He understood what mattered. The bodies would molder into the earth; the testimony would reach a people across an ocean and a millennium. He spent his life as the sober child commissioned to observe and record, and here, at the end, the record is the one thing he carries out of the fire — handed to his son, who alone among the next generation could be trusted to finish it.

The battle itself is rendered with a terrible restraint, and then a roll call that makes the abstraction unbearable. "My men were hewn down, yea, even my ten thousand who were with me, and I fell wounded in the midst; and they passed by me that they did not put an end to my life." And then, from the top of the hill the next morning, Mormon counts the dead — not as a number but by *name*: "the ten thousand of my people who were hewn down, being led in the front by me. And we also beheld the ten thousand of my people who were led by my son Moroni. And behold, the ten thousand of Gidgiddonah had fallen... and Lamah had fallen with his ten thousand; and Gilgal had fallen with his ten thousand; and Limhah... and Jeneum... and Cumenihah, and Moronihah, and Antionum, and Shiblom, and Shem, and Josh, had fallen with their ten thousand each. And... there were ten more who did fall by the sword, with their ten thousand each." Count them: twenty-three named ten-thousands, plus Mormon's and Moroni's — well over two hundred thousand dead. But the genius and the agony of the passage is the *naming*. Mormon does not write "hundreds of thousands fell." He names the captains — Gidgiddonah, Lamah, Gilgal, Limhah — each a man he knew, each leading ten thousand more, all dead. The roll call refuses to let the dead be a statistic. Every name is a man, and behind every name, ten thousand more. And then the final image strips away even the names: "their flesh, and bones, and blood lay upon the face of the earth, being left by the hands of those who slew them to molder upon the land, and to crumble and to return to their mother earth." A whole people reduced to flesh moldering in the open, unburied, returning to the dust — the end of the children of Lehi, a thousand years after the family sailed from Jerusalem.

And then the lament, which is among the most piercing passages in all of scripture (6:16-22). "My soul was rent with anguish, because of the slain of my people, and I cried: *O ye fair ones, how could ye have departed from the ways of the Lord!*" The cry is not anger, not even primarily judgment — it is *grief*, the grief of a father over children, of a shepherd over a slaughtered flock. And the center of the grief, verse 17, names the true tragedy: "O ye fair ones, how could ye have *rejected that Jesus, who stood with open arms to receive you!*" This is the deepest sorrow — not that they died, but that they need not have, because Jesus stood the whole time *with open arms*. The image is Christ's own: "his arms of mercy are extended towards them, and they will not" (2 Ne 26:7); "how oft would I have gathered you as a hen gathereth her chickens... and ye would not" (3 Ne 10:5). The arms were open the entire time. The Nephites did not fall because God abandoned them or because fate decreed it; they fell because they turned away from open arms. "Behold, if ye had not done this, ye would not have fallen." The whole catastrophe was *avoidable* — that is what makes it unbearable. Every one of those moldering bodies could have been clasped in the arms of Jesus. Mormon's anguish is the anguish of watching a preventable death, multiplied by hundreds of thousands, and knowing that the One who would have saved them never stopped offering.

And the litany of verse 19 makes the dead human in a way the ten-thousands could not: "O ye fair sons and daughters, ye fathers and mothers, ye husbands and wives, ye fair ones, how is it that ye could have fallen!" These were not soldiers in the abstract — they were *families*. Sons and daughters, fathers and mothers, husbands and wives. The relationships are named one by one because that is what was destroyed: not an army but a web of love, every bond severed by the sword. Mormon grieves them as what they were to each other, not as combatants. And then the helplessness that every grief knows: "ye are gone, and my sorrows cannot bring your return." No amount of mourning reverses death. The prophet who could lead armies cannot bring back one of the dead.

But the lament does not end in despair, and the turn is the measure of Mormon's faith. From the moldering bodies he lifts his eyes to the resurrection: "the day soon cometh that your mortal must put on immortality, and these bodies which are now moldering in corruption must soon become incorruptible bodies; and then ye must stand before the judgment-seat of Christ" (6:21; 1 Cor 15:53-54). The bodies crumbling into the earth will rise. And the dead will be judged "according to their works" — and Mormon does not pronounce their doom. He leaves the door open: "if it so be that ye are righteous, then are ye blessed with your fathers who have gone before you." Even of a people destroyed for wickedness, he will not presume to declare them lost; the judgment is Christ's, not his. And the final committal, verse 22, is the act of faith that makes the whole lament bearable: "O that ye had repented before this great destruction had come upon you. But behold, ye are gone, and the Father, yea, the Eternal Father of heaven, *knoweth your state; and he doeth with you according to his justice and mercy*." Mormon hands his dead to God. He does not know their eternal fate, and he does not claim to — but he knows two things about the God who does: that God *knows their state* (every heart, every circumstance, every degree of light they had), and that God acts "according to his justice *and mercy*." Not justice alone, which would terrify; not mercy alone, which would be sentimental; but both, perfectly held. After watching his entire people destroy themselves by rejecting Christ, the last word Mormon can say over their moldering bodies is a word of *trust* — trust that the Father who knows them will do right by them, in justice and in mercy. That is where a faithful man lands when he has done everything he could and it was not enough: not in despair, not in presumption, but in committing the unbearable to a God he trusts with both justice and mercy.
