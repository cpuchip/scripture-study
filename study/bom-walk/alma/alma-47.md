# Alma 47 — "come down from the mount": the anatomy of treachery

**Binding idea:** Amalickiah climbs to the Lamanite throne by a chain of betrayals, each one weaponizing someone's trust. Given command of the king's loyal troops, he secretly conspires with the rebel leader Lehonti — but only after luring him off his secure mountain ("come down... and bring his guards with him," 47:12). Then he poisons Lehonti "by degrees" (47:18), inherits the command by custom, marches on the king, has a servant stab the trusting king "to the heart" (47:24), frames the king's servants, marries the widowed queen by false witnesses, and "by his fraud... obtained the kingdom" (47:35). And Mormon's editorial on the Nephite dissenters who back him: "having been instructed in the same knowledge of the Lord, nevertheless, it is strange to relate, not long after their dissensions they became... more wild, wicked and ferocious than the Lamanites" (47:36).

**Read:** Alma 47 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

This chapter is the dark portrait the war record needs — a study of pure, patient treachery, set against Moroni's transparency in the chapters around it. Amalickiah is the anti-Moroni: where Moroni rends his coat in public and prays before he acts, Amalickiah works entirely by "secret embassy" (v10), poison administered "by degrees" (v18), and servants who lie on command (v34). And the structure of his rise is worth tracing, because it's a recognizable anatomy: every step uses someone's trust as the lever. The king trusts him with command — he uses it to position himself. Lehonti trusts the deal — he poisons him. The king trusts the token of peace — he's stabbed mid-handshake. The pursuers trust Amalickiah's feigned grief — they chase the innocent. The queen trusts the witnesses — they're the murderers. Treachery isn't brute force; it's the systematic conversion of other people's trust into the means of their destruction. Ambition without conscience doesn't break trust openly; it harvests it.

The Lehonti episode (vv10-19) is the chapter's most quoted, and the image is unforgettable. Lehonti and his rebels are safe — fortified "upon the top of the mount which was called Antipas" (v7). Amalickiah cannot touch him there, so he sends, four times, "desiring him to come down" (vv10-12). Lehonti refuses the first three times — he "durst not go down." But on the fourth, when Amalickiah comes up "nearly to Lehonti's camp" and asks him to come down only a little, with his guards, Lehonti relents. And that descent is his death. The lesson is old but exact: the adversary cannot reach you on your secure high ground, so he invites you to come down — just a little, just to talk, just to negotiate, and bring your guards so it feels safe. The compromise looks small and protected. It is fatal. Nehemiah, facing the same trick, gave the opposite answer: "I am doing a great work, so that I cannot come down" (Neh 6:3). Lehonti came down and was poisoned by degrees. The high ground was never the problem; leaving it was. And the poison "by degrees" is its own lesson — the destruction wasn't a single blow but a gradual, hidden administration, fatal before the victim knew he was dying. That's how corruption usually works on a soul that has come down off its ground: not a sudden fall but a slow poisoning, increment by increment, each dose small enough to ignore.

The murder of the king (vv22-27) is the chapter's coldest moment, and the detail is precise: the king "put forth his hand to raise them, as was the custom with the Lamanites, as a token of peace, which custom they had taken from the Nephites" — and "when he had raised the first from the ground, behold he stabbed the king to the heart." A gesture of peace, learned from the righteous Nephites, turned into the cover for assassination. Amalickiah corrupts even the symbols of trust. And then the masterstroke of fraud: he blames the king's own fleeing servants, performs grief, sends the loyal to "pursue" the innocent, and "by his fraud gained the hearts of the people" (v30). The man who committed the murder becomes the chief mourner and the avenger. It's the oldest move of the guilty — accuse the innocent of your own crime, loudly, first. And it works completely; he marries the queen and takes the throne. Evil that is patient, organized, and shameless can win, for a season. The Book of Mormon doesn't pretend treachery always fails fast — Amalickiah gets everything he schemed for. (Though the careful reader knows the pit-digger's end is coming — Teancum's javelin in chapter 51.)

The chapter's closing verse (v36) returns to the principle the war record keeps circling, and Mormon flags it as a genuine puzzle — "it is strange to relate." The Nephite dissenters who follow Amalickiah were "instructed in the same knowledge of the Lord," yet "not long after their dissensions they became more hardened and impenitent, and more wild, wicked and ferocious than the Lamanites... entirely forgetting the Lord their God." This is the Alma 24:30 law again (apostasy from light is worse than never having had it), and Mormon admits it's strange — why would those who knew the most become the worst? But the war keeps proving it: Amalickiah, the most dangerous enemy the Nephites face, is not a Lamanite by blood but a Nephite who knew the truth and left it. The dissenter outruns the native in wickedness because the rejected light becomes its own fuel — the conscience that was educated and then silenced is harder than the conscience that was never taught. "Strange to relate," and true to relate, and a warning to everyone who has been given much.
