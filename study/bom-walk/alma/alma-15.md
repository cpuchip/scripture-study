# Alma 15 — The Fever of Guilt and the Faith That Heals: Zeezrom Made Whole

*(Alma and Amulek reach Sidom and find the cast-out believers; Zeezrom lies sick with a guilt-fever and is healed by his faith in Christ, baptized, and made a preacher; Ammonihah refuses; Amulek's costly discipleship. ~81 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Zeezrom's conviction (chs 11-14) reaches its crisis — a literal fever caused by his guilt. His healing binds body and soul together: he is made well *by* the faith that resolves the guilt that sickened him. What does his complete turnaround show, and what did following the truth cost Amulek?

**Read:** Alma and Amulek come to Sidom and find "all the people who had departed out of... Ammonihah, who had been **cast out and stoned**" (v1). "Zeezrom lay sick at Sidom, **with a burning fever, which was caused by the great tribulations of his mind on account of his wickedness**" — he "supposed that they had been slain because of his iniquity. And this great sin... did **harrow up his mind until it did become exceedingly sore**... therefore he began to be **scorched with a burning heat**" (v3). Hearing they lived, "his heart began to take courage," and he sent for them (v4); they came "immediately," and he "besought them that they would **heal him**" (v5). Alma: "**Believest thou in the power of Christ unto salvation?**" (v6); "Yea, I believe all the words that thou hast taught" (v7); "**If thou believest in the redemption of Christ thou canst be healed**" (v8); "Yea, I believe according to thy words" (v9). Alma prayed: "have mercy on this man, and **heal him according to his faith which is in Christ**" (v10); "**Zeezrom leaped upon his feet, and began to walk**" (v11). "Alma baptized Zeezrom unto the Lord; and **he began from that time forth to preach unto the people**" (v12). A church established in Sidom; many baptized (vv13-14). "But... the people that were in the land of Ammonihah... yet remained a hard-hearted and a stiffnecked people; and they repented not... **ascribing all the power of Alma and Amulek to the devil**; for they were of the profession of Nehor" (v15). "Amulek having **forsaken all his gold, and silver, and his precious things... for the word of God, he being rejected by those who were once his friends and also by his father and his kindred**" (v16). Alma "took Amulek... to his own house, and did **administer unto him in his tribulations, and strengthened him in the Lord**" (v18).

**Thoughts:**
- *Guilt as a burning fever — the pains of hell made bodily (vv3-5).* Zeezrom's sin does not stay in his conscience; it sickens his body. His guilt "did harrow up his mind until it did become exceedingly sore... therefore he began to be **scorched with a burning heat**" — the "pains of hell" of 14:6 manifesting as a literal fever. The record names the cause precisely: "the great tribulations of his mind on account of his wickedness," specifically his belief that he had gotten Alma and Amulek killed. Unresolved guilt — especially guilt over harming others — is not merely a feeling; it can scorch the body. Zeezrom is the Book of Mormon's clearest picture of the psychosomatic reality of sin: the soul's torment burning through into the flesh. The man who once felt nothing while bribing and trapping now burns with what he did.
- *Healed by the faith that resolves the guilt — body and soul together (vv6-11).* Alma does not heal Zeezrom on demand; he first confirms the faith: "**Believest thou in the power of Christ unto salvation?**... If thou believest in the redemption of Christ **thou canst be healed**." The physical healing is bound to the spiritual — Zeezrom is healed "according to his faith which is in Christ." This is the key: the fever was *caused* by guilt, so it is *cured* by the faith that resolves the guilt. The redemption that cleanses the conscience heals the body the conscience was scorching. Zeezrom is not merely cured of an illness; he is made whole — the sin forgiven, the guilt lifted, the fever gone, and the man "leaped upon his feet, and began to walk" (the lame man at the temple, Acts 3:8). Salvation and healing are one act, because the sickness was sin all along.
- *The complete turnaround — bribing lawyer to preacher (v12).* Zeezrom's arc is now complete, and it is one of the great conversions in the record. The man who offered six onties to make Amulek deny God (11:22) "**began from that time forth to preach unto the people**." Trace it: bribe → tremble (11:46) → inquire diligently (12:8) → harrowed-up guilt and public confession (14:7) → the fever of guilt → healed by faith → baptized → *preacher*. The chief accuser becomes a herald. This is the Paul pattern (the persecutor turned apostle) and the Alma-the-younger pattern (the destroyer turned testifier) once more — the truth's fiercest enemy, once he stops hardening, becomes its most committed voice. No one is too far gone; Zeezrom was *the* corrupt lawyer, and he ends up preaching.
- *Ammonihah refuses — ascribing God's power to the devil (v15).* Against Zeezrom's conversion stands the hardened collective. Ammonihah "yet remained hard-hearted... **ascribing all the power of Alma and Amulek to the devil**" — the exact move of the Pharisees who said Christ cast out devils by Beelzebub (Matt 12:24), the most dangerous direction a heart can take, calling the works of God satanic. The contrast is the chapter's structural point: the *individual* (Zeezrom) can turn even from the depths; the *hardened collective* (Ammonihah) will not. Ammonihah is the anti-Salem — where Melchizedek's wicked city repented (13:18), Ammonihah refuses the same word from the same kind of preacher, and the refusal seals its destruction (ch 16). The same gospel that heals Zeezrom is rejected by Ammonihah; the difference is the hardening of the heart (12:10-11 again).
- *Amulek's cost — forsaking all (v16).* The chapter pauses to count what following the truth cost Amulek. He "**forsaken all his gold, and silver, and his precious things... for the word of God, he being rejected by those who were once his friends and also by his father and his kindred**." The once-prosperous "man of no small reputation" (10:4) lost everything — wealth, standing, friends, and his own *father and family*. The reluctant convert who "knew, yet would not know" (10:6) paid the full price once he stopped refusing: Luke 14:33's "whosoever forsaketh not all that he hath cannot be my disciple," lived to the letter. Discipleship for Amulek was not an addition to a comfortable life; it was the loss of that life. And he did not regret it — but the cost was real, and the record honors it by naming it.
- *Alma carries the bereft companion (v18).* The chapter ends in quiet tenderness: Alma "took Amulek... to his own house, and did **administer unto him in his tribulations, and strengthened him in the Lord**." Amulek, having lost his home, family, and fortune, is taken into Alma's home and strengthened. This is the baptismal covenant of Mosiah 18:8 ("bear one another's burdens") lived between the two missionaries — the senior prophet does not leave the man who lost everything for the gospel to bear it alone. The ministry is not only preaching to the lost; it is carrying the brother who paid the cost.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The Ammonihah arc resolves in **Zeezrom's healing** — and the chapter's doctrine is that his sickness *was* his guilt: a literal "**burning fever... caused by the great tribulations of his mind on account of his wickedness**" (v3, the pains of hell of 14:6 made bodily). So the cure is bound to faith: "**if thou believest in the redemption of Christ thou canst be healed**" (v8) — **body and soul healed together**, because the fever was sin all along, and the redemption that cleanses the conscience heals the flesh it scorched. Zeezrom's **conversion completes** the great arc (bribe → tremble → inquire → guilt → healed → baptized → *preacher*, v12): the chief accuser becomes a herald — no one too far gone. Against him, **Ammonihah refuses** — "ascribing all the power... to the devil" (v15, the Beelzebub move, Matt 12:24); the *individual* turns from the depths while the *hardened collective* will not (the anti-Salem, sealing ch 16's destruction). And the chapter counts **Amulek's cost** — he "forsaken all his gold, silver... rejected by once-friends and by his father and his kindred" (v16, Luke 14:33 lived to the letter) — closing with Alma **bearing the burden of the brother who paid it**, sheltering and strengthening the bereft Amulek (v18, Mosiah 18:8). Next: Alma 16 (Ammonihah destroyed in one day by the Lamanites — "Desolation of Nehors," fulfilling 9:4; the captives recovered; Alma and Amulek preach; the people prosper — a chapter-summary of several years).
