# Alma 25 — Abinadi's fire fulfilled, and the law read as a type

**Binding idea:** The martyrdom's ripple spreads, and inside it an old prophecy comes true. Abinadi, burned for his belief ~60 years earlier, had told Noah's priests "what ye shall do unto me shall be a type of things to come" (25:10, quoting Mosiah 13:10) — and now the seed of those priests (Amulon's line) burn believing Lamanites by fire, then are themselves "hunted" and "slain... even as a sheep having no shepherd" (25:12), exactly as Abinadi foretold. Meanwhile the converts keep multiplying, bury their weapons like their brethren, and grasp the thing the Nehorites never did — that the law of Moses "did serve to strengthen their faith in Christ," not to save by itself (25:16).

**Read:** Alma 25 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

This chapter is Mormon collecting a prophecy he's been holding open for sixty years. Back in Mosiah, Abinadi stood before Noah's priests and, as they prepared to burn him, said his death would be "a type of things to come" and that the priests' *seed* would cause many to die the same way and be scattered and slain "as a sheep having no shepherd." Mormon now closes the loop with editorial precision: the seed of Amulon (Noah's chief priest) "usurped the power... over the Lamanites, caused that many of the Lamanites should perish by fire because of their belief" (v5) — the same fire, two generations later, now turned on the new converts. Then the Lamanites turn on Amulon's seed, hunt them, and "they are hunted at this day" (vv8-9). "Thus the words of Abinadi were brought to pass" (v9). The prophecy didn't just predict a death; it predicted a *pattern* — fire begetting fire, the persecutor's children becoming the hunted — and the pattern ran true across decades. I find this is one of the quiet arguments of the whole record: Mormon plants a prophecy in one book and harvests it in another, and the harvest is exact.

There's a grim symmetry worth naming. Abinadi was burned for belief; the priests who burned him produced descendants who burn believers; and those descendants are then driven and slain. The violence the priests of Noah introduced didn't end with them — it propagated down their line and finally consumed them. Sin has descendants, and so does the judgment on it. The seed of the men who lit Abinadi's fire end their existence as fugitives hunted through the wilderness. (And the contrast with the Anti-Nephi-Lehies is total: one lineage buried its weapons and inherited peace; the other kept the sword and inherited the hunt.)

But the chapter's center of gravity, for me, is verses 15-16, because it shows the converts understanding the law better than the apostates ever did. They "did keep the law of Moses; for it was expedient that they should keep the law of Moses as yet, for it was not all fulfilled. But notwithstanding the law of Moses, they did look forward to the coming of Christ, considering that the law of Moses was a type of his coming." And then the precise line: "they did not suppose that salvation came by the law of Moses; but the law of Moses did serve to strengthen their faith in Christ." That is exactly right — the law as schoolmaster, the outward performances as types pointing past themselves to the Redeemer (Gal 3:24; 2 Ne 11:4; Jacob 4:5). Newly converted Lamanites, a generation out of idolatry, grasp what the sophisticated Nehorite priests with their synagogues never did: the forms are not the salvation; they are the finger pointing at the salvation. Counterfeit religion (chapter 21) made the forms an end in themselves and removed the need to repent; true conversion keeps the forms but knows what they're *for*. The difference between Aaron's converts and the Amalekites isn't ritual observance — both have it — it's whether the ritual points beyond itself to Christ.

And the cure keeps spreading even through the war. The Lamanites who came to destroy can't overpower the Nephites, go home, and "many of them came over to dwell in the land of Ishmael and the land of Nephi, and did join themselves to the people of God" (v13), burying their weapons "according as their brethren had" (v14). The grievance-cure isn't a one-time event; it's a widening circle. Every wave of violence against the converts seems to produce another wave of converts. The chapter ends with Ammon and his brethren rejoicing that the Lord "had also verified his word unto them in every particular" (v17) — the prayer of Alma 17:9, that they might be instruments to bring the Lamanites to truth, answered down to the detail.
