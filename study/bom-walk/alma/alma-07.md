# Alma 7 — That He May Know How to Succor: The God Who Suffered to Understand

*(Alma's sermon to the faithful church at Gideon — Christ born of Mary; the empathic atonement ("that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people"); be born again; faith, hope, charity. ~83 BC. Mini-study — the empathic-atonement jewel completes the Abinadi-Christology thread.)*

**Binding idea:** Alma comes to Gideon and finds, unlike Zarahemla, a humble and faithful people — so the sermon is comfort, not woe. At its center is the most tender statement of the Atonement in the record: Christ takes our infirmities not only to *pay* for them but to *know* them. Why would an omniscient God need to suffer in order to succor?

**Read:** Alma, now free of the judgment-seat (vv1-2), comes hoping to find them "not in the **awful dilemma** that our brethren were in at Zarahemla" (v3) — and rejoices that "they are established again in the way of his righteousness" (v4); he trusts they "do worship the true and the living God" and "look forward for the remission of your sins, with an everlasting faith" (v6). "There is one thing which is of more importance than they all—for behold, the time is not far distant that the **Redeemer liveth and cometh among his people**" (v7). Then a careful qualification: "I do not say that he will come among us [the Nephites] at the time of his dwelling in his mortal tabernacle... the Spirit hath not said unto me that this should be the case. Now as to this thing **I do not know**; but this much I do know, that the Lord God hath power to do all things" (v8). "The Son of God cometh upon the face of the earth" (v9); "he shall be **born of Mary, at Jerusalem which is the land of our forefathers, she being a virgin**, a precious and chosen vessel, who shall be overshadowed and conceive by the power of the Holy Ghost" (v10). "He shall go forth, **suffering pains and afflictions and temptations of every kind**; and this that the word might be fulfilled which saith **he will take upon him the pains and the sicknesses of his people**" (v11). "And he will take upon him death, that he may loose the bands of death... and he will take upon him their infirmities, **that his bowels may be filled with mercy, according to the flesh, that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people according to their infirmities**" (v12). "Now the **Spirit knoweth all things**; nevertheless the Son of God suffereth according to the flesh that he might take upon him the sins of his people... and now behold, this is the testimony which is in me" (v13). "Ye must repent, and **be born again**... come and be baptized" (vv14-15). God "cannot walk in crooked paths; neither doth he vary... neither hath he a **shadow of turning**... his course is **one eternal round**" (v20); "neither can filthiness... be received into the kingdom of God; therefore... he who is filthy shall remain in his filthiness" (v21). "Be **humble, and be submissive and gentle; easy to be entreated; full of patience and long-suffering**" (v23); "see that ye have **faith, hope, and charity**, and then ye will always abound in good works" (v24); keep "your garments spotless" (v25); the peace of God upon you (v27).

**Thoughts:**
- *The empathic atonement — the chapter's jewel (vv11-13).* This is the most pastorally tender statement of the Atonement in scripture, and its key word is *know*. Christ takes upon him "the pains and the sicknesses of his people," and "their infirmities" — and the *reason* given is not only to pay the debt but "**that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people according to their infirmities**." The Atonement is *empathic* as well as substitutionary: Christ suffers not merely *for* us but *with* us, so that his mercy is not abstract knowledge but felt experience. And verse 13 sharpens the wonder: "the Spirit **knoweth all things**; nevertheless the Son of God suffereth according to the flesh." Omniscience already *knew* about every human pain — yet that was not enough; the Son *entered* the pain "according to the flesh," learning it from the inside. This is the doctrine the Abinadi-Christology study flagged as Alma's *extension* of Abinadi: Mosiah 14-15 gave the substitution ("he hath borne our griefs"); Alma 7 adds the empathy ("that he may know... how to succor"). The result is the deepest comfort in the gospel — you are not consoled by a God who merely knows *about* your suffering from a distance, but by one whose "**bowels [are] filled with mercy, according to the flesh**" because he has *felt* exactly what you feel. He succors not as a physician reading a chart but as one who bore the disease. (Heb 2:18, 4:15 — "touched with the feeling of our infirmities.")
- *Alma's intellectual honesty — "I do not know" (v8).* A striking model of revelatory integrity sits right beside the great Christology. Alma carefully marks the boundary of his knowledge: he will *not* claim that Christ comes among the Nephites in mortality, "for the Spirit hath not said unto me that this should be the case... as to this thing **I do not know**; but this much I do know, that the Lord God hath power to do all things." A prophet, in the middle of a confident testimony, openly says *I don't know that part*. He testifies boldly of what the Spirit confirmed and falls honestly silent on what it didn't — and his caution proves *correct* (Christ does not come among the Nephites in mortality; he comes resurrected, 3 Ne 11). This is the discipline of true testimony: not claiming more than the Spirit has given, distinguishing revelation from inference even at the cost of seeming less certain. The honest "I do not know" is part of what makes the "this much I do know" trustworthy.
- *Born of Mary, in the land of Jerusalem (v10).* A century before Bethlehem, Alma names the mother (Mary), the virgin conception, and the place — "at **Jerusalem which is the land of our forefathers**." The verse clarifies its own geography: not the city Jerusalem but the *land* (the region — Bethlehem lies within it), which is why Alma specifies "the land of our forefathers." The Nephite prophets' Christology is strikingly specific and consistent: Benjamin's angel named Mary (Mosiah 3:8), Nephi saw the virgin (1 Ne 11:13-21), and Alma names her again — one tradition, one Christ, named with precision long before his birth.
- *The Gideon church — pure testimony adapts to the people (vv1-6, 17-19).* The same prophet who flayed prideful Zarahemla with forty searching questions (ch 5) comes to humble Gideon with *joy and affirmation*: "I perceive that ye are in the paths of righteousness... making his paths straight" (v19). Pure testimony is not one-size; it fits the hearer — woe to the proud, comfort to the faithful. Alma reads the people and gives Gideon what it needs (encouragement and deeper doctrine), not the rebuke Zarahemla needed. The shepherd's voice changes with the sheep's condition, though the truth is the same.
- *God's unchangeableness — "one eternal round" (vv20-21).* The ground of all trust: God "cannot walk in crooked paths; neither doth he vary... neither hath he a **shadow of turning**... his course is **one eternal round**" (v20; James 1:17; D&C 3:2). Because he does not change, his promises hold and his holiness is constant — which is also *why* "filthiness... [cannot] be received into the kingdom" and "he who is filthy shall remain in his filthiness" (v21). The same unchangeableness that makes God perfectly trustworthy makes him unable to wink at uncleanness; the constancy cuts both ways. (And it is no contradiction with the empathic mercy of v12 — the God who entered our pain to succor us is the same God whose holiness we must be cleansed to enter; mercy reaches into the filth to lift us out, but does not redefine the kingdom to admit it.)
- *The disciple's character — faith, hope, charity (vv23-24).* The sermon's practical close is a catalog of the new-birth life: "humble... submissive and gentle; easy to be entreated; full of patience and long-suffering... temperate... diligent... always returning thanks." And the summary triad: "**faith, hope, and charity, and then ye will always abound in good works**" (1 Cor 13; Moroni 7 will expand it into a full sermon). These are not entry requirements but *fruits* — what the born-again life looks like when it is actually lived. The empathic Christ who succors produces succoring disciples.
