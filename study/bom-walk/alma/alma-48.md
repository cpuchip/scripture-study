# Alma 48 — "if all men had been like unto Moroni": greatness is character, not capacity

**Binding idea:** Mormon sets the two great men of the war side by side and shows that the difference is not ability but direction. "While Amalickiah had thus been obtaining power by fraud and deceit, Moroni, on the other hand, had been preparing the minds of the people to be faithful unto the Lord their God" (48:7). Then the fullest character portrait in the book: Moroni was "a man of a perfect understanding; yea, a man that did not delight in bloodshed; a man whose soul did joy in the liberty and the freedom of his country... firm in the faith of Christ" (48:11-13). And the supreme commendation: "if all men had been, and were, and ever would be, like unto Moroni, behold, the very powers of hell would have been shaken forever; yea, the devil would never have power over the hearts of the children of men" (48:17). His greatness isn't generalship; it's that he "did glory... not in the shedding of blood but in doing good, in preserving his people" (48:16) — and that the Nephites went to war grieved, "sorry to be the means of sending so many of their brethren out of this world... unprepared to meet their God" (48:23).

**Read:** Alma 48 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

Verse 7 is the hinge of the whole portrait, because it puts the two men in the same sentence and lets the contrast do the work: "while Amalickiah had thus been obtaining power by fraud and deceit, Moroni, on the other hand, had been preparing the minds of the people to be faithful." Both are men of enormous capacity. Both are tireless, strategic, persuasive. The difference is *what they were building and for whom*. Amalickiah builds power for himself, by deceit. Moroni builds faith and defense for his people, by labor. Capacity is morally neutral — the same gifts that made Amalickiah a brilliant usurper would have made him a brilliant defender, and the same gifts in Moroni could have made him a tyrant. What separates them isn't talent; it's the object of the will. That's worth sitting with, because it means greatness of ability is no guarantee of goodness, and the question for any capable person isn't "how much can I do?" but "what am I doing it for, and how?"

The character composite in verses 11-13 is the most detailed the Book of Mormon gives anyone, and the surprising thing about it is how little of it is about being a warrior. "A strong and a mighty man" — yes — but then: "a man of a perfect understanding; yea, a man that did not delight in bloodshed; a man whose soul did joy in the liberty and the freedom of his country... a man whose heart did swell with thanksgiving to his God... a man who did labor exceedingly for the welfare and safety of his people... firm in the faith of Christ." The chief captain's defining traits are understanding, restraint, joy in liberty, gratitude, labor, and faith. The one martial line — "he had sworn with an oath to defend his people... even to the loss of his blood" — is framed entirely as *defense and sacrifice*, not conquest or glory. Moroni is great precisely because he is not what we expect a great general to be: he doesn't love war, doesn't love blood, doesn't love power. He loves the people he protects and the God he serves. The competence is in service of the love, not the other way around.

And then verse 17, the highest praise the book gives any human being who isn't Christ: "if all men had been, and were, and ever would be, like unto Moroni, behold, the very powers of hell would have been shaken forever; yea, the devil would never have power over the hearts of the children of men." The claim is staggering and worth parsing. It's not that Moroni's *military skill* would defeat hell — it's that his *character*, multiplied across all men, would leave the devil no foothold. The devil's power is over "the hearts of the children of men"; a world of Moronis — men who don't delight in bloodshed, who joy in liberty, whose hearts swell with thanksgiving and are firm in Christ — would give that power nowhere to land. The antidote to hell, Mormon says, is not force but a certain kind of person. (And the depth of Mormon's admiration is sealed by a fact outside this chapter: he named his own son, the last Nephite prophet, Moroni — after this man.) The praise is also carefully democratized in vv18-19: Moroni was "like unto Ammon... and also Alma and his sons," and Helaman and the preachers "were no less serviceable... than was Moroni." The general and the missionary are set as equals. Saving souls by the word and saving lives by the shield are the same order of service. The book refuses to rank the sword above the gospel or the gospel above the shield; both are the work of "men of God."

The portrait of the reluctant warrior in verses 23-24 is the part that most separates this war record from every glorying-in-battle epic. The Nephites "were sorry to take up arms against the Lamanites, because they did not delight in the shedding of blood." But Mormon goes deeper than physical reluctance: "yea, and this was not all—they were sorry to be the means of sending so many of their brethren out of this world into an eternal world, unprepared to meet their God." Their grief isn't mainly that men die; it's that men die *unprepared*, sent to judgment with their sins unrepented. They mourn the eternal state of the enemies they're killing. That's an almost unbearable tenderness in a soldier — to grieve, mid-battle, not for your own dead but for the souls of the men you're forced to slay. It's the two-mournings of Alma 28 applied to the enemy. And yet (v24) "they could not suffer to lay down their lives, that their wives and their children should be massacred by the barbarous cruelty of those who were once their brethren." The reluctance is real and the duty is real, and they hold both: they will fight, because they must protect the innocent, but they will never enjoy it, never glory in it, and they will grieve the souls they take. That is the only morally serious posture toward necessary violence — to do it without ever loving it. Moroni's whole greatness is that he fights like a man who would rather not have to, for a cause that justifies the fighting, grieving the cost the entire time.

## Notable

Yes — the Book of Mormon's fullest character portrait and its highest praise of a non-prophet (48:17, "if all men were like unto Moroni... the very powers of hell would have been shaken"), the Amalickiah/Moroni contrast that locates greatness in character rather than capacity (48:7), the defense-never-offense war-faith (48:14-16), the equal honor of preacher and general (48:18-19), and the reluctant warrior who grieves the unprepared dead (48:23). Mormon named his own son after this man.
