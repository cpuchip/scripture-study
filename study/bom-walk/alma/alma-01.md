# Alma 1 — Nehor and Priestcraft: The First Test of the Reign of Judges

*(The new order's first crisis is internal, not external — Nehor introduces priestcraft, slays Gideon, and is executed; the crime-vs-belief distinction; the true self-supporting order vs. priestcraft; prosperity without setting the heart on riches. ~91–88 BC. First chapter of Alma. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Mosiah dissolved the monarchy and handed the people a system that depends on their virtue. The very first challenge to it is not a Lamanite army but a charismatic preacher selling a comfortable gospel for money. What is priestcraft, why would it "prove their entire destruction," and where is the line the law may not cross?

**Read:** Mosiah dead, "leaving none to reign in his stead," but his laws "acknowledged by the people" (v1). In Alma's first year a man is brought to judgment — large, strong (v2) — who "preach[ed]... that which he termed to be the word of God, **bearing down against the church**; declaring... that every priest and teacher ought to become **popular**; and they ought not to labor with their hands, but that they ought to be **supported by the people**" (v3); and "that **all mankind should be saved** at the last day, and that they need not fear nor tremble... the Lord had created all men, and had also **redeemed all men**; and, in the end, all men should have eternal life" (v4). Many believed and "**give him money**" (v5); "lifted up in the pride of his heart... costly apparel... establish a church after the manner of his preaching" (v6). He meets **Gideon** — the aged deliverer of Limhi's people, now a teacher — who "withstood him with the words of God"; Nehor, wroth, "drew his sword and began to smite him," and Gideon "was slain" (vv7-9). Brought to Alma, Nehor "pled for himself with much boldness" (v11). Alma: "this is the **first time that priestcraft has been introduced among this people**... thou art not only guilty of priestcraft, but hast **endeavored to enforce it by the sword**; and were priestcraft to be enforced among this people it would prove their **entire destruction**" (v12); "thou hast shed the blood of a righteous man" (v13); "condemned to die, according to the law... given us by Mosiah" (v14). Nehor is carried to the hill Manti and there "did acknowledge... that what he had taught... was contrary to the word of God; and there he suffered an **ignominious death**" (v15). Priestcraft spreads anyway "for the sake of riches and honor" (v16); but "they durst not lie, if it were known, for fear of the law... and now **the law could have no power on any man for his belief**" (v17). Non-members persecute the church (v19); some church members proudly "contend warmly with their adversaries, even unto blows" (v22), causing "much trial" — names blotted out, withdrawals (vv23-24); the faithful "**steadfast and immovable**... bore with patience" (v25). The true order: the priests "left their labor to impart the word... then all returned again diligently unto their labors; and the priest, **not esteeming himself above his hearers**... thus they were all equal, and they did all labor, every man according to his strength" (v26), imparting "to the poor, and the needy, and the sick" (v27). They grow "exceedingly rich" (v29) "**but they did not set their hearts upon riches; therefore they were liberal to all**... whether out of the church or in the church, having no respect to persons" (v30). The law enforced on transgressors brought "much peace... until the fifth year" (vv32-33).

**Thoughts:**
- *The first threat is internal, and it is priestcraft (vv2-15).* Mosiah designed the reign of judges against the wicked *king*; the first thing that actually attacks it is the wicked *preacher*. The new order's opening crisis is not invasion but priestcraft — and the record marks it as a first ("the first time that priestcraft has been introduced among this people," v12). Nehor's gospel has two prongs, and both are seductive: a **paid, popular clergy** ("priests ought to become popular... supported by the people," the exact inverse of the self-supporting order of Mosiah 18:24 and 27:5), and **universal salvation without accountability** ("all men should have eternal life... need not fear nor tremble," v4) — a gospel that removes the moral stakes and so frees both preacher and hearer to do as they please. This is the Book of Mormon's recurring anti-Christ template (Sherem before, Korihor after): flattery, a doctrine that dissolves accountability, and self-enrichment. Alma's verdict is severe because the danger is civilizational: priestcraft "would prove their entire destruction" — a church that exists to enrich and flatter its leaders is no longer a church, and a people taught they need not fear judgment will not govern themselves, which is the one thing the reign of judges requires.
- *The law may punish acts, but never belief (vv12, 17-18).* The chapter draws a line that is the foundation of religious liberty and the rule of law together. Nehor is *not* executed for his false doctrine — he is executed for **murder** and for "endeavor[ing] to enforce [priestcraft] by the sword" (v12). His belief, however false, was untouchable: "the law could have no power on any man for his **belief**" (v17). Men "durst not lie... steal... rob, nor murder, for fear of the law" — but they were perfectly free to *preach* error, and "pretended to preach according to their belief." The distinction is exact and profound: the law governs *deeds* (fraud, theft, violence), not *thought*. You may believe and preach anything; you may not murder anyone. This is the Articles of Faith 1:11 principle ("we claim the privilege of worshiping... and allow all men the same privilege") embedded in Nephite law a century before Christ — and it will be tested again, deliberately, with Korihor (Alma 30:7-11). A free society protects even the heretic's right to be wrong, and punishes only what he *does*.
- *Priestcraft vs. the true order — paid-and-elevated vs. unpaid-and-equal (vv26-27).* The chapter sets Nehor's model against the true one point for point. Nehor's priests want to be "popular" and "supported by the people"; the true priests "**left their labor** to impart the word... then all returned again diligently unto their labors" — they preach and then go back to work. Nehor's priests are "lifted up"; the true priest is "**not esteeming himself above his hearers, for the preacher was no better than the hearer**... they were all equal, and they did all labor, **every man according to his strength**" (v26 — the very phrase of Mosiah 4:27). Nehor's priests take money; the true priests "impart of their substance... to the poor" (v27). Priestcraft inverts the gospel's economy: it makes the ministry a means of *getting* rather than *giving*, and elevates the minister above the people rather than keeping him among them. The test of true ministry is whether the minister labors alongside those he serves and gives more than he takes.
- *Prosperity is not the sin — the heart set on riches is (vv29-31).* The chapter makes the same careful distinction Jacob drew (Jacob 2:17-19). The faithful church becomes "exceedingly rich... silk and fine-twined linen" — and that is *not* condemned. What matters is the next clause: "**they did not set their hearts upon riches; therefore they were liberal to all**... having no respect to persons" (v30). The proof that the heart is not set on riches is concrete and behavioral: "they did not send away any who were naked, or that were hungry... or that were sick." Wealth held with an open hand, given to all regardless of membership ("whether out of the church or in the church"), is the prosperity covenant rightly lived. The sin is never the abundance; it is the closed hand and the elevated heart — which is exactly what Nehor's costly apparel and pride displayed. The same riches damn one man and bless another, by what his heart does with them.
- *Gideon falls to the first priestcraft (vv7-9).* A sober note. Gideon — the wise, strong man who delivered Limhi's people from bondage by stratagem (Mosiah 20-22), who read Abinadi's prophecy fulfilled — dies in his old age, murdered by the charismatic young heretic for "withstand[ing] him with the words of God." The man who once saved a nation by wisdom is cut down defending true doctrine against the first priestcraft. And his death is what makes Nehor's crime capital — priestcraft is not punishable, but priestcraft *enforced by the sword* is murder. The faithful old teacher's blood is the cost of the new order's first lesson: error preached is free; error enforced is death.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The **book of Alma opens** with the reign of judges' first test — and it is **internal, not external**: not a Lamanite army but **Nehor and priestcraft** (vv2-15). Nehor's gospel is the seductive double of a **paid, popular clergy** (the inverse of the self-supporting order) and **universal salvation without accountability** ("need not fear nor tremble," v4) — the anti-Christ template (Sherem → Nehor → Korihor) that dissolves the self-governance the new order requires; Alma says it "would prove their entire destruction" (v12). The chapter establishes the foundational distinction that **the law punishes acts, not belief** — Nehor dies for *murder* and for enforcing priestcraft *by the sword*, not for his doctrine; "the law could have no power on any man for his belief" (v17, religious liberty + rule of law, AoF 1:11, tested again with Korihor). It contrasts **the true self-supporting equal priesthood** (priests who labor, "no better than the hearer," give to the poor, v26-27) against priestcraft's getting-and-elevating, and reaffirms **prosperity without setting the heart on riches** (the church grows rich but is "liberal to all... no respect to persons," v30 — Jacob 2's distinction; the proof is no hungry person sent away). The aged **Gideon is murdered** by the first priestcraft (vv7-9). Next: Alma 2 (Amlici — a follower of Nehor — seeks to be king by the voice of the people, is rejected, rebels, and wars; the voice-of-the-people principle of Mosiah 29 immediately tested; the Amlicites mark themselves and become Lamanites).
