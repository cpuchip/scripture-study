# Alma 22 — "I will give away all my sins to know thee"

**Binding idea:** Aaron runs the identical conversion play Ammon ran on Lamoni — Great Spirit → God → creation → fall → the plan of redemption through Christ's atonement — and the over-king receives it because the seed Ammon's selfless love planted in chapter 20 had already left him "troubled in mind" (22:3). The conversion's hinge is the king's prayer: "I will give away all my sins to know thee" (22:18) — and the conjunction is *and*, not *then*. He doesn't bargain (give up sins, then receive God); the giving-away of every sin, the kingdom included, *is* how he comes to know God. (This is the verse behind Michael's [give-away-all-my-sins](../../give-away-all-my-sins.md) study.)

**Read:** Alma 22 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

The chapter is built to show that the missionary method is reproducible. Aaron, "led by the Spirit" to the palace (v1), meets a king already prepared — not by Aaron, but by Ammon's love two chapters earlier. The king refuses to let the brethren be his servants ("I will insist that ye shall administer unto me; for I have been somewhat troubled in mind because of the generosity and the greatness of the words of thy brother Ammon," v3, footnote → Acts 2:37, "pricked in their heart"). The seed planted in chapter 20 — a Nephite who held a sword to his throat and asked for nothing — has been working in the dark. By the time Aaron arrives, the soil is turned. This matters for the grievance-cure thesis: the over-king who recited Mosiah 10's grievance verbatim in chapter 20 is now troubled by the *generosity* of the people that grievance taught him to hate. Love did what no argument could; Aaron just brought the words.

And the words are the exact template Ammon used on Lamoni. "Believest thou that there is a God?" — the king reaches for his only frame: "Is God that Great Spirit that brought our fathers out of the land of Jerusalem?" (v9). Aaron, like Ammon, builds on the fragment: "Yea, he is that Great Spirit, and he created all things" (v10). Then the full arc — creation of man in God's image, the fall, "their carnal state and also the plan of redemption, which was prepared from the foundation of the world, through Christ" (v13). Verse 14 is a compact atonement creed: "since man had fallen he could not merit anything of himself; but the sufferings and death of Christ atone for their sins, through faith and repentance... he breaketh the bands of death, that the grave shall have no victory, and that the sting of death should be swallowed up in the hopes of glory." Grace (man "could not merit anything of himself," Eph 2:8) and resurrection (1 Cor 15:55, "sting of death") in two breaths. Two missionaries, two kings, one reproducible gospel. The method isn't Ammon's charisma — it's the plan, taught from where the hearer stands.

The king's response is the rich-young-ruler story rewritten with the opposite ending. "What shall I do that I may have this eternal life... that I may be born of God, having this wicked spirit rooted out of my breast?" (v15, the Acts 2:37 "what shall we do" again) — and then "I will give up all that I possess, yea, I will forsake my kingdom, that I may receive this great joy." The rich young ruler was asked to sell all and walked away sorrowful because he had great possessions; this king volunteers his entire kingdom before he's even asked. The footnotes know it — Matt 13:46 (the pearl of great price) and Matt 19:21 (the rich young ruler) sit right on the verse. He found the pearl and he's already reaching for his purse.

Then verse 18, which I want to read slowly because it's load-bearing. He prays: "O God, Aaron hath told me that there is a God; and if there is a God, and if thou art God, wilt thou make thyself known unto me, and I will give away all my sins to know thee, and that I may be raised from the dead, and be saved at the last day." Two things. First, the verb: he says "give *away*," not "give *up*." You give up what you'd rather keep; you give away what you want to be rid of. He's not reluctantly surrendering treasured sins — he's discarding garbage to get the pearl. Second, the conjunction Michael's study turned on: it's "I will give away all my sins to know thee, *and* that I may be raised." Not "if I give up my sins, *then* you'll reveal yourself." There's no bargain, no sequence where the payment comes first and the reward later. The giving-away and the knowing are one motion. To know God *is* to be emptied of what kept you from him; the repentance and the revelation arrive together. That's why he's "struck as if dead" (v18) the instant he prays it — the trade was immediate, not conditional.

The rest replays Lamoni's house at scale. The queen, like Lamoni's queen, thinks the prophet killed her husband and orders him slain; the servants refuse because "one of them is mightier than us all" (v20, echoing 18:3); Aaron raises the king — "Stand" — and "he stood upon his feet, receiving his strength" (v22). The raised king ministers, and "his whole household were converted unto the Lord" (v23). Then the multitude is pacified, and the king sends a proclamation of religious freedom through all the land (v27) — the same outward turn Lamoni made, now over the whole kingdom, which sets up the Anti-Nephi-Lehies of chapter 23.

Mormon closes with a geography lesson (vv27-34) — the land divided between Nephites and Lamanites, the "narrow strip of wilderness," Bountiful and Desolation, the "small neck of land between the land northward and the land southward." It reads like an aside, but Mormon is the careful editor orienting his reader before the next movement: the land Desolation is where the Jaredites died ("of whose bones we have spoken," v30, → Mosiah 8/28), and the whole map will matter when the war chapters come. He even notes "the more idle part of the Lamanites" (v28, footnote → 2 Ne 5:24) — the old curse-language surfacing as description, not decree.
