# Alma 49 — preparation defeats numbers, and two responses to one deliverance

**Binding idea:** Moroni's fortifications (ch 48) meet their test, and the result is lopsided: against the Lamanites' superior numbers and newly-acquired armor, the Nephite forts hold so completely that "more than a thousand of the Lamanites were slain; while... there was not a single soul of the Nephites which was slain" (49:23). Moroni had even anticipated the enemy's logic — foreseeing they'd be "frightened at the city Ammonihah" and target the formerly-weak city of Noah, he fortified Noah *most* (49:14-15). And the same deliverance produces opposite responses: Amalickiah "did curse God, and also Moroni... because Moroni had kept the commandments of God in preparing for the safety of his people" (49:27), while the Nephites "did thank the Lord their God, because of his matchless power" (49:28). The chapter closes on a quiet grace: Corianton, the son who strayed, now preaches among those bringing "exceedingly great prosperity in the church" (49:30).

## Thoughts

This is the chapter where chapter 48's character pays off in outcomes, and the lesson is that righteous preparation is not the opposite of trusting God — it's how God works. The Lamanites come expecting Ammonihah to be "an easy prey" (they'd destroyed it once), and they've even learned from their earlier defeat: this time they bring "shields, and with breastplates... and garments of skins, yea, very thick garments to cover their nakedness" (v6). They fixed the exact weakness that got them slaughtered in chapter 43. But Moroni had moved the contest to a new level entirely — earthen ridges so high the arrows can't reach, a single guarded entrance, a deep ditch. The Lamanites' adaptation is irrelevant because Moroni didn't fight the last war; he fortified against the next one. "They were prepared... in a manner which never had been known among the children of Lehi" (v8). The proverb fits exactly: "the horse is prepared against the day of battle: but safety is of the LORD" (Prov 21:31). Moroni prepares the horse to the utmost *and* gives the glory to God. Preparation and providence aren't rivals; the preparation is the form the providence takes.

The detail that most impresses me is verse 15: "this was wisdom in Moroni; for he had supposed that they would be frightened at the city Ammonihah; and as the city of Noah had hitherto been the weakest part of the land, therefore they would march thither." Moroni thought from inside the enemy's head. He knew the Lamanites would avoid the fortified city and seek the soft target — so he made the soft target the hardest of all and put Lehi (the captain they feared from Sidon) in command of it. That's strategic foresight as a discipline: not just defending your strong points but anticipating where the adversary will probe and reinforcing precisely there. The spiritual application writes itself — the enemy of the soul doesn't attack where you're strong; he marches toward your "weakest part," your formerly-soft places. The wise defense is to fortify the weak point most, because that's exactly where the assault will come.

The two responses in verses 27-28 are the chapter's heart, and they echo the Zerahemnah exchange from chapter 44. Amalickiah, hearing of the defeat, "did curse God, and also Moroni, swearing with an oath that he would drink his blood" — and the reason given is staggering: "this because Moroni had kept the commandments of God in preparing for the safety of his people." Amalickiah curses God *for the very thing that should have converted him*. The Nephites won because Moroni was faithful; Amalickiah's takeaway is to hate God because faithfulness works. He's looking at clear evidence that righteousness is protected and concluding that righteousness is his enemy. Meanwhile the Nephites "did thank the Lord their God, because of his matchless power in delivering them." Same event, same evidence — gratitude in one heart, blasphemy in the other. The difference, again, isn't in what happened; it's in what the heart does with what happened. A defeat that humbles one man hardens another into swearing to drink his enemy's blood. Amalickiah's rage is the measure of how far a heart can run from the obvious — he'd rather curse God than admit God was in it.

And then the chapter's quietest, loveliest line, easy to miss in a war narrative. Verse 30: the great prosperity in the church came "because of their heed and diligence which they gave unto the word of God, which was declared unto them by Helaman, and Shiblon, and Corianton, and Ammon and his brethren." *Corianton.* The son of chapter 39 — who forsook the ministry for the harlot Isabel, whose conduct stopped the Zoramites from believing, whom Alma harrowed and rebuilt with three chapters of doctrine — is now named among the preachers whose word brings the church to "exceedingly great prosperity." The reclamation worked. Alma sent him in chapter 42 to "declare the word with truth and soberness, that thou mayest bring souls unto repentance," and here he is doing exactly that, listed beside his faithful brothers without distinction. The book doesn't make a fanfare of it; it just quietly puts his name in the list, restored. That's the payoff of the whole Corianton arc, and it's a hope worth holding: the son who strayed didn't end as a cautionary tale. He ended as a preacher. Repentance really was the beginning of his ministry, not the end of it.
