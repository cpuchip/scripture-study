# Alma 8 — Go Back: The Prophet Sent Again to the City That Cast Him Out

*(Alma preaches in Melek, is reviled and cast out of Ammonihah, and is commanded by an angel to return; he meets Amulek — prepared by his own vision — and the two begin the Ammonihah mission. ~82 BC. Mini-study — sets up the Alma–Amulek discourse of chs 9-14.)*

**Binding idea:** Alma is spat on and thrown out of Ammonihah, and leaves "weighed down with sorrow." Then an angel tells him to go *back* — to the city that just humiliated him. What kind of obedience is that, and how is the ground already prepared before he returns?

**Read:** After Gideon, Alma rests, then preaches and baptizes throughout Melek (vv1-5) before coming to Ammonihah (v6), where "**Satan had gotten great hold upon the hearts of the people**" (v9). Alma "labored much in the spirit, **wrestling with God in mighty prayer**" (v10), but they hardened: "we are not of thy church, and we do not believe in such **foolish traditions**" (v11); "thou hast no power over us; and thou hast delivered up the judgment-seat unto Nephihah; therefore thou art **not the chief judge over us**" (v12). They "reviled him, and spit upon him, and caused that he should be cast out" (v13). Leaving "**weighed down with sorrow, wading through much tribulation and anguish of soul**" (v14), Alma meets an angel: "Blessed art thou, Alma... lift up thy head and rejoice... thou hast been faithful... from the time which thou receivedst thy first message from him. **Behold, I am he that delivered it unto you**" (v15). "**Return to the city of Ammonihah, and preach again**... except they repent the Lord God will destroy them" (v16); "they do study at this time that they may **destroy the liberty of thy people**" (v17). "Alma... **returned speedily**... and he entered the city by another way" (v18). Hungry, he asks a man for food; the man: "I am a Nephite, and I know that thou art a holy prophet of God, for thou art the man whom **an angel said in a vision: Thou shalt receive**" (v20). The man, **Amulek**, takes him in and feeds him (vv21-22). Alma tarries "many days with Amulek" (v27); then "the word came" and "Alma went forth, and also Amulek... and they were filled with the Holy Ghost" (vv29-30). "They had power given unto them, insomuch that they could not be confined in dungeons; neither was it possible that any man could slay them; **nevertheless they did not exercise their power until they were bound in bands and cast into prison. Now, this was done that the Lord might show forth his power in them**" (v31).

**Thoughts:**
- *Go back — the hardest obedience (vv13-18).* Alma is the former chief judge and the high priest of the whole land, and Ammonihah reviles him, spits on him, and throws him out. He leaves in genuine anguish, "about to set my back towards this land forever" (v24). And the angel's command is the one thing flesh least wants to hear: *go back*. Return to the city that humiliated you, immediately, and preach again. And Alma "**returned speedily**" (v18) — no delay, no negotiation. This is obedience at its costliest: not going somewhere hard for the first time, but going *back* to the place that already rejected you, on God's word alone. Most would shake the dust and count the rejection as release; the command reverses the release into a re-assignment. (And he enters "by another way" — quietly, from the south — not storming back in triumph but slipping in to begin again. Humble persistence, not vindicated return.)
- *The same angel — the messenger of his conversion recommissions him (v15).* The angel identifies himself: "I am he that delivered [thy first message] unto you" — the very angel who stopped Alma in his rebellion and converted him (Mosiah 27:11-16). The messenger of his *conversion* is now the messenger of his *recommissioning*. The God who once interrupted his sin to save him now interrupts his discouragement to send him. And the blessing notes the span between: "thou hast been faithful... **from the time which thou receivedst thy first message**" — God has been watching the whole arc, the years of faithfulness since the angel first knocked him down. The discouraged prophet is reminded that his life is a single, noticed faithfulness, bracketed by the same heavenly voice.
- *Amulek prepared before the prophet arrives (vv19-22).* Alma does not come to a cold city with no friend; God had already prepared one. Amulek had received his *own* angelic vision — "thou art the man whom an angel said in a vision: Thou shalt receive" — readying him to take in the rejected prophet. The two halves of a divine appointment — Alma *sent*, Amulek *prepared* — meet at the most ordinary point imaginable: a hungry man asking a stranger for food. The great Ammonihah mission, which will produce the doctrinal masterwork of chapters 9-14 and the martyrdom of chapter 14, *begins with a meal given to a hungry stranger*. God's largest works often turn on the smallest obediences — a man willing to feed someone at his door because an angel said he would. (The Elijah-and-the-widow pattern, 1 Kgs 17:8-16.)
- *The hardened heart dismisses every authority (vv11-12).* Ammonihah's rejection is calculated and total. They reject Alma on *two* grounds at once — "we are not of thy church... foolish traditions" (religious) *and* "thou hast delivered up the judgment-seat... thou art not the chief judge over us" (civil). Having stripped him of both kinds of power over them, they feel free to revile him. This is the Nehor stronghold's logic: a heart determined not to hear will always find a reason it doesn't *have* to. And their hardness is not passive — verse 17 reveals they "study... that they may destroy the liberty of thy people"; the rejection of the prophet and the conspiracy against liberty are the same wickedness. (A people who will not hear the word soon move against the freedom that protects the word.)
- *The power they would not use (v31).* A striking restraint. Alma and Amulek "had power given unto them" such that they could not be imprisoned or killed — *and they did not use it*, allowing themselves to be "bound in bands and cast into prison," "that the Lord might show forth his power in them." They could have walked away untouchable; instead they let the city do its worst, so that God's deliverance (the prison's collapse, ch 14) would testify louder than their escape ever could. The miracle withheld until the moment it would mean the most — and it sets up the hardest scene in Alma, where these two, who *could* have stopped it, are made to watch the believers burn (ch 14) before the power is finally shown. Power submitted to God's timing, even when using it would spare immediate pain.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off (the doctrinal core of the Ammonihah mission is **chs 9-13**, with **12-13** a flagged spin-off candidate; this is the narrative setup). The chapter's hardest lesson is **"go back"** — Alma, reviled, spat on, and cast out, leaving in real anguish, is commanded by an angel to *return to the very city that humiliated him*, and "returned speedily" (v18), entering humbly "by another way." It is **the same angel** who converted him (Mosiah 27 — "I am he that delivered it unto you," v15): the messenger of his conversion recommissions him, noting "thou hast been faithful from the time... thou receivedst thy first message." And the ground was already prepared — **Amulek had his own vision** ("an angel said... Thou shalt receive," v20), so the great mission (and the masterwork discourse of 9-14, and the martyrdom of 14) begins with **a meal given to a hungry stranger**. Ammonihah, a Nehor stronghold, **dismisses every authority** (religious *and* civil, vv11-12) and conspires to "destroy the liberty of thy people" (v17). And Alma and Amulek hold a **protective power they will not use** (v31) — letting themselves be imprisoned "that the Lord might show forth his power," which sets up the hardest scene in Alma (ch 14).
