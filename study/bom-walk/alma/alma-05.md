# Alma 5 — Can Ye Feel So Now?: Testimony as a Mirror Made of Questions

*(Alma's great sermon to the church at Zarahemla — the searching-questions discourse; the mighty change; the two shepherds; the call to repent and be born again. ~83 BC. Mini-study — deep synthesis in spin-off `alma5_searching-questions`.)*

**Binding idea:** Having concluded that pride cannot be legislated away — only reached by "pure testimony" (4:19) — Alma does something a declaration could never do: he asks the proud church roughly forty heart-searching questions. Why questions instead of accusations, and what is the one change he keeps pressing them to verify *now*?

**Read:** Alma roots the appeal in memory: "have you **sufficiently retained in remembrance the captivity of your fathers**?" (v6) — "he **changed their hearts**; yea, he awakened them out of a deep sleep" (v7), "their souls did expand, and they did sing redeeming love... they are saved" (v9). His father Alma believed Abinadi and "according to his faith there was a **mighty change wrought in his heart**" (vv11-12), and the same change in the fathers (v13). Then the questions begin: "have ye **spiritually been born of God? Have ye received his image in your countenances? Have ye experienced this mighty change in your hearts?**" (v14); "Do ye exercise faith in the redemption of him who created you?" (v15); "can ye look up to God at that day with a **pure heart and clean hands**... having the image of God engraven upon your countenances?" (v19); "can ye think of being saved when you have yielded yourselves to become subjects to the devil?" (v20); "there can no man be saved except his garments are **washed white**... through the blood" (v21); "how will any of you feel, if ye shall stand before the bar of God, having your garments stained?" (v22). The signature: "if ye have experienced a change of heart, and if ye have felt to sing the song of redeeming love, I would ask, **can ye feel so now?**" (v26); "Could ye say, if ye were called to die at this time... that ye have been sufficiently humble?" (v27); "are ye **stripped of pride**? ... if ye are not ye are not prepared to meet God" (v28); "is there one among you who is not **stripped of envy**?" (v29). Then the invitation: "the **arms of mercy are extended** towards them, and he saith: Repent, and I will receive you" (v33); "Come unto me and ye shall partake of the **fruit of the tree of life**" (v34). The two shepherds: "the **good shepherd** doth call you; yea, and in his own name... which is the name of Christ; and if ye will not hearken... ye are not the sheep of the good shepherd" (v38); "the **devil is your shepherd**, and ye are of his fold" (v39); "for his wages he receiveth death" (v42). Alma's own witness: "they are made known unto me by the Holy Spirit of God... I have **fasted and prayed many days that I might know these things of myself**... this is the spirit of revelation which is in me" (v46); "I know that **Jesus Christ shall come**, yea, the Son, the Only Begotten of the Father" (v48). "Cry unto them that they must **repent and be born again**" (v49); "the **ax is laid at the root of the tree**" (v52). The final challenge: "will ye still persist in the wearing of **costly apparel**... supposing that ye are better one than another... persist in **turning your backs upon the poor**?" (vv53-55). "Come ye out from the wicked, and be ye separate" (v57). "I speak by way of **command unto you that belong to the church; and unto those who do not belong... by way of invitation**: Come and be baptized" (v62).

**Thoughts:**
- *Testimony as a mirror made of questions (vv14-31).* This is the chapter's method and the fruit of Alma's decision in chapter 4. He does not stand up and tell the proud church members that they are proud — he *asks* them, roughly forty times, whether they could stand before God *now*. "Have ye received his image in your countenances?" "Could ye say, if ye were called to die at this time, that ye have been sufficiently humble?" "Are ye stripped of pride?" The genius of the method is precise: **you can argue with a declaration, but you cannot argue with a question put to your own conscience.** A man told "you are proud" defends himself; a man asked "are ye stripped of pride?" must answer to himself, in the silence, before God — and there the Spirit, not the preacher, does the convicting. This is "pure testimony" (4:19) in its purest form: Alma hands the people a mirror and steps back. And the questions are not historical curiosities addressed to an ancient congregation; every one of them is put, across the page, to the reader. "Can ye feel so now?" has no expiration.
- *"Can ye feel so now?" — the change re-verified in the present (v26).* The sermon's signature line, and its sharpest edge. Alma grants the premise — "*if* ye have experienced a change of heart, and *if* ye have felt to sing the song of redeeming love" — and then turns it from past to present: "can ye feel so *now*?" The mighty change (the new-birth thread of Mosiah 5:2, 27:24-26, and Alma's own conversion) is not a one-time transaction to be remembered but a *present state* to be re-verified. A conversion years ago is not the question; the question is whether the song of redeeming love is in your heart *today*. Faith is not a deposit; it is a pulse, and Alma asks the church to check theirs. (This is the same anti-stagnation truth as Benjamin's "retaining a remission" — what was once received must be presently retained.)
- *"His image in your countenances" (vv14, 19).* The mighty change is not invisible; it shows. "Have ye received his image in your countenances... the image of God engraven upon your countenances?" The new birth re-marks the face — the inverse of the natural man's countenance, and a quiet counterpoint to the *mark* of Alma 3: there, rebellion marks the forehead; here, the new birth engraves God's image on the countenance. What a soul becomes is written on its face. (And the question is unanswerable by performance — you cannot fake the image of God in a countenance; it is the fruit of an actual change.)
- *Two shepherds, two folds, no third option (vv37-42, 57-60).* Alma collapses the middle ground. There are exactly two shepherds — the good shepherd, Christ, who "call[s]... in his own name, which is the name of Christ" (v38; the name taken in covenant, Mosiah 5:8), and the devil. "If ye will not hearken unto the voice of the good shepherd... ye are not the sheep of the good shepherd" — and "the devil is your shepherd, and ye are of his fold" (vv38-39). There is no fold of the merely-respectable or the nominally-churched; you follow the voice whose works you bring forth (v41), and you receive the wages of the master you follow (v42; cf 3:27). The church members who imagined their membership made them safe are told that membership is not the question — *which voice they actually hearken to* is.
- *The severity rests on an invitation (vv33-35, 62).* For all its woes, the sermon's center is mercy. "The **arms of mercy are extended** towards them" (v33 — the same image as Mosiah 16:12, but here open and inviting rather than refused); "Come unto me and ye shall partake of the fruit of the tree of life" (v34 — Lehi's tree, 1 Ne 8, 11). And it closes not on condemnation but on a door: "I speak by way of command unto you that belong to the church; and unto those who do not belong to the church... by way of **invitation**: Come and be baptized" (v62). The questions cut precisely so that the invitation can be received — Alma wounds to heal. (And the command/invitation distinction marks the covenant's higher accountability: to those who have taken the name, command; to those who have not, invitation. Much given, much required.)
- *Alma knows "of myself" (vv45-48).* Before he asks them to verify their own hearts, Alma grounds his testimony in his own: "they are made known unto me by the Holy Spirit of God... I have **fasted and prayed many days that I might know these things of myself**... this is the spirit of revelation which is in me." His witness is not inherited, not hearsay, not office — it is personal revelation, sought and received. And that is precisely the pattern he is pressing on them: each must come to know *for himself* (the answer to the rising-generation unbelief of Mosiah 26, which could not inherit the fathers' conversion). The pure testimony works because the one bearing it has paid the price to know, and asks the hearers to pay it too.

Folded-back findings: (1) **more than forty distinct questions** across the chapter (42 question marks; past forty-three once compound interrogatives are split; densest in vv14-31, the diagnostic cascade) — the canonical "40+" confirmed; (2) the **three-generation conversion chain** — Abinadi → Alma the elder (who "believed the words... delivered by the mouth of Abinadi," 5:11-12) → the fathers → Alma the younger himself (Mosiah 27:24-28, "I am born of God"); in ch 5 **Alma is re-asking his whole congregation the very conversion question he once had to answer about himself**; (3) **"his image in your countenances"** (5:14) reaches back to Gen 1:26-27 (Webster cites that verse under *image*) — the new birth as *restoration of the divine image* the Fall obscured (held as interpretive inference, not overclaimed); (4) the sermon is **bracketed by mercy** — it opens with memorial questions (vv6-9) and closes with the invitation (v62), the scouring questions framed by the open door; (5) the "mighty change" is the **exact phrase** of Mosiah 5:2 (Benjamin's people) — Alma asks Zarahemla whether it can make the same declaration. (The study names the honest tension: questions could be heard as Socratic guilt-tripping; the distinction is that rhetoric manipulates from outside while these questions *create space for the Spirit to convict from within*.) This is **Alma's great sermon and the first full exercise of the "pure testimony" he resolved to bear** (4:19). Its method is the chapter's gift: **testimony as a mirror made of questions** — Alma does not accuse the proud, he *asks* them whether they could stand before God now; you can argue with a declaration but not with a question put to your own conscience, so the Spirit (not the preacher) convicts. The signature line, **"can ye feel so now?"** (v26), turns the mighty change from a past transaction to a *present* state to be re-verified — faith is a pulse, not a deposit. The new birth shows as **the image of God in the countenance** (v14 — a quiet counterpoint to Alma 3's mark of rebellion). There are **two shepherds and two folds, no third** (vv38-39 — membership is not the question; which voice you hearken to is), and for all the woes the **center is invitation** — "the arms of mercy are extended... come unto me and partake of the fruit of the tree of life" (vv33-34). Alma grounds it in **knowing "of myself" by the Spirit** (v46 — sought by fasting and prayer; the pattern he presses on them, answering the rising-generation unbelief of Mosiah 26), and closes with **command to the church, invitation to the world** (v62).
