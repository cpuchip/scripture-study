# Alma 3 — They Marked Themselves: The Curse a Man Brings on His Own Head

*(The war's aftermath; the Amlicites mark their own foreheads, fulfilling the word to Nephi; the meaning of the mark; "every man that is cursed bringeth upon himself his own condemnation." ~87–86 BC. Mini-study. **Bin-4 / curse-mark language — new datapoints logged for the with-Michael 2 Ne 5:21 study; the harder questions are flagged, not resolved here.**)*

**Binding idea:** The Amlicites, who are by descent *Nephites*, mark their own foreheads with red — and in doing so unknowingly fulfill an old prophecy about the Lamanite mark. The chapter uses that fact to make a claim about *all* curses. What does it mean that a people imposed the mark on *themselves*, and what does the chapter say a curse actually is?

**Read:** After burying the uncountable dead (vv1-3), "the Amlicites were distinguished from the Nephites, for they **had marked themselves with red in their foreheads** after the manner of the Lamanites" (v4). "The skins of the Lamanites were dark, according to the mark which was set upon their fathers, which was a curse upon them because of their **transgression and their rebellion** against their brethren... who were just and holy men" (v6); "the Lord God set a mark upon them, yea, upon Laman and Lemuel" (v7). "And this was done that their seed might be distinguished... that thereby the Lord God might **preserve his people, that they might not mix and believe in incorrect traditions which would prove their destruction**" (v8); "whosoever did mingle his seed with that of the Lamanites did bring the same curse upon his seed" (v9). "Whosoever **suffered himself to be led away** by the Lamanites was called under that head, and there was a mark set upon him" (v10); but "**whosoever would not believe in the tradition of the Lamanites, but believed those records... and kept [the commandments], were called the Nephites**" (v11), "and it is they who have kept the records which are true" (v12). The Amlicites "**set the mark upon themselves**, yea, even a mark of red upon their foreheads" (v13) — fulfilling the word to Nephi: "the Lamanites have I cursed, and I will set a mark on them that they and their seed may be separated... **except they repent of their wickedness and turn to me that I may have mercy upon them**" (v14); "a mark upon him that mingleth his seed... that fighteth against thee... he that departeth from thee shall no more be called thy seed" (vv15-17). "The Amlicites knew not that they were fulfilling the words of God when they began to mark themselves... nevertheless they had come out in **open rebellion against God**; therefore it was expedient that the curse should fall upon them" (v18). "Now I would that ye should see that **they brought upon themselves the curse; and even so doth every man that is cursed bring upon himself his own condemnation**" (v19). Another Lamanite army comes; Alma, wounded (2:30), sends an army that drives them out (vv20-24). All in the fifth year (v25). "In one year were thousands and tens of thousands of souls sent to the eternal world, that they might **reap their rewards according to their works**... according to the spirit which they listed to obey" (v26); "every man receiveth **wages of him whom he listeth to obey**" (v27).

**Thoughts:**
- *The Amlicites marked **themselves** (vv4, 13) — and that is the chapter's pivot.* The single most striking fact is grammatical and deliberate: the Amlicites "**marked themselves**," "**set the mark upon themselves**." The mark is *self-imposed* — a red sign these men chose to put on their own foreheads as an outward declaration of the side they had taken. And critically, the Amlicites are by descent *Nephites* — Mulekite-Nephite stock who followed Nehor and Amlici into rebellion. They are not Lamanites by blood; they *became* "under that head" by *choosing* the Lamanite cause, and they marked themselves to show it. The chapter is using the Amlicites as a living demonstration that the "mark" tracks *chosen allegiance and belief*, not lineage: a Nephite who rebels takes the mark; the category is defined by what one *believes and does*, not by descent. (Verse 11 makes this explicit — the line between Nephite and Lamanite is drawn at *belief*: "whosoever would not believe in the tradition of the Lamanites, but believed those records... were called the Nephites." It is a belief-and-tradition distinction, with the mark as its outward sign.)
- *The curse is self-authored — the chapter's own thesis (vv18-19).* The text does not leave the meaning to the reader; it states it. "I would that ye should see that **they brought upon themselves the curse; and even so doth every man that is cursed bring upon himself his own condemnation**" (v19). The chapter generalizes the whole curse-and-mark question into a principle about *agency*: a curse is not an arbitrary divine branding laid on the innocent; it is the consequence a person *brings on his own head* by rebellion. The Amlicites "knew not that they were fulfilling the words of God" — but they *did* "come out in open rebellion against God," and the curse followed *that choice*. This is the same principle as the self-authored loss of Mosiah 16:12 ("the arms of mercy... they would not") and the wages-of-obedience of vv26-27 here: "every man receiveth wages of him whom he listeth to obey." The chapter reads the curse not as something done *to* a people but as something a people *does to itself* by choosing whom to obey.
- *The mark's stated purpose: quarantine the destroying tradition (v8).* Why a mark at all? The text gives the reason: "that thereby the Lord God might preserve his people, **that they might not mix and believe in incorrect traditions which would prove their destruction**." The mark's function is *protective separation* — keeping the covenant people from absorbing the "incorrect traditions" (the grievance tradition studied at Mosiah 10) that destroy. This ties the mark directly to the grievance-tradition thread: the danger is not Lamanite *blood* but the Lamanite *false tradition*, and the separation exists to keep that destroying lie from spreading into the people who kept the true records. (Which is why the cure, when it comes, is *teaching the true tradition* — the sons of Mosiah's mission, Mosiah 28 — not a change of skin.)
- *The curse is conditional and removable (v14).* The word to Nephi includes its own off-ramp: the separation stands "from this time henceforth and forever, **except they repent of their wickedness and turn to me that I may have mercy upon them**." The curse is not permanent or fated; it is conditional on rebellion and lifts on repentance. (The footnote points to 2 Ne 30:6 — the documented "white/pure" verse — keeping the removable, repentance-conditioned nature of the whole thing in view.) A curse a man brings on himself by turning away is a curse he can have lifted by turning back.
- *Wages of the spirit you obey (vv26-27).* The chapter closes the year's carnage with the agency principle that frames everything: tens of thousands "sent to the eternal world, that they might reap their rewards according to their works... according to the spirit which they listed to obey," for "every man receiveth wages of him whom he listeth to obey" (Rom 6:16). This is the same self-authorship as v19 in eternal terms: you are paid by the master you choose to serve. The curse on the Amlicites and the rewards of the dead are the same doctrine — consequence follows chosen allegiance.
