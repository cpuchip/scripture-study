# Alma 55 — the city taken without blood: Moroni refuses to kill the helpless

**Binding idea:** Moroni retakes the city of Gid by a bloodless stratagem — sending a man named Laman to entice the Lamanite guards into drinking strong wine until they fall into a "deep sleep" (55:13-15), then silently arming the Nephite prisoners inside and surrounding the drunken guards. He has the enemy utterly at his mercy: "had they awakened the Lamanites... the Nephites could have slain them. But behold, this was not the desire of Moroni; he did not delight in murder or bloodshed, but he delighted in the saving of his people... he would not fall upon the Lamanites and destroy them in their drunkenness" (55:18-19). The guards wake surrounded, surrender "pleading for mercy" (55:23), and the city is taken without a death. It is the war record's clearest moral peak: a commander who could win by slaughter and refuses, because the slaughter "would bring upon him injustice."

## Thoughts

The chapter opens by naming what makes Moroni angriest, and it's revealing: "he knew that Ammoron had a perfect knowledge of his fraud; yea, he knew that Ammoron knew that it was not a just cause" (v1). Moroni isn't enraged by an honest enemy who believes his own cause — he's enraged by a man who knows he's in the wrong and fights anyway. Ammoron's letter (ch 54) wasn't sincere conviction; it was a justification Ammoron himself didn't believe. That's a particular kind of evil — not the deceived but the knowingly false — and it's what Moroni won't negotiate with. He resolves to take his people back by action rather than trade more power to a man arguing in bad faith.

The wine stratagem (vv4-15) is a small masterpiece of cleverness, and the psychological detail is delicious. Laman tells the guards "let us keep of our wine till we go against the Nephites to battle" — and the reverse psychology works exactly as intended: "this saying only made them more desirous to drink of the wine" (v10). Tell a weary, appetite-driven man to *save* the pleasure for later and you only sharpen his craving for it now. The guards' own undisciplined appetite is the weapon used against them; Moroni doesn't have to force the wine on them, only offer it and let their weakness do the rest. (Proverbs 20:1 hangs over the scene — "wine is a mocker.") It's the "no sin to defend by stratagem" principle (43:30) at its cleanest, and crucially this stratagem is designed to *avoid* bloodshed, not enable it. Moroni's cleverness serves his mercy.

And the moral peak is verses 18-19, which I'd put among the most important sentences in the entire war record. The drunken guards are in a "deep sleep," utterly defenseless. The total, easy, complete victory is right there: "had they awakened the Lamanites... the Nephites could have slain them." Every instinct of war, every logic of finishing the threat permanently, says strike now. And Moroni refuses: "this was not the desire of Moroni; he did not delight in murder or bloodshed, but he delighted in the saving of his people from destruction; and for this cause he might not bring upon him injustice, he would not fall upon the Lamanites and destroy them in their drunkenness." Two reasons, and both matter. First, his character — he doesn't *delight* in bloodshed; killing the helpless would give him no satisfaction because his joy was always in saving, not slaying. Second, the principle — slaughtering defenseless men, even enemies, even when you could, "would bring upon him injustice." Winning is not worth becoming unjust to get it. This is exactly David sparing the sleeping Saul (1 Sam 26) — "I could, the chance is given me, and I will not, because it would be wrong." The greatness isn't in the victory; it's in the victory he declined to take by atrocity. A lesser man slaughters the drunk and calls it decisive; Moroni arms the prisoners, surrounds the enemy, and waits for them to wake and surrender — and takes the city without a single death. You can win a battle and lose your soul; Moroni wins the battle and keeps his soul, and the record makes sure you see he could have done otherwise.

The closing detail (vv30-32) is a wise coda. The Lamanites, having lost Gid to the wine trick, try the same trick back — offering wine to the Nephites "that they might destroy them with poison or with drunkenness." But "the Nephites were not slow to remember the Lord their God in this their time of affliction. They could not be taken in their snares; yea, they would not partake of their wine, save they had first given to some of the Lamanite prisoners" (v31). They test the wine on prisoners first — "if their wine would poison a Lamanite it would also poison a Nephite" (v32). Two things in that: the spiritual ("not slow to remember the Lord") and the practical (a sensible precaution). The Nephites don't fall for the trick they just used, because vigilance and remembering God travel together. There's a quiet lesson about not being fooled by the very tactic you're capable of — knowing how the deception works is the best defense against it, and the people who keep God in mind in affliction stay sharp rather than getting careless. Moroni's whole approach in this chapter — clever, merciful, vigilant, undefeated, and bloodless — is the war record's portrait of how a righteous people fight: to save, not to slay; by wisdom, not atrocity; remembering God, not just trusting the sword.

- **Matt 5:44-45; Rom 12:21** — not becoming what you fight; mercy even when killing is possible.
