# Alma 35 — the word that destroys craft, and the refugees who become refuge

**Binding idea:** The Zoramite mission ends the way the word always ends among those who profit from the status quo — "they were angry because of the word, for it did destroy their craft" (35:3), so they expel everyone the preaching reached. But the cast-out converts are received by the people of Ammon, who — themselves former refugees taken in by the Nephites — "did receive all the poor of the Zoramites... did nourish them, and did clothe them, and did give unto them lands for their inheritance" (35:9). The sheltered become shelterers; the cure propagates. And Alma, grieved that "the hearts of the people began to wax hard, and... began to be offended because of the strictness of the word" (35:15), turns from the public mission that the hardened rejected to the one stewardship still open to him: he gathers his sons "that he might give unto them every one his charge, separately" (35:16).

**Read:** Alma 35 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Closes the Zoramite block (31-35); opens the counsel-to-the-sons chapters (36-42).

## Thoughts

The verb in verse 3 is the whole sociology of the rejection: the word "did destroy their craft." The Zoramite leaders weren't offended by bad theology; they were threatened in their *business* — the priestcraft that Korihor falsely accused the true priests of, and that the Zoramites actually ran. When a gospel that says "impart of your substance to the needy" (34:28) lands in a system built on despising and excluding the poor, it isn't a debate, it's a threat to the revenue. So they do what threatened power does: they "found out privily the minds of all the people" — a secret loyalty poll — and purged everyone the preaching had reached (vv5-6). The same move Demetrius made at Ephesus when the gospel threatened the silversmiths. The word doesn't just persuade; it dismantles the structures that profit from the lie, and the profiteers fight back.

But the chapter's quiet glory is the people of Ammon. The Zoramite chief ruler "breathed out many threatenings" demanding they expel the refugees, and "the people of Ammon did not fear their words; therefore they did not cast them out" (vv8-9). Instead they received the poor of the Zoramites, nourished them, clothed them, gave them land. And the resonance is the point: the people of Ammon were *themselves* refugees a few chapters ago — converted Lamanites who couldn't stay in their own land, taken in by the Nephites who gave them Jershon and bled to defend them (ch 27). Now they do for the cast-out Zoramites exactly what was done for them. The mercy they received, they extend. This is how the cure spreads — not just convert-to-convert, but received-to-receiving. People who were once sheltered become people who shelter, and the grievance-and-exclusion machine keeps losing ground to it. (The footnote on "nourish" points to Mosiah 4:26, King Benjamin's command to feed the poor — the people of Ammon are living Benjamin's sermon.)

The war that follows is the cost. The Zoramites, enraged, ally with the Lamanites against both the people of Ammon and the Nephites (vv10-11). The people of Ammon, bound by their buried-weapons oath, withdraw from Jershon to Melek and "give place" for the Nephite armies (v13) — again the armed bearing the sword so the conscience-bound don't have to. But the new Zoramite converts, *not* under that oath, "have taken up arms to defend themselves, and their wives, and children, and their lands" (v14). It's a careful distinction: the people of Ammon's pacifism was a specific covenant, not a universal rule; the new converts defend their families with the sword and the record doesn't fault them. The buried weapons were the Anti-Nephi-Lehies' particular vow, not a blanket pattern imposed on everyone the gospel reaches.

And then the pivot that defines the rest of Alma. Alma is "grieved for the iniquity of his people," and the specific grief is that "the hearts of the people began to wax hard, and that they began to be offended because of the strictness of the word" (v15). The word that destroyed the Zoramites' craft also offends the Nephites' comfort — the gospel's "strictness" is a scandal to people who'd rather have it loose. Alma has preached "among all the people in every city," and the public field is hardening. So he does what a faithful steward does when the wide field resists: he turns to the field he can still tend. "He caused that his sons should be gathered together, that he might give unto them every one his charge, separately" (v16). The pivot from prophet-to-the-nation to father-to-his-sons is not a retreat; it's a reordering of stewardship toward where it can still take root. When you cannot soften a hardening people, you can still pour everything you have into the few souls actually entrusted to you. That's the move that opens the most personal chapters in the book — Alma to Helaman, to Shiblon, to Corianton, each charge "separately," because each son needed a different word.

## Connections

- **Mosiah 4:26** — King Benjamin's command to "impart of your substance to the poor"; the people of Ammon nourish and clothe the refugees (v9), living Benjamin's sermon.
- **Alma 27:21-26** — the people of Ammon were themselves refugees given Jershon; now they shelter the cast-out Zoramites — mercy received becomes mercy extended.
- **Alma 34:28** — Amulek's "impart of your substance"; the people of Ammon do exactly what the Zoramites wouldn't.
- **Acts 19:23-27** — the gospel destroying the Ephesian silversmiths' craft; the word that threatens revenue (v3, priestcraft).
- **John 6:60-66; Matt 13:21** — "this is an hard saying"; offended by the strictness of the word (v15).
- **Alma 24:17-19; 27:23-24** — the buried-weapons covenant; the new converts (not under it) take up arms to defend their families (v14).
- **Alma 36-42** — the counsel to Helaman, Shiblon, Corianton; the charge "separately" (v16) opens the block.
- **Alma 43:3** — the war account promised "hereafter" (v13).
