# Alma 33 — worship everywhere, and the scandal of the simple look

**Binding idea:** When the poor ask *which* seed to plant and *how* to begin (33:1), Alma answers from the scriptures. From Zenos he proves worship is bound to no building: God heard him "in the wilderness," "in my field," "in my house," "in my closet," "in the midst of thy congregations," and "when I have been cast out" — and always "because of thy Son" (33:4-11). From Zenock he shows a second prophet testified of the Son and was stoned for it (33:15-17). And from Moses he gives the seed itself: the brazen serpent "raised up in the wilderness, that whosoever would look upon it might live" — yet "many were so hardened that they would not look, therefore they perished... because they did not believe that it would heal them" (33:19-20). The cure was a mere look, and people died rather than do something so easy. The seed to plant is the word of Christ and his atonement (33:22-23); the only barrier is the pride that scorns a simple way.

## Thoughts

Alma's first move is to send the poor to the scriptures: "ye ought to search the scriptures; if ye suppose that they have taught you this, ye do not understand them" (v2). The Zoramites had taught these people that being barred from the synagogue barred them from God, and Alma's correction isn't his own opinion — it's "go read." Then he hands them Zenos, a prophet on the brass plates whose words we'd otherwise have lost, and the whole quoted prayer is a catalog of places God heard a man pray: wilderness, field, house, closet, congregation, and — the line aimed straight at the cast-out poor — "thou hast also heard me when I have been cast out and have been despised by mine enemies" (v10). God is not in the building. God heard Zenos when he was thrown out, exactly the situation these people are in. The Rameumptom cage isn't just wrong about where to worship; it's refuted by ancient scripture the Zoramites claim to revere.

And inside Zenos's prayer is the doctrine that carries the rest: every mercy is "because of thy Son... thou hast turned thy judgments away from me, because of thy Son" (v11). A prophet who lived centuries before Bethlehem attributes God's hearing of prayer to the Son who hadn't yet come. This is the through-line Alma is building toward — the reason any prayer is heard, in any place, in any age, is the Atonement. Zenos knew it; Zenock knew it (and was stoned for it, v17); the scriptures the Zoramites read are saturated with the Christ the Zoramites deny. Alma's argument is devastatingly simple: "if ye have read the scriptures, how can ye disbelieve on the Son of God?" (v14). The denial of Christ requires not reading the very texts you claim.

The brazen serpent is the chapter's center, and it's the deepest thing in it. Moses "raised up in the wilderness" a serpent on a pole, "that whosoever would look upon it might live. And many did look and live" (v19, Num 21:9). But — and this is the part that stops me — "there were many who were so hardened that they would not look, therefore they perished. Now the reason they would not look is because they did not believe that it would heal them" (v20). People died of snakebite with the cure in plain sight, refusing to turn their eyes, because the remedy was too simple to credit. Alma drives it home: "if ye could be healed by merely casting about your eyes... would ye not behold quickly, or would ye rather harden your hearts in unbelief, and be slothful?" (v21). This is the scandal of simplicity, and it's one of the truest things about how pride works. The gospel's offer — just believe in Christ, just look — is so easy that the proud refuse it precisely *because* it's easy. They'd rather have a way they can be impressed by, a way that rewards their effort and their election, than a free look that anyone, even a beggar, can give. The Zoramites built a high stand and a memorized prayer; the cure was always just to turn your eyes to the Son and live. (Nephi named this exact thing in 1 Ne 17:41 — "because of the simpleness of the way, or the easiness of it, there were many who perished.")

So the seed, finally named (answering chapter 32's open question), is the word of Christ: "begin to believe in the Son of God, that he will come to redeem his people, and that he shall suffer and die to atone for their sins; and that he shall rise again from the dead" (v22). That's the seed to plant — not a generic spirituality, not "a Great Spirit," but the specific word of the atoning, dying, rising Christ. And the chapter closes by binding the seed back to the affliction these poor people carry: "plant this word in your hearts... it will become a tree, springing up in you unto everlasting life. And then may God grant unto you that your burdens may be light, through the joy of his Son" (v23). The same "burdens made light" promised to Alma-the-elder's enslaved people in Mosiah 24 — offered now to the cast-out poor, through the joy of the Son. And the last clause keeps it on agency: "even all this can ye do if ye will." The way is simple, the cure is a look, the seed is Christ, the burden-lightening is real — and it waits on the will to turn the eyes.
