# Alma 34 — Amulek: the infinite atonement, mercy satisfying justice, and "this life is the time"

**Binding idea:** Amulek brings the Zoramite sermon to its doctrinal climax. The atonement must be "infinite and eternal" because a finite sacrifice — of man, beast, or fowl — can only pay a finite debt, and "there can be nothing which is short of an infinite atonement which will suffice for the sins of the world" (34:10-12). Through it, "mercy can satisfy the demands of justice, and encircles them in the arms of safety" (34:16) — mercy doesn't abolish justice; the Son pays it so mercy can act. The response is continual prayer over everything (34:17-27) — but "if ye turn away the needy... your prayer is vain... ye are as hypocrites who do deny the faith" (34:28), the exact rebuke of Zoramite exclusion. And the time is now: "this life is the time for men to prepare to meet God... do not procrastinate the day of your repentance" (34:32-33), because "that same spirit which doth possess your bodies at the time that ye go out of this life... will have power to possess your body in that eternal world" (34:34).

## Thoughts

Amulek's argument for *why* the atonement must be infinite is the most rigorous in scripture, and it runs on the logic of the law these people lived under. A finite sacrifice pays a finite debt: "there is not any man that can sacrifice his own blood which will atone for the sins of another" (v11). Under the law, a murderer's life is required for the life he took — one life for one life, finite for finite. So no accumulation of finite sacrifices, no quantity of bulls or even of men, can cover the sins of *the whole world*; the debt is of a different order. Only "an infinite and eternal sacrifice" (v10) — a sacrifice by someone whose life is itself infinite — can suffice. Hence it "will be the Son of God, yea, infinite and eternal" (v14). This is the answer to the entire law of Moses, which Amulek says is "every whit pointing to that great and last sacrifice" (v14). Every lamb on every altar for a thousand years was a finite pointer to the one infinite payment. The Zoramites kept the forms while denying the Christ the forms were *for*; Amulek shows the forms collapse into nonsense without him.

The mercy-justice passage (vv15-16) is the doctrinal heart, and the precision matters. "To bring about the bowels of mercy, which overpowereth justice... thus mercy can satisfy the demands of justice, and encircles them in the arms of safety." Notice mercy doesn't *cancel* justice or *ignore* it — mercy *satisfies* its demands. Justice is paid in full by the infinite sacrifice; mercy is then free to act, and it "encircles" the repentant "in the arms of safety." But the door is conditional: "he that exercises no faith unto repentance is exposed to the whole law of the demands of justice" (v16). Mercy isn't automatic amnesty; it's the safety the atonement makes available to those who exercise faith unto repentance. (This is the seed of the fuller mercy/justice argument Alma will make to Corianton in chapter 42.) The image is gorgeous and exact: justice is fully met, and the repentant are gathered into encircling arms — not exempted from the law, but bought out from under it.

Then the prayer sermon (vv17-27), which is Zenos's worship-everywhere doctrine (ch 33) turned into a manual. Cry over your fields, your flocks, your household morning-midday-and-evening, against your enemies, against the devil, over your crops; pour out your soul in your closet and secret places and wilderness (vv20-26). And the verse that completes it: "when you do not cry unto the Lord, let your hearts be full, drawn out in prayer unto him continually for your welfare, and also for the welfare of those who are around you" (v27). Even your silence is to be prayerful; the heart stays drawn out toward God and toward your neighbor between the spoken prayers. This is the total opposite of the Rameumptom's one weekly recitation — prayer is woven through every place and hour and concern of an ordinary life.

But Amulek won't let prayer become a substitute for mercy, and verses 28-29 are aimed like a spear at the Zoramites. "After ye have done all these things, if ye turn away the needy, and the naked, and visit not the sick and afflicted, and impart not of your substance... your prayer is vain, and availeth you nothing, and ye are as hypocrites who do deny the faith." This is the whole Zoramite crime named and condemned — they cast the poor out of the synagogues and then prayed from the stand. Amulek tells them their prayers are worth nothing while they turn away the very people whose poverty they despise. And then the devastating reversal: "if ye do not remember to be charitable, ye are as dross, which the refiners do cast out" (v29). The Zoramites called the poor "dross" and cast them out (32:3); Amulek says it's the *uncharitable* who are the dross, fit to be cast out. The insult boomerangs exactly. You cannot pray your way past the beggar at your door.

The chapter's most quoted lines are the call to repent now (vv31-35), and the reasoning is sobering. "This life is the time for men to prepare to meet God; yea... the day of this life is the day for men to perform their labors" (v32). "Do not procrastinate the day of your repentance" (v33), because "then cometh the night of darkness wherein there can be no labor performed." And the verse that's both comfort and warning: "that same spirit which doth possess your bodies at the time that ye go out of this life, that same spirit will have power to possess your body in that eternal world" (v34). You don't become a different person at death; you carry across the character you built. There's no deathbed reset where a lifetime of hardening suddenly reverses — "ye cannot say... I will repent... when ye are brought to that awful crisis." This isn't God being harsh; it's the simple physics of character. Repentance is the gradual reshaping of who you are, and that work has to happen *here*, in "the day of this life," because the person who arrives at death is the person who leaves it. That's why now matters so much, and why procrastination isn't a scheduling problem but a soul-shaping one.

Amulek's close is tender toward the poor he's preaching to. "Work out your salvation with fear before God" (v37, Paul's line); worship "in whatsoever place ye may be in, in spirit and in truth" (v38, freeing them again from the building); "live in thanksgiving daily" (v38). And the final counsel, which only someone who loved them would give: "do not revile against those who do cast you out because of your exceeding poverty, lest ye become sinners like unto them" (v40). The danger to the oppressed poor isn't only despair; it's becoming bitter, becoming like their oppressors. Bear it "with a firm hope that ye shall one day rest from all your afflictions" (v41). Don't let what was done to you make you into what did it. That's a word I want to keep — the cast-out are warned not to let the casting-out turn their hearts.
