# Alma 30 — Korihor: the anti-Christ creed, and "all things denote there is a God"

**Binding idea:** Korihor is the Book of Mormon's fullest staged case for unbelief — atheism, empiricism, no afterlife, no sin, might-makes-right — protected by a state that punishes crimes but never belief (30:7-11). Alma refutes him without the law's help: first by argument ("what evidence have ye that there is no God... ye have none, save it be your word only," 30:40; "all things denote there is a God," 30:44), then, only when Korihor demands a sign and the danger to others is clear, by the sign of being struck dumb. And Korihor's written confession exposes the whole thing: "I always knew that there was a God" (30:52). The denial was never honest doubt; it was a lying spirit he chose and then came to believe through its success.

**Read:** Alma 30 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

The chapter opens with a genuinely remarkable constitutional principle, and it's easy to skip past. "There was no law against a man's belief; for it was strictly contrary to the commands of God that there should be a law which should bring men on to unequal grounds" (v7). The Nephite state punishes *crimes* — murder, robbery, adultery — but never *belief*. "A man was punished only for the crimes which he had done; therefore all men were on equal grounds" (v11). So Korihor, the atheist agitator, has full legal protection to preach atheism. The God-ordained civic order refuses to criminalize unbelief — because compelled belief is no belief, and the whole plan runs on agency ("Choose ye this day whom ye will serve," v8, quoting Joshua 24:15). This is striking: the silencing of Korihor, when it finally comes, comes from *God*, not from the magistrate. Alma the chief-judge-and-high-priest never uses the state's power against him. He argues with him.

And Korihor's creed is worth laying out plainly, because it's the most complete anti-belief platform in scripture and it is strikingly modern. (1) Anti-prophecy: "no man can know of anything which is to come" (v13); the prophecies are "foolish traditions of your fathers" (v14). (2) Strict empiricism: "ye cannot know of things which ye do not see... therefore ye cannot know that there shall be a Christ" (v15) — if you can't see it, you can't know it. (3) Religion as psychology: the remission of sins is "the effect of a frenzied mind... derangement" caused by tradition (v16) — faith reduced to a mental disorder. (4) Naturalism and might-makes-right: "every man prospered according to his genius, and that every man conquered according to his strength; and whatsoever a man did was no crime" (v17) — success is talent, power is virtue, and there is no sin. (5) No afterlife: "when a man was dead, that was the end thereof" (v18). That's the full secular materialist package — naturalism, positivism, moral relativism, annihilation at death — preached in 74 B.C. Nephi saw it coming in 2 Nephi 28 ("eat, drink, and be merry... there is no God"); Korihor is the prophecy walking around.

His strategy is accusation-by-projection, and it's clever. He charges the priests with keeping the people "in bondage" and "ignorance" so they can "glut yourselves with the labors of their hands" (v27, v31) — priestcraft. But priestcraft is exactly what *Nehor* did (Alma 1), and what the Amalekite synagogues ran on. Korihor accuses the true priests of the apostate priests' crime. And Alma's answer cuts it off cleanly with evidence: "I have labored... with mine own hands for my support... I have never received so much as even one senine for my labor" (vv32-33). The Nephite leaders are unpaid; they work for their own bread (the Mosiah 18:24 / Paul's-tentmaking model, Acts 20:34). The priestcraft charge is simply false, and Alma proves it from his own calloused hands.

Then comes the confrontation that makes this chapter a master class in answering unbelief. Alma asks the direct question — "Believest thou that there is a God?" — Korihor: "Nay" (vv37-38). And Alma's response is not a sign, not yet; it's an argument about the burden of proof: "what evidence have ye that there is no God... I say unto you that ye have none, save it be your word only. But, behold, I have all things as a testimony that these things are true" (vv40-41). The atheist's denial is asserted; the believer claims a universe of evidence. And then the famous statement, which is the design argument in one sentence: "all things denote there is a God; yea, even the earth, and all things that are upon the face of it, yea, and its motion, yea, and also all the planets which move in their regular form do witness that there is a Supreme Creator" (v44). The order of the cosmos is the testimony. Notably, Alma engages the *argument* before he ever produces a *sign* — reason first, the miracle only under duress. He's not anti-intellectual; he meets Korihor on the ground of evidence and wins there.

The sign comes only because Korihor demands it and won't relent, and Alma's reasoning for granting it is sobering: "it is better that thy soul should be lost than that thou shouldst be the means of bringing many souls down to destruction" (v47). The sign isn't to convince Korihor (Alma already told him "thou hast had signs enough," v44); it's to stop the spread. Korihor is struck dumb — fitting, for a man whose only weapon was his voice. And then the confession, written because he can no longer speak, is the chapter's devastating center: "I know that nothing save it were the power of God could bring this upon me; yea, and I always knew that there was a God" (v52). *He always knew.* Alma had discerned exactly this — "I know that thou believest, but thou art possessed with a lying spirit" (v42). The atheism was never a conclusion; it was a chosen lie. And the genesis of it (v53) is the most frightening detail: the devil "appeared unto me in the form of an angel" (2 Cor 11:14, the angel of light) and taught him what to say; Korihor preached it "because they were pleasing unto the carnal mind... until I had much success, insomuch that I verily believed that they were true." He started knowing it was false and *ended believing his own lie because it worked.* Success laundered the lie into a conviction. That is a real and terrifying mechanism — you can talk yourself into believing what you first knew was false, if it flatters you and it sells.

The end is just. Korihor begs Alma to lift the curse; Alma refuses, not vindictively but protectively: "If this curse should be taken from thee thou wouldst again lead away the hearts of this people" (v55). Korihor goes begging house to house — the man who told everyone they could conquer "according to his strength" reduced to dependence — and is finally "run upon and trodden down" among the Zoramites until dead (v59). Mormon's epitaph: "the devil will not support his children at the last day, but doth speedily drag them down to hell" (v60). It's the Sherem pattern exactly (Jacob 7) — the anti-Christ who demands a sign, gets struck, confesses on the way down, and is abandoned by the master he served. You are paid by the master you serve (Alma 3:27), and the devil's wages are delivered the moment they can no longer be used.

## Connections

- **Jacob 7 (Sherem)** — the anti-Christ template: deny Christ → demand a sign → struck down → deathbed confession → destroyed. Korihor is the second of the pattern (the footnotes lean on Jacob 7 throughout).
- **2 Ne 28:5-9** — "there is no God... eat, drink, and be merry"; Nephi's prophecy of the latter-day Korihors; v17-18 is its embodiment.
- **2 Cor 11:14** — Satan "transformed into an angel of light"; the devil appearing to Korihor "in the form of an angel" (v53).
- **Ps 14:1** — "the fool hath said in his heart, There is no God" (v40, footnote).
- **Job 12:7-10; Moses 6:63; Hel 12:11-15; Rom 1:20** — creation testifies of the Creator; "all things denote there is a God... the planets which move in their regular form" (v44); the design/cosmological argument.
- **Mosiah 18:24; Acts 20:34; 1 Thes 2:9** — unpaid ministry / tentmaking; Alma "labored with mine own hands... never received... one senine" (vv32-33); the priestcraft charge refuted.
- **Alma 1:4, 16** — Nehor's "whatsoever a man did was no crime" + priestcraft; Korihor extends Nehor's relativism and projects Nehor's crime onto the true priests.
- **Alma 1:17** — "no law against a man's belief"; the reign-of-judges religious-freedom principle.
- **Josh 24:15** — "choose ye this day whom ye will serve" (v8); agency as the reason belief can't be compelled.
- **Alma 3:27; 5:41-42; D&C 29:45** — you are paid by the master you serve; the devil won't support his own (v60).
