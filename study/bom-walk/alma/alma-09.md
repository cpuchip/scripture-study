# Alma 9 — More Tolerable for the Lamanites: Light Sinned Against Is the Measure

*(Alma's first sermon to Ammonihah — the warning of destruction; the prosperity covenant; the Lamanites "more tolerable" than the favored Nephites who sin against greater light; the prophecy of the Lamanite conversion. ~82 BC. Mini-study — proof-text for the grievance-tradition study's partial-exculpation finding.)*

**Binding idea:** Ammonihah assumes its favored Nephite status makes it safe. Alma's whole sermon inverts that assumption: the more light a people has been given, the *more* — not less — accountable they are. Why are the Lamanites "more tolerable" in the day of judgment than the people who have everything?

**Read:** They contend: "**Who art thou? Suppose ye that we shall believe the testimony of one man?**" (v2); "we will not believe thy words if thou shouldst prophesy that this great city should be **destroyed in one day**" (v4); "**Who is God**, that sendeth no more authority than one man?" (v6). Alma testifies boldly: "how have ye forgotten the **tradition of your fathers**... the commandments of God" (v8) — remember Lehi brought out of Jerusalem (v9), delivered many times (vv10-11). "He commandeth you to repent; and except ye repent, ye can in nowise inherit the kingdom... or he will utterly destroy you" (v12). The covenant: "**Inasmuch as ye shall keep my commandments, ye shall prosper in the land**... Inasmuch as ye will not keep my commandments ye shall be cut off from the presence of the Lord" (v13); the Lamanites were cut off for not keeping it (v14). "**It shall be more tolerable for them in the day of judgment than for you, if ye remain in your sins**" (v15); "there are many promises... extended to the Lamanites; **for it is because of the traditions of their fathers that caused them to remain in their state of ignorance; therefore the Lord will be merciful unto them**" (v16); "**at some period of time they will be brought to believe in his word, and to know of the incorrectness of the traditions of their fathers; and many of them will be saved**" (v17). But the Nephites, if they persist, "the **Lamanites shall be sent upon you**" (v18) — for they sinned "after having had **so much light and so much knowledge**" (v19), "favored above every other nation" (v20), "visited by the Spirit... conversed with angels... the voice of the Lord... gifts" (v21), "delivered... out of Jerusalem... brought out of bondage time after time... prospered" (v22). "**If this people, who have received so many blessings... should transgress contrary to the light and knowledge which they do have... it would be far more tolerable for the Lamanites than for them**" (v23). The angel's cry: repent, "**not many days hence the Son of God shall come in his glory**" (vv25-26), "he cometh to redeem those who will be baptized unto repentance" (v27); "all men shall reap a reward of their works" (v28). "My beloved brethren, **for ye are my brethren, and ye ought to be beloved**" (v30); they rage and seek to imprison him (vv31-32), but "the Lord did not suffer them" yet (v33); Amulek stands forth (v34).

**Thoughts:**
- *Light sinned against is the measure of guilt (vv15-24).* This is the sermon's spine and its great reversal. Ammonihah — and the Nephites generally — assume their favored status makes them *safe*: they are the covenant people, the chosen, the recipients of every gift. Alma turns it exactly around: that favored status makes them *more accountable*, not less. "It shall be **more tolerable** for [the Lamanites] in the day of judgment than for you" — because the Lamanites sinned in ignorance, while the Nephites would sin "contrary to the **light and knowledge** which they do have" (v23). And he piles up the catalog of that light (vv19-22): angels, revelation, the voice of the Lord, spiritual gifts, deliverance from Egypt-like bondage "time after time," prosperity. Every blessing is also a *bill* — to whom much is given, much is required (Luke 12:48; Matt 11:22-24, where Sodom is "more tolerable" than the cities that saw Christ's works and didn't repent). The covenant people's privilege is not a shield; it is a weight. The more you have been shown, the more your sin is a sin against what you *knew*.
- *The Lamanites "more justified" — the grievance tradition's partial exculpation (vv16-17).* Alma states plainly the doctrine the grievance-tradition study identified: "it is **because of the traditions of their fathers that caused them to remain in their state of ignorance; therefore the Lord will be merciful unto them**." The Lamanites' inherited false tradition (Mosiah 10) does not excuse their sins but it *mitigates their culpability* — they were *taught* wrong, not choosing wrong against full light. And verse 17 is the explicit prophecy of the cure: "they will be brought to believe in his word, and to **know of the incorrectness of the traditions of their fathers**; and many of them will be saved." This is the grievance-tradition healing (Mosiah 28's mission; Alma 17-26) prophesied here as certain — and it is the doctrinal ground beneath the sons of Mosiah's whole errand: God Himself has promised the Lamanites will be brought to the truth, so the mission is cooperation with a decree, not a long shot. The ignorant who were lied to are nearer mercy than the enlightened who rebel.
- *"Who is God?" — Ammonihah's Pharaoh moment (vv2, 6).* The hardened heart dismisses God by dismissing His messenger: "Suppose ye that we shall believe the testimony of **one man**?" (v2) and "**Who is God**, that sendeth no more authority than one man?" (v6) — the same defiance as Pharaoh (Ex 5:2) and Noah (Mosiah 11:27). But the "one man" objection contains its own answer: the law itself requires *two* witnesses (Deut 17:6), which is precisely why God has sent Amulek as the second witness (ch 10). Ammonihah's scoff at the single prophet is met by the divine pattern of pairing — the objection is refuted by the structure of the mission, not just its content.
- *The dramatic irony — "destroyed in one day" (v4).* Ammonihah scoffs at the very thing that will happen: "we will not believe... that this great city should be **destroyed in one day**." And in chapter 16, Ammonihah *is* destroyed in a single day by the Lamanites — so utterly that the site is named "Desolation of Nehors." Their scornful disbelief names the exact form of their doom. The reader, who knows the end, hears the unbearable irony: they mocked the prophecy by stating it precisely.
- *The prosperity covenant, applied (vv13-14, 18).* Alma restates the founding covenant — "inasmuch as ye keep my commandments, ye shall prosper... inasmuch as ye will not, ye shall be cut off" (2 Ne 1:20; Mosiah 1:7) — and applies it as a *two-edged historical fact*: the Lamanites were already "cut off from the presence of the Lord" by it (v14), and the Nephites will be cut off too — "the Lamanites shall be sent upon you" (v18) — if they persist. The same covenant that prospered them will destroy them; it does not exempt the favored. (And note: the instrument of Nephite destruction will be the very Lamanites they despise — God uses the less-culpable to judge the more-culpable.)
- *"Ye ought to be beloved" — love under the woe (v30).* After the harshest possible rebuke — wicked, perverse, hard-hearted, stiffnecked, lost, fallen — Alma frames the whole thing in love: "my beloved brethren, for **ye are my brethren, and ye ought to be beloved**." The severity is not contempt; it is the alarm of a man who loves them and sees them walking into one-day destruction. Pure testimony (ch 5) wounds *to heal*; the woe is severe precisely because the love is real. A prophet who did not love Ammonihah would not have gone back (ch 8) to tell it the truth.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The sermon's spine is the great reversal — **light sinned against is the measure of guilt**: Ammonihah assumes its favored Nephite status makes it *safe*, and Alma turns it exactly around — the more light a people has (angels, revelation, gifts, deliverances, prosperity, vv19-22), the *more* accountable, so "it shall be **more tolerable for the Lamanites** in the day of judgment than for you" (v15; Luke 12:48). This chapter is the **proof-text for the grievance-tradition study's partial-exculpation finding**: the Lamanites' inherited false tradition keeps them in "ignorance" and so *mitigates* their culpability (v16), and their conversion is **prophesied as decreed** — "they will be brought to believe... and to know of the incorrectness of the traditions of their fathers; and many of them will be saved" (v17) — the doctrinal ground beneath the sons of Mosiah's mission (Alma 17-26). Ammonihah's "**who is God?**" (v6) is Pharaoh's defiance, and its scoff at "the testimony of one man" is answered by the two-witness pattern (Amulek, ch 10). Its scornful "**destroyed in one day**" (v4) is the exact form of its doom (ch 16 — "Desolation of Nehors") — unbearable dramatic irony. And under the harshest woe runs real love: "**ye are my brethren, and ye ought to be beloved**" (v30).
