# Alma 52 — the dynasty of treachery continues, and Mulek retaken by decoy

**Binding idea:** Amalickiah's death doesn't end the war — his brother Ammoron is "appointed to reign in his stead" (52:3) and inherits both the throne and the vendetta, commanding his people to hold the cities "taken by the shedding of blood" (52:4). Evil regimes are self-perpetuating; the head dies and the body fights on. Against the fortified cities Teancum wisely refuses to "attack them in their forts" (52:5, 17) — you don't assault a stronghold on the enemy's terms. Instead Moroni decoys Jacob's army out of Mulek with a feigned retreat (Teancum as bait), takes the emptied city, and closes the trap with fresh forces front and rear (52:21-31, the ambush of Ai). And even in the closing trap, Moroni offers terms — "If ye will bring forth your weapons of war and deliver them up... we will forbear shedding your blood" (52:37) — so that "the number of prisoners... exceeded more than the number of those who had been slain" (52:40).

## Thoughts

The first thing the chapter establishes is sobering: killing Amalickiah didn't kill the war. His brother Ammoron steps into the throne the same night and prosecutes the identical campaign — "thus king Ammoron, the brother of king Amalickiah, was appointed to reign in his stead" (v3). The dynasty of treachery is self-renewing. Teancum's javelin removed one man, but the machine of hatred Amalickiah built — the stirred-up Lamanites, the captured cities, the Zoramite captains, the standing grievance — rolls on under new management. This is a hard truth about confronting organized evil: cutting off the visible head rarely ends the body, because the body was built to outlast its head. The Nephites had hoped Amalickiah's death would break the invasion; instead they face a brother with the same blood-oath energy and a stronger position (he holds the eastern cities now). Evil that has become institutional doesn't die with its founder.

The military wisdom of the chapter is worth naming because it's a discipline, not just tactics. Twice — Teancum at the start (v5) and again before Moroni arrives (v17) — the Nephite commanders look at the fortified cities and decide *not* to attack. "He saw that it was impossible that he could overpower them while they were in their fortifications; therefore he abandoned his designs" (v17). They refuse to throw their men against a stronghold on the enemy's terms. Instead they wait for reinforcement, prepare, and look for "stratagem or some other way" (v10). That restraint is itself a kind of courage — the harder thing, often, is to *not* attack when attacking would be heroic and futile. And when they do move, it's the classic ambush-of-Ai pattern (Josh 8): Moroni can't get Jacob to "come out... upon fair grounds" (v21), so he decoys — Teancum's small force baits the Lamanites into a pursuit, drawing them out of Mulek, while Moroni's hidden army takes the emptied city, and then fresh troops (Lehi's, then Moroni's) catch the wearied pursuers in a pincer. The whole thing depends on coordination, timing, and the willingness to use a small force as bait and a retreat as a weapon. It's the "no sin to defend by stratagem" principle (43:30) executed at full scale — and it works because the Nephites think while the enemy reacts.

The detail I want to hold is that even here, at the moment the trap closes and the Lamanites are surrounded, wearied, and confused, Moroni's instinct is the same as it's been since chapter 43: offer the off-ramp. "If ye will bring forth your weapons of war and deliver them up, behold we will forbear shedding your blood" (v37). And the result is the line that quietly defines the whole Nephite way of war: "the number of prisoners who were taken exceeded more than the number of those who had been slain" (v40). More captured alive than killed. A commander fighting for conquest or vengeance maximizes the body count; Moroni, fighting to stop a threat, maximizes the surrenders. The mercy isn't sentimentality — it's the logical extension of the cause. If you fight only to protect, then every enemy who lays down his weapon is a victory, not a missed kill. Jacob the Zoramite, by contrast, fights with "an unconquerable spirit" to the death (v33-35) — the dissenter's signature ferocity again — and dies, while the native Lamanites under him surrender and live. Even the pattern of who-dies-and-who-yields keeps preaching the same sermon: the apostate's hardness destroys him; the one who relents is spared.

It's a chapter heavier on logistics than doctrine, but the through-lines hold: organized evil outlives its leader, wisdom refuses to fight on the enemy's terms, and mercy keeps the body count low because the goal was never the killing. The war grinds on — Mulek retaken, but Ammoron alive and the eastern cities still mostly held — and the careful reader feels the weariness setting in. This is the long middle of a long war, and Mormon doesn't glamorize it. He counts the prisoners.

## Notable

Moderately — a strategy chapter, but with durable through-lines: organized evil outlives its leader (Ammoron inherits the war, 52:3), the discipline of not fighting on the enemy's terms (52:5, 17), the decoy-and-pincer stratagem (52:21-31, the ambush of Ai), and the signature Nephite mercy that takes more prisoners than it kills (52:40). The dissenter Jacob's ferocity destroying him while his Lamanites surrender keeps the apostate-vs-native pattern visible.
