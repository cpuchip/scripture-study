# Alma 58 — "he did speak peace to our souls," and the refusal to murmur when abandoned

**Binding idea:** Helaman's army, besieging Manti, is left to wait "many months, even until we were about to perish for the want of food" (58:7), receiving only a fraction of the reinforcement they need and no explanation for the neglect. Their response is prayer — and God's answer is not immediate rescue but something deeper: "the Lord our God did visit us with assurances that he would deliver us; yea, insomuch that he did speak peace to our souls, and did grant unto us great faith, and did cause us that we should hope for our deliverance in him" (58:11). Peace, faith, and hope come *before* the deliverance, and the peace is itself the strengthening. Then they take Manti by a new stratagem "without the shedding of blood" (58:28). And though abandoned by their own government — "we do not know the cause that the government does not grant us more strength" (58:34) — Helaman refuses to murmur: "it mattereth not—we trust God will deliver us" (58:37). His sons of Ammon "stand fast in that liberty wherewith God has made them free... not one soul has been slain" (58:39-40).

**Read:** Alma 58 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Closes Helaman's epistle to Moroni.

## Thoughts

The spiritual center of the chapter is verses 10-11, and it's one of the Book of Mormon's clearest teachings on what answered prayer actually looks like. Helaman's situation is dire — outnumbered, abandoned, starving, "filled with fear, lest by any means the judgments of God should come upon our land, to our overthrow and utter destruction" (v9). They pour out their souls asking God to "strengthen us and deliver us." And the answer comes: "the Lord our God did visit us with assurances that he would deliver us; yea, insomuch that he did speak peace to our souls." Notice what God gives and what he withholds. He does *not* immediately route an army to them or fill their storehouses. He gives "assurances," "peace to our souls," "great faith," and "hope for our deliverance in him" — internal gifts, before any external change. And crucially, those internal gifts are not a consolation prize for an unanswered prayer; they *are* the answer. The peace and faith are what enable the next verse: "we did take courage with our small force... fixed with a determination to conquer" (v12). God answered the prayer for deliverance by first delivering them *from despair* so they could go win the deliverance they'd asked for. This is the D&C 6:23 pattern exactly — "did I not speak peace to your mind concerning the matter? What greater witness can you have than from God?" The peace is the witness, and the peace is the strength. When I pray in a crisis and don't get the rescue I asked for but find an unexpected steadiness, this chapter says that steadiness *is* God answering — the peace that "passeth all understanding" doing its work (Philip 4:7) so that I can act rather than collapse.

The stratagem (vv1, 13-28) shows wisdom adapting. The old decoy trick won't work anymore: "they remembered that which we had hitherto done; therefore we could not decoy them away from their strongholds" (v1). An enemy that learns can't be beaten with yesterday's plan. So Helaman invents a new one — provoke the chase, slip his army past the hidden Gid and Teomner who then take the emptied city, then feint toward Zarahemla, then night-march back to arrive at Manti *before* the returning Lamanites. It's intricate and it works, and again it ends "without the shedding of blood" (v28). The bloodless victory is becoming the Nephite signature — Gid, Mulek, now Manti taken with minimal or no killing, because the goal is to recover the land, not to slaughter the enemy.

But the thing I most want to mark is verses 34-37, because they set up one of the most human moments in the whole war. Helaman's army has been effectively abandoned by their own government — no adequate reinforcement, no explanation, left to nearly perish. He doesn't know why. And here's his response: "we do not know but what ye are unsuccessful, and ye have drawn away the forces into that quarter of the land; if so, we do not desire to murmur. And if it is not so, behold, we fear that there is some faction in the government... But, behold, it mattereth not—we trust God will deliver us" (vv35-37). Helaman, with every earthly reason to feel betrayed by his own side, deliberately refuses to murmur. He considers the charitable explanation first (maybe Zarahemla itself is under attack), and even if the worst is true (a faction withholding support), he lands on "it mattereth not — we trust God." This is a striking model of how to respond to being failed by the people who should have helped you: assume the best you reasonably can, refuse the murmur, and anchor in God rather than in the institution that let you down. And the contrast it sets up is deliberate — in just two chapters, Captain Moroni, in the identical situation of being denied support, will write Pahoran a furious letter of accusation (ch 60). Two righteous men, the same neglect, opposite responses: Helaman refuses to murmur and trusts God; Moroni murmurs hard and accuses the governor. Mormon places them side by side, and he doesn't simply condemn Moroni — but Helaman's quiet "it mattereth not, we trust God" stands as the gentler model, and the resolution in ch 61 (Pahoran was himself driven from the seat by rebels) will vindicate Helaman's instinct to assume the best before accusing.

The epistle closes on the stripling warriors one last time, and the final word on them is endurance, not just the famous preservation. "Not one soul has been slain. But behold, they have received many wounds; nevertheless they stand fast in that liberty wherewith God has made them free; and they are strict to remember the Lord their God from day to day" (vv39-40). The miracle (none slain) is real, but Helaman's emphasis lands on the steadiness: they *stand fast*, they are *strict to remember* God *from day to day*, their *faith is strong*. The preservation was the sign; the daily faithfulness was the substance. And "the liberty wherewith God has made them free" (Gal 5:1's phrasing) ties the political liberty they fought for to the spiritual liberty God gave them — they defend the one because they've tasted the other. Helaman signs it "I am Helaman, the son of Alma" — the son of the man who was harrowed three days and cried to Jesus, now a general who knows how to pour out his soul and receive peace. The faith ran down the generations: Alma's conversion, Helaman's leadership, the stripling warriors' courage, all the same trust expressed in different offices.

## Notable

Yes — **"he did speak peace to our souls" (58:11)**, one of the clearest teachings on answered prayer (the inner gift of peace/faith/hope is the answer, before and instead of immediate rescue; the D&C 6:23 pattern). And the deliberate setup of the Helaman-vs-Moroni contrast (58:34-37): Helaman refuses to murmur when abandoned by his own government, two chapters before Moroni accuses Pahoran over the identical neglect. The warriors' enduring daily faithfulness (58:39-40) is the final word on them.
