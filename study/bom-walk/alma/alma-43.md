# Alma 43 — Captain Moroni and the "better cause": the doctrine of defensive war

**Binding idea:** The record turns from doctrine to war, and the war is given a moral frame from the first battle. Captain Moroni — chief captain at twenty-five (43:17) — prepares his men with defensive armor (43:18-21), combines revelation with strategy (he inquires of the Lord through Alma where to defend, 43:23-24, and "thought it no sin that he should defend them by stratagem," 43:30), and fights for a strictly defensive cause: "they were not fighting for monarchy nor power but they were fighting for their homes and their liberties, their wives and their children... their rites of worship and their church" (43:45). The Lord's standard is explicit — "Inasmuch as ye are not guilty of the first offense, neither the second, ye shall not suffer yourselves to be slain... Ye shall defend your families even unto bloodshed" (43:46-47). And victory ends in mercy: when the enemy is surrounded and terrified, Moroni "commanded his men that they should stop shedding their blood" (43:54).

**Read:** Alma 43 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). Opens the war chapters (43-62).

## Thoughts

The first thing the record does with the war is establish *why* it's being fought, on both sides, because the moral asymmetry is the whole point. Zerahemnah, the Lamanite commander, appoints Amalekite and Zoramite apostates as his chief captains specifically because they are "of a more wicked and murderous disposition," to "preserve their hatred towards the Nephites" and bring them "into bondage" (vv6-8). The aggressor's war runs on manufactured hatred and the lust for power and subjugation. The Nephite design is the exact inverse: "to support their lands, and their houses, and their wives, and their children... their rights and their privileges, yea, and also their liberty, that they might worship God according to their desires" (v9). The continuing pattern holds — the war is driven by *dissenters*, the apostate Zoramites and Amalekites who are worse than the native Lamanites (the 24:30 principle again: those who knew the light and left are the most dangerous). The Lamanites are largely tools of the apostates' hatred.

Captain Moroni enters at twenty-five, and the chapter establishes his three signatures right away. First, *preparation*: he armors his men with "breastplates and with arm-shields... and also shields to defend their heads, and also they were dressed with thick clothing" (v19), while the Lamanites are "naked, save it were a skin." The armor so terrifies the larger Lamanite army that they refuse to engage at Jershon and detour (vv21-22). This is foresight as a moral act — Moroni doesn't trust to numbers or zeal; he prepares, and the preparation saves lives (vv37-38, the armored Nephites lose few while the unarmored Lamanites die "almost at every stroke"). Second, *revelation*: Moroni, "knowing of the prophecies of Alma, sent certain men unto him, desiring him that he should inquire of the Lord whither the armies of the Nephites should go" (v23). The general consults the prophet for strategic guidance — military intelligence and divine revelation working together, neither despised. Third, *lawful cunning*: "he thought it no sin that he should defend them by stratagem" (v30). Moroni conceals armies, uses the terrain (hill Riplah, the river Sidon), and springs an encirclement — and the record explicitly blesses the cleverness. Defending the innocent doesn't require fighting stupidly or "fair"; wisdom and tactics are righteous when the cause is.

The doctrinal heart is verses 45-47, the Book of Mormon's clearest statement on when war is justified. "The Nephites were inspired by a better cause, for they were not fighting for monarchy nor power but they were fighting for their homes and their liberties, their wives and their children, and their all, yea, for their rites of worship and their church." The *cause* is what makes the fighting righteous or not — defense of home, family, freedom, and worship, never conquest or power. And the Lord's standard is precise: "Inasmuch as ye are not guilty of the first offense, neither the second, ye shall not suffer yourselves to be slain by the hands of your enemies. And again... Ye shall defend your families even unto bloodshed." This is the same law later given in D&C 98 — bear the offense once, twice, even thrice, and only then defend; never strike first. The Nephites are permitted bloodshed precisely *because* they are not the aggressors and have borne the provocation. It's a war doctrine with a conscience built in: defensive only, proportionate, and aimed at protection rather than victory-for-its-own-sake. The pacifist people of Ammon kept their covenant by not fighting at all; the Nephites who bear the sword for them do so under a strict moral license, not a blank check.

And the cause shapes the conduct. When Moroni's men are about to break and flee from the Lamanites' fury, he doesn't shame them or threaten them — he "inspired their hearts with these thoughts—yea, the thoughts of their lands, their liberty, yea, their freedom from bondage" (v48). He reminds them what they're fighting *for*, and they "cried with one voice unto the Lord their God for their liberty," and the battle turns in "that selfsame hour" (vv49-50, the Exodus 17 pattern — Israel prevails while the appeal to God is held up). Then the most telling detail: at the moment of total victory, with the Lamanites surrounded on both sides of the river and "struck with terror," Moroni "commanded his men that they should stop shedding their blood" (v54). The defensive cause means the goal was never to kill the enemy but to stop the threat — and the instant the threat is contained, the killing stops. A war fought for power slaughters the helpless; a war fought for protection sheathes the sword the moment protection is achieved. That restraint, mid-massacre, is what sets up the negotiation with Zerahemnah in chapter 44, and it's the difference between Moroni and the men he's fighting.

## Notable

Yes — Captain Moroni introduced (preparation + revelation + stratagem + mercy), and the Book of Mormon's clearest doctrine of *defensive* war (43:45-47, the "better cause," the D&C 98 law of bearing the offense before defending). The restraint at v54 (stop the bloodshed when the threat is contained) is the moral signature that separates Moroni from his enemies.
