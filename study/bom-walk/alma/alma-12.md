# Alma 12 — The Chains of Hell and the Flaming Sword of Mercy

*(Alma takes over from Amulek as Zeezrom trembles — the portion of the word given by heed; the chains of hell as a self-tightening spiral; the Fall, the flaming sword, and the probationary state; "harden not your hearts" → "enter into my rest." ~82 BC. Mini-study — cross-chapter synthesis in spin-off `alma12-13_redemption-and-priesthood`.)*

**Binding idea:** Zeezrom, trembling under guilt, now asks sincerely. Alma answers with two images that turn out to be one doctrine: the *chains of hell* (a heart hardening into ever-less light) and the *flaming sword* barring the tree of life (which Alma reveals to be mercy). How do they connect, and what does each turn on?

**Read:** Alma, seeing Zeezrom "tremble under a consciousness of his guilt" (v1), tells him "thou hast not lied unto men only but thou hast lied unto God; for behold, **he knows all thy thoughts**" (v3); the bribe was "a snare of the adversary... that he might encircle you about with his **chains**" (v6). Zeezrom "began to tremble more exceedingly... convinced more and more of the power of God" (v7) and "**began to inquire of them diligently, that he might know more concerning the kingdom of God**" (v8). Alma: "It is given unto many to know the mysteries of God; nevertheless they... shall not impart only according to the **portion of his word which he doth grant**... **according to the heed and diligence which they give unto him**" (v9); "he that will **harden his heart**, the same receiveth the **lesser portion** of the word; and he that will not harden his heart, to him is given the **greater portion**... until he know them in full" (v10); "they that will harden their hearts... **until they know nothing** concerning his mysteries; and then they are **taken captive by the devil**, and led by his will down to destruction. **Now this is what is meant by the chains of hell**" (v11). "Our words will condemn us... our works will condemn us... our thoughts will also condemn us... we would fain be glad if we could command the rocks and the mountains to fall upon us to hide us" (v14). Antionah asks about "**cherubim and a flaming sword** on the east of the garden of Eden, lest our first parents should... partake of the fruit of the tree of life, and live forever" (v21). Alma: "by his fall, **all mankind became a lost and fallen people**" (v22); had Adam partaken of the tree of life then, "there would have been no death, and the word would have been void, making God a liar" (v23); "there was a **space granted unto man in which he might repent; therefore this life became a probationary state; a time to prepare to meet God**" (v24); "if it had not been for the **plan of redemption, which was laid from the foundation of the world**, there could have been no resurrection" (v25); had they partaken, "they would have been **forever miserable, having no preparatory state**; and thus the plan of redemption would have been frustrated" (v26). God "sent angels to converse with them" (v29) and "made known... the plan of redemption... according to their faith and repentance and their holy works" (v30). "**If ye will repent, and harden not your hearts, then will I have mercy upon you, through mine Only Begotten Son**" (v33); "whosoever repenteth, and hardeneth not his heart... shall have claim on mercy... and these shall **enter into my rest**" (v34); "whosoever will harden his heart... shall not enter into my rest" (v35); "let us repent, and harden not our hearts... but let us **enter into the rest of God**" (v37).

**Thoughts:**
- *The chains of hell — a self-tightening spiral, not external shackles (vv9-11).* This is the chapter's first great image and its most psychologically precise doctrine. Knowledge of God is given "according to the **heed and diligence**" a person gives — and the consequence runs in two self-reinforcing directions. Soften (heed, diligence): "to him is given the greater portion of the word, until he know them in full." Harden: "the same receiveth the lesser portion... **until they know nothing** concerning his mysteries; and then they are taken captive by the devil... **this is what is meant by the chains of hell**." The chains are not arbitrary shackles God locks on a sinner; they are the *self-tightening consequence of refusing light*. Each refusal narrows what the heart can receive, which makes the next refusal easier and the next portion smaller, until there is nothing left to resist with and the devil simply leads. It is a spiral, and there is no neutral standing on it — every heart is either softening into more light or hardening into less. The terrifying mercy is that the spiral is *self-imposed*: a man forges his own chains link by link, by what he will not heed. (And Zeezrom is the live demonstration of the *reverse* spiral — he stopped hardening, "began to inquire diligently" (v8), and so begins to receive the greater portion; the doctrine is enacted in the man hearing it.)
- *The flaming sword was mercy — death as the gift of probation (vv21-27).* Antionah throws the cherubim-and-flaming-sword (Gen 3:24) as a gotcha — surely it proves Adam could never live forever. Alma turns it into mercy. Had Adam partaken of the tree of life *in his fallen state*, "there would have been no death," and "they would have been **forever miserable, having no preparatory state**." Locking man out of the tree of life *preserved* the one thing that makes redemption possible: "a **space granted unto man in which he might repent**." Death — the very thing that looks like the curse — is the gift that creates the "**probationary state, a time to prepare to meet God**" (v24). Without death, the Fall would be permanent, the fallen state eternal, repentance impossible; the flaming sword that barred immortality is what *opened the door to repentance*. (This is 2 Nephi 2's logic — the Fall and its consequences are within the plan, mercy woven into the apparent curse. Mortality is not the catastrophe; it is the chance.)
- *The two images are one doctrine, hinged on "harden not your hearts" (vv33-37).* The chains of hell (the heart hardening into captivity) and the flaming sword (mortality as the time to repent) meet in the discourse's hinge: God's whole appeal is "**If ye will repent, and harden not your hearts, then will I have mercy upon you, through mine Only Begotten Son... and these shall enter into my rest**." The probationary time the flaming sword preserved (v24) exists precisely so the heart can *choose to soften rather than harden* — and that choice determines both the portion of light received (vv10-11) and the entrance into the rest (vv34-37). The same verb governs everything: *harden*. Harden, and the chains tighten and the rest is barred; soften, and the word grows and the rest opens. The flaming sword gave you the time; the unhardened heart is what you do with it. (And the goal — "**the rest of the Lord**" — runs straight into chapter 13, where the priesthood and ordinances exist to bring people into that rest.)
- *The redemption was laid before the Fall (vv25, 30, 33).* Three times Alma anchors the plan "from the foundation of the world." The plan of redemption is not God's reaction to Adam's failure — it was prepared *before* the problem it solves (Mosiah 4:6; 15:19). The Fall did not catch God off guard and force an improvised rescue; the rescue was ready before the fall happened. Which means the whole arc — Fall, probation, death, redemption — is one designed economy, not a disaster patched. (A deep comfort: your own falls have not exhausted a plan made on the fly; the redemption was laid for them before the world was.)
- *Zeezrom's softening, in real time (vv1-8).* The chapter opens with the corrupt lawyer trembling "under a consciousness of his guilt" (v1) and, crucially, *turning the right direction*: he "began to inquire of them diligently, that he might know more concerning the kingdom of God" (v8). The man who came to trap with a bribe is now the model of the heed-and-diligence Alma is about to describe (v9). His conscience, awakened, did not flee the discomfort but pressed toward the light — which is exactly the choice that determines the portion. Zeezrom is the chapter's living proof that the spiral can reverse: a hardened heart, once it stops hardening and starts inquiring, begins to receive.

**Notable / flag:** **Spin-off RUNNING** (`alma12-13_redemption-and-priesthood`, background Sonnet) — the full synthesis of the two-chapter discourse lands there. This chapter's two great images turn out to be one doctrine. The **chains of hell** (vv9-11) are a *self-tightening spiral*: knowledge comes "according to the heed and diligence" — harden the heart and you receive an ever-*lesser* portion "until [you] know nothing" and are "taken captive by the devil"; soften and you receive the greater portion "until [you] know them in full." The chains are self-forged by refusing light, and there is no neutral footing on the spiral. The **flaming sword** barring the tree of life (vv21-27) was *mercy*: had Adam eaten it in his fallen state he would be "forever miserable, having no preparatory state" — so barring immortality preserved "a space granted... in which he might repent," making this life "a **probationary state, a time to prepare to meet God**." Death, the apparent curse, is the gift that makes repentance possible. Both images meet at the discourse's hinge — **"harden not your hearts... and these shall enter into my rest"** (vv33-37) — the same verb (*harden*) governing both the portion of light and the entrance into the rest, the theme that runs straight into ch 13. And the **plan was laid before the Fall** (vv25, 30, 33 — a designed economy, not an improvised rescue). Zeezrom's **real-time softening** (vv1-8 — trembling → diligent inquiry) is the reverse-spiral enacted in the man hearing it.
