# Alma 10 — I Knew, Yet I Would Not Know: The Second Witness and the City's Unseen Shield

*(Amulek's testimony — his Manasseh lineage, his reluctant conversion, the angel who sent him to feed Alma; the second witness; the prayers of the righteous preserve the wicked city; the corrupt lawyers lay the foundation of destruction; Zeezrom introduced. ~82 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Ammonihah scoffed at "the testimony of one man" (9:2). God's answer is a second witness — and not a stranger, but a prosperous local who had spent years refusing to hear. What does Amulek's own account reveal about how a comfortable man avoids God, and what is actually holding the doomed city up?

**Read:** "I am Amulek; I am the son of Giddonah... a descendant of Nephi, who was the son of Lehi... a descendant of **Manasseh, who was the son of Joseph who was sold into Egypt**" (vv2-3). "I am also a man of no small reputation... I have also acquired much riches by the hand of my industry" (v4). "Nevertheless... I never have known much of the ways of the Lord... I did harden my heart, **for I was called many times and I would not hear; therefore I knew concerning these things, yet I would not know**; therefore I went on rebelling against God" (vv5-6). The angel told him: "return to thine own house, for thou shalt feed a prophet of the Lord... he is an hungered" (v7); "I obeyed the voice of the angel" (v8); "I know that the things whereof he hath testified are true; for... as the Lord liveth, even so has he sent his angel to make these things manifest unto me" (v10). "The people began to be astonished, seeing there was **more than one witness**" (v12). The lawyers seek "to catch them in their words" (vv13-16); Amulek "perceived their thoughts" (v17): "O ye wicked and perverse generation, ye lawyers and hypocrites, for ye are laying the foundations of the devil" (v17), "to bring down the wrath of God... even to the utter destruction of this people" (v18). "Well did Mosiah say... that if the time should come that the **voice of this people should choose iniquity**... they would be ripe for destruction" (v19). "**If it were not for the prayers of the righteous, who are now in the land, that ye would even now be visited with utter destruction**" — not by flood "but... by famine, and by pestilence, and the sword" (v22); "**it is by the prayers of the righteous that ye are spared; now therefore, if ye will cast out the righteous from among you then will not the Lord stay his hand**" (v23). The people rage; Amulek: "have I testified against your law? ... I have spoken **in favor of your law, to your condemnation**" (v26); "the foundation of the destruction of this people is beginning to be laid by the **unrighteousness of your lawyers and your judges**" (v27). They call him "a child of the devil" (v28). "There was one among them whose name was **Zeezrom**... the foremost to accuse... having much business" (v31); "the object of these lawyers was to **get gain**" (v32).

**Thoughts:**
- *"I knew, yet I would not know" — the willful not-knowing of the comfortable (vv4-6).* Amulek's self-portrait is one of the most honest in scripture about how a successful man avoids God. He is not ignorant — "a man of no small reputation... much riches by the hand of my industry," well-connected, respected. And his distance from God was not a lack of knowledge but a refusal of it: "**I was called many times and I would not hear; therefore I knew concerning these things, yet I would not know**." The most precise phrase — *I knew, yet I would not know* — names the chosen deafness of the comfortable. He had heard the call repeatedly and declined to let it land, going "on rebelling against God in the wickedness of my heart." His was not the sin of the ignorant Lamanite (Alma 9:16) but of the *informed* man who keeps the knowledge at arm's length because acting on it would cost his comfort. And it took an *angel* and a *hungry prophet at his door* to break through — God reached the man who wouldn't be reached by ordinary means, at the ordinary point of a stranger needing food. The reluctant convert becomes the bold second witness; the man who would not know becomes the one who declares "I know."
- *The second witness, raised from within the city (vv7-12).* Ammonihah rejected "the testimony of one man" (9:2). God's reply is the two-witness law (Deut 17:6): Amulek, "a man of no small reputation" *among them*, now stands beside Alma, and "the people began to be astonished, seeing there was **more than one witness**" (v12). The second witness is not imported — he is one of their own, a local of standing whom they cannot dismiss as an outside agitator. God answered their objection not by overwhelming them with authority but by raising a witness from inside the very community, prepared (ch 8) before the prophet arrived. The hardest audiences are met by the testimony of one of their own.
- *The prayers of the righteous are the city's unseen shield (vv22-23).* The chapter's most arresting doctrine: "**if it were not for the prayers of the righteous... ye would even now be visited with utter destruction**," and "**it is by the prayers of the righteous that ye are spared**." The wicked city is being held up — without knowing it — by the intercession of the righteous minority living in it. This is Abraham bargaining for Sodom (Gen 18:23-32 — for ten righteous the city would be spared): the presence and prayers of the few righteous stay the judgment on the many wicked. And the chilling corollary Amulek names: "if ye will **cast out the righteous** from among you then will not the Lord stay his hand." The moment the wicked expel the righteous, they remove the very thing protecting them. Ammonihah will do exactly this — cast out and burn the believers (ch 14) — and is then destroyed in a single day (ch 16). The righteous they despise are the shield over their heads, and they will tear it down with their own hands. (A sobering inversion of how the world weighs people: the "useless" praying righteous are, invisibly, what keep the city alive.)
- *"In favor of your law, to your condemnation" (v26).* Amulek is careful: he has not attacked the law — "I have spoken in favor of your law, to your condemnation." The law itself is good; it is the *corruption* of it by the lawyers and judges that condemns them. He upholds the law precisely in order to convict them by it — they are guilty *against* the very law they claim to defend. This is the prophet's posture toward institutions: not anti-law, but anti-corruption-of-law; the standard is honored, and its violators are exposed by it.
- *Corrupt lawyers — priestcraft in legal form (vv14-15, 27, 31-32).* The lawyers of Ammonihah are Nehor's priestcraft translated into the legal profession: "learned in all the arts and cunning," seeking to "catch them in their words," and — the diagnostic — "the **object of these lawyers was to get gain**" (v32). Where Nehor preached for money (Alma 1:3-5), these manipulate the law for money. And Amulek names the civilizational stakes: "the **foundation of the destruction of this people is beginning to be laid by the unrighteousness of your lawyers and your judges**" (v27). Corrupt institutions — a justice system that serves gain rather than justice — are the *foundation* of a nation's destruction. This is also the fulfillment of Mosiah's prophecy (29:27, cited in v19): the reign of judges fails when "the voice of this people should choose iniquity," and Ammonihah, with its corrupt lawyers getting gain, is that failure localized. The system Mosiah built to protect against a wicked king cannot protect against a wicked *people* — and the rot starts in the courts. (Zeezrom, v31, the foremost of these corrupt lawyers, is now set up for the great cross-examination of ch 11 — and, remarkably, his own conversion.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The chapter answers Ammonihah's "testimony of one man" scoff (9:2) with **a second witness raised from within** — Amulek, a respected local (v12, "more than one witness"; Deut 17:6). His self-portrait is the most honest in scripture about how the comfortable avoid God: **"I knew, yet I would not know"** (v6) — called many times, refusing to hear, kept at arm's length because acting would cost his comfort; reached only by an angel and a hungry prophet at his door, the reluctant convert become the bold witness. Its most arresting doctrine: **the prayers of the righteous are the wicked city's unseen shield** — "it is by the prayers of the righteous that ye are spared... if ye cast out the righteous... will not the Lord stay his hand" (vv22-23; Abraham's Sodom, Gen 18) — and Ammonihah will tear down that shield with its own hands (cast out the believers, ch 14 → destroyed, ch 16). Amulek upholds the law "**to your condemnation**" (v26 — anti-corruption, not anti-law), and names **corrupt institutions as the foundation of destruction** (the gain-seeking lawyers, v27, 32 — Nehor's priestcraft in legal form), fulfilling **Mosiah's prophecy** that the reign of judges fails when the voice chooses iniquity (v19 → Mosiah 29:27). Zeezrom, the foremost corrupt lawyer (v31), is set up for ch 11. Also: the only explicit statement of **Lehi's tribe — Manasseh** (v3).
