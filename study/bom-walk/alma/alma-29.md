# Alma 29 — "O that I were an angel": holy ambition corrected into holy contentment

**Binding idea:** Alma's most famous longing is also his most honest self-correction. "O that I were an angel... that I might... cry repentance unto every people!" (29:1) — and then, immediately, "But behold, I am a man, and do sin in my wish; for I ought to be content with the things which the Lord hath allotted unto me" (29:3). The desire to save everyone, however holy it sounds, becomes sin when it refuses the bounds of one's actual stewardship. And the resolution is relief, not resignation: he doesn't have to reach every nation because God already "doth grant unto all nations, of their own nation and tongue, to teach his word" (29:8). You are not the only instrument; you are responsible for your portion.

**Read:** Alma 29 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

This soliloquy comes straight out of chapter 28's mourning, and you have to read it that way. Alma has just watched the cost — widows, the heaps of dead, the two mournings. So his cry isn't abstract piety; it's the natural eruption of a man who has seen too much sorrow and wants to end it: "I would declare unto every soul, as with the voice of thunder, repentance and the plan of redemption... that there might not be more sorrow upon all the face of the earth" (v2). The wish to be an angel is the wish to stop the grief he just witnessed. It's the most sympathetic ambition imaginable — and Alma names it as sin anyway.

That's the move that arrests me. "I am a man, and do sin in my wish." Not a sin of laziness or cowardice — a sin of *over-reaching*, of wanting a stewardship bigger than the one allotted. "I ought not to harrow up in my desires the firm decree of a just God" (v4). The holy version of ambition still has to submit to bounds. This is the exact counterpart to Mosiah 4:27 — "it is not requisite that a man should run faster than he has strength" — and to the covenant's honor_scope clause (D&C 107:99, "let every man learn his duty, and to act in the office in which he is appointed"). Alma wants to run faster than his allotment, to be more than the office he was given, and he catches himself doing it. The discipline isn't to want less good; it's to want the good *that is yours to do* and trust God with the rest. I feel the bite of this directly: the impulse to do everything, reach everyone, fix all of it, can wear the costume of zeal while actually being a refusal to accept the size of my own hands.

And the theology underneath the correction is dense. Verses 4-5: "he granteth unto men according to their desire... he allotteth unto men... decrees which are unalterable, according to their wills." God gives people what they actually want — "whether he desireth good or evil, life or death, joy or remorse of conscience." The judgment, in this frame, is largely God ratifying the desire a person spent their life cultivating. Which cuts two ways for Alma: if God grants according to desire, then Alma's job isn't to override anyone's will with angelic thunder — it's to offer the choice and let desire do its work. An angel shaking the earth might compel; a man preaching invites. Maybe that's part of why the wish is a sin — it would short-circuit the very agency the plan depends on.

The relief comes in verse 8, and it's the pastoral heart of the chapter: "the Lord doth grant unto all nations, of their own nation and tongue, to teach his word, yea, in wisdom, all that he seeth fit that they should have." Alma doesn't have to be the trumpet to every people, because God has already stationed teachers among every people, each in their own language, each given what they're ready for. This is the same God-mindful-of-every-people doctrine from Ammon's psalm (26:37), now turned into permission to put down the impossible burden. You are not the world's only hope; you are one instrument in a vast, distributed work. That ought to be the most freeing sentence a tired disciple ever reads. "Why should I desire more than to perform the work to which I have been called?" (v6) — not defeat, but rest. (Another quiet 5:21-adjacent datapoint: God teaching *every* nation in *its own tongue* is hard to square with any reading where some peoples are simply outside his care.)

And the chapter resolves, like Ammon's, into glory-in-God-not-self and joy-in-others' success. "I do not glory of myself, but I glory in that which the Lord hath commanded me... this is my glory, that perhaps I may be an instrument in the hands of God to bring some soul to repentance" (v9) — note "some soul," singular, the opposite of the angelic "every people." He's scaled his ambition down to its right size: one soul at a time, through his actual calling. He remembers his own deliverance ("his merciful arm which he extended towards me," v10, his Mosiah 27 conversion) and his fathers' deliverance from bondage, the God of Abraham who "delivered them out of the hands of the Egyptians" (v12). And then the generosity that completes the correction: "I do not joy in my own success alone, but my joy is more full because of the success of my brethren" (v14). The man who wanted to be the angel who saves everyone ends by rejoicing hardest in what *other people* accomplished — "my soul is carried away, even to the separation of it from the body... so great is my joy" (v16, the same joy-collapse as Ammon in 27:17). Holy ambition, fully corrected, becomes joy in a shared and distributed work where your part is enough and others' parts are cause for celebration, not comparison.
