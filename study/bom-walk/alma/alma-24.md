# Alma 24 — the weapons buried deep: the cure's deepest proof

**Binding idea:** The grievance-cure reaches its furthest point. The Anti-Nephi-Lehies, forgiven of "many murders," bury their weapons "deep in the earth" and covenant that "rather than shed the blood of their brethren they would give up their own lives" (24:17-18). When attacked, they prostrate themselves and praise God as they are cut down — 1,005 slain without resistance. And the unresisting death does what the sword never could: the attackers, "stung for the murders which they had committed," throw down their own weapons and convert (24:25), so that "the people of God were joined that day by more than the number who had been slain" (24:26). The refusal to continue the cycle of blood breaks the cycle.

**Read:** Alma 24 (full, verbatim via gospel_get).

## Thoughts

The king's speech (vv7-16) is the theological engine, and its logic about the swords is precise and strange and beautiful. He starts in pure gratitude — God sent the Nephites to convince them "of the traditions of our wicked fathers" (v7, the grievance named as the thing repented of), softened their hearts, convinced them "of our sins, and of the many murders which we have committed," forgave them and "taken away the guilt from our hearts, through the merits of his Son" (vv8-10). They were, he says, "the most lost of all mankind" (v11). Then the sword reasoning: "since God hath taken away our stains, and our swords have become bright, then let us stain our swords no more with the blood of our brethren" (v12). And the part that makes this a covenant of absolute pacifism rather than mere reluctance: "perhaps, if we should stain our swords again they can no more be washed bright through the blood of the Son of our great God" (v13). They fear that for *them* — a people forgiven of murder — a return to killing might forfeit the atonement that cleansed them (the D&C 42:18 weight, that the murderer "hath not forgiveness"; Heb 10:26's "if we sin wilfully after... knowledge"). This isn't policy. It's the reasoning of people who know exactly what their hands have done, and dare not pick the sword back up lest the cleansing not hold a second time. So they bury the weapons "deep in the earth" as "a testimony to our God at the last day" (vv15-17).

Verse 18 is the covenant, and it's structured as three reversals of the grievance tradition's three vices. The Lamanites had "delighted in murdering" — now "rather than shed the blood of their brethren they would give up their own lives." They had robbed and plundered to avoid labor — now "rather than take away from a brother they would give unto him." They were the "idle" people of the curse-texts — now "rather than spend their days in idleness they would labor abundantly with their hands." Every term of who they were is inverted in the covenant of who they've become. The buried weapons aren't just a peace treaty; they're the physical sign of a complete reversal of the inherited identity. Mormon's own line catches it: "they buried their weapons of peace, or they buried the weapons of war, for peace" (v19) — the weapons become instruments of peace precisely by being buried.

Then the test comes, and they pass it at the cost of their lives. The army attacks; "they went out to meet them, and prostrated themselves before them to the earth, and began to call on the name of the Lord" (v21). Not flight, not surrender-with-hands-up — worship, in the path of the swords. "Without meeting any resistance, they did slay a thousand and five of them" (v22). And here is the thing the whole arc was driving toward: "when the Lamanites saw that their brethren would not flee from the sword... but that they would lie down and perish, and praised God even in the very act of perishing" (v23) — the attackers break. "Many whose hearts had swollen in them for those of their brethren who had fallen under the sword... repented" (v24), and "threw down their weapons of war... for they were stung for the murders which they had committed; and they came down even as their brethren, relying upon the mercies of those whose arms were lifted to slay them" (v25). The murderers convert mid-massacre. You cannot keep killing people who lie down and praise God while you kill them; the grievance that called them bloodthirsty liars cannot survive the sight of them dying without raising a hand. The unresisting death is the final, unanswerable sermon.

And the math is the point. "The people of God were joined that day by more than the number who had been slain" (v26) — more converted than died. The martyrdom is net-positive. Tertullian's line, that the blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church, is dramatized here with a body count that runs in the church's favor: 1,005 dead, "more than a thousand brought to the knowledge of the truth" (v27). The sword that the Nephites used for centuries to "deliver" themselves never converted a single Lamanite; the refusal to use the sword converted a thousand in an afternoon. This is the grievance-cure's deepest proof, and it's why the converts had to bury the weapons specifically — the cure *works by* refusing to perpetuate the cycle, not merely by adding believers.

Mormon closes with the doctrinal capstone that's been building since chapter 21. The killers were "Amalekites and Amulonites... after the order of the Nehors" (v28); of those who joined, "there were none who were Amalekites or Amulonites, or who were of the order of Nehor, but they were actual descendants of Laman and Lemuel" (v29). The native Lamanites convert; the apostate Nehorites kill and don't. Then verse 30, stated as a general law: "after a people have been once enlightened by the Spirit of God, and have had great knowledge of things pertaining to righteousness, and then have fallen away into sin and transgression, they become more hardened, and thus their state becomes worse than though they had never known these things." This is the New Testament's hardest warning (Heb 10:26; 2 Pet 2:20-21; Matt 12:45) stated plainly: apostasy from light is worse than never having received it. The whole sons-of-Mosiah mission is, at this level, a controlled demonstration of that principle — the never-enlightened ferocious Lamanites were curable; the once-enlightened Nehorite dissenters were not. It's a sobering verse to sit under as someone who has received much.
