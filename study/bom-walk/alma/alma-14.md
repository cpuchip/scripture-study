# Alma 14 — The Spirit Constraineth Me: Why the Prophets Did Not Save the Burning

*(The believers and the holy scriptures burned; Alma and Amulek bound and made to watch, forbidden to use their power; Zeezrom's conviction; the Christic silence before accusers; the prison rent and the tormentors crushed. ~82–81 BC. Mini-study — v11 is one of the Book of Mormon's hardest and most important verses.)*

**Binding idea:** Amulek, watching women and children burn, begs to use the power of God to save them — and Alma says no, the Spirit forbids it. This is the Book of Mormon's most direct confrontation with the question: why does God let the innocent suffer when He could stop it? Alma answers under the most agonizing conditions imaginable.

**Read:** Some believe and "search the scriptures" (v1); the majority, angry at "the plainness" of the preaching, want them destroyed (vv2-3). Bound before the chief judge, the people testify against them (v5). **Zeezrom**, "astonished," "knew concerning the blindness of the minds which he had caused... his soul began to be **harrowed up under a consciousness of his own guilt**; yea, he began to be **encircled about by the pains of hell**" (v6); he cries, "**I am guilty, and these men are spotless before God**," and pleads for them — and is spit upon and cast out with the believers, stoned (v7). "And whosoever believed... they caused that they should be **cast into the fire**; and they also brought forth their records which contained the **holy scriptures, and cast them into the fire also**" (v8). Alma and Amulek are carried to "the place of martyrdom... that they might witness the destruction" (v9). Amulek, "pained": "**How can we witness this awful scene? Therefore let us stretch forth our hands, and exercise the power of God which is in us, and save them from the flames**" (v10). Alma: "**The Spirit constraineth me that I must not stretch forth mine hand; for behold the Lord receiveth them up unto himself, in glory; and he doth suffer that they may do this thing... according to the hardness of their hearts, that the judgments which he shall exercise upon them in his wrath may be just; and the blood of the innocent shall stand as a witness against them**" (v11). "Perhaps they will burn us also." "**Be it according to the will of the Lord. But, behold, our work is not finished; therefore they burn us not**" (v13). The chief judge — "after the order and faith of **Nehor**" (v16) — smites them: "ye see that ye had not power to save those who had been cast into the fire" (v15); "Alma and Amulek **answered him nothing**" (v17). In prison, mocked — "**If ye have such great power why do ye not deliver yourselves?**" (v20), "How shall we look when we are damned?" (v21) — food and water withheld, clothes taken (v22). On the twelfth day all the tormentors smite them again: "**If ye have the power of God deliver yourselves**... and then we will believe" (v24). "When the last had spoken... **the power of God was upon Alma and Amulek**, and they rose" (v25); Alma: "O Lord, **give us strength according to our faith which is in Christ, even unto deliverance**" (v26). They break the cords; "the earth shook mightily, and the **walls of the prison were rent in twain**... and the chief judge, and the lawyers, and priests, and teachers, who smote upon Alma and Amulek, were **slain by the fall**" (v27); the two came forth unhurt, "**every soul within the walls... save it were Alma and Amulek, was slain**" (v28); the people fled "even as a goat fleeth with her young from two lions" (v29).

**Thoughts:**
- *Why the prophets did not save the burning — the hardest verse (vv10-11).* This is the Book of Mormon's most direct answer to the oldest question — why does a God who *could* intervene let the innocent suffer? — and it is given by a man watching women and children burn, holding the power to stop it, told by the Spirit not to. Amulek's instinct is the human one: "let us stretch forth our hands... and save them from the flames." Alma's restraint, and his three reasons, are the answer. First: "**the Lord receiveth them up unto himself, in glory**" — death is not the worst thing that can happen to the righteous; the martyrs pass instantly into glory, so what looks like the ultimate loss is, for them, gain. Second: "he doth suffer that they may do this thing... **that the judgments which he shall exercise upon them in his wrath may be just**" — God permits the wicked to *complete* their wickedness so that the judgment falling on them is unimpeachably just; to stop them mid-atrocity would be to judge them for what they intended rather than what they fully chose. Third: "**the blood of the innocent shall stand as a witness against them**" — the suffering itself is not wasted; it becomes evidence at the last day. This is not a comfortable answer, and Alma does not offer it comfortably — he offers it through tears, constrained, while the people he could save die in front of him. But it is honest: God's non-intervention is not absence or impotence. It is the hard intersection of the martyr's glory, the agent's full accountability, and the testimony of innocent blood. The restraint costs the prophet everything (he must watch), and that cost is part of what makes the answer credible — Alma is not theorizing about others' suffering from safety; he is bearing the agony of *not acting* when every fiber wants to.
- *"Our work is not finished; therefore they burn us not" (v13).* The flip side of the restraint clarifies the logic. Amulek fears they too will burn, and Alma is wholly submitted — "Be it according to the will of the Lord" — yet adds: "our work is not finished; therefore they burn us not." The believers' work *was* finished (they were received in glory, v11); Alma and Amulek's was not, so they are preserved. The same God who let the believers die preserves the prophets — not by favoritism but by *mission*. This is the shield of 8:31 made precise: protection lasts exactly as long as the work, and no longer. The believers were not less loved; their course was simply run, and death was their entrance into glory, not their abandonment. (Which means Alma's restraint was not "saving himself" — he expected he might burn too and accepted it; the line is the will of the Lord and the unfinished work, not self-preservation.)
- *Zeezrom's conviction — the pains of hell as awakened guilt (vv6-7).* Zeezrom's arc reaches its agony here. The man who bribed (11:22), then trembled (11:46), then inquired diligently (12:8), is now "**harrowed up under a consciousness of his own guilt**... encircled about by the **pains of hell**." This is the chains-of-hell doctrine (12:11) experienced from the inside — the "pains of hell" are precisely the torment of awakened guilt, the soul seeing its own wickedness clearly. And conviction produces confession and courage: "**I am guilty, and these men are spotless before God**," and he pleads for them at the cost of being cast out and stoned himself. The corrupt lawyer becomes, in his guilt, a truer witness than the "righteous" who burn the innocent. (His healing — body and soul — comes in ch 15, where Alma seeks him out.)
- *The Christic silence and the cross-echoes (vv17-24).* Alma and Amulek "answered him nothing" — repeatedly, under smiting and mockery (vv17, 18, 19, 20). This is deliberately Christ before Pilate and the Sanhedrin: "he answered him to never a word" (Matt 27:12-14). And the mockers' taunts are the cross's own: "**If ye have such great power why do ye not deliver yourselves?**" (v20) is "if thou be the Son of God, come down from the cross" (Matt 27:40); "I have power to deliver you up unto the flames" (v19) is Pilate's "I have power to crucify thee" (John 19:10). The whole Ammonihah martyrdom is patterned on the Passion — the silent righteous before the mocking powerful, the demand for a self-saving sign refused. The prophets will not perform on command; they will not buy their lives or the crowd's belief with a miracle staged for mockers (the sign-seeking of v24). The power is God's to give in His time, not theirs to spend on a dare.
- *Deliverance after the full measure (vv24-29).* The power comes only "**when the last had spoken**" (v25) — after *every* tormentor had smitten them and the measure of their guilt was full. The deliverance God withheld through the whole ordeal (8:31) is finally shown, and it is terrible: the earth shakes, the prison walls are "rent in twain," and the entire Nehor establishment that smote them — chief judge, lawyers, priests, teachers — is "slain by the fall." Alma and Amulek walk out unhurt, loosed, and the people flee "as a goat fleeth with her young from two lions." The same restraint that would not save the burning believers is now the power that crushes their killers — because the timing was always God's. The innocent blood that "shall stand as a witness" (v11) begins, in the falling prison, to be answered. (And the scriptures they burned, v8, could not be destroyed — copies burn; the word survives.)

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off — but **v11 is one of the Book of Mormon's hardest and most important verses**, handled at full depth. This is the harrowing climax of the Ammonihah mission: the believers and **the holy scriptures are burned** (v8), and Amulek, in agony, begs to use the power of God to save the women and children — and Alma refuses: "**the Spirit constraineth me that I must not stretch forth mine hand**." His three-part answer is the BoM's most direct confrontation with *why God permits the innocent to suffer when He could stop it*: (1) **the martyrs are "received up unto himself, in glory"** (death is not the worst thing for the righteous); (2) God permits it "**that the judgments... may be just**" (the wicked must complete their guilt — intervention would judge intent, not full choice); (3) "**the blood of the innocent shall stand as a witness against them**" (the suffering is not wasted but testifies). Non-intervention is not absence or impotence — and Alma gives the answer *through tears, able to act, told not to*, which is what makes it credible. The prophets are preserved only because "**our work is not finished**" (v13 — protection by mission, not favoritism; the believers' work *was* done). **Zeezrom's conviction** completes — "harrowed up... encircled by the pains of hell," confessing "I am guilty, these men are spotless" (vv6-7 — the chains-of-hell as awakened guilt). The prophets keep the **Christic silence** ("answered him nothing") under cross-echoing mockery ("deliver yourselves if ye have power," v20 = Matt 27:40), refusing the **sign-seeking dare** (v24). And the **deliverance comes only "when the last had spoken"** (v25, the measure full) — the prison rent, the whole Nehor establishment crushed, the two walking out unhurt (8:31's withheld power finally shown). Next: Alma 15 (the aftermath — Alma and Amulek go to Sidom; they find the cast-out believers; **Alma heals Zeezrom**, body and soul, and baptizes him; Amulek's costly discipleship — he had forsaken his gold, kindred, and friends).
