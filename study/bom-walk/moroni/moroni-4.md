# Moroni 4 — the sacrament prayer on the bread: remembrance, the threefold covenant, and the promise of the Spirit

**Binding idea:** "The manner of their elders and priests administering the flesh and blood of Christ unto the church... according to the commandments of Christ; wherefore we know the manner to be true" (4:1). Kneeling with the church, the priest prays: "O God, the Eternal Father, we ask thee in the name of thy Son, Jesus Christ, to bless and sanctify this bread to the souls of all those who partake of it; that they may eat in remembrance of the body of thy Son, and witness unto thee, O God, the Eternal Father, that they are willing to take upon them the name of thy Son, and always remember him, and keep his commandments which he hath given them, that they may always have his Spirit to be with them. Amen" (4:3).

## Thoughts

Moroni preserves the sacrament prayer on the bread in its exact, full form — and these are the very words used in the Church today, unchanged across fourteen centuries because Moroni wrote them down while hiding for his life. The authority of the ordinance is stated plainly: it was administered "according to the commandments of Christ; wherefore we know the manner to be true" (4:1). The sacrament is not a human invention or an evolving tradition; Christ Himself instituted it among the Nephites (3 Ne 18), and the manner is "true" because He gave it. What Moroni preserves is therefore not a custom but a *commandment kept exactly*.

And the prayer itself, brief as it is, contains a complete theology of the sacrament. First, *remembrance*: the bread is eaten "in remembrance of the body of thy Son" (4:3; Luke 22:19). The ordinance is fundamentally an act of memory — turning the partaker, every week, back to the body of Christ that was given for them. Second, a *threefold covenant*, made by the act of eating: the partakers "witness unto thee, O God, the Eternal Father, that they are willing to (1) take upon them the name of thy Son, and (2) always remember him, and (3) keep his commandments which he hath given them." This is precisely the baptismal covenant (Mosiah 18:8-10) — to take Christ's name, to remember Him always, to keep His commandments — *renewed*. The sacrament is the weekly renewal of baptism; what was promised once at the water is re-witnessed each Sabbath at the table. And third, the *promise*: "that they may always have his Spirit to be with them." The covenant is not one-directional. The partaker witnesses willingness; God answers with the abiding companionship of the Spirit. "Always" appears twice and bears the weight: *always* remember Him, that they may *always* have His Spirit. The constancy of the remembering is matched by the constancy of the promised presence — the Spirit's companionship is conditioned on, and proportioned to, the remembering. To always remember Christ is to always have His Spirit; the two are bound. There is the whole purpose of the sacrament: a weekly act of remembrance, by which the baptismal covenant is renewed, and through which the constant companionship of the Holy Ghost is secured to those who will always remember Him.
