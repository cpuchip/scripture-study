# Moroni 1 — "I have not as yet perished": the last man writes on, refusing to deny Christ

**Binding idea:** Moroni, having finished the book of Ether, "had supposed not to have written more, but I have not as yet perished" (1:1). The Lamanites "put to death every Nephite that will not deny the Christ. And I, Moroni, will not deny the Christ; wherefore, I wander whithersoever I can for the safety of mine own life" (1:2-3). So he writes "a few more things, contrary to that which I had supposed... that perhaps they may be of worth unto my brethren, the Lamanites, in some future day, according to the will of the Lord" (1:4).

**Read:** Moroni 1 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 4 verses. The last book begins — the lone survivor, hunted, writing on.

## Thoughts

Four verses, and they are among the most quietly moving in the whole record, because of where Moroni is when he writes them. He had ended the book of Ether and "supposed not to have written more" — he thought his work was done, the record finished, his task complete. And then: "but I have not as yet perished." He is still alive. The end he expected has not come. And so, finding himself still living when he thought he would be dead, he does not coast or wait passively for the end — he *keeps working*. "I write a few more things, contrary to that which I had supposed." The man who thought he was finished discovers he is not finished, because he is not yet gone, and so he takes up the pen again. There is a lesson here for anyone who has thought a task complete and then found themselves still standing with strength remaining: as long as you have "not as yet perished," there is more to give. Moroni does not treat his unexpected survival as a reprieve from labor but as an extension of it. The book of Moroni — including chapter 7, Mormon's sermon on charity, and chapter 10, the promise — exists *only because* Moroni, expecting death, lived a little longer and chose to fill the time with more writing rather than mere survival.

And the reason he is still hunted, and the reason he writes in secret, is the testimony he will not surrender (1:2-3): "because of their hatred they put to death every Nephite that will not deny the Christ. And I, Moroni, will not deny the Christ; wherefore, I wander whithersoever I can for the safety of mine own life." This is the simplest and starkest statement of fidelity in the Book of Mormon. The Lamanites have made the line clear: deny Christ and live, confess Him and die. Every Nephite who will not deny is put to death. And Moroni — the last of them — "will not deny the Christ." He could buy his life with a single denial. The hunt would end; he could come out of hiding; he could live. All it would cost is the renunciation of the One he has seen face to face (Ether 12:39). And he will not pay it. So he "wander[s] whithersoever I can for the safety of mine own life" — not to escape the demand to deny (he has already refused that) but simply to stay alive long enough to finish the work, hunted, homeless, alone. There is no drama in his telling, no boast of courage; just the plain fact: they kill those who won't deny, I won't deny, so I wander. This is what faithfulness looks like stripped to its bones — not a grand gesture but a steady refusal, day after day, to save your life by a lie, even when the lie is the only thing standing between you and safety. Moroni's whole remaining life is one long, quiet "no" to the denial that would save him.

And the motive for writing on is the same grace that closed Mormon's portion of the record (1:4): he writes "that perhaps they may be of worth unto my brethren, the Lamanites, in some future day." The Lamanites are *hunting him to death* — and he writes for *their* good, for the worth his words might have to *their* descendants "in some future day." The hunted man writes for his hunters' children. This is the love of enemies carried to the same furthest extreme his father reached (Morm 7): a man whose entire people were exterminated by the Lamanites, who is himself being hunted by them, spends his last strength writing words he hopes will bless the Lamanites' posterity centuries later. He calls them "my brethren" — the people killing him are his brethren. There is no bitterness, no curse, only the labor of love for a future generation of the very people who would destroy him. And it is all "according to the will of the Lord" — Moroni does not write for his own legacy or even his own comfort but in submission to God's will for a people not yet born. The last Nephite, alone, hunted, refusing to deny Christ, bends his remaining days to writing for the eternal good of the descendants of his enemies. That is the spirit in which the final book of the Book of Mormon is written — and it is the spirit of Christ Himself, who, hunted to death, prayed for those who killed Him.
