# Moroni 10 — the last chapter: Moroni's farewell, the promise, the gifts, and "come unto Christ, and be perfected in him"

**Binding idea:** "I would exhort you that ye would ask God, the Eternal Father, in the name of Christ, if these things are not true; and if ye shall ask with a sincere heart, with real intent, having faith in Christ, he will manifest the truth of it unto you, by the power of the Holy Ghost" (10:4). And the whole book's final word: "come unto Christ, and be perfected in him, and deny yourselves of all ungodliness... then is his grace sufficient for you, that by his grace ye may be perfect in Christ" (10:32). The last living Nephite seals the record and turns the entire 239-chapter witness into a single invitation to the reader.

**Read:** Moroni 10 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 34 verses, ending "The End." Written ~AD 421 by Moroni, alone, the last of his people. The capstone of the entire Book of Mormon — Moroni's own farewell, the promise of revealed truth (10:3-5), the catalog of the gifts of the Spirit (10:8-18), the call not to deny the power of God, and the final exhortation to come unto Christ and be sanctified.

## Thoughts

This is the end of the whole thing — not just of Moroni, not just of the Nephite record, but of the entire Book of Mormon. A thousand years of writing, from Lehi leaving Jerusalem to this. And the last man alive sits down to seal it, knowing no one of his people remains, and writes "unto my brethren, the Lamanites" (10:1) — to the descendants of the very people who destroyed his own. There is no bitterness in it. The man who buried a civilization writes his closing words *to the children of its killers*, in love, that they might come unto Christ. That alone is a sermon. And the whole chapter is an exhortation — Moroni isn't narrating history anymore; every paragraph but the catalog of gifts begins "I would exhort you." He has one purpose left: to reach the reader.

**The promise (10:3-5) is the most consequential paragraph in the book, and its structure is worth reading slowly because every clause is load-bearing.** First, *before* the asking, comes a remembering: "remember how merciful the Lord hath been unto the children of men, from the creation of Adam even down until the time that ye shall receive these things, and ponder it in your hearts" (10:3). The promise doesn't begin with prayer; it begins with pondering the mercy of God across the whole sweep of scripture you've just read. You read the book, you see the long pattern of God's mercy, you *ponder* it — and only then do you ask. The asking is the fruit of the pondering. Then the conditions, four of them, all internal: "ask with a sincere heart, with real intent, having faith in Christ" (10:4). Sincere heart (no pretense, honestly wanting the answer), real intent (meaning to act on what you receive — the "real intent" that has run through Moroni 6, 7, and 8 as the hinge of every genuine spiritual act), faith in Christ (not faith in your own ability to feel something, but faith in Him). With those, the promise is unconditional: "he *will* manifest the truth of it unto you, by the power of the Holy Ghost." Not might. Will. And then it expands past the book itself to the widest claim in scripture about how truth is known: "by the power of the Holy Ghost ye may know the truth of *all things*" (10:5). The same method that confirms the Book of Mormon confirms all truth — the Holy Ghost as the universal means of knowing. This is the epistemology of the whole Restoration in two verses: truth is not finally settled by argument or evidence but witnessed by the Spirit to a heart that asks honestly, intending to obey.

**Verse 6 quietly carries forward Mormon's discernment test from chapter 7, and ties it to Christ.** "Whatsoever thing is good is just and true; wherefore, nothing that is good denieth the Christ, but acknowledgeth that he is." This is the converse of Moroni 7:16-17 — there, everything that invites to good is of Christ; here, everything good *acknowledges* Christ. Good and Christ are inseparable from both directions: what is good comes from Him, and what is good points back to Him. So the test for truth (10:5) and the test for good (10:6) and the witness of the Spirit all converge on the same Person.

**Then the heart of the chapter's middle: do not deny the gifts of God (10:8-18).** Moroni lists them — the word of wisdom, the word of knowledge, exceeding great faith, healing, working mighty miracles, prophecy, beholding angels, tongues, interpretation (10:9-16, Paul's catalog from 1 Corinthians 12 reproduced). And he frames them with three claims that matter more than the list. First, the *diversity-in-unity*: "there are different ways that these gifts are administered; but it is the same God who worketh all in all... they come unto every man severally, according as he will" (10:8, 17). The gifts are distributed differently to different people, but from one source, by the Spirit's own choosing — no one has all of them, no one is without some, and the variety is by design. Second, "every good gift cometh of Christ" (10:18) — again, all good traces to Him. Third, and this is the drumbeat of the whole final movement: "he is the same yesterday, today, and forever, and... all these gifts... never will be done away, even as long as the world shall stand, *only according to the unbelief of the children of men*" (10:19). The gifts never cease on God's side. He is unchanging (the same immutable God of Mormon 9, Moroni 7 and 8). The only thing that can end the gifts is unbelief on *our* side. This is Moroni's last and most emphatic statement of the theme his father preached: God has not changed, the heavens are not closed, and if the miracles seem gone it is never because God withdrew them but because men stopped believing (10:24 — "if the day cometh that the power and gifts of God shall be done away among you, it shall be because of unbelief").

**Verses 20-23 give the faith-hope-charity ladder one final time, now as the very condition of salvation.** "There must be faith; and if there must be faith there must also be hope; and if there must be hope there must also be charity. And except ye have charity ye can in nowise be saved in the kingdom of God" (10:20-21). Moroni takes his father's sermon (chapter 7) and presses it to the sharpest point: these three aren't merely virtues to cultivate, they are the *requirement* — no charity, no salvation; no faith, no salvation; no hope, no salvation. And he adds the diagnosis of their absence: "if ye have no hope ye must needs be in despair; and despair cometh because of iniquity" (10:22). Despair isn't a mood or a misfortune; at root it "cometh because of iniquity" — the loss of hope traces back to sin, because sin severs you from the atonement that hope rests on. The way out of despair, then, is not positive thinking but repentance — restore the connection to Christ and hope returns. (Which is exactly the counterweight Mormon gave his son in 9:25 — "may Christ lift thee up.")

**And then the most haunting image in the chapter: Moroni speaking from the dust.** "Ye shall see me at the bar of God; and the Lord God will say unto you: Did I not declare my words unto you, which were written by this man, like as one crying from the dead, yea, even as one speaking out of the dust?" (10:27). Moroni knows he is about to die and that his words will not be read for centuries — that when they are read, he will be long dead, his voice literally coming up out of the earth where the plates lay buried. Isaiah 29:4 made flesh: "thou shalt be brought down, and shalt speak out of the ground... thy speech shall whisper out of the dust." He is writing to people who will hold his book after he has been dead fourteen hundred years, and he tells them: when you stand at the judgment, God will confirm that these words were His, delivered through a dead man's record. The witness reaches across the grave and across the centuries. There is something almost unbearable in the loneliness and the faith of it — a man entirely alone, his people gone, writing to a future he will never see, trusting that God will carry his voice out of the dust to people not yet born. And he is *right*. We are the people. The book is in our hands. The voice did come up out of the ground.

**The final exhortation (10:30-33) is the destination of the entire Book of Mormon, and it is pure Christ.** "Come unto Christ, and lay hold upon every good gift" (10:30) — the same "lay hold upon every good thing" from Mormon's sermon (7:19-25), now the closing command. Then the Isaiah call to Zion to awake and put on her beautiful garments (10:31, from Isaiah 52:1 and 54:2), tying the personal invitation to the covenant restoration of all Israel. And then verse 32, which is the summit of the whole book — the clearest statement anywhere of how grace and works finally relate:

"Come unto Christ, and be perfected in him, and deny yourselves of all ungodliness; and if ye shall deny yourselves of all ungodliness, and love God with all your might, mind and strength, then is his grace sufficient for you, that by his grace ye may be perfect in Christ."

Read the order carefully, because it answers the oldest argument in Christianity. We do not earn perfection — "by his grace ye may be perfect in Christ." It is grace that perfects. *But* the grace becomes sufficient as we deny ourselves of all ungodliness and love God with everything. The denying and the loving don't *purchase* the perfection; they are the turning toward Christ that lets His grace do its perfecting work. Grace is sufficient — but it is sufficient *for those who come, who deny ungodliness, who love God with all their might*. Not grace alone as a substitute for the whole heart, and not works as a substitute for grace, but the whole heart turned toward Christ so that grace can finish what we cannot. "By his grace ye may be perfect in Christ" — the perfection is in Christ, by grace, and the condition is the whole-souled turning. And it completes in sanctification: "ye are sanctified in Christ by the grace of God, through the shedding of the blood of Christ... that ye become holy, without spot" (10:33). The entire arc — come, be perfected, deny ungodliness, love God wholly, receive sufficient grace, be sanctified by His blood, become holy without spot. That is the whole gospel, and it is the last doctrine the Book of Mormon teaches before it closes. The book that began with a family leaving Jerusalem ends by telling the reader exactly how to become holy.

**And the farewell (10:34) is Moroni's, and it is at peace.** "I soon go to rest in the paradise of God, until my spirit and body shall again reunite, and I am brought forth triumphant through the air, to meet you before the pleasing bar of the great Jehovah, the Eternal Judge of both quick and dead. Amen. The End." The last living Nephite, who has watched everyone he loved die, faces his own death not with dread but with *rest* — "I soon go to rest." And resurrection — "my spirit and body shall again reunite." And confidence — "brought forth triumphant." And reunion — "to meet you," the reader, at the judgment bar. He calls it the *pleasing* bar (Jacob 6:13's phrase) — for those who came unto Christ, the judgment is not terror but a glad meeting. And the Judge is named: "the great Jehovah" — Christ Himself, the God of the Old Testament, here at the very last word identified as the Judge of quick and dead. (The final, climactic datapoint for the is-Christ-God study: the Book of Mormon's last verse names Jesus as Jehovah, the Eternal Judge.) The book ends with a lonely man at peace, trusting he will rise and meet his future readers before the throne of Christ. "The End" — but the gesture is entirely toward beginning: come unto Christ.

## Connections

- **D&C 9:7-9; James 1:5; 1 Ne 10:17-19** — the promise of revealed knowledge to the sincere asker; "ask with a sincere heart, with real intent, having faith in Christ" (10:4); the pattern of receiving truth by the Spirit.
- **1 Cor 12:4-11; D&C 46:8-30** — the gifts of the Spirit, diverse but from one God (10:8-17); Paul's catalog reproduced and framed by Moroni.
- **Moroni 7:16-25, 33-37, 45-48** — Mormon's sermon, which Moroni's closing draws on directly: every good thing of Christ, the gifts never ceasing but by unbelief, the faith-hope-charity ladder; the son completing the father's teaching.
- **Heb 13:8; Mormon 9:9; Moroni 8:18** — "he is the same yesterday, today, and forever" (10:19); the unchanging God as the ground that the gifts never cease.
- **Isa 29:4; 2 Ne 3:19-20; 2 Ne 33:13** — "speaking out of the dust" (10:27); the voice of the dead prophets crying from the ground, fulfilled in the coming-forth of the record.
- **Isa 52:1; Isa 54:2** — "awake, and arise from the dust, O Jerusalem... put on thy beautiful garments... enlarge thy borders" (10:31); the covenant restoration of Israel tied to the personal invitation.
- **2 Cor 12:9; Ether 12:27; 3 Ne 27:27** — "his grace is sufficient... by his grace ye may be perfect in Christ" (10:32); the grace that perfects, completing the "even as I am" arc.
- **Moroni 6:4; 2 Ne 25:23; 2 Ne 31:19-20** — "relying alone upon the merits of Christ" / "by grace... after all we can do"; verse 32 as the book's final word on grace and the whole heart.

## Notable

Yes — the last chapter of the entire Book of Mormon, and it is dense with capstone doctrine. **Moroni's promise — ask with a sincere heart, real intent, faith in Christ, and the Spirit will manifest the truth** (10:4, after pondering God's mercy); **by the power of the Holy Ghost ye may know the truth of all things** (10:5, the Restoration's epistemology); **nothing that is good denieth Christ but acknowledgeth that he is** (10:6, 18); **deny not the gifts of God — diverse in administration, one in source, given as the Spirit wills** (10:8-18); **the gifts never cease except by unbelief, because God is the same forever** (10:19, 24 — Moroni's last word on his father's theme); **faith, hope, and charity are the condition of salvation, and despair comes of iniquity** (10:20-22); **Moroni speaks from the dust — the witness reaching across the grave and the centuries** (10:27, Isaiah 29:4 fulfilled, and we are the readers); the summit — **come unto Christ and be perfected in him; grace is sufficient for the whole-souled turning** (10:32-33, grace perfects, made sufficient as we deny all ungodliness and love God with all our might, completed in sanctification by His blood); and the close — **the Book of Mormon ends naming Jesus as the great Jehovah, the Eternal Judge** (10:34, Moroni at peace, trusting to meet the reader at the pleasing bar).
