# Moroni 9 — Mormon's second letter: the depravity of a fallen people, and "be faithful in Christ"

**Binding idea:** "My son, be faithful in Christ; and may not the things which I have written grieve thee, to weigh thee down unto death; but may Christ lift thee up, and may his sufferings and death... and his mercy and long-suffering, and the hope of his glory and of eternal life, rest in your mind forever" (9:25). The darkest chapter in the book — Mormon describing to his son a people "past feeling" — ends not in despair but in a father commending his son to Christ.

**Read:** Moroni 9 (full, verbatim via gospel_get). 26 verses. The second and last of Mormon's letters that Moroni preserves, written ~AD 401, near the very end. Mormon catalogs the mutual atrocities of Nephites and Lamanites and his own inability to stop them, then closes by lifting his son's eyes to Christ.

## Thoughts

This is the hardest chapter in the Book of Mormon to read, and it's meant to be. Mormon is writing to his son near the end of everything, and he says plainly at the outset what the letter is: "I write unto you again that ye may know that I am yet alive; but I write somewhat of that which is grievous" (9:1). It's first of all a sign of life — *I'm still here* — from a father to a son who may not see each other again. And then it's a record of horrors that he himself can barely set down. The chapter exists in the canon for a reason: it is the unflinching bottom of what happens when "the Spirit of the Lord hath ceased striving" (9:4) with a people. Scripture does not look away from it, and neither should the reader.

**The diagnosis in verse 4 is one of the most penetrating descriptions of a hardened conscience ever written.** Mormon says of his labor: "when I speak the word of God with sharpness they tremble and anger against me; and when I use no sharpness they harden their hearts against it." This is the trap of the truly hardened heart — there is no register in which the word can reach them. Speak it bluntly and they rage; speak it gently and they dismiss it. A soft heart can be reached either way; a hardened heart has closed both doors. That's how Mormon knows "the Spirit of the Lord hath ceased striving with them" — not that God withdrew arbitrarily, but that the people have made themselves unreachable by any approach. The withdrawal of the Spirit is the diagnosis, not the disease; the disease is a heart that no word can enter.

**And the marks of that condition are exact: they "have lost their love, one towards another" (9:5), and they are "past feeling" (9:20).** This is what total hardening produces — not more feeling but *less*, the death of feeling itself. "Past feeling" is the terminal stage: the conscience so calloused it registers nothing, neither the horror of what they do nor the suffering they cause. (Paul's exact phrase in Ephesians 4:19 — "being past feeling have given themselves over.") The loss of love between people and the loss of feeling are the same wound. When the Spirit ceases striving, what goes first is love, and what goes last is the capacity to feel anything at all. A people "past feeling" can do the unspeakable things of verses 7-10 precisely *because* they no longer feel them.

**Mormon's even-handedness in cataloging the atrocities is itself a kind of integrity.** He records the Lamanites' abominations — feeding captive women and children the flesh of their slain husbands and fathers (9:7-8) — and then says, devastatingly, "notwithstanding this great abomination of the Lamanites, it doth not exceed that of our people in Moriantum" (9:9). He will not spare his own. The Nephites' crime — the rape, torture, murder, and cannibalism of Lamanite daughters "for a token of bravery" (9:9-10) — he names as *worse*: "their wickedness doth exceed that of the Lamanites" (9:20). The historian who could have written propaganda for his own side instead writes the truth that his own people had become the more depraved. This is the same Mormon who in chapter 8 defended innocent children; here he records that his own nation murders innocent children. The record is honest even when honesty is unbearable, even when it condemns the writer's own people. That honesty is part of what makes the Book of Mormon trustworthy — it does not flatter the Nephites.

**Verse 6 is the hinge that keeps the chapter from being only darkness.** In the middle of "notwithstanding their hardness," Mormon writes: "let us labor diligently; for if we should cease to labor, we should be brought under condemnation; for we have a labor to perform whilst in this tabernacle of clay, that we may conquer the enemy of all righteousness, and rest our souls in the kingdom of God." This is the answer to the question the whole chapter forces: *why keep working for people who cannot be saved?* Mormon's answer is not that he expects to save them — he's already said the Spirit has ceased striving with them. His answer is that *the labor is a duty owed to God, not a transaction measured by results.* We labor because ceasing would bring *us* under condemnation; we labor to conquer the enemy of all righteousness in *ourselves* and rest *our* souls. The faithfulness is not contingent on the outcome. A man can be required to keep laboring for a cause he knows is lost, because the laboring is what keeps *him* unhardened. To stop because it's hopeless would be to join the hardening. (This is the deepest answer in the walk to "how do you keep loving people you can't save" — Mormon 5's "leading without hope" stated now as a principle.)

**The most wrenching verses are 21-22, where Mormon makes a distinction that should stop anyone.** "I cannot recommend them unto God lest he should smite me" (9:21). He has reached a point where he can no longer pray *for* his people — not because he's stopped loving them, but because they're so set against God that to commend them would be to ask God to bless the unrepentant in their rebellion, and Mormon fears the asking itself would bring judgment on him. That is how far gone they are. *But* — and the "but" carries the whole hope of the chapter — "I recommend thee unto God, and I trust in Christ that thou wilt be saved" (9:22). He cannot pray for the nation; he can still pray for his son. The father's love narrows to the one soul he can still hope for. And what he asks for Moroni is not safety but *witness*: "I pray unto God that he will spare thy life, to witness the return of his people unto him, or their utter destruction" (9:22). Even his prayer for his son's survival is in service of the record — that Moroni live to finish witnessing, one way or the other.

**And the chapter ends — this chapter, of all chapters — with Christ.** After everything, Mormon's last words to his son are not a warning but a blessing, and the blessing is entirely Christward: "be faithful in Christ; and may not the things which I have written grieve thee, to weigh thee down unto death; but may Christ lift thee up" (9:25). He knows what he has just written could crush his son — could weigh him "down unto death" — and so he names the only counterweight: Christ. Not "don't think about it," not "be strong," but "may *Christ* lift thee up." And then he lists exactly what about Christ should "rest in your mind forever": "his sufferings and death, and the showing his body unto our fathers, and his mercy and long-suffering, and the hope of his glory and of eternal life" (9:25). The atonement, the resurrected visit (3 Nephi), the mercy, the hope of eternal life — these are the things a father gives his son to hold onto in the middle of a genocide. The remedy for a mind that has seen the worst of human depravity is to fill it with Christ. And the final benediction names the Father and the Son together, "our Lord Jesus Christ, who sitteth on the right hand of his power, until all things shall become subject unto him" (9:26) — the exalted Christ reigning, the assurance that the horror is not the last word, that all things will yet be subject to Him. The darkest chapter in scripture ends with the reigning Christ and a father's blessing. That contrast is the whole point: when everything human has collapsed into depravity, Christ is what remains, and Christ is enough.

- **Moroni 8:28; Mormon 5:16; D&C 1:33** — "the Spirit of the Lord hath ceased striving with them" (9:4); the companion to the first letter's fear; the withdrawal of the Spirit as the result of total hardening.
- **Eph 4:19; 1 Ne 17:45; Mormon 4:5** — "past feeling" (9:20); the terminal hardness of conscience that no longer registers good or evil.
- **Mormon 5:1-2** — Mormon "leading without hope"; here the principle stated — labor not for results but as a duty owed to God (9:6).
- **Ether 15:15-31; Jacob 5:44** — "if they perish it will be like unto the Jaredites... seeking for blood and revenge" (9:23); the Nephites repeating the Jaredite self-destruction the walk just finished in Ether.
- **D&C 121:41-43** — reproving "with sharpness" then showing forth increased love; Mormon's dilemma in 9:4 (sharpness vs. no sharpness, both rejected by the hardened).
- **3 Ne 11:14-15; Moroni 8:25** — "the showing his body unto our fathers" (9:25); the resurrected Christ's visit as one of the anchors a father gives his son.
- **Moroni 7:47-48; 8:26** — "may Christ lift thee up... the hope of his glory and of eternal life" (9:25); charity and hope as the counterweight to despair.

## Notable

Yes — the darkest chapter in the book, and it ends in Christ. **The hardened heart rejects both sharpness and gentleness — no word can reach it** (9:4, how Mormon knows the Spirit has ceased striving); **"past feeling" and the loss of love are the terminal marks of a people without the Spirit** (9:5, 20 — hardening produces the death of feeling itself); **Mormon's honest record condemns his own people as worse than their enemies** (9:9, 20 — the record does not flatter the Nephites); **we labor not for results but as a duty owed to God, lest we be hardened too** (9:6 — faithfulness not contingent on outcome); **a people can become so set against God that the prophet cannot pray for them** (9:21-22 — yet he still prays for his son); and the close — **the counterweight to a mind that has seen the worst is to be filled with Christ** (9:25-26, "may Christ lift thee up," ending on the reigning, exalted Christ).
