# 2 Nephi 29 — "A Bible! A Bible!": Why a Closed Canon Requires a God Who Quit

*(Nephi's own prophecy — the defense of additional scripture. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** The Gentiles will say "we have got a Bible... there cannot be any more." Nephi's God answers not with a proof-text but with an argument from His own nature and reach. Why does a closed canon imply a God who stopped speaking?

**Read:** When God does "a marvelous work" and sets "my hand again the second time to recover my people" (v1), "the words of your seed should proceed forth out of my mouth unto your seed; and my words shall hiss forth unto the ends of the earth" (v2). "And because my words shall hiss forth — many of the Gentiles shall say: **A Bible! A Bible! We have got a Bible, and there cannot be any more Bible**" (v3). The Lord's reply: "O fools, they shall have a Bible; and it shall proceed forth from the Jews, mine ancient covenant people. And what thank they the Jews for the Bible which they receive from them?" (v4) — "ye have cursed them, and have hated them, and have not sought to recover them... for I the Lord have not forgotten my people" (v5). "**Know ye not that there are more nations than one?**... I bring forth my word unto the children of men, yea, even upon all the nations of the earth" (v7). "Wherefore murmur ye, because that ye shall receive more of my word? Know ye not that **the testimony of two nations is a witness unto you that I am God**?" (v8). "**I am the same yesterday, today, and forever**... because that I have spoken one word ye need not suppose that I cannot speak another; **for my work is not yet finished**" (v9). "Because that ye have a Bible ye need not suppose that it contains all my words; neither need ye suppose that I have not caused more to be written" (v10). "I command all men, both in the east and in the west... that they shall write the words which I speak unto them; **for out of the books which shall be written I will judge the world**" (v11). "I shall speak unto the Jews and they shall write it; and... the Nephites... and... the other tribes... and... **all nations of the earth and they shall write it**" (v12); and one day all those records "shall run together" (v8) and "my word also shall be gathered in one" (v14).

**Thoughts:**
- *The argument from God's sameness (v9).* The deepest answer to "there cannot be any more Bible" is not "yes there can" but "consider who God is." "I am the same yesterday, today, and forever... because that I have spoken one word ye need not suppose that I cannot speak another; for my work is not yet finished." A closed canon does not merely limit scripture; it requires a *God who quit speaking* — a God who was talkative in one era and silent in this one. But that God would not be "the same." The constancy of God is the engine of continuing revelation: precisely because He does not change, the One who spoke then speaks now. To close the canon is to assert a change in God He never made.
- *"More nations than one" (v7, 11-12).* The objection assumes God speaks to one people in one book. Nephi's God is universal: "I bring forth my word... upon all the nations of the earth," and "I command all men, both in the east and in the west, and in the north, and in the south, and in the islands of the sea, that they shall write the words which I speak unto them." This is a staggering claim — God has been speaking everywhere, and there are records (the lost tribes', "all nations'") not yet gathered. The Bible is not the whole of God's voice; it is the portion that came "from the Jews." Other portions exist.
- *Two witnesses, not competition (v8).* "The testimony of two nations is a witness unto you that I am God." The Book of Mormon is not a rival to the Bible but a second witness — the law of witnesses (Deut 19:15) applied to scripture itself. Two records of the same God, from two peoples, confirming each other. When they "run together" they do not cancel; they corroborate. The point of a second scripture is the same as the point of a second eyewitness: establishment, not replacement.
- *The ingratitude rebuke (vv4-5).* The sharpest turn: the Gentiles cherish the Bible while despising the people who produced it — "ye have cursed them, and have hated them." You treasure the gift and hate the giver. This is a rebuke of anti-Semitism rooted in scripture itself: the book you call yours came through the very people you have persecuted, and God "hath not forgotten" them. Gratitude for the Bible should produce love for the Jews, not contempt.
- *"We have enough" forfeits (carries from 28:30).* The closed-canon posture is the doctrinal form of the complacency chapter 28 warned against — "we have received... we need no more" (28:29). Here it is answered head-on: "ye need not suppose that it contains all my words." The receptive get more (28:30); the satisfied close the heaven over their own heads.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The chapter's spine is **the argument from God's sameness**: "A Bible! A Bible!" is answered not by asserting more scripture but by observing that a closed canon requires "a God who quit" — and God is "the same yesterday, today, and forever; my work is not yet finished" (v9). The constancy of God is the *engine* of continuing revelation. Paired with God's universality ("more nations than one," v7) and the two-witnesses logic (v8), it grounds the open canon in who God is, not merely in what He has done. The **ingratitude rebuke** (vv4-5) is a scriptural rebuke of treasuring the Bible while hating the Jews who gave it.
