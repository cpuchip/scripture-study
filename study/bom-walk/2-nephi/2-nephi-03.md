# 2 Nephi 3 — The Joseph Prophecies: An Ancient Joseph Names a Latter-day One

**Binding idea:** How does the Joseph-typology thread (held since chs 3/5) resolve — and what did Joseph of Egypt see about the last days?

**Read:** Lehi blesses Joseph, "my last-born... born in the wilderness of mine afflictions." He recalls that he descends from "Joseph who was carried captive into Egypt," and that "Joseph truly saw our day" — obtaining a promise that "out of the fruit of his loins the Lord God would raise up a righteous branch... not the Messiah, but a branch which was to be broken off." Then Lehi quotes Joseph of Egypt directly: "A choice seer will I raise up out of the fruit of thy loins... he shall do a work for the fruit of thy loins... even to the bringing of them to the knowledge of the covenants which I have made with thy fathers." This seer will be "great like unto Moses"; "out of weakness he shall be made strong." "The fruit of thy loins shall write [the record of Joseph]; and the fruit of the loins of Judah shall write [the record of Judah]; and... shall grow together, unto the confounding of false doctrines... and establishing peace." The seer's "name shall be called after me [Joseph]; and it shall be after the name of his father." As if "the fruit of thy loins had cried unto them from the dust... even repentance unto their brethren." And Lehi closes to little Joseph: "hearken unto the words of thy brother, Nephi... Remember the words of thy dying father."

**Thoughts:**
- *The Joseph thread lands — type becomes a named person (v6-15).* Across chs 3 and 5, Lehi and Nephi kept shading into Joseph of Egypt — mocked dreamers, younger sons raised to rule and to preserve, descendants of Joseph. Here the typology resolves into prophecy: Joseph of Egypt himself "truly saw our day" and foretold "a choice seer" of his own loins, whose "name shall be called after me [Joseph], and... after the name of his father [Joseph Sr.]." The Latter-day Saint reading is Joseph Smith. What was a pattern through 1 Nephi becomes, in Lehi's deathbed blessing, an explicit ancient prophecy of a latter-day Joseph — preserved on the brass plates the family died to obtain (the "plain and precious" payoff, again).
- *Two records grow together (v12) — the Bible and the Book of Mormon.* "The fruit of thy loins shall write; and the fruit of the loins of Judah shall write; and... shall grow together." Joseph's record (the Book of Mormon) and Judah's (the Bible) joined — Ezekiel's two sticks (Ezek 37:15-20), the book's purpose stated through a third witness now (after 1 Ne 13:41 and the title page). The point of the second record is the same: confound false doctrine, lay down contention, establish peace, restore the knowledge of the covenants.
- *"Out of weakness... made strong" (v13, 21).* The seer and the writers alike: "the weakness of their words will I make strong in their faith." Nephi already confessed his weakness in writing (19:6); Moroni will name it as a fear (Ether 12:23-27). The promise here is that faith, not eloquence, is what God strengthens — the record's power is not its prose but the faith behind it.
- *"Cry from the dust" (v19-20).* The Book of Mormon described as a voice "as if the fruit of thy loins had cried unto them from the dust... even repentance unto their brethren... according to the simpleness of their words." Isaiah's image (Isa 29:4 — a people that "speak out of the ground... whisper out of the dust") becomes the book itself: the buried record crying repentance to the living remnant. And the "simpleness" is the point (cf 17:41) — the cry works by being plain.
- *The Moses pattern and the spokesman (v9-10, 17-18).* The seer is "great like unto Moses," and the prophecy reuses the Moses template: "I will give power unto him in a rod... I will not loose his tongue... but I will make a spokesman for him" (Ex 4:16, Aaron for Moses). The deliverer-prophet is characteristically *not* eloquent — given the word and a mouthpiece. God's pattern repeats: He works through the unimpressive speaker carrying a true word.
- *A father's blessing to his youngest (v1, 25).* Joseph was "born in the wilderness of mine afflictions... in the days of my greatest sorrow." The grandest of the Joseph prophecies is wrapped, again, in tenderness to a particular child — "thou art little; wherefore hearken unto... Nephi... Remember the words of thy dying father." Lehi's last recorded counsel is to his smallest son.
