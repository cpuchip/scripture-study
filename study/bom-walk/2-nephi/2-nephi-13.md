# 2 Nephi 13 — Isaiah 3: Judgment by Withdrawal, and the Ground Faces of the Poor

*(Isaiah block — Isaiah 3. Full protocol: exegesis + variants + likening. Compare Isaiah 3.)*

**Binding idea:** What does the Day-of-the-Lord pride of chapter 12 look like on the ground — and why is the central charge "ye grind the faces of the poor"?

**Read:** "The Lord, the Lord of Hosts, doth take away from Jerusalem, and from Judah, the stay and the staff... the mighty man, and the man of war, the judge, and the prophet, and the prudent, and the ancient; the captain of fifty, and the honorable man, and the counselor, and the cunning artificer, and the eloquent orator" (vv1-3). In their place: "I will give children unto them to be their princes, and babes shall rule over them" (v4); "the child shall behave himself proudly against the ancient, and the base against the honorable" (v5). Things collapse so far that a man grabs his brother — "Thou hast clothing, be thou our ruler, and let **not** this ruin come under thy hand" (v6) — and the brother swears off the job: "I will not be a healer; for in my house there is neither bread nor clothing; make me not a ruler" (v7). "Jerusalem is ruined, and Judah is fallen, because their tongues and their doings have been against the Lord" (v8); "the show of their countenance doth witness against them, and doth declare their sin to be even as Sodom, and **they cannot hide it**" (v9). The either/or: "Say unto the righteous that it is well with them; for they shall eat the fruit of their doings. Wo unto the wicked... for the reward of their hands shall be upon them" (vv10-11). Then the indictment of the leaders: "The Lord standeth up to plead, and standeth to judge the people... ye have eaten up the vineyard and the spoil of the poor in your houses. What mean ye? **Ye beat my people to pieces, and grind the faces of the poor**, saith the Lord God of Hosts" (vv13-15). And the daughters of Zion — "haughty, and walk with stretched-forth necks and wanton eyes... and making a tinkling with their feet" (v16) — lose the whole catalog of finery (vv18-23), and "instead of sweet smell there shall be stink... and burning instead of beauty" (v24); the gates "lament and mourn; and she shall be desolate, and shall sit upon the ground" (v26).

**Exegesis:**
- *Judgment as removal, not fire (vv1-7).* God's judgment here is subtraction. He "doth take away" the competent — leaders, providers, even bread and water — and what remains is chaos by absence: children and babes ruling, neighbor oppressing neighbor, the social order inverted (the base against the honorable). This is a quieter and more terrifying picture than fire from heaven: a society hollowed of everyone who could hold it together. The withdrawal of the steady is itself the punishment.
- *Leadership becomes a thing to flee (vv6-7).* The qualification to rule has shrunk to almost nothing — "thou hast clothing" — and even *that* man refuses: "make me not a ruler." When the ruin is deep enough, the competent stop volunteering. Nobody wants to be the healer of a wound this size. (The footnote glosses "healer" as "a binder up of a wound" — "I cannot solve your problems.")
- *The central charge: economic oppression (vv14-15).* "The Lord standeth up to plead" — to *contend* (the Hebrew) — and the case is specific: "ye have eaten up the vineyard and the spoil of the poor in your houses... Ye beat my people to pieces, and grind the faces of the poor." The sin that pulls God to His feet as prosecutor is not abstract idolatry; it is the rich consuming the poor. The image is brutal and physical — *grinding faces* — and it is laid at the feet of "the ancients... and the princes," the establishment.
- *The daughters of Zion (vv16-24) — the visible form of the same sin.* The long inventory of ornaments is not a standalone fashion-shaming; it sits directly after "the spoil of the poor is in your houses." The finery *is* the conspicuous display of wealth extracted from the ground faces of v15. So the judgment strips it: the tinkling ornaments, the cauls, the chains, the rings — all taken, and "instead of sweet smell there shall be stink... burning instead of beauty." The pride that wore the spoil is stripped to sackcloth.

**The Book of Mormon variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- *v6 — "let NOT this ruin come under thy hand."* KJV Isaiah 3:6 reads "be thou our ruler, and let this ruin be under thy hand" (take charge of the existing ruin). The Book of Mormon reads "and let **not** this ruin come under thy hand" — the plea becomes *rule us so the ruin does not fall*, a desperate attempt to *prevent* collapse rather than merely manage it. And he still refuses (v7). The variant sharpens the leadership-flight: people beg him to avert ruin, and he will not even try.
- *v9 — "they cannot hide it."* KJV reads "they declare their sin as Sodom, they hide it not" (brazen, unashamed). The Book of Mormon reads "they cannot hide it" — the sin is involuntarily *exposed*: "the show of their countenance doth witness against them." The face gives them away whether they intend it or not. KJV's defiance becomes the Book of Mormon's helpless self-betrayal. Sin written where it cannot be concealed.
- (Minor: v1 "staff of bread" for KJV "stay of bread"; v10 "it is well with them" for "it shall be well with him"; v14 restructures "eaten up the vineyard and the spoil of the poor.")

**Nephite framing — likening (four questions):**
- *Why did Nephi include Isaiah 3?* Chapter 12 (Isaiah 2) gave the cosmic frame — all pride leveled at the Day of the Lord. Chapter 13 shows what that pride *is* on the ground: a society where leaders grind the poor, the wealthy parade the spoil, and the competent flee. It is the concrete diagnosis under the abstract warning.
- *Was it for us, last-dispensation readers?* Nephi himself liken this forward. His own last-days prophecy in 2 Ne 28 reuses this exact charge — a church that "robs the poor because of their fine sanctuaries... their fine clothing" (28:13). The economic indictment of Isaiah 3 is, in Nephi's hands, explicitly aimed at the latter days.
- *How does it apply to us?* "Grind the faces of the poor," and finery built on their spoil, is a live question for any prosperous society — including ours. And the civic pathology of vv1-7 (the institutions hollowed out, nobody competent willing to lead the ruin) is recognizable, not antique.
- *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* "The Lord standeth up to plead" (v13) — the Judge who contends for the poor is the Redeemer Nephi saw (11:2), the One who "suffereth the pains of all... who belong to the family of Adam" (9:21). In that frame, grinding the faces of the poor is not a lesser, social sin; it is a direct offense against the suffering Christ, who felt every one of those ground faces.
