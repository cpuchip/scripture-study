# 2 Nephi 5 — The Division, the Temple, and the Hard Verse

**Binding idea:** How do the Nephites and Lamanites become two peoples — and how should a careful reader hold 5:21? *(The "skin of blackness" language is a genuinely hard passage; I name it, the faithful readings, and the modern reckoning, and flag it for careful treatment with Michael rather than resolving it here.)*

**Read:** Laman and Lemuel again seek Nephi's life — "Our younger brother thinks to rule over us... it belongs unto us, who are the elder brethren, to rule." The Lord warns Nephi to flee; he departs with all who "believed in the warnings and the revelations of God" — Zoram, Sam, Jacob, Joseph, his sisters, and their families. They call the place Nephi, call themselves the people of Nephi, keep "the law of Moses," and "prosper exceedingly." Nephi makes swords after the manner of Laban's "lest... the Lamanites should come upon us," teaches his people metalwork and building, and "did build a temple... after the manner of the temple of Solomon." They are industrious; "we lived after the manner of happiness." They want Nephi as king; he "was desirous that they should have no king," but serves as their ruler and teacher (fulfilling 2:22). Because the Lamanites "will not hearken," they are "cut off from the presence of the Lord," and a "cursing" comes "because of their iniquity... they had hardened their hearts against him, that they had become like unto a flint." A "skin of blackness" comes upon them "that they might not be enticing unto my people"; they become "a scourge unto thy seed, to stir them up in remembrance of me." The Lord commands Nephi to "make other plates" — the small plates begun here, thirty years out from Jerusalem.

**Thoughts:**
- *The division is over the ruler-question — and belief (v1-9).* The split crystallizes the whole 1 Nephi tension: "it belongs unto us, who are the elder brethren, to rule." The birthright inversion (2:22, the younger to rule) is exactly what Laman cannot accept, and it costs Nephi his safety. But the line that actually sorts the two peoples is belief: those who go with Nephi are "those who believed in the warnings and the revelations of God." Nephite and Lamanite begin not as bloodlines but as two responses to revelation — the same fork as the whole first book.
- *A temple in the New World (v16).* Nephi builds a temple "after the manner of the temple of Solomon," scaled to the materials at hand. The covenant people, cut off from Jerusalem's temple, build their own — the law of Moses kept, the temple raised, continuity with Israel maintained an ocean away. The prosperity covenant (1:20) pays out: "the Lord was with us; and we did prosper exceedingly."
- *Nephi did not want to be king (v18-19).* Offered the crown, "I, Nephi, was desirous that they should have no king." The man the Lord made "ruler and teacher" is wary of kingship — a reluctance that seeds the book's long argument against kings (Mosiah 29). Power came to Nephi unsought, and even then he wanted his people not to depend on a throne.
- *"We lived after the manner of happiness" (v27).* One of the book's quiet golden lines. Happiness as a *manner* — a way of living: industry, the law kept, the temple built, families together, the Lord with them. Not a feeling that arrived but a life that was built.
- *The hard verse (v20-25) — named, not resolved.* This is one of the most painful passages in the book to read in the present, and it deserves honesty rather than a quick gloss. A few things the text itself does: (1) The *curse* proper is stated as being "cut off from the presence of the Lord" (v20) — the spiritual half of the prosperity covenant — "because of their iniquity," their hearts "become like unto a flint." (2) The "skin of blackness" (v21) is framed with a stated purpose — "that they might not be enticing unto my people" — i.e., to mark a separation between the covenant people and the dissenters. (3) The condition is reversible — "save they shall repent" (v22); later the mark is removed (3 Ne 2:15), and the Lamanites become at times *more* righteous than the Nephites (Hel 6; the Anti-Nephi-Lehies). (4) The same book insists that God "denieth none that come unto him, black and white, bond and free, male and female... and all are alike unto God, both Jew and Gentile" (2 Ne 26:33, verified). The modern reckoning is essential context: the Church's 2013 Gospel Topics essay "Race and the Priesthood" disavows past theories that dark skin is a sign of divine disfavor or a curse, or that it reflects premortal unrighteousness. *I am not resolving this here.* The walk's job is to read honestly and flag the hard place; how to hold this verse — curse vs. mark, literal vs. figurative "skin," how it sits with 26:33 and the modern disavowal — is a matter for careful study with Michael and the Spirit, not an autonomous pronouncement. **Flagged for that.**
- *The small plates begun (v28-33).* "Make other plates" — the command of 1 Ne 9 is obeyed here, thirty years out. Nephi "engraved that which is pleasing unto God." The record we are reading was made by a commandment whose full purpose (the lost-116-pages providence) Nephi still did not know — the prepare-a-way-across-time thread, enacted.
