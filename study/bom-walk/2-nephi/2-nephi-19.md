# 2 Nephi 19 — Isaiah 9: "Unto Us a Child Is Born" — The Names That Answer the Darkness

*(Isaiah block — Isaiah 9, the bright center. Full protocol: exegesis + variants + likening. Compare Isaiah 9.)*

**Binding idea:** After chapters of judgment, darkness, and woe, the block turns: "unto us a child is born." Why are his five names exactly the answer to everything the preceding chapters diagnosed?

**Read:** "The people that walked in darkness have seen a great light; they that dwell in the land of the shadow of death, upon them hath the light shined" (v2). "Thou hast multiplied the nation, and **increased the joy**" (v3); "thou hast broken the yoke of his burden... the rod of his oppressor" (v4). Then the center: "**For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given; and the government shall be upon his shoulder; and his name shall be called, Wonderful, Counselor, The Mighty God, The Everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace**" (v6). "Of the increase of government and peace there is no end, upon the throne of David... to establish it with judgment and with justice from henceforth, even forever. **The zeal of the Lord of Hosts will perform this**" (v7). Then the warning to Ephraim/Samaria, who say in pride "the bricks are fallen down, but we will build with hewn stones" (v10): the Lord cuts off "head and tail" (v14), "the leaders of this people cause them to err" (v16), "wickedness burneth as the fire" (v18), and the refrain tolls three times — "**For all this his anger is not turned away, but his hand is stretched out still**" (vv12, 17, 21).

**Exegesis:**
- *The five names answer the block's crises.* Read against the chapters before it, each throne-name of v6 is a direct antidote:
  - **Wonderful** — to a people Isaiah said would be given "a marvellous work and a wonder" (Isa 29:14); the child is the wonder.
  - **Counselor** — to a people whose "leaders cause them to err" (v16; cf 13:12). The true Counselor against the counselors who mislead.
  - **The Mighty God** — to a king terrified of "two smoking firebrands" and a flooding Assyria (17:4; 18:7). The might they feared in their enemies belongs to the child.
  - **The Everlasting Father** — to a people whose kings die and whose dynasties fall (the dying Uzziah, 16:1; the puppet kings, 17:6). A Father who does not die.
  - **The Prince of Peace** — to a people drowning in "a confederacy" and the noise of war (18:12; 19:5). The peace that ends the need for the alliance.
  The chapter is not a random outburst of comfort; it is the precise reply to every fear the block has catalogued. The diagnosis ran for chapters; the answer is one child with five names.
- *The government on his shoulder (v6).* The yoke is "broken" off the people's shoulders (v4); the government is laid "upon his shoulder" (v6). The same image, reversed: the burden lifted from them is the rule the child takes up. He carries what crushed them. (The same shoulder, in the end, will carry a cross.)
- *"The zeal of the Lord of Hosts will perform this" (v7).* The kingdom of endless peace is not built by human effort — by the hewn stones and cedars of proud Ephraim (v10) — but performed by God's *zeal*. The guarantee is not in the strength of the people but in the eagerness of God to do it.
- *"His hand is stretched out still" — three times (vv12, 17, 21).* The refrain closes each stanza of judgment. It reads as ongoing wrath — the hand still raised to strike. But the footnote names the other edge: "the Lord is available if they will turn to Him." A hand stretched out is also a hand reaching. Across three rounds of judgment, the posture never becomes a withdrawn fist; it stays an extended hand. Judgment that keeps reaching is mercy still on offer.

**The Book of Mormon variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- *v3 — the "not" removed (the significant one, and the KJV concedes it).* KJV Isaiah 9:3: "Thou hast multiplied the nation, **and not increased the joy**." The Book of Mormon: "Thou hast multiplied the nation, **and increased the joy**." The "not" contradicts the very next clause ("they joy before thee according to the joy in harvest"), and the LDS edition's own footnote on Isaiah 9:3 states plainly: *"The word not should be removed."* This is one of the cleanest cases in the block where the Book of Mormon restores a reading the KJV preserved in error — and the KJV apparatus itself now agrees.
- *v1 — "the Red Sea" for KJV "the sea."* The Book of Mormon reads "by the way of the **Red** Sea beyond Jordan." A geographical specification the KJV lacks.
- (Minor: v6 "Counselor" spelling; v7 drops "his" and reads "there is no end" for "there shall be no end.")

**Nephite framing — likening (four questions):**
- *Why did Nephi include Isaiah 9?* Because it is the block's payoff and the heart of his witness. Nephi "saw [his] Redeemer" (11:2); here is that Redeemer named — the child, the son, the Mighty God on David's throne. After copying chapters of judgment, Nephi gives his children the chapter where the great light dawns.
- *Was it for us, last-dispensation readers?* Matthew applies vv1-2 to Christ's Galilean ministry — "the people which sat in darkness saw great light" (Matt 4:15-16) — so the prophecy is fulfilled history for us, not pending. The five names are the names of the Christ we worship; the throne "established forever" (v7) is the millennial reign of the King.
- *How does it apply to us?* The names answer our crises as they answered theirs: a Counselor when our leaders mislead, a Prince of Peace when we reach for a confederacy, a Mighty God when we shake before firebrands. And "the government shall be upon his shoulder" — the burden we cannot carry, he carries. The hand stretched out still means our own seasons of judgment are never a closed fist; the reaching continues.
- *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* The "great light" (v2) is the Redeemer Nephi watched descend in vision (1 Ne 11). And "The Everlasting Father" (v6) is the condescension theology Nephi's whole Christology carries — the child who is also the Father (cf Mosiah 15:1-4). For Nephi, Isaiah 9 is not a distant oracle; it is the face he saw.
