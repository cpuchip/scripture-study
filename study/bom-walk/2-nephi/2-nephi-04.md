# 2 Nephi 4 — Lehi's Death and the Psalm of Nephi

**Binding idea:** What does Nephi do with his own wretchedness — and why does the most exalted seer in the book sing a song about battling despair and anger?

**Read:** Lehi finishes his blessings — to the children of Laman and Lemuel (promising that if they are cursed, "the cursing may be taken from you and be answered upon the heads of your parents"), to the sons of Ishmael, and to faithful Sam ("thou shalt be even like unto thy brother [Nephi]... and thy seed like unto his seed"). Then: "he waxed old. And it came to pass that he died, and was buried." Not many days later, Laman, Lemuel, and the sons of Ishmael are "angry with me because of the admonitions of the Lord." Nephi turns inward and writes "the things of my soul... for my soul delighteth in the scriptures." Then the psalm: "Notwithstanding the great goodness of the Lord... my heart exclaimeth: O wretched man that I am! ... my soul grieveth because of mine iniquities. I am encompassed about, because of the temptations and the sins which do so easily beset me." But: "nevertheless, I know in whom I have trusted." He rehearses God's faithfulness — "My God hath been my support... he hath filled me with his love... angels came down and ministered unto me... carried away upon exceedingly high mountains." Then he rebukes himself: "Why should my heart weep and my soul linger in the valley of sorrow... why should I yield to sin... Why am I angry because of mine enemy?" And the turn: "Awake, my soul! No longer droop in sin. Rejoice, O my heart, and give place no more for the enemy of my soul." He prays: "wilt thou make me that I may shake at the appearance of sin... encircle me around in the robe of thy righteousness... I have trusted in thee, and I will trust in thee forever. I will not put my trust in the arm of flesh... God will give liberally to him that asketh."

**Thoughts:**
- *"O wretched man that I am... nevertheless, I know in whom I have trusted" (v17-19) — the hinge.* The most striking thing about the Psalm of Nephi is *who* is singing it. This is the man who saw the tree, built the ship, was filled with the power of God until his brothers dared not touch him — and his heart's cry is "O wretched man that I am" (Paul's exact words, Rom 7:24). Spiritual stature did not retire the inner war. But the wretchedness does not get the last word; "nevertheless" does. He holds both at once: real grief over real sin, *and* settled trust. Honesty about the self and confidence in God are not enemies — Nephi keeps them in the same breath.
- *Even Nephi battles anger and despair (v26-29).* The questions are self-directed: "why should my heart weep and my soul linger in the valley of sorrow... why should I yield to sin... Why am I angry because of mine enemy?" The seer who had every reason to feel vindicated still has to fight droop, sin, and anger toward his brothers. The psalm is not a victory lap; it is a man talking himself off the ledge of his own discouragement. That it is *Nephi* doing this is the comfort — the wrestle is not evidence you have failed; it is the normal interior of even the faithful.
- *"Awake, my soul!... Rejoice, O my heart" (v28-30) — the will commands the feelings.* The turn is volitional. Nephi does not wait to *feel* better; he *commands* his soul to awake, his heart to rejoice, the enemy to be given "place no more." Joy here is an act before it is a mood — a decision to stop drooping and to "cry unto the Lord." The remedy for the valley of sorrow is not to analyze it but to address God out of it.
- *"That I may shake at the appearance of sin" (v31).* Nephi prays not to be sinless but to be *sensitive* — to recoil at sin's mere approach. He asks for a finer conscience, the opposite of the "past feeling" he diagnosed in his brothers (1 Ne 17:45). The petition is for the gates of hell "shut continually" and a path so "strict" and "plain" that he stays on it. Holiness asked for as a kind of holy flinch.
- *"I will not put my trust in the arm of flesh" (v34).* Even now — patriarch dead, brothers murderous, the family fracturing — Nephi anchors not in his own competence or in men but in God: "cursed is he that putteth his trust in the arm of flesh" (Jer 17:5). The psalm ends on the rock: "my rock and mine everlasting God."
- *Lehi shields the grandchildren (v3-9).* Before the psalm, a quiet mercy: Lehi blesses Laman's and Lemuel's children so that "if ye are cursed... the cursing may be taken from you and be answered upon the heads of your parents." The innocent generation is not bound by the parents' rebellion (D&C 68:25; "train up a child," Prov 22:6). And Sam — the faithful, nearly silent brother — is given full parity with Nephi (v11). The blessings sort by faithfulness, and they protect the children.
- *"God will give liberally to him that asketh" (v35).* The psalm closes on James 1:5 — the very verse that would send a fourteen-year-old Joseph Smith into the grove. Nephi's last word is the promise that grounds all asking: God gives liberally to the one who asks "not amiss." The seek-and-find of 1 Ne 10-15, now in the first person, sung from grief into trust.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off — the Psalm of Nephi is devotional and personal, best engaged in the walk's own voice. Among the most beloved passages in the book; the standout: spiritual greatness does not end the inner war (even Nephi sings "O wretched man"), and joy is *commanded* ("Awake, my soul"), not awaited. Strong teaching verses (4:31 "shake at the appearance of sin"; 4:34-35).
