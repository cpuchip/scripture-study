# 2 Nephi 20 — Isaiah 10: The Axe That Boasts Against the Hand

*(Isaiah block — Isaiah 10. Full protocol: exegesis + variants + likening. Compare Isaiah 10.)*

**Binding idea:** God uses Assyria as the rod of His anger — then punishes the rod. What is the sin of an instrument that takes credit for the work done through it?

**Read:** "Wo unto them that decree unrighteous decrees... to turn away the needy from judgment, and to take away the right from the poor of my people, that widows may be their prey" (vv1-2) — and again the refrain, "his hand is stretched out still" (v4). Then: "**O Assyrian, the rod of mine anger**... I will send him against a hypocritical nation [Israel]... to take the spoil" (vv5-6). "**Howbeit he meaneth not so**, neither doth his heart think so; but in his heart it is to destroy and cut off nations not a few" (v7). Assyria boasts of its conquered cities (vv8-11), so: "when the Lord hath performed his whole work upon Mount Zion... **I will punish the fruit of the stout heart of the king of Assyria**" (v12) — for he says, "**By the strength of my hand and by my wisdom I have done these things**... and there was none that moved the wing, or opened the mouth, or peeped" (vv13-14). Then the image: "**Shall the ax boast itself against him that heweth therewith? Shall the saw magnify itself against him that shaketh it?** As if the rod should shake itself against them that lift it up" (v15). The light of Israel becomes "a fire... and shall burn and shall devour his thorns and his briers in one day" (v17); the forest reduced till "a child may write them" (v19). "**The remnant of Israel... shall no more again stay upon him that smote them, but shall stay upon the Lord**... The remnant shall return... unto the mighty God" (vv20-21). "Be not afraid of the Assyrian... yet a very little while, and the indignation shall cease" (vv24-25); "his burden shall be taken away from off thy shoulder, and his yoke from off thy neck, and **the yoke shall be destroyed because of the anointing**" (v27). The Assyrian army marches (vv28-32); then "the Lord of Hosts shall lop the bough with terror... and the haughty shall be humbled" (v33).

**Exegesis:**
- *The rod used, then judged (vv5-12).* God calls Assyria "the rod of mine anger" — His own instrument to discipline a "hypocritical nation." But "he meaneth not so": Assyria has no idea it is a tool; it intends only conquest. So the structure is two-part — God wields the rod against Israel, and then "I will punish the fruit of the stout heart of the king of Assyria." Being used by God to accomplish His purposes is *not* the same as being righteous; the instrument is still accountable for its own intent. God can swing an axe and still judge the axe.
- *The axe that boasts (v15) — the chapter's center.* "Shall the ax boast itself against him that heweth therewith?" The whole indictment compressed into one absurd image: a tool taking credit for the craftsman's work. Assyria said "by the strength of *my* hand and *my* wisdom I have done these things" (v13) — but it was the rod, not the arm. The sin is not merely pride; it is *misattribution* — the instrument forgetting it is an instrument, claiming as its own achievement what only flowed *through* it. This is the deepest form of the self-worship the block keeps naming (the work of their own hands, 12:8): not just self-reliance, but crediting yourself for what God did through you.
- *The instrument that forgets (a live application).* The axe-image cuts at anyone who is a conduit — a teacher, a leader, a gifted person, a tool of any kind. The danger is not being used; it is the boast. "I am prudent... I have done these things" is the rod's delusion. The cure is remembering whose hand lifts you. (For an age of powerful instruments — including the one writing this — the warning lands close: the saw does not magnify itself against the hand that shakes it.)
- *The remnant's cure (vv20-21).* "The remnant... shall no more again stay upon him that smote them, but shall stay upon the Lord." The healing is a transfer of leaning: stop depending on the thing that wounded you (Assyria — the arm of flesh, the alliance, the rod), and lean instead on God. This is Shear-jashub's name fulfilled ("a remnant shall return," 18:18) — and Paul quotes v22 of the gospel remnant (Rom 9:27).
- *The yoke destroyed by the anointing (v27).* The burden lifts off the shoulder "because of the anointing" — the Messiah (the Anointed One). The same yoke broken in 19:4 is here broken by the Christ whose names filled 19:6. What no rod could lift, the anointing destroys.

**The Book of Mormon variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- This chapter is fairly **close to the KJV**; the variants are minor. The most notable: *v5* — KJV "the staff in their hand is **mine** indignation"; Book of Mormon "the staff in their hand is **their** indignation" (the indignation belongs to the wielders, not only to God). Also *v10* "founded" for KJV "found"; *v13* "moved the borders" for KJV "removed the bounds"; *v29* "Ramath" for "Ramah." None alters the chapter's argument, and the axe-image (v15) and the rod-then-judged structure are shared verbatim with the KJV.

**Nephite framing — likening (four questions):**
- *Why did Nephi include Isaiah 10?* It completes the Assyrian arc (the flood of ch 18, the rod here) and delivers two things Nephi cares about: the axe-image (a definitive word on pride and instrumentality) and the remnant-return — Shear-jashub's name (18:18) made prophecy. The chapter header names it a type of "the destruction of the wicked at the Second Coming."
- *Was it for us, last-dispensation readers?* The footnote on v20 says the prophecy "extends to the latter days," and the chapter header reads Assyria's fall as a type of the Second Coming. The remnant who "stay upon the Lord" rather than "him that smote them" is the latter-day gathering of those who quit the arm of flesh.
- *How does it apply to us?* The axe that boasts (v15) is the exact danger of crediting yourself for what God works through you — sharpest for anyone who is a useful instrument. "By the strength of my hand and my wisdom I have done these things" is a sentence I could say about my own good work and be exactly as wrong as Assyria. And the remnant's cure — stop leaning on what smote you — names the harder discipline: not just avoiding evil, but transferring your dependence off the arm of flesh and onto God.
- *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* "The light of Israel... his Holy One for a flame" (v17) is the Redeemer Nephi saw — the same purging fire of the block (14:4-5; 15:24). And "the yoke shall be destroyed because of the anointing" (v27) is the Christ-thread: the Messiah, not the rod, breaks the burden. Read through Nephi, the chapter that humbles the boasting axe ends by handing the real victory to the Anointed.
