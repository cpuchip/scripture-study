# 2 Nephi 24 — Isaiah 14: The Five "I Will"s — Pride Traced to Its Root

*(Isaiah block — Isaiah 14, the **last chapter of Nephi's great Isaiah block** (chs 12-24 = Isaiah 2-14). Full protocol: exegesis + variants + likening. Compare Isaiah 14.)*

**Binding idea:** The block has leveled human pride for thirteen chapters. Here it traces pride to its cosmic root — Lucifer's five "I will"s — and ends, against that, on Zion founded for the poor. What is the anatomy of the fall, and why this as the block's last word?

**Read:** "For the Lord will have mercy on Jacob, and will yet choose Israel, and set them in their own land; and the strangers shall be joined with them" (v1); the gathered "shall rule over their oppressors" and the Lord "shall give thee rest from thy sorrow, and from thy fear, and from the hard bondage" (vv2-3). Then the taunt-song against the king of Babylon: "How hath the oppressor ceased!... The Lord hath broken the staff of the wicked" (vv4-5); "the whole earth is at rest, and is quiet; they break forth into singing" (v7); even the trees rejoice that "no feller is come up against us" (v8). Hell stirs to greet him; the dead kings rise: "Art thou also become weak as we?" (vv9-10); "the worm is spread under thee" (v11). Then the height of it: "**How art thou fallen from heaven, O Lucifer, son of the morning!**" (v12). "For thou hast said in thy heart: **I will ascend into heaven, I will exalt my throne above the stars of God... I will ascend above the heights of the clouds; I will be like the Most High**" (vv13-14). "**Yet thou shalt be brought down to hell, to the sides of the pit**" (v15). The onlookers: "Is this the man that made the earth to tremble?" (v16). The block closes: "The Lord of Hosts hath sworn... as I have purposed, so shall it stand" (v24); "**this is the hand that is stretched out upon all nations. For the Lord of Hosts hath purposed, and who shall disannul? And his hand is stretched out, and who shall turn it back?**" (vv26-27); and the final word — "**the Lord hath founded Zion, and the poor of his people shall trust in it**" (v32).

**Exegesis:**
- *The five "I will"s — the anatomy of pride (vv13-14).* "I will ascend into heaven. I will exalt my throne above the stars of God. I will sit also upon the mount of the congregation. I will ascend above the heights of the clouds. I will be like the Most High." Five times the self is the subject and God's own place is the object. This is pride dissected: not merely wanting good things, but wanting *God's seat* — the will to occupy the throne rather than bow to it. The footnotes tie it to the premortal rebellion (Moses 4:1-4; D&C 76:25-29): the same "I will" that made Lucifer the devil. Every human pride is a smaller rehearsal of these five sentences.
- *The inversion: up is down (v15).* The one who said "I will ascend... I will be like the Most High" is met with "Yet thou shalt be brought down to hell, to the sides of the pit." The grasp upward produces the deepest fall. This is the exact inverse of the condescension Nephi saw in vision (1 Ne 11): God *descended* — born in a stable, lifted on a cross — and was exalted; Lucifer *ascended* in ambition and fell to the pit. Pride goes up and falls; love goes down and is lifted. The block that kept insisting "the Lord alone shall be exalted" (12:11) here shows the fate of everything that tries to exalt itself instead.
- *The hand stretched out — now sovereign purpose (vv26-27).* The refrain "his hand is stretched out" tolled through the judgment chapters as anger; in ch 22 it resolved as comfort. Here it returns a third way: as *irreversible purpose*. "His hand is stretched out, and who shall turn it back?" The same hand is anger, comfort, and unstoppable decree — and against it no Babylon, no Lucifer, no nation can prevail.
- *The block ends on Zion for the poor (v32).* The whole movement bookends. It opened with "the mountain of the Lord's house" rising and all nations flowing to it (12:2); it closes with "the Lord hath founded Zion, and the poor of his people shall trust in it." The poor — the ones whose faces were ground by the proud (13:14-15), whose right was taken away (20:2) — are, at the end, the ones who trust in the founded Zion. After all the leveling of pride, the last word is the refuge of the poor. The block's arc is: the mountain rises, all pride falls (down to its root in Lucifer), and Zion stands for the lowly.

**The Book of Mormon variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- *v2 — restoration strengthened.* The Book of Mormon adds "they shall return to their lands of promise" (where the KJV moves directly to "the house of Israel shall possess them"). The addition reinforces the gathering theme — consistent with the mercy-and-restoration tilt seen across the block's reworkings.
- *v19 — "remnant" for "raiment."* KJV: "as the **raiment** of those that are slain"; Book of Mormon: "the **remnant** of those that are slain." A word-level change (clothing → survivors).
- *v25 — "bring" for "break."* KJV: "I will **break** the Assyrian in my land"; Book of Mormon: "I will **bring** the Assyrian in my land" (and tread him under foot).
- *The Lucifer passage itself (vv12-15) is shared with the KJV* — "O Lucifer, son of the morning" and the five "I will"s are the famous KJV text too; not a Restoration distinctive. (Minor: v12 "Art thou cut down" for KJV "how art thou cut down.")

**Nephite framing — likening (four questions):**
- *Why did Nephi include Isaiah 14?* It completes the block by tracing pride to its origin — Lucifer — and ending on Zion for the poor. For Nephi, the son of the morning who would "be like the Most High" is the antitype of the condescension of God he saw (1 Ne 11): the one who grasped upward and fell, against the One who stooped down and was lifted.
- *Was it for us, last-dispensation readers?* The footnotes point to Moses 4:1-4 and D&C 76:25-29 — the premortal-council backstory of Lucifer's rebellion, restored scripture that makes Isaiah 14:12-15 the clearest Old Testament window onto the war in heaven. And "Zion founded... the poor of his people shall trust in it" (v32) is the latter-day refuge.
- *How does it apply to us?* The five "I will"s are the precise shape of self-exaltation, and they are sayable in miniature every day — the will to sit on the throne rather than bow to it. The cure is the inversion: "whosoever exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted" (Luke 14:11). Up is down; down is up. And the block's last word locates the safe place — not on the heights the proud grasp for, but in the Zion where the poor trust.
- *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* Set ch 24 beside 1 Ne 11 and the two poles of the whole Book of Mormon appear: Lucifer ascends in pride and is cast to the pit; Christ descends in love and becomes the fruit of the tree of life. The condescension and the fall are mirror images, and the entire Isaiah block resolves between them — ending not with the proud on their thrones but with the poor trusting in Zion.
