# 2 Nephi 22 — Isaiah 12: "Thine Anger Is Turned Away" — The Song After the Storm

*(Isaiah block — Isaiah 12, the short doxology sealing the Isaiah 2-12 movement. Full protocol; near-identical to KJV. Compare Isaiah 12.)*

**Binding idea:** After chapters whose refrain was "his anger is not turned away," the block resolves into a six-verse song. What does it mean that the first thing the redeemed sing is "thine anger *is* turned away"?

**Read:** "And in that day thou shalt say: **O Lord, I will praise thee; though thou wast angry with me thine anger is turned away, and thou comfortedst me**" (v1). "Behold, God is my salvation; I will trust, and not be afraid; **for the Lord Jehovah is my strength and my song**; he also has become my salvation" (v2). "**Therefore, with joy shall ye draw water out of the wells of salvation**" (v3). "Praise the Lord, call upon his name, declare his doings among the people, make mention that his name is exalted" (v4). "Sing unto the Lord; for he hath done excellent things; this is known in all the earth" (v5). "**Cry out and shout, thou inhabitant of Zion; for great is the Holy One of Israel in the midst of thee**" (v6).

**Exegesis:**
- *The resolution of the refrain.* Three times in the judgment chapters the line tolled: "his anger is not turned away, but his hand is stretched out still" (15:25; 19:12, 17, 21; 20:4). The hand stayed stretched out through all the woe and the rod and the axe. Here the song answers it directly: "though thou wast angry with me, **thine anger is turned away**, and thou comfortedst me." The hand that stayed extended all through the judgment was, all along, reaching toward *this* — the day it would close around comfort instead of striking. The refrain was never a clenched fist; it was a reaching that had not yet arrived. Isaiah 12 is where it arrives.
- *The Song of Moses, sung again (v2).* "The Lord Jehovah is my strength and my song; he also has become my salvation" is almost word-for-word the Song of Moses at the Red Sea (Ex 15:2). The previous chapter promised a new Exodus — a highway for the remnant "like as it was to Israel in the day that he came up out of the land of Egypt" (21:16). Now the redeemed of the *second* Exodus sing the song of the *first*. The deliverance rhymes, and so does the music. (The footnote also notes this is one of only four places the KJV spells "Jehovah" out in full — the song names the divine name plainly.)
- *Joy as drawing water (v3).* "With joy shall ye draw water out of the wells of salvation." Not a one-time rescue but a repeated drawing — the redeemed life is going back to the well, again and again, and the well does not run dry. Joy is the bucket; salvation is the water; the action is daily.
- *"In the midst of thee" (v6).* The block opened with the mountain of the Lord's *house* (12:2) and the Day of the Lord; it closes with the Holy One of Israel "in the midst of thee." The whole movement from temple-on-the-mountain to God-in-your-midst is the Immanuel thread (17:14; 18:10) brought home: not God on a far hill, but God among the people. The destination of the entire Isaiah 2-12 arc is presence.

**The Book of Mormon variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- This is one of the **most nearly identical** chapters in the block — the only difference is v2's "he also **has** become my salvation" for the KJV's "he also **is** become my salvation," a trivial verb form. The doxology comes through verbatim, exactly as the meta-pattern predicts: **the songs and the glory are reproduced faithfully; the variants live in the judgment chapters.** Isaiah 12 is pure praise, and the Restoration text leaves it untouched.

**Nephite framing — likening (four questions):**
- *Why did Nephi include Isaiah 12?* It is the doxological seal on the whole Isaiah 2-12 movement — the song that resolves all the preceding judgment. For Nephi, "the Lord Jehovah is my strength and my song" names the Redeemer he saw (Jehovah = Christ, 1 Ne 19); the block ends with the people singing about the One Nephi's whole record points to.
- *Was it for us, last-dispensation readers?* "In that day" is the gathered, millennial day — the song of the recovered remnant of the previous chapter. For us it is also the personal promise: the anger turned away is the end every season of chastening is reaching toward.
- *How does it apply to us?* If you are under the refrain — feeling the hand still stretched out, the anger not yet turned — the block's own arc is the promise: it resolves. "Though thou wast angry with me, thine anger is turned away, and thou comfortedst me." And the redeemed life is not a single rescue but a daily drawing "with joy" from wells that do not empty.
- *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* The song names Jehovah — the Redeemer Nephi saw — as "my strength and my song," and it echoes Moses at the sea. Read through Nephi, this is the second-Exodus people singing the first-Exodus song to the same God who delivered both: the Holy One of Israel, now "in the midst of thee."
