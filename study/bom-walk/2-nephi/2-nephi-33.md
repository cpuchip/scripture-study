# 2 Nephi 33 — "I Glory in My Jesus": Nephi's Farewell

*(Nephi's closing testimony — the LAST chapter of 2 Nephi. Mini-study + brief book synthesis.)*

**Binding idea:** Nephi ends not with a doctrine but with a man — weeping, glorying, and saying farewell across the centuries. What does he glory in, and what does it mean that he and the reader will "stand face to face before his bar"?

**Read:** "Neither am I mighty in writing, like unto speaking; for **when a man speaketh by the power of the Holy Ghost the power of the Holy Ghost carrieth it unto the hearts of the children of men**" (v1). But "many... harden their hearts against the Holy Spirit, that it hath no place in them" (v2). "I esteem it as of great worth... For I pray continually for them by day, and **mine eyes water my pillow by night, because of them**" (v3); "the words which I have written in weakness will be made strong unto them... it speaketh of Jesus, and persuadeth them to believe in him, and to endure to the end" (v4); "it speaketh harshly against sin, according to the plainness of the truth; wherefore, **no man will be angry at the words which I have written save he shall be of the spirit of the devil**" (v5). Then his creed: "**I glory in plainness; I glory in truth; I glory in my Jesus, for he hath redeemed my soul from hell**" (v6). "I have charity for my people... for the Jew... for the Gentiles. But... for none of these can I hope except they shall be reconciled unto Christ, and enter into the narrow gate, and walk in the strait path... and continue in the path until the end of the day of probation" (vv7-9). "If ye believe not in these words believe in Christ. **And if ye shall believe in Christ ye will believe in these words, for they are the words of Christ**" (v10). "You and I shall stand face to face before his bar" (v11). "I speak unto you as **the voice of one crying from the dust: Farewell until that great day shall come**" (v13). "For what I seal on earth, shall be brought against you at the judgment bar; for thus hath the Lord commanded me, and **I must obey. Amen**" (v15).

**Thoughts:**
- *"Carrieth it unto the hearts" (v1) — to the door, not through it.* The Spirit "carrieth it *unto* the hearts of the children of men" — and the next verse shows why the preposition matters: many "harden their hearts... that it hath no place in them." The Spirit delivers truth *to* the heart's door; whether it finds "place" inside is the hearer's choice. This is the whole gospel's respect for agency in one preposition: God's power brings the message all the way to you, and stops at the threshold of a hardened heart. (The famous "unto, not into" reading rests on this verse's own wording.)
- *Nephi's tears (v3).* "Mine eyes water my pillow by night, because of them." The prophet who foresaw his nation's destruction (26:7, "O the pain... of my soul for the loss of the slain of my people") closes weeping for the people who will reject his words. This is not the posture of a man who got the last word; it is a man who loves the people he is leaving and cannot make them receive. The whole record is wet with that grief.
- *The three glories (v6) — Nephi's creed.* After thirty-three chapters, here is what he glories in, in ascending order: "**plainness... truth... my Jesus, for he hath redeemed my soul from hell.**" Plainness is his method, truth is his content, and Jesus is his reason — and the reason is personal: not "Jesus the Redeemer" but "*my* Jesus," who redeemed "*my* soul from hell." The whole plain, true record exists because one man was personally pulled out of the pit. He glories in plainness because plainness pointed him to the One who saved him.
- *The interlock of the book and Christ (v10).* "If ye believe not in these words believe in Christ. And if ye shall believe in Christ ye will believe in these words." Nephi does not demand belief in the book as the price of entry. He points past the book to Christ — believe *him* — and trusts that belief in Christ will circle back to the words, "for they are the words of Christ." The book is not the object of faith; it is a finger pointing, and the One it points to vindicates it. (The "we talk of Christ" of 25:26 made into the book's own apologetic.)
- *Face to face at the bar (vv11, 15).* "You and I shall stand face to face before his bar." Across twenty-six centuries, the writer and the reader will meet — and what Nephi "seal[s] on earth" will be there. It is an intimate and sobering close: this is not an anonymous ancient text but a named man's testimony, who will be present when you are judged by it. The voice from the dust (v13, Isa 29:4) has a face, and you will see it.

**Brief 2 Nephi synthesis (book complete):** The book runs from Lehi's deathbed blessings (chs 1-4) through the Nephite/Lamanite division (5), Jacob's Atonement sermon (6-10), Nephi's preface (11), the great Isaiah block (12-24), and Nephi's own plain teaching (25-33). Its spine is **the doctrine of Christ rendered plain**: Lehi's "opposition in all things" and the Fall-as-forward (2), Jacob's "awful monster" and the gate with no servant (9), Isaiah's mountain-rises-pride-falls arc resolving in Zion for the poor (12-24), and Nephi's gate-and-path with "feast upon the words of Christ" naming the rod of Lehi's dream (31-32). The book ends as it aimed: "we talk of Christ" (25:26) → "I glory in my Jesus" (33:6). And it leaves two flagged threads for Michael — 5:21's complexion language, answered in-text by 26:33 ("all are alike") and 30:6 (white→pure).
