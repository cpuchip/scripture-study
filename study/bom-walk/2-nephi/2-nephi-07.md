# 2 Nephi 7 — Isaiah 50: "I Gave My Back to the Smiter"

*(Isaiah block — Jacob reading Isaiah 50. Exegesis + variant + likening. Compare Isaiah 50.)*

**Binding idea:** Did God cast Israel off — and who is the Servant who gives his back to the smiters?

**Read:** The Lord asks: "Have I put thee away, or have I cast thee off forever? ... Where is the bill of your mother's divorcement? ... Behold, for your iniquities have ye sold yourselves." His power is undiminished: "Is my hand shortened at all that it cannot redeem...? at my rebuke I dry up the sea." Then the Servant speaks: "The Lord God hath given me the tongue of the learned, that I should know how to speak a word in season... He waketh mine ear to hear as the learned. The Lord God hath opened mine ear, and I was not rebellious... I gave my back to the smiter, and my cheeks to them that plucked off the hair. I hid not my face from shame and spitting. For the Lord God will help me... I have set my face like a flint, and I know that I shall not be ashamed." His accusers "shall wax old as a garment, and the moth shall eat them up." The close: he who "feareth the Lord" and "walketh in darkness and hath no light" should trust the Lord — but those who "kindle fire... walk in the light of your fire and in the sparks which ye have kindled... shall lie down in sorrow."

**Exegesis + the BoM variant (v1, verified):**
- Isaiah 50 is the third Servant Song. It opens by answering Israel's sense of abandonment: there is no "bill of divorcement" — God did not put Israel away; "for your iniquities have ye sold yourselves." The separation is self-inflicted, not a divine rejection.
- *The variant (v1), verified against both texts this session:* KJV Isaiah 50:1 begins "Thus saith the Lord, Where is the bill of your mother's divorcement, whom I have put away?" The Book of Mormon prepends a reassurance — "Have I put thee away, or have I cast thee off forever?" — sharpening the chapter's comfort: the question is rhetorical, and the answer is *no*. God has not cast Israel off forever; Israel sold itself. The added opening tilts the whole chapter from accusation toward mercy.
- *The Servant's passion (v6):* "I gave my back to the smiter, and my cheeks to them that plucked off the hair. I hid not my face from shame and spitting." The suffering Servant foretells Christ's passion in precise detail — the scourging, the plucked beard, the spitting (Matt 27:26, 30) — and does so *voluntarily* (the same "he suffereth it" of 1 Ne 19:9). The obedient ear (v4-5, "I was not rebellious") is the inverse of the stiff-necked Israel of ch 6.
- *"I have set my face like a flint" (v7):* the Servant's resolve — the same image Luke uses of Christ, who "stedfastly set his face to go to Jerusalem" (Luke 9:51). Confidence not in being spared but in being vindicated: "the Lord is near, and he justifieth me... who will contend with me?" (Paul echoes it exactly, Rom 8:33-34).

**Nephite framing — likening (brief; Jacob is the expositor here):**
- *Why Jacob reads it:* to a people who are themselves "broken off" (1 Ne 21:1), Isaiah 50's first word is the one they most need — God has *not* cast Israel off forever; the separation is reversible and self-caused, not divine abandonment. The chapter is comfort to the scattered.
- *For us:* God does not abandon; estrangement is something we do to ourselves ("ye sold yourselves"), and it is not final. And the closing warning lands on anyone: those who "kindle [their own] fire" and "walk in the light of your fire" — self-made illumination, confidence manufactured apart from God — "shall lie down in sorrow." Walk in God's light or in your own sparks; only one does not end in the dark.
- *The Servant is the Holy One of Israel Jacob just named (6:9):* the one who gives his back to the smiters is the same Jehovah-Messiah — the Christology runs straight through the Isaiah Jacob quotes.
