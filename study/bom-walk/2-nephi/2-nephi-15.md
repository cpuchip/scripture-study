# 2 Nephi 15 — Isaiah 5: The Vineyard That Got Everything and Gave Wild Grapes

*(Isaiah block — Isaiah 5. Full protocol: exegesis + variants + likening. Compare Isaiah 5.)*

**Binding idea:** When God has done everything for the vineyard and it still yields wild grapes, where does the fault lie — and what are the specific wild grapes (the six woes) He names?

**Read:** "My well-beloved hath a vineyard in a very fruitful hill. And he fenced it, and gathered out the stones thereof, and planted it with the choicest vine, and built a tower... and made a wine-press therein; and he looked that it should bring forth grapes, and it brought forth wild grapes" (vv1-2). The appeal: "judge, I pray you, betwixt me and my vineyard. **What could have been done more to my vineyard that I have not done in it?**" (vv3-4). So he will take away the hedge, break the wall, lay it waste, command no rain (vv5-6). "For the vineyard of the Lord of Hosts is the house of Israel... and he looked for judgment, and behold, oppression; for righteousness, but behold, a cry" (v7). Then six woes: **(1)** "Wo unto them that join house to house, till there can be no place, that they may be placed alone in the midst of the earth!" (v8); **(2)** "Wo unto them that rise up early in the morning, that they may follow strong drink... but they regard not the work of the Lord" (vv11-12); **(3)** "Wo unto them that draw iniquity with cords of vanity, and sin as it were with a cart rope" (v18); **(4)** "Wo unto them that call evil good, and good evil, that put darkness for light, and light for darkness, that put bitter for sweet, and sweet for bitter!" (v20); **(5)** "Wo unto the wise in their own eyes, and prudent in their own sight!" (v21); **(6)** "Wo unto the mighty to drink wine... who justify the wicked for reward, and take away the righteousness of the righteous from him!" (vv22-23). "As the fire devoureth the stubble... because they have cast away the law of the Lord of Hosts, and despised the word of the Holy One of Israel" (v24); "his anger is not turned away, but **his hand is stretched out still**" (v25). Finally: "he will lift up an **ensign** to the nations from far, and will hiss unto them from the end of the earth; and behold, they shall come with speed swiftly" (v26) — a tireless gathering host, "none shall slumber nor sleep" (v27), arrows sharp, hoofs like flint, "their roaring like a lion" (vv28-30).

**Exegesis:**
- *The vineyard song answers "whose fault?" before it asks (vv1-4).* The owner did everything a vinedresser can do — cleared the stones, planted the best vine, built the tower and the press. The grief is in the question: "What could have been done more to my vineyard that I have not done in it?" The husbandry is not the variable. When a fully-tended vine yields wild grapes, the failure is in the vine, not the keeper. God removes Himself from the dock and puts the fruit there.
- *The wordplay that is the whole indictment (v7).* "He looked for judgment, and behold, oppression; for righteousness, but behold, a cry." In Hebrew this is a pun — *mishpat* (justice) but *mispach* (bloodshed); *tsedaqah* (righteousness) but *tse'aqah* (an outcry). He planted justice and harvested its near-homophone, injustice. The wild grapes look almost like the real thing; the corruption is a near-rhyme of the good.
- *Six wild grapes (the woes).* They are the specific produce of v7's failure, and they read as a portrait of a prosperous, self-satisfied society: **land/wealth monopoly** (v8 — absorbing property "till there can be no place" for the poor, the same grinding as 13:14-15); **substance and pleasure that crowds out God** (vv11-12 — feasts of wine and music, but "they regard not the work of the Lord"); **deliberate, laborious sin** (v18 — dragging iniquity "with a cart rope," sin as effortful work, hauled behind you like an ox its load); **moral inversion** (v20 — calling evil good, light darkness, bitter sweet); **self-conceit** (v21 — "wise in their own eyes"); and **justice corrupted for bribes** (vv22-23). Two of the six (v8 land, vv22-23 justice) are economic-oppression woes; one (v20) is epistemic; one (v21) is the pride of self-sufficiency Jacob already named (9:28).
- *"His hand is stretched out still" (v25).* The refrain (it recurs through Isaiah 9-10) is double-edged. Stretched out *in judgment* — the smiting is not over. But "stretched out" is also the posture of reaching: the hand that strikes has not been withdrawn. Judgment that keeps its hand extended is judgment that has not yet closed the door.
- *The ensign reverses the desolation (v26).* The song of ruin does not end in ruin. The Lord "will lift up an ensign to the nations... and hiss unto them from the end of the earth." After the vineyard is laid waste, a signal goes up and a people gathers. The wild-grape vineyard is not the end of the covenant; the ensign is.

**The Book of Mormon variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- *v8 — the BoM is shorter.* KJV Isaiah 5:8: "join house to house, **that lay field to field**, till there be no place." The Book of Mormon omits the parallel clause: "join house to house, till there **can** be no place." It drops "that lay field to field" and sharpens "be" to "can be" — the consuming runs until no place *can* remain. (Where 12:16 had the BoM with *more* than the KJV, here it has *less*.)
- *v29 — the BoM is shorter again.* KJV: "**Their roaring shall be like a lion**, they shall roar like young lions." The Book of Mormon drops the first clause: "They shall roar like young lions; yea, they shall roar." Two omissions in one chapter — a useful corrective to any simple theory that the BoM uniformly *expands* Isaiah. The variants run both directions.
- (Minor: v1 "And then" for KJV "Now"; v4 declarative "it brought forth wild grapes" for KJV's question; v5 adds "I will"; v7 "and behold" for the first "but behold.")

**Nephite framing — likening (four questions):**
- *Why did Nephi include Isaiah 5?* It is the covenant relationship in parable: God did everything, Israel produced wild grapes, and the six woes name exactly what the wild grapes were. It is the indictment of chapters 12-13 set to music — and, crucially, it ends not in waste but in the ensign, the gathering.
- *Was it for us, last-dispensation readers?* The "ensign to the nations" (v26) is the latter-day gathering signal — the word the Restoration took for its own (cf 2 Ne 29:2; D&C 64; the Church's long-running magazine bore the name). And the six woes describe a prosperous modern society with discomforting precision.
- *How does it apply to us?* "Call evil good, and good evil... put darkness for light" (v20) and "wise in their own eyes" (v21) are live temptations, not antique ones. And "what could have been done more to my vineyard?" turns the failure back on the fruit-bearer: God's investment is not the missing variable, ours is. The vineyard had every advantage and still went wild — which is the prosperity-breeds-forgetting cycle Nephi's own family lived (1 Ne 18:9; 2 Ne 1:20).
- *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* The sign-seekers of v19 — "let him make speed... that we may see it" — want to *see* before they believe. Nephi, who *saw* the Redeemer (11:2), reads them as the inverse of true witness: they demand the sign as a condition; he received the vision as a gift to one who already believed (1 Ne 11:1-6). Wild grapes include the refusal to believe without proof.

**Notable / flag:** Isaiah block, full protocol. The vineyard song (vv1-7) with its grief-question "what could have been done more?" and the **justice→oppression wordplay** (v7) is the spine; the **six woes** (vv8-23) are the wild grapes itemized; the **ensign** (v26) keeps judgment from being the last word. The variants are the inverse of ch 12 — here the **BoM is shorter** (v8, v29 omit clauses), which matters for understanding the variants as selective both ways. The fuller vineyard allegory (Jacob 5, Zenos) is the deep treatment to come, so no spin-off here.
