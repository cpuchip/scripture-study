# 2 Nephi 31 — The Doctrine of Christ: The Gate Is Not the Destination

*(Nephi's own teaching — "the doctrine of Christ." A keystone chapter. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Nephi reduces the whole gospel to a gate and a path. If baptism is the gate, what is the path — and why, the moment you are through, does Nephi ask "is all done?" and answer "Nay"?

**Read:** "My soul delighteth in plainness... he speaketh unto men according to their language, unto their understanding" (v3). The pattern begins with Christ's own baptism: "if the Lamb of God, he being holy, should have need to be baptized by water, to fulfil all righteousness, O then, **how much more need have we, being unholy, to be baptized**" (v5); his baptism shows "the straitness of the path, and the narrowness of the gate, by which they should enter, **he having set the example**" (v9). "Follow thou me" (v10). The Father: "Repent ye... and be baptized in the name of my Beloved Son" (v11); the Son: "He that is baptized in my name, to him will the Father give the Holy Ghost... follow me, and do the things which ye have seen me do" (v12). Done "with full purpose of heart, acting no hypocrisy... but with real intent" (v13), baptism brings "the baptism of fire and of the Holy Ghost." "**He that endureth to the end, the same shall be saved**" (v15). "**The gate by which ye should enter is repentance and baptism by water; and then cometh a remission of your sins by fire and by the Holy Ghost**" (v17); "and then are ye in this strait and narrow path which leads to eternal life... ye have entered in by the gate" (v18). Then the pivot: "after ye have gotten into this strait and narrow path, **I would ask if all is done? Behold, I say unto you, Nay**; for ye have not come thus far save it were by the word of Christ with unshaken faith in him, **relying wholly upon the merits of him who is mighty to save**" (v19). "Wherefore, ye must **press forward with a steadfastness in Christ, having a perfect brightness of hope, and a love of God and of all men**... press forward, **feasting upon the word of Christ, and endure to the end**... Ye shall have eternal life" (v20). "And now, behold, my beloved brethren, **this is the way; and there is none other way nor name given under heaven whereby man can be saved**... this is the doctrine of Christ" (v21).

**Thoughts:**
- *The gospel as gate + path.* Nephi gives the gospel its cleanest architecture: a **gate** (faith in Christ → repentance → baptism by water → the Holy Ghost, vv11-17) and then a **path** (press forward, feast, endure to the end, vv19-20). The gate is entry, not arrival. This maps exactly onto two earlier images of the walk: the gate is the same gate Jacob named — "the keeper of the gate is the Holy One of Israel; and he employeth no servant there" (2 Ne 9:41); and the path is the same strait, narrow path of Lehi's dream, walked by holding the rod of iron (1 Ne 8:20-24). Now Nephi names what holding the rod *is*: "feasting upon the word of Christ" (v20). The whole tree-of-life dream resolves into this chapter's instruction.
- *"Is all done? Nay" (v19) — against the once-entered complacency.* The most important word in the chapter is the pivot. The moment you are through the gate, Nephi anticipates the thought "now I'm safe" and refuses it: "I would ask if all is done? Behold, I say unto you, Nay." Baptism is not the finish line; it is the trailhead. This is the doctrinal cure for chapter 28's "all is well" and chapter 29's "we have enough" — entering the path is not the same as walking it. You are saved by enduring to the end (v15), not by having once begun.
- *Even the sinless one was baptized (vv5-9).* If Christ — holy, needing no remission — submitted to baptism "to fulfil all righteousness," the argument is *a fortiori*: how much more we. And his baptism is pedagogical: "he having set the example." He went down into the water first so the narrowness of the gate would have a footprint in it. The doctrine of Christ is not abstract; it is "do the things which ye have seen me do" (v12) — discipleship as imitation of a demonstrated path.
- *"Relying wholly upon the merits of him who is mighty to save" (v19).* The press-forward is not self-powered. You got through the gate "by the word of Christ with unshaken faith... relying wholly upon the merits" of Christ — and you continue the same way. This is the grace of 25:23 made operational: the enduring is real effort, but it leans wholly on Christ's merits, not its own. Feasting on *his* word, hoping in *his* brightness, saved by *his* might.
- *The triad of endurance (v20).* "Steadfastness in Christ, a perfect brightness of hope, and a love of God and of all men." Faith (steadfastness), hope (perfect brightness), charity (love of God and all men) — the three theological virtues as the fuel of the path. And the verb is "feasting" — not nibbling, not maintaining, but feasting on the word. The path is sustained by abundance, not rationing.
