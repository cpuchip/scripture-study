# 2 Nephi 10 — "We Are Not Cast Off": The Isaiah Arc Lands as an Altar Call

*(Jacob's sermon concludes, 6-10. Mini-study. The day-2 application of the Isaiah he read in 7-8.)*

**Binding idea:** Why did Jacob read three chapters of Isaiah to his people — and what does the whole arc come down to when he finally turns it on them directly?

**Read:** Jacob returns "concerning this righteous branch." He gives the name: "for in the last night the angel spake unto me that this should be his name" — **Christ** — "should come among the Jews, among those who are the more wicked part of the world; and they shall crucify him... and there is none other nation on earth that would crucify their God" (v3). Were the mighty miracles wrought among other nations "they would repent" (v4); but "because of priestcrafts and iniquities, they at Jerusalem will stiffen their necks against him, that he be crucified" (v5). They will be scattered "among all nations" (v6). But "when the day cometh that they shall believe in me, that I am Christ," they shall be restored to their lands (v7); "the kings of the Gentiles shall be nursing fathers unto them, and their queens shall become nursing mothers" (v9 — Isaiah 49:23 fulfilled). Then the land doctrine: "this land shall be a land of liberty unto the Gentiles, and there shall be no kings upon the land" (v11); "I, the Lord, the king of heaven, will be their king" (v14); "he that fighteth against Zion shall perish" (v13). Those who fight Zion — "both Jew and Gentile, both bond and free, both male and female" — "are they who are the whore of all the earth; for they who are not for me are against me" (v16). "I will afflict thy seed by the hand of the Gentiles; nevertheless, I will soften the hearts of the Gentiles, that they shall be like unto a father to them; wherefore, the Gentiles shall be blessed and numbered among the house of Israel" (v18). Then the turn inward: "let us remember him, and lay aside our sins, and not hang down our heads, for **we are not cast off**... we have been led to a better land... and we are upon an isle of the sea" (v20). "As it says isles, there must needs be more than this, and they are inhabited also by our brethren" (v21). "The Lord remembereth all them who have been broken off, wherefore he remembereth us also" (v22). "**Cheer up your hearts, and remember that ye are free to act for yourselves** — to choose the way of everlasting death or the way of eternal life" (v23). "Reconcile yourselves to the will of God... and remember, after ye are reconciled unto God, that **it is only in and through the grace of God that ye are saved**" (v24).

**Thoughts:**
- *The whole point of the Isaiah was this one line: "we are not cast off" (v20).* Jacob read Isaiah 50-52 (chs 7-8) to a people who felt broken off from Israel, and the variant in 7:1 (verified earlier) had God asking "Have I put thee away, or have I cast thee off forever?" — answer, no. Now Jacob lands it pastorally: "lay aside our sins, and not hang down our heads, **for we are not cast off**." The exegesis was never academic. He read three chapters of prophecy so he could say to his own discouraged people, *the comfort God spoke to scattered Israel is yours.* The Isaiah block resolves into an altar call.
- *Reconcile, then grace (v24) — the order matters.* "Reconcile yourselves to the will of God... and **after** ye are reconciled... it is only in and through the grace of God that ye are saved." The turning is ours; the saving is his. This is the same shape as the gate with no servant (9:41): you come; he receives. It pairs with 2 Ne 25:23 ("by grace we are saved, after all we can do") to give the Book of Mormon's grace theology — not grace instead of effort, and not effort instead of grace, but effort that turns toward a grace that does the actual saving.
- *"Free to act for yourselves" (v23) — agency as the pastoral payoff.* Jacob ends not with fate but with choice, echoing his father's 2 Ne 2:27 ("free to choose liberty and eternal life... or captivity and death"). After all the prophecy of scattering and gathering — things that happen *to* Israel — the closing word is what they get to *do*: choose. The sermon about being remembered ends by handing them their freedom.
- *Jacob reasoning from the plural "isles" (v21).* "As it says isles, there must needs be more than this." He reads the prophecy closely and infers from a single plural that the Lehites are not the only broken-off branch — there are other scattered brethren on other isles. It is a small act of careful reading with a tender conclusion: *we are not alone; God has led others away too, and he remembers all of them.* The likening method (which he inherited from Nephi, 6:5) includes reasoning from the very grammar of the text.
- *The hard verse (v3-5), handled with care.* "There is none other nation on earth that would crucify their God" reads harshly, and has been misused as anti-Jewish. But Jacob is himself a Jew speaking of his own people, and the logic is the opposite of ethnic contempt: the crucifixion happened at Jerusalem precisely *because* it was the most spiritually privileged place — other nations, he says, "would repent" at the miracles (v4). The indictment is of "priestcrafts and iniquities" (v5), the hardened religious establishment, not of a bloodline. And the same chapter promises that very people full restoration when "they shall believe... that I am Christ" (v7). It is prophetic self-critique, not condemnation of a race.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off (Jacob's pastoral conclusion; the doctrinal depth was 2 Ne 9). The standout is structural — **the entire Isaiah reading (chs 7-8) exists to deliver "we are not cast off" (v20)**, which directly answers the verified 7:1 variant. The **reconcile-then-grace** order (v24) is the Book of Mormon's grace theology in miniature. The hard v3-5 ("none other nation would crucify their God") is noted with care: self-critique of privilege + priestcraft, not ethnic condemnation — but it is the kind of verse worth Michael's eye if it ever surfaces for teaching.
