# 2 Nephi 14 — Isaiah 4: The Same Fire That Burns Becomes the Fire That Shelters

*(Isaiah block — Isaiah 4, the short chapter. Full protocol; minimal variants. Compare Isaiah 4.)*

**Binding idea:** After the judgment of chapter 13, how does the same fire that *burns* become the fire that *shelters* — and what changes when the pillar of the Exodus moves from one tabernacle to every home?

**Read:** "In that day, seven women shall take hold of one man, saying: We will eat our own bread, and wear our own apparel; only let us be called by thy name to take away our reproach" (v1 — the tail of ch 13's war-judgment: so many men fallen by the sword that women outnumber them seven to one). Then the turn: "In that day shall the **branch of the Lord** be beautiful and glorious; the fruit of the earth excellent and comely to them that are escaped of Israel" (v2). "They that are left in Zion and remain in Jerusalem shall be called holy, every one that is written among the living in Jerusalem" (v3) — "when the Lord shall have **washed away the filth of the daughters of Zion**, and shall have purged the blood of Jerusalem from the midst thereof **by the spirit of judgment and by the spirit of burning**" (v4). "And the Lord will create upon **every dwelling-place of mount Zion**, and upon her assemblies, a **cloud and smoke by day and the shining of a flaming fire by night**; for upon all the glory of Zion shall be a defence" (v5); "there shall be a tabernacle for a shadow in the daytime from the heat, and for a place of refuge, and a covert from storm and from rain" (v6).

**Exegesis:**
- *The same fire, two offices.* The "spirit of burning" that purges the filth and blood of Jerusalem (v4) and the "flaming fire by night" that defends Zion (v5) are the same fire doing two things. Judgment-fire and glory-fire are not opposites; the fire that consumes what is filthy is the fire that then shines as protection. This is the thread from the close of 1 Nephi — "the same fire" that devours the wicked and preserves the righteous (1 Ne 22:15-17) — now compressed into two adjacent verses. The cleansing is the precondition of the sheltering.
- *Judgment becomes redemption for the same daughters.* Chapter 13 stripped "the daughters of Zion" for their haughty finery (13:16-24); chapter 14 says the Lord "shall have washed away the filth of the daughters of Zion" (v4). The very ones judged are the ones cleansed. Nephi keeps the pair intact: the burning of ch 13 was not the end of the daughters of Zion but the purging that makes them holy. Judgment in Isaiah is rarely terminal; it is usually the front half of a redemption.
- *The pillar democratized (v5).* In the Exodus the cloud-by-day and fire-by-night stood over the *one* tabernacle (Ex 13:21). Here the Lord creates it "upon **every dwelling-place** of mount Zion." The localized glory becomes distributed — every home gets its own pillar, every dwelling its own holy of holies. What was concentrated in one tent over one people is, in the redeemed Zion, spread across every household. The presence is no longer a place you travel to; it is a covering over where you live.
- *The Branch of the Lord (v2).* A messianic title (Jer 23:5, "I will raise unto David a righteous Branch") — beautiful and glorious "in that day." It is also "the branch" of the broken-off remnant (2 Ne 3:5, the branch of Joseph; Jacob 2:25). The Christ-Branch and the remnant-branch are the same word: the line that was broken off is glorified *in* the Messiah who is the Branch.

**The Book of Mormon variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- This is one of the **near-identical** chapters — Isaiah 4 belongs to the roughly two hundred verses that match the KJV closely. The differences are minor: v3 reads plural "they that are left... and remain" for KJV's singular "he that is left... and he that remaineth"; v5 adds "of Zion" ("upon all the glory of Zion shall be a defence"); v2 trims "shall be." No variant alters the sense. *That a chapter can be this close is itself a datapoint* — the Book of Mormon's Isaiah is not uniformly reworked; some chapters are reproduced almost verbatim, which makes the substantive variants elsewhere (12:9, 13:6) stand out as deliberate rather than systematic.

**Nephite framing — likening (four questions):**
- *Why did Nephi include Isaiah 4?* Because it is the redemptive resolution of chapter 13. He will not give the judgment without the mercy that completes it: the desolate city of 13:26 becomes the sheltered Zion of 14:6; the stripped daughters of 13:16-24 become the washed daughters of 14:4. The short chapter is the hinge from wrath to glory.
- *Was it for us, last-dispensation readers?* "In that day" is the millennial Zion, and the latter-day reading is explicit in the cross-references: the refuge of v6 is D&C 45:66-72 (the latter-day Zion as "a place of refuge... the only people that shall not be at war"). The cloud-and-fire over every dwelling is the promised divine presence over the gathered.
- *How does it apply to us?* The cleansing is "by the spirit of judgment and by the spirit of burning" — the refining fire of the earlier studies (the crucible that purifies rather than destroys). And the democratized pillar reframes holiness: it is meant to rest "upon every dwelling-place," not only the temple. The home is intended to carry its own covering of glory.
- *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* The Branch (v2) is Nephi's "all things typify Christ" (11:4) made concrete, and it is also his own people's branch (2 Ne 3:5). For a broken-off remnant, "the branch of the Lord... beautiful and glorious" is the promise that their severed line is not lost but will be glorified in the Messiah who is himself the Branch.
