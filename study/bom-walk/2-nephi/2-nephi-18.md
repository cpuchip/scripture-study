# 2 Nephi 18 — Isaiah 8: Two Waters, Two Fears, and the Stone That Saves or Trips

*(Isaiah block — Isaiah 8. Full protocol: exegesis + variants + likening. Compare Isaiah 8.)*

**Binding idea:** Why does Isaiah set the soft waters of Shiloah against the flood, the fear of men against the fear of God — and how can the same stone be both a sanctuary and a stumbling block?

**Read:** Isaiah is told to write "Maher-shalal-hash-baz" (Heb: "to speed to the spoil, he hasteneth the prey") with faithful witnesses; his son by the prophetess gets that name, and before the child can say "my father, my mother," Assyria will plunder Damascus and Samaria (vv1-4). "Forasmuch as this people refuseth **the waters of Shiloah that go softly**, and rejoice in Rezin and Remaliah's son; now therefore... the Lord bringeth up upon them **the waters of the river, strong and many, even the king of Assyria**... he shall reach even to the neck... O Immanuel" (vv6-8). The nations' plots fail: "Take counsel together, and it shall come to naught; speak the word, and it shall not stand; **for God is with us**" (v10). God instructs Isaiah not to walk in this people's way: "Say ye not, A confederacy... **neither fear ye their fear, nor be afraid. Sanctify the Lord of Hosts himself, and let him be your fear, and let him be your dread**" (vv12-13). "And he shall be **for a sanctuary; but for a stone of stumbling, and for a rock of offense** to both the houses of Israel... and many among them shall stumble, and fall" (vv14-15). "Bind up the testimony, seal the law among my disciples" (v16); "I will wait upon the Lord, that hideth his face from the house of Jacob" (v17); "I and the children whom the Lord hath given me are for signs and for wonders" (v18). Then: "when they shall say unto you, Seek unto them that have familiar spirits, and unto wizards that peep and mutter — should not a people seek unto their God for the living to hear from the dead? **To the law and to the testimony; and if they speak not according to this word, it is because there is no light in them**" (vv19-20).

**Exegesis:**
- *Two waters (vv6-8).* "The waters of Shiloah that go softly" — the quiet stream feeding Jerusalem, an image of unspectacular trust in God and the Davidic promise. The people "refuse" it; they want the impressive alliance, the strong current. So they get one: "the waters of the river, strong and many, even the king of Assyria," a flood that reaches "even to the neck." They refused the trickle and received the torrent — the very power they preferred, now drowning them. God's help often comes softly; rejecting it does not get you no water, it gets you a flood you cannot steer.
- *Two fears (vv12-13).* "Neither fear ye their fear... Sanctify the Lord of Hosts himself, and let him be your fear, and let him be your dread." The cure for the fear of men is not fearlessness; it is a larger, rightly-aimed fear. The dread of the confederacy is displaced by the dread of God. You will fear something; the only question is whether you fear the right thing. Reverence for God is the fear that dissolves all the smaller ones.
- *The stone: sanctuary and stumbling block (vv14-15).* The same stone is two things to two kinds of people: "a sanctuary" to those who trust, "a stone of stumbling, and a rock of offense" to those who do not. The New Testament reads this stone as Christ (Rom 9:33; 1 Pet 2:8; and Simeon's "set for the fall and rising of many," Luke 2:34). Christ does not change; the response does. The rock that shelters you is the rock you trip over if you will not stand on it. He is not neutral — you cannot meet him and remain unmoved; he is either your refuge or your offense.
- *To the law and to the testimony (vv19-20).* Against the appetite for hidden knowledge from illegitimate sources — "wizards that peep and mutter," mediums who claim to fetch the dead — Isaiah sets the standard: go to God, and test every voice against the written word. "To the law and to the testimony; and if they speak not according to this word, it is because there is no light in them." Any voice, however impressive or uncanny, that does not accord with the revealed word carries no light. It is the discernment charter — and the same thing Nephi is doing by copying Isaiah at all: holding every claim to the testimony of the prophets.

**The Book of Mormon variants (verified by direct comparison this session):**
- *v19 — the clarified question.* KJV Isaiah 8:19 ends cryptically: "should not a people seek unto their God? for the living to the dead?" The Book of Mormon clarifies it: "should not a people seek unto their God **for the living to hear from the dead**?" The added "to hear from" resolves the elliptical KJV phrase — *why consult mediums to hear from the dead, when the living should seek God?* The variant turns an opaque fragment into a clear rhetorical question.
- *v4 — restructured.* KJV: "For before the child shall have knowledge to cry, My father, and my mother, the riches of Damascus... shall be taken away." BoM: "For behold, the child shall **not** have knowledge to cry, My father, and my mother, **before** the riches of Damascus... shall be taken away." The clause order shifts but the sense (the plunder comes before the child can talk) holds.
- (Minor: v1 adds "the word of"; v22 "and shall be driven" for "and they shall be driven.")

**Nephite framing — likening (four questions):**
- *Why did Nephi include Isaiah 8?* For the stone-of-stumbling (a Christ-prophecy, per "all things typify Christ," 11:4) and for "to the law and to the testimony" — which is the charter for Nephi's whole project. He tests everything against the prophetic word, and here Isaiah names that exact standard.
- *Was it for us, last-dispensation readers?* "To the law and to the testimony" (v20) is the canonical discernment rule, cited across the dispensations. The "wizards that peep and mutter" are every age's version of seeking answers from voices that have no authority to give them. And the stone that is sanctuary-or-stumbling is Christ, now: he is still not neutral.
- *How does it apply to us?* The waters of Shiloah: we refuse God's quiet, unimpressive help and prefer a torrent — the dramatic fix, the powerful alliance, the arm of flesh — and get a flood we cannot control. And the discernment test is sharply live: in an age full of voices that "peep and mutter" — channeled, sensational, or merely confident — the rule holds. Test every voice against the word; if it does not accord, "there is no light in them," however impressive the muttering.
- *How do we read it better through Nephi's framing?* The stone (v14) is the Redeemer Nephi saw; "God is with us" (v10) is the Immanuel of the previous chapter — the condescension. And Isaiah's children as "signs and wonders" (v18), names that are walking prophecies (Shear-jashub, "a remnant shall return"; Maher-shalal-hash-baz, "speed to the spoil"), are likening made flesh — exactly what Nephi does when he turns Isaiah's words onto his own children.
