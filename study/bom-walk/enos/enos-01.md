# Enos 1 — The Wrestle, and the Prayer That Kept Widening

*(The whole Book of Enos — one chapter. Enos's mighty prayer, remission of sins, the record covenant, his serene farewell. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Enos's wrestle begins with his own soul and does not stop there. Where does the prayer go after his guilt is swept away — and what does its widening reveal about what forgiveness does to a person?

**Read:** Enos, son of Jacob, taught "in the nurture and admonition of the Lord" (v1), tells "of the **wrestle** which I had before God, before I received a remission of my sins" (v2). Hunting, his father's words about eternal life "sunk deep into my heart" (v3); "my soul hungered; and I kneeled down before my Maker, and I cried unto him in **mighty prayer**... **and all the day long did I cry unto him; yea, and when the night came I did still raise my voice high that it reached the heavens**" (v4). The voice: "**Enos, thy sins are forgiven thee**" — "my guilt was swept away" (vv5-6). "Lord, how is it done?" — "**Because of thy faith in Christ, whom thou hast never before heard nor seen**... thy faith hath made thee whole" (vv7-8). Then the widening: "I began to feel a desire for the welfare of my brethren, the **Nephites**; wherefore, I did pour out my whole soul unto God for them" (v9). Then further: "I prayed unto him with many long strugglings for my brethren, the **Lamanites**" (v11) — the very people who "swore in their wrath that... they would destroy our records and us" (v14) — asking that God "would **preserve a record**... that it might be brought forth at some future day unto the Lamanites, that, perhaps, they might be **brought unto salvation**" (v13). God covenants to bring the records forth "in his own due time" (v16); "thy fathers have also required of me this thing" (v18). Enos ministers; "our labors were vain; their hatred was fixed" (v20). At the end: "I must soon go down to my grave... I have declared [the word] in all my days, and have rejoiced in it above that of the world" (v26). "I soon go to **the place of my rest, which is with my Redeemer**... then shall I **see his face with pleasure**, and he will say unto me: Come unto me, ye blessed" (v27).

**Thoughts:**
- *The prayer keeps widening (vv4-16) — the geometry of a forgiven heart.* Enos's wrestle starts narrow: "supplication for **mine own soul**" (v4). The moment his guilt is swept away, the prayer turns outward — first to "the welfare of my brethren, the **Nephites**" (v9), then, remarkably, to "my brethren, the **Lamanites**" (v11), his enemies, then to the preservation of a record so *they* might be saved in a future day (v13). Forgiveness received does not curve inward into private relief; it expands. The proof that Enos was truly forgiven is that he immediately began to hunger for others — and not just his own people, but the people sworn to destroy him. The widening *is* the conversion. (The same shape as Alma 36: the instant Alma's pain is gone, he longs to bring others to the same joy.)
- *Praying for the enemy, and the Book of Mormon's own origin (vv11-16).* Enos prays "with many long strugglings for... the Lamanites" — and his specific petition is that the record be preserved and brought to them "that, perhaps, they might be brought unto salvation." He is interceding for the salvation of the very people who want to destroy his record and his life. And God *covenants* to it. So the Book of Mormon's preservation and latter-day emergence to the descendants of the Lamanites is, in part, the answer to one man's wrestling prayer for his enemies. Love for enemies is not a sentiment here; it is intercession that shapes history.
- *Faith in a Christ never seen (v8).* "Because of thy faith in Christ, whom thou hast never before heard nor seen." Enos is made whole centuries before the Incarnation, by faith reaching forward across time to a Savior not yet born. The healing does not wait for Christ to arrive; faith lays hold of him in advance. (The same forward faith Jacob named, 4:4: "we knew of Christ... many hundred years before his coming.")
- *The all-day, all-night wrestle (v4).* "All the day long did I cry unto him; yea, and when the night came I did still raise my voice." The mighty prayer that breaks through is not a quick petition but a sustained wrestle (v2 — the word evokes Jacob wrestling the angel, Gen 32, until the blessing came). Some answers come only to the prayer that will not let go. (And note: the answer came *during* the labor — "while I was thus struggling in the spirit," v10 — not only at its end.)
- *Death as homecoming (v27).* Enos closes not with dread but serenity: "I soon go to the place of my rest, which is with my Redeemer... then shall I see his face with pleasure." Where Jacob's bar "striketh the wicked with awful dread" (6:13), Enos anticipates the *pleasing* side — "with pleasure" — because he spent his life on the word and "rejoiced in it above that of the world." The forgiven man who prayed his life outward meets death as an invitation: "Come unto me, ye blessed."
