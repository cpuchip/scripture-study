# Words of Mormon 1 — "For a Wise Purpose": Obedience in the Dark Closes the Loop

*(Mormon's bridge — the seam between the small plates and his abridgment. ~A.D. 385. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** A thousand years after Nephi, Mormon includes the small plates "for a wise purpose" he cannot see, only feels prompted to do. What loop does that close — and what does obedience-in-the-dark accomplish that knowledge could not?

**Read:** Mormon, "about to deliver up the record... into the hands of my son Moroni," has "witnessed almost all the destruction of my people, the Nephites" (v1); he prays Moroni may "survive them, that he may write... somewhat concerning Christ, that perhaps some day it may **profit** them" (v2). After abridging the large plates "down to the reign of this king Benjamin," he "searched among the records" and "found these plates" — the small account "from Jacob down to... king Benjamin, and also many of the words of Nephi" (v3). They please him "because of the prophecies of the coming of Christ" (v4). "I shall take these plates... and put them with the remainder of my record, for they are **choice unto me**" (v6). "And I do this **for a wise purpose**; for thus it whispereth me, according to the workings of the Spirit of the Lord which is in me. And now, **I do not know all things; but the Lord knoweth all things which are to come; wherefore, he worketh in me to do according to his will**" (v7). His prayer: "that they may once again come to the knowledge of God... that they may once again be a **delightsome people**" (v8). "I know that they will be preserved; for there are great things written upon them, out of which my people and their brethren shall be **judged** at the great and last day" (v11). Then king Benjamin — fighting "with the sword of Laban" "in the strength of the Lord" (vv13-14), and, with the holy prophets who "did use much **sharpness** because of the stiffneckedness of the people" (v17), "**by laboring with all the might of his body and the faculty of his whole soul**, did once more establish peace in the land" (v18).

**Thoughts:**
- *"For a wise purpose... I do not know all things; but the Lord knoweth" (v7) — the loop closes.* This is the most providentially loaded verse of the walk so far, because it answers a thread opened a thousand years earlier. In 1 Ne 9:5-6, Nephi made the small plates "for a wise purpose in [the Lord], which purpose I know not," trusting that "the Lord knoweth all things." Now Mormon, abridging a millennium later, is prompted to *include* those same small plates "for a wise purpose" he also "know[s] not." Two prophets, a thousand years apart, each obeying a prompting whose reason was hidden from both — and the reason (D&C 10:38-45) was hidden until 1828: Joseph Smith would lose the 116-page abridgment of the large plates covering this very period, and the small plates Mormon was prompted to include would cover it instead. The "wise purpose" was the Lord seeing fourteen centuries and an ocean ahead. **Neither Nephi nor Mormon could have known why; both obeyed anyway; and the Book of Mormon's opening survives because they did.** Obedience in the dark accomplished what no foresight available to them could.
- *The abridger in the ruins (vv1-2, 8).* Mormon works on this record having "witnessed almost all the destruction of my people," expecting his son to see "the entire destruction." He labors over a record for a future he will not live to see, for the descendants of the people destroying him, praying they "may once again... be a delightsome people." The whole abridgment is an act of hope against the evidence — a man gathering the words of his dying nation so that "perhaps some day it may profit them" (the same enemy-directed intercession as Enos 1:13). The Book of Mormon is, at its seam, a grief-soaked labor of hope.
- *The seam itself.* Words of Mormon is the literal stitch where the small plates (Nephi's spiritual record, just completed at Omni) join Mormon's abridgment of the large plates (the historical narrative beginning at Mosiah). It is the one place the abridger steps out from behind the curtain to explain the construction — and what he explains is not editorial method but *providence*: he is following a whisper he cannot account for. The book's architecture is, by its own testimony, the Spirit's, not the editor's.
- *"Whole soul" labor (v18).* King Benjamin establishes peace "by laboring with all the might of his body and the **faculty of his whole soul**." It echoes Amaleki's closing charge one book earlier — "offer your whole souls as an offering" (Omni 1:26). The transition from small plates to abridgment carries the same note across the seam: the work of God, whether record-keeping or kingship, takes the *whole* soul, not a portion. Benjamin (whose great sermon is coming in Mosiah) is introduced as a king who held nothing back.
- *Records as the judgment standard (v11).* "Great things written upon them, out of which my people... shall be judged at the great and last day." The recurring claim (2 Ne 25:22; 29:11; 33:11) that the records are not merely history but the *standard of judgment* — which raises the stakes of Mormon's whole labor: he is preserving the very text against which his people will be measured.
