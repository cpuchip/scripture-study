# Scratch — 2 Nephi 2: The Fall, Agency, and Opposition

**Binding question:** How does 2 Nephi 2 explain why there *must* be opposition, why the Fall was a fall *upward*, and how the Atonement makes us "free to choose"?

**Source:** Study spin-off from the Book of Mormon walk. Written as a mini-study for `study/bom-walk/studies/2ne2_fall-agency-opposition.md`.

---

## Outline

1. The address and its stakes — Lehi to Jacob, "in the days of my tribulation"
2. The logical necessity of opposition — verses 11-14 (the philosophical argument)
3. The Eden setup — verses 15-16 (the two trees; enticement as precondition for agency)
4. The Fall as felix culpa — verses 22-25 (what Eden-permanence would have meant; Adam and Eve's own testimony, Moses 5:10-11)
5. The Atonement answers — verses 6-10 (justice, intercession, resurrection; the "great Mediator")
6. Agency: acted upon vs. acting — verses 13-14, 26-27 (the devil's misery trap; what "free forever" means)
7. Hard questions named — transgression vs. sin; opposition and the problem of evil
8. Connections to existing workspace studies

### Moses 5:10-11 — read via gospel_get this session ✓

v10 (Adam): "Blessed be the name of God, for because of my transgression my eyes are opened, and in this life I shall have joy, and again in the flesh I shall see God."
v11 (Eve): "Were it not for our transgression we never should have had seed, and never should have known good and evil, and the joy of our redemption, and the eternal life which God giveth unto all the obedient."

KEY OBSERVATION: Both Adam and Eve use the word "transgression" (not "sin"). Both frame the Fall as gain: opened eyes, seed (posterity), knowledge of good and evil, joy of redemption, eternal life. Eve's voice especially. She is not lamenting — she is rejoicing. She names five things that would not have been without the Fall.

NOTE: D&C 29:40 uses "transgressed" language but also says Adam "became subject to the will of the devil." This is the most sober rendering — it doesn't celebrate the Fall uncritically. Adam yielded; there were real consequences.

### D&C 93:30 — read via gospel_get this session ✓

"All truth is independent in that sphere in which God has placed it, to act for itself, as all intelligence also; otherwise there is no existence."

KEY: This is the metaphysical root of 2 Ne 2:11-14. Independence (self-determination) is constitutive of existence itself. To destroy agency is to unmake the thing. Lehi's "compound in one" and D&C 93:30's "otherwise there is no existence" are saying the same thing.

### Moses 6:55-56 — read via gospel_get this session ✓

v55: "they taste the bitter, that they may know to prize the good."
v56: "it is given unto them to know good from evil; wherefore they are agents unto themselves"

NOTE: Moses 6:55 is a beautiful miniature of the Lehi argument — the bitter is not incidental suffering but the condition for prizing the good. The causal arrow is explicit.

### Webster 1828 — read via mcp__webster__define this session ✓

OPPOSITION (n.): "The act of opposing; an attempt to check, restrain, or defeat; resistance." Also: "Repugnance; contrariety of sentiment, interest, or purpose." And the astronomical sense: "the situation of a heavenly body with respect to another when in the part of the heavens directly opposite to it."

OBSERVATION: The astronomical definition is interesting — opposition as two bodies at maximum distance from each other, each defined by the other's position. Lehi's "compound in one" is the collapse of that distance into a single undifferentiated mass.

TRANSGRESSION (n.): "The act of transgressing, or of passing over or beyond any law, civil or moral; the violation of a law or known principle of rectitude; breach of command; fault; offense; crime; sin."

OBSERVATION: Transgression etymologically means "going across" or "stepping over" a boundary. It does not inherently carry the moral weight of willful rebellion. In the Eden account, the consistent scriptural term is "transgression" — which Lehi, Moses, Adam, and Eve all use. The text of 1 John 3:4 says "sin is the transgression of the law" — collapsing them — but the Restoration distinguishes them in the Eden context.

MEDIATOR (n.): "One who mediates; especially, one who interposes between parties at variance for the purpose of reconciling them; hence, an intercessor."

OBSERVATION: The word "Mediator" appears twice in 2 Ne 2 (vv. 27, 28). In both cases it is the antidote to the devil's trap. Lehi doesn't say "Savior" or "Redeemer" there — he says "Mediator," the one who stands between two parties at variance. The variance is between us-fallen and the law that condemns.

## Gap & Critical Analysis

### Strongest claims against the text

1. **"The Fall was a fall upward"** — Does the text actually say this? Almost. Lehi says the Fall was necessary for existence, for joy, for children. Moses 5:10-11 is the clearest "upward" testimony. Moses 4:28 (God confirms knowing good and evil). BUT: the text also says Adam "became subject to the will of the devil" (D&C 29:40), that days were "prolonged that they might repent" (v21), and that men "perish from that which is good, and become miserable forever" without the Atonement (v5). The felix culpa reading is supported but not unqualified.

2. **The "compound in one" argument (vv. 11-13)** — Lehi's argument is that without opposition, things would be a "compound in one" and therefore dead, purposeless. This is a real philosophical claim. The strongest version: without the possibility of not-X, X has no meaning. Without misery, "happiness" is just a name for a uniform state, not a real experience. This holds. The harder question: does this mean God *needed* evil to exist, or just that God knew that any world with agents would have opposition? The text doesn't resolve this — it asserts necessity, not origin.

3. **Agency requires enticement (v16)** — "man could not act for himself save it should be that he was enticed by the one or the other." This is a strong claim: the devil's existence is not incidental to agency; it is constitutive of it. D&C 29:39 confirms: "it must needs be that the devil should tempt the children of men, or they could not be agents unto themselves." This is surprising. The tempter is not merely an unfortunate presence; the presence of temptation is what makes choice real.

### Weakest links

- **Transgression vs. sin** — The text consistently uses "transgression" for Eden (vv. 19, 22, 23; Moses 5:10, 11), but the study should hold this distinction carefully. Modern LDS teaching (e.g. Dallin Oaks' October 1993 "The Great Plan of Happiness") draws the distinction explicitly. I have not read that talk this session. I should either read it or mark this as paraphrase-territory only.
- **"The way is prepared from the fall of man" (v4)** — This verse makes salvation "free." What does "free" mean here? Footnote fn-4c links to TG Grace. The Webster 1828 sense of "free" as unconstrained, available without cost, matters here. I haven't verified this deeply enough to make a strong claim.
- **"Felix culpa" language is my gloss, not the text's.** The text never says the Fall was "fortunate." It says it was necessary, purposeful, and followed by joy. The Latin framing is an external interpretive tradition. Label it as such.

### Missing voices

- Modern prophets on the Fall / transgression distinction: Dallin Oaks 1993 is the canonical treatment. Not read this session — paraphrase only, not quoted.
- The D&C 29 broader context (vv. 36-45) covers Satan's fall and Adam's transgression together. Read vv. 39-40; the surrounding context strengthens the picture.
- The problem of evil: the study should name it but not pretend to resolve it. Lehi's argument answers *why opposition exists in the plan*, not *why evil exists in the world at the level of suffering*. These are different questions.

### Ring check

Binding question: "How does 2 Nephi 2 explain why there *must* be opposition, why the Fall was a fall *upward*, and how the Atonement makes us 'free to choose'?"

The text answers all three:
- Must: vv. 11-14 (logical necessity; "compound in one"; no existence without it)
- Upward: vv. 22-25 + Moses 5:10-11 (what Eden-stasis would have been; Adam and Eve's own testimony)
- Free to choose: vv. 26-27 (Atonement redeems from the Fall, restoring agency; Mediator as the means)

The ring closes. But there is a complication the study should surface: the freedom in v27 is *not* the freedom Adam and Eve had before the Fall (naive innocence). It is a *harder* freedom — knowing good and evil, now choosing between real alternatives with moral weight. The Atonement does not return us to Eden; it makes us something Eden could not have made us.

### Posture check

Am I discovering or confirming? The "felix culpa" reading is the dominant Restoration reading, and I arrived knowing it. The corrective: I found genuine tensions in D&C 29:40 ("became subject to the will of the devil"), in Moses 4:28 (God's confirmation that something real was gained), and in the limit of Lehi's argument (it explains why opposition is necessary given a plan with agents; it doesn't explain the origin of that necessity). I'm not hiding those. The study names them.
