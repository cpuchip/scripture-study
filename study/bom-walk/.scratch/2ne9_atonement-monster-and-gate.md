# 2 Nephi 9 — Source Log

*Working document. Kept as research provenance — traces how observations were reached.*

---

## Binding Question

How does Jacob's framing of death and hell as an "awful monster" structure the Atonement as an act of escape — and why does he move directly from cosmic deliverance (vv. 6–13) into the series of woes and the keeper of the gate (vv. 25–41), as though the two halves of the sermon belong to the same argument?

## Verified Quotes

### 2 Nephi 9:1 — Purpose Statement
> "And now, my beloved brethren, I have read these things that ye might know concerning the covenants of the Lord that he has covenanted with all the house of Israel—"

**Observation:** Jacob is explicit that the Isaiah reading was for covenant knowledge — not abstract theology but family knowledge, corporate hope. The sermon begins from a relational posture, not a lectern.
**Connects to:** Section I (The Occasion)

---

### 2 Nephi 9:3–4 — The Pastoral Note
> "Behold, my beloved brethren, I speak unto you these things that ye may rejoice, and lift up your heads forever, because of the blessings which the Lord God shall bestow upon your children." (v. 3)
> "For I know that ye have searched much, many of you, to know of things to come; wherefore I know that ye know that our flesh must waste away and die; nevertheless, in our bodies we shall see God." (v. 4)

**Observation:** Jacob acknowledges the congregation has been searching. This is not ignorant people being taught — it is troubled people being given a framework they half-knew but couldn't complete. The sermon resolves a question already in the room.
**Connects to:** Section I

---

### 2 Nephi 9:5 — The Logical Necessity
> "Yea, I know that ye know that in the body he shall show himself unto those at Jerusalem, from whence we came; for it is expedient that it should be among them; for it behooveth the great Creator that he suffereth himself to become subject unto man in the flesh, and die for all men, that all men might become subject unto him."

**Observation:** "Behooveth" — the Atonement is a necessity, not an option. The word implies obligation arising from the nature of the case. Jacob runs a logical argument, not an appeal to sentiment.
**Connects to:** Section II

---

### 2 Nephi 9:6 — The Must
> "For as death hath passed upon all men, to fulfil the merciful plan of the great Creator, there must needs be a power of resurrection, and the resurrection must needs come unto man by reason of the fall; and the fall came by reason of transgression; and because man became fallen they were cut off from the presence of the Lord."

**Observation:** "Must needs be" — this is Jacob's structural logic. Death → Fall → Transgression → Cut off. The chain is airtight. Resurrection is demanded by the same logic that made the Fall real.
**Connects to:** Section II

---

### 2 Nephi 9:7 — "Infinite Atonement"
> "Wherefore, it must needs be an infinite atonement—save it should be an infinite atonement this corruption could not put on incorruption. Wherefore, the first judgment which came upon man must needs have remained to an endless duration. And if so, this flesh must have laid down to rot and to crumble to its mother earth, to rise no more."

**Observation:** Jacob names what kind of Atonement is required before describing what happens if it doesn't come. The argument is contrafactual — "if not, then here is the horror." The language is physical: "rot and crumble to its mother earth."
**Connects to:** Section II

---

### 2 Nephi 9:8–9 — "Like unto him"
> "O the wisdom of God, his mercy and grace! For behold, if the flesh should rise no more our spirits must become subject to that angel who fell from before the presence of the Eternal God, and became the devil, to rise no more." (v. 8)
> "And our spirits must have become like unto him, and we become devils, angels to a devil, to be shut out from the presence of our God, and to remain with the father of lies, in misery, like unto himself; yea, to that being who beguiled our first parents, who transformeth himself nigh unto an angel of light, and stirreth up the children of men unto secret combinations of murder and all manner of secret works of darkness." (v. 9)

**Observation:** "Become like unto him" — this is the structural horror at the center of the argument. Without Atonement, human nature does not merely suffer punishment; it *conforms* to the nature of the punisher. The Fall's endpoint is not extinction but identity transformation — becoming devils. This is the deepest statement of what the Atonement saves from.
**Connects to:** Section II, binding question

---

### 2 Nephi 9:10 — The Monster Named
> "O how great the goodness of our God, who prepareth a way for our escape from the grasp of this awful monster; yea, that monster, death and hell, which I call the death of the body, and also the death of the spirit."

**Observation:** Jacob defines his own terms: "monster, death and hell" = death of body + death of spirit. The "escape" language is deliberate — not triumph over the monster but rescue from its grasp. The monster does not die here; a way of escape is provided. The monster is still real.
**Connects to:** Section II, binding question

---

### 2 Nephi 9:11–12 — The Reversal
> "And because of the way of deliverance of our God, the Holy One of Israel, this death, of which I have spoken, which is the temporal, shall deliver up its dead; which death is the grave." (v. 11)
> "And this death of which I have spoken, which is the spiritual death, shall deliver up its dead; which spiritual death is hell; wherefore, death and hell must deliver up their dead, and hell must deliver up its captive spirits, and the grave must deliver up its captive bodies, and the bodies and the spirits of men will be restored one to the other; and it is by the power of the resurrection of the Holy One of Israel." (v. 12)

**Observation:** Both deaths reverse. The grave surrenders bodies; hell surrenders spirits. This is the structural answer to vv. 7–9. The monster has to let go.
**Connects to:** Section IV

---

### 2 Nephi 9:13 — Restoration and Perfect Knowledge
> "O how great the plan of our God! For on the other hand, the paradise of God must deliver up the spirits of the righteous, and the grave deliver up the body of the righteous; and the spirit and the body is restored to itself again, and all men become incorruptible, and immortal, and they are living souls, having a perfect knowledge like unto us in the flesh, save it be that our knowledge shall be perfect."

**Observation:** Restoration brings perfect knowledge — including, v. 14 makes clear, perfect knowledge of guilt. The judgment is not God reviewing a record; it is persons remembering themselves perfectly. This makes the woes section coherent: the woes are not threats, they are descriptions of what a person who chose those paths will know at the bar.
**Connects to:** Section IV, Section VI (woes as pre-judgment descriptions)

---

### 2 Nephi 9:14 — The Robe of Righteousness
> "Wherefore, we shall have a perfect knowledge of all our guilt, and our uncleanness, and our nakedness; and the righteous shall have a perfect knowledge of their enjoyment, and their righteousness, being clothed with purity, yea, even with the robe of righteousness."

**Observation:** "Nakedness" and the "robe of righteousness" — echoing Genesis 3's nakedness after the Fall. The righteous are re-clothed; the unclean stand naked in perfect knowledge of it.
**Connects to:** Section IV

---

### 2 Nephi 9:19 — The Monster Named Again
> "O the greatness of the mercy of our God, the Holy One of Israel! For he delivereth his saints from that awful monster the devil, and death, and hell, and that lake of fire and brimstone, which is endless torment."

**Observation:** Second use of "awful monster" — this time expanded. The first (v. 10) = death of body + death of spirit. This one adds "the devil" explicitly and "lake of fire and brimstone." The monster has grown in the telling because now Jacob has established what permanent subjection to it would mean (v. 9: "become like unto him").
**Connects to:** Section II, III

---

### 2 Nephi 9:20–21 — The Two Attributes
> "O how great the holiness of our God! For he knoweth all things, and there is not anything save he knows it." (v. 20)
> "And he cometh into the world that he may save all men if they will hearken unto his voice; for behold, he suffereth the pains of all men, yea, the pains of every living creature, both men, women, and children, who belong to the family of Adam." (v. 21)

**Observation:** Holiness grounds omniscience, and omniscience grounds the Atonement. God can suffer for all because he knows all. v. 10's "goodness" (producing escape) and v. 20's "holiness" (producing knowledge sufficient to suffer for every person) are two different claims — one about what God does, one about what makes it possible.
**Connects to:** Section III

---

### 2 Nephi 9:25–26 — The Law Pivot
> "Wherefore, he has given a law; and where there is no law given there is no punishment; and where there is no punishment there is no condemnation; and where there is no condemnation the mercies of the Holy One of Israel have claim upon them, because of the atonement; for they are delivered by the power of him." (v. 25)
> "For the atonement satisfieth the demands of his justice upon all those who have not the law given to them, that they are delivered from that awful monster, death and hell, and the devil, and the lake of fire and brimstone, which is endless torment; and they are restored to that God who gave them breath, which is the Holy One of Israel." (v. 26)

**Observation:** The Atonement covers both those without law AND — by implication — makes the situation of those WITH law more serious, not less. The escape route is provided. Those who know about it and refuse it are in a categorically different position from those who never heard.
**Connects to:** Section V (pivot to woes)

---

### 2 Nephi 9:27–29 — The First Woe and Its Corrective
> "But wo unto him that has the law given, yea, that has all the commandments of God, like unto us, and that transgresseth them, and that wasteth the days of his probation, for awful is his state!" (v. 27)
> "O that cunning plan of the evil one! O the vainness, and the frailties, and the foolishness of men! When they are learned they think they are wise, and they hearken not unto the counsel of God, for they set it aside, supposing they know of themselves, wherefore, their wisdom is foolishness and it profiteth them not. And they shall perish." (v. 28)
> "But to be learned is good if they hearken unto the counsels of God." (v. 29)

**Observation:** v. 27 opens with the law-possessor — us, the reader. v. 28 traces the mechanism of failure: the cunning plan exploits learning itself. The corrective (v. 29) is eight words. The problem gets 40 words; the solution gets 8. Jacob's prose is tight here.
**Connects to:** Section VI

### 2 Nephi 9:30–38 — The Ten Woes (verified, exact)
> v. 30: "wo unto the rich, who are rich as to the things of the world. For because they are rich they despise the poor, and they persecute the meek, and their hearts are upon their treasures; wherefore, their treasure is their god."
> v. 31: "wo unto the deaf that will not hear; for they shall perish."
> v. 32: "Wo unto the blind that will not see; for they shall perish also."
> v. 33: "Wo unto the uncircumcised of heart, for a knowledge of their iniquities shall smite them at the last day."
> v. 34: "Wo unto the liar, for he shall be thrust down to hell."
> v. 35: "Wo unto the murderer who deliberately killeth, for he shall die."
> v. 36: "Wo unto them who commit whoredoms, for they shall be thrust down to hell."
> v. 37: "Yea, wo unto those that worship idols, for the devil of all devils delighteth in them."
> v. 38: "And, in fine, wo unto all those who die in their sins; for they shall return to God, and behold his face, and remain in their sins."

**Observation:** Note the arc: woes 1–3 (rich, deaf, blind) describe the posture of closure — treasure, hearing, sight. Woes 4–6 (uncircumcised, liar, murderer) describe specific sins. Woes 7–9 (whoredoms, idolaters, those who die in sins) expand outward. The final woe (v. 38) is the summary — "die in their sins" — and its consequence is not fire but something worse: they see God and remain in their sins. Face-to-face, still unchanged. The monster's permanent victim is not a sinner who dies — it is a sinner who meets God and stays a sinner.
**Connects to:** Section VI, binding question

---

### 2 Nephi 9:39 — The Summary Verse
> "O, my beloved brethren, remember the awfulness in transgressing against that Holy God, and also the awfulness of yielding to the enticings of that cunning one. Remember, to be carnally-minded is death, and to be spiritually-minded is life eternal."

**Observation:** "Carnally-minded" and "spiritually-minded" are orientation words, not action words. Jacob is describing a direction of mind, not a single act. This verse is the hinge between the woes (what the carnally-minded become) and the keeper of the gate (what awaits the spiritually-minded).
**Connects to:** Sections VI–VII

---

### 2 Nephi 9:41–42 — The Keeper of the Gate
> "O then, my beloved brethren, come unto the Lord, the Holy One. Remember that his paths are righteous. Behold, the way for man is narrow, but it lieth in a straight course before him, and the keeper of the gate is the Holy One of Israel; and he employeth no servant there; and there is none other way save it be by the gate; for he cannot be deceived, for the Lord God is his name." (v. 41)
> "And whoso knocketh, to him will he open; and the wise, and the learned, and they that are rich, who are puffed up because of their learning, and their wisdom, and their riches—yea, they are they whom he despiseth; and save they shall cast these things away, and consider themselves fools before God, and come down in the depths of humility, he will not open unto them." (v. 42)

**Observation:** "He employeth no servant there" — this is the most striking verse in the chapter. The great irony is that the learned, the wealthy, the connected are precisely those who would most expect that a servant with credentials could manage the gate on their behalf. There is no such servant. The same Christ who provides the escape from the monster is the one who stands at the gate. The chapter is unified: the escape route and the gate are the same person.
**Connects to:** Section VII, binding question

### 2 Nephi 9:50–51 — The Isaiah 55 Echo
> "Come, my brethren, every one that thirsteth, come ye to the waters; and he that hath no money, come buy and eat; yea, come buy wine and milk without money and without price." (v. 50)
> "Wherefore, do not spend money for that which is of no worth, nor your labor for that which cannot satisfy. Hearken diligently unto me, and remember the words which I have spoken; and come unto the Holy One of Israel, and feast upon that which perisheth not, neither can be corrupted, and let your soul delight in fatness." (v. 51)

**Observation:** Compare with Isaiah 55:1–2 (verified):
- Isa 55:1: "Ho, every one that thirsteth, come ye to the waters, and he that hath no money; come ye, buy, and eat; yea, come, buy wine and milk without money and without price."
- Isa 55:2: "Wherefore do ye spend money for that which is not bread? and your labour for that which satisfieth not? hearken diligently unto me, and eat ye that which is good, and let your soul delight itself in fatness."

Jacob's v. 50 replaces "Ho" with "Come, my brethren, every one that thirsteth" — he personalizes Isaiah's universal invitation. His v. 51 adds "come unto the Holy One of Israel" which Isaiah 55:2 does not. Jacob inserts the name. After a chapter naming Christ repeatedly as the Holy One, the invitation has a face.
**Connects to:** Section VIII

**Observation:** Lehi's argument — "no flesh can dwell in the presence of God save through the Messiah" — is the presupposition Jacob inherits and extends. Jacob takes Lehi's "no flesh" and traces the contrafactual: if no Atonement, the flesh rots, the spirit conforms to the devil.

## Webster 1828

### "behooveth" (v. 5: "it behooveth the great Creator")
- 1828: "To be necessary for; to be fit for; to be meet for, with respect to **necessity, duty, or convenience**" — Webster's own Luke 24:46 example: "And thus it behooved Christ to suffer."
- **Study relevance:** "Behooveth" carries both necessity AND fitness. Jacob is not saying the Atonement is merely appropriate or kind — he is saying it is *required* by the nature of the case. This is the logic engine of vv. 6–9: if it must happen, then it DID happen.

### "infinite" (v. 7: "an infinite atonement")
- 1828 def. 1: "Unlimited or boundless, in time or space"
- 1828 def. 2: "Without limit in power, capacity, knowledge, or excellence; boundless; immeasurably or inconceivably great; perfect"
- **Study relevance:** "Infinite" in v. 7 is a precise claim about scope. The atonement must be infinite because the problem (corruption, death, identity-transformation into devil-likeness) is permanent — "endless duration" — without it. A finite atonement could cover some but not all. The infinite atonement must cover every person in every time. Jacob's v. 21 ("every living creature, both men, women, and children, who belong to the family of Adam") is the application.

### "carnally" (v. 39: "to be carnally-minded is death")
- 1828: "According to the flesh, to the world, or to human nature; in a manner to gratify animal appetites and lusts; sensually." Webster's own citation: "For to be carnally minded is death; but to be spiritually minded is life and peace. Rom. viii. 6."
- **Study relevance:** Paul's exact phrase in Romans 8:6 is Webster's own example sentence. Jacob v. 39 is not original — it is a conscious echo of Paul. The 1828 confirms the construction was recognized as Pauline at the time of the translation. This is Jacob at the intersection of two scriptural traditions.

### "probation" (v. 27: "wasteth the days of his probation")
- 1828 def. 2c: "Moral trial; the state of man in the present life, in which he has the opportunity of proving his character, and becoming qualified for a happier state."
- **Study relevance:** "Probation" is not penal — it is the testing period. The days of probation are the days given to prove character. To waste them is not simply to sin; it is to squander the testing period. v. 27's "awful is his state" describes someone who used up the proving time without proving anything.

## Gap & Critical Analysis

### Confirmed Strong
- The pivot structure (vv. 25–27): "Wherefore" in v. 25 explicitly connects cosmic deliverance to the law/wo sequence. The two halves are one argument. **Confirmed from text.**
- "Become like unto him" (v. 9) as the deepest horror: verified verbatim. **Frame as Jacob's contrafactual, not as a description of sinners now.**
- The keeper of the gate (v. 41) as resolution. "Holy One of Israel" appears across both halves — same figure provides the escape and keeps the gate. **Confirmed.**
- Ring structure: v. 1 "covenants of the Lord" / v. 53 "how great the covenants of the Lord" — verbal match, textually independent of thesis. **Promote this.**
- Isaiah 55 echo in vv. 50–51: verified both sides. Use "closely mirrors" not "deliberately echoes."

### Needs Qualification
- The woe-arc reading (woes 1–3 = posture; 4–6 = acts; 7+ = expansion): pattern observed, not Jacob's explicit schema. Label as such.
- v. 38 framing: don't call it "worse than fire" — Jacob doesn't rank. Frame as: "the final consequence is not annihilation but a specific exposure — face-to-face with God, still unchanged."
- "He employeth no servant there" (v. 41): Do not reach to priesthood ordinances arguments. The verse is about who opens the gate, not about the path.

### Acknowledged Gaps
- Romans 8:6 (Paul's "carnally-minded is death") is the direct background for v. 39 — Webster's 1828 cites it. Acknowledge briefly as Pauline resonance.
- Mosiah 3:7 parallels Jacob's "suffer the pains" — paraphrase only, do not cite as primary source.
- D&C 19 (infinite punishment): not fetched. Not required — existing sources are sufficient.

### Tensions to Name
- Jacob is preaching to people who know they will be scattered and destroyed. The pastoral warmth (vv. 3, 40, 44) alongside hard doctrine is not contradiction — it is the point. Note it.
- "He employeth no servant there" raises mediation questions Jacob does not close. Name this as open.

### Wo Count Confirmed
**10 woes, vv. 27–38.** v. 28 is an exclamation, not a "wo unto." All 10 verbatim-verified.
