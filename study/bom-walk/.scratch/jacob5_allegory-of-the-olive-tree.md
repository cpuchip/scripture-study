# Jacob 5 — Allegory of the Olive Tree: Source Log

*Working document. Kept as research provenance — traces how observations were reached.*

---

## Binding Question

What does the allegory of the olive tree reveal about the labor and grief of a God who keeps coming back to a decaying vineyard — and why does it end with the servants laboring "for the last time" alongside the master?

---

## Outline

1. **Introduction** — Zenos, Jacob, and why this allegory exists here
2. **First Movement (vv. 1–14):** Decay, intervention, scattering — the Lord's first act
3. **Second Movement (vv. 15–28):** The first return — grafted branches bear good fruit; hidden natural branches in varied ground
4. **Third Movement (vv. 29–49):** The later returns — corruption spreading; grief of God; "What could I have done more?"
5. **Fourth Movement (vv. 50–77):** The last labor — servants multiply, natural branches restored, the final pruning and harvest
6. **The Visit-Count:** Dispensational reading mapped to the returns
7. **The Grief of God:** v41, Isaiah 5:4, and the question that has no good answer
8. **The Servants:** Who labors alongside the master, and what "for the last time" means
9. **Becoming**

## Verified Quotes

### Jacob 5:1-14 — The Setup, Decay, and First Scattering

> "For behold, thus saith the Lord, I will liken thee, O house of Israel, like unto a tame olive tree, which a man took and nourished in his vineyard; and it grew, and waxed old, and began to decay." (v. 3)

> "And it came to pass that the master of the vineyard went forth, and he saw that his olive tree began to decay; and he said: I will prune it, and dig about it, and nourish it, that perhaps it may shoot forth young and tender branches, and it perish not." (v. 4)

> "And it came to pass that the master of the vineyard saw it, and he said unto his servant: It grieveth me that I should lose this tree; wherefore, go and pluck the branches from a wild olive tree, and bring them hither unto me; and we will pluck off those main branches which are beginning to wither away, and we will cast them into the fire that they may be burned." (v. 7)

> "And behold, saith the Lord of the vineyard, I take away many of these young and tender branches, and I will graft them whithersoever I will; and it mattereth not that if it so be that the root of this tree will perish, I may preserve the fruit thereof unto myself; wherefore, I will take these young and tender branches, and I will graft them whithersoever I will." (v. 8)

> "And the Lord of the vineyard caused that it should be digged about, and pruned, and nourished, saying unto his servant: It grieveth me that I should lose this tree; wherefore, that perhaps I might preserve the roots thereof that they perish not, that I might preserve them unto myself, I have done this thing." (v. 11)

> "And these will I place in the nethermost part of my vineyard, whithersoever I will, it mattereth not unto thee; and I do it that I may preserve unto myself the natural branches of the tree; and also, that I may lay up fruit thereof against the season, unto myself; for it grieveth me that I should lose this tree and the fruit thereof." (v. 13)

> "And it came to pass that the Lord of the vineyard went his way, and hid the natural branches of the tame olive tree in the nethermost parts of the vineyard, some in one and some in another, according to his will and pleasure." (v. 14)

**Observations:**
- VISIT 1 begins at v. 4 — the master goes forth, sees the decay, acts. This is the initial period of covenant history (ancient Israel pre-exile or early Nephite separation era).
- "It grieveth me" appears THREE TIMES in vv. 7, 11, 13 — grief is the emotional signature of this first movement. Not anger. Grief.
- v. 8: the root is the anchor. Even if the tree perishes, the root preserves. This is why the scattered natural branches can be restored later — the root lives.
- "Whithersoever I will" (vv. 8, 13) — the scattering is not chaos; it is purposeful placement. The Lord hides them.
- v. 14: "hid" — not abandoned. Hid. There is intentionality in the dispersal.
- **Connects to:** Section II (First Movement), Section VI (Visit count)

### Jacob 5:15-28 — First Return: The Grafted Branches Bear, the Hidden Branches Checked

> "And it came to pass that a long time passed away, and the Lord of the vineyard said unto his servant: Come, let us go down into the vineyard, that we may labor in the vineyard." (v. 15)

> "And it came to pass that the Lord of the vineyard looked and beheld the tree in the which the wild olive branches had been grafted; and it had sprung forth and begun to bear fruit. And he beheld that it was good; and the fruit thereof was like unto the natural fruit." (v. 17)

> "And he said unto the servant: Behold, the branches of the wild tree have taken hold of the moisture of the root thereof, that the root thereof hath brought forth much strength; and because of the much strength of the root thereof the wild branches have brought forth tame fruit. Now, if we had not grafted in these branches, the tree thereof would have perished. And now, behold, I shall lay up much fruit, which the tree thereof hath brought forth; and the fruit thereof I shall lay up against the season, unto mine own self." (v. 18)

> "And the Lord of the vineyard said unto him: Counsel me not; I knew that it was a poor spot of ground; wherefore, I said unto thee, I have nourished it this long time, and thou beholdest that it hath brought forth much fruit." (v. 22)

> "And he said unto the servant: Look hither and behold the last. Behold, this have I planted in a good spot of ground; and I have nourished it this long time, and only a part of the tree hath brought forth tame fruit, and the other part of the tree hath brought forth wild fruit; behold, I have nourished this tree like unto the others." (v. 25)

> "But behold, the servant said unto him: Let us prune it, and dig about it, and nourish it a little longer, that perhaps it may bring forth good fruit unto thee, that thou canst lay it up against the season." (v. 27)

**Observations:**
- VISIT 2 begins at v. 15 — "a long time passed away" then the return. This is likely the meridian of time / Christ's ministry, or possibly the beginning of the Restoration era.
- v. 17: wild branches bear fruit "like unto the natural fruit" — the Gentile mission produces covenant people who are indistinguishable from covenant Israel. Not lesser fruit. The same.
- v. 18: the mechanism — wild branches draw from the root (covenant power, Abrahamic promises), not from their own wildness. The tame fruit comes from the root, not from grafted branch character.
- v. 21: the servant questions poor-spot placement. v. 22: "Counsel me not." Sovereignty over placement. God knows the poor spots.
- v. 25: fourth branch location — good ground, but mixed fruit. This may map to covenant people who don't fully covenant (Jews/Israelites with partial faithfulness?).
- v. 27: the servant pleads for more time — mercy advocating for more nourishment before judgment. The servant is the voice of mercy throughout.
- **Connects to:** Section III (Second Movement), Section VIII (The Servants)

### Jacob 5:29-46 — Third Visit: Corruption Everywhere; v41 Weeping

> "And it came to pass that a long time had passed away, and the Lord of the vineyard said unto his servant: Come, let us go down into the vineyard, that we may labor again in the vineyard. For behold, the time draweth near, and the end soon cometh; wherefore, I must lay up fruit against the season, unto mine own self." (v. 29)

> "And it came to pass that the Lord of the vineyard and the servant went down into the vineyard; and they came to the tree whose natural branches had been broken off, and the wild branches had been grafted in; and behold all sorts of fruit did cumber the tree." (v. 30)

> "But behold, this time it hath brought forth much fruit, and there is none of it which is good. And behold, there are all kinds of bad fruit; and it profiteth me nothing, notwithstanding all our labor; and now it grieveth me that I should lose this tree." (v. 32)

> "And the Lord of the vineyard wept, and said unto the servant: What could I have done more for my vineyard?" (v. 41)

> "And now, behold, notwithstanding all the care which we have taken of my vineyard, the trees thereof have become corrupted, that they bring forth no good fruit; and these I had hoped to preserve, to have laid up fruit thereof against the season, unto mine own self. But, behold, they have become like unto the wild olive tree, and they are of no worth but to be hewn down and cast into the fire; and it grieveth me that I should lose them." (v. 46)

**Observations:**
- VISIT 3 begins at v. 29 — "a long time had passed away" (same formula as visit 2 in v. 15). Third explicit return.
- v. 30: "all sorts of fruit did cumber the tree" — the mixed/corrupted state. The wild branches' overgrowth is the problem (explained in v. 48 by the servant: "the branches have overcome the roots").
- v. 32: "it profiteth me nothing, notwithstanding all our labor" — labor does not guarantee fruit. This is not indifference; it's grief over wasted effort.
- **v. 41: "the Lord of the vineyard wept"** — this is one of only a few places in all scripture where God weeps. The text says "wept" not "was displeased." Compare Moses 7:28-29 (God weeps over the wickedness before the flood). The grief is not performed; it's real.
- v. 41: "What could I have done more?" — rhetorical. The answer is nothing. God has done everything. This echoes Isaiah 5:4.
- v. 43: "that which was choice unto me above all other parts" — the Jerusalem/Zion branch in the best ground, now fully corrupt. This seems to map to Judah/Jerusalem.
- v. 46: "it grieveth me that I should lose them" — the fifth occurrence of "grieveth me" in the chapter. Grief is not a moment; it's the sustained emotional register of God's relationship to this vineyard.
- **Connects to:** Section IV (Third Movement), Section VII (Grief of God)

### Jacob 5:47-60 — The Servant Pleads; The Final Grafting Plan Announced

> "But what could I have done more in my vineyard? Have I slackened mine hand, that I have not nourished it? Nay, I have nourished it, and I have digged about it, and I have pruned it, and I have dunged it; and I have stretched forth mine hand almost all the day long, and the end draweth nigh. And it grieveth me that I should hew down all the trees of my vineyard, and cast them into the fire that they should be burned. Who is it that has corrupted my vineyard?" (v. 47)

> "And it came to pass that the servant said unto his master: Is it not the loftiness of thy vineyard—have not the branches thereof overcome the roots which are good? And because the branches have overcome the roots thereof, behold they grew faster than the strength of the roots, taking strength unto themselves. Behold, I say, is not this the cause that the trees of thy vineyard have become corrupted?" (v. 48)

> "And it came to pass that the Lord of the vineyard said unto the servant: Let us go to and hew down the trees of the vineyard and cast them into the fire, that they shall not cumber the ground of my vineyard, for I have done all. What could I have done more for my vineyard?" (v. 49)

> "But, behold, the servant said unto the Lord of the vineyard: Spare it a little longer." (v. 50)

> "And the Lord said: Yea, I will spare it a little longer, for it grieveth me that I should lose the trees of my vineyard." (v. 51)

> "Wherefore, let us take of the branches of these which I have planted in the nethermost parts of my vineyard, and let us graft them into the tree from whence they came; and let us pluck from the tree those branches whose fruit is most bitter, and graft in the natural branches of the tree in the stead thereof." (v. 52)

> "And, behold, the roots of the natural branches of the tree which I planted whithersoever I would are yet alive; wherefore, that I may preserve them also for mine own purpose, I will take of the branches of this tree, and I will graft in unto them the branches of their mother tree, that I may preserve the roots also unto mine own self, that when they shall be sufficiently strong perhaps they may bring forth good fruit unto me, and I may yet have glory in the fruit of my vineyard." (v. 54)

> "And because that I have preserved the natural branches and the roots thereof, and that I have grafted in the natural branches again into their mother tree, and have preserved the roots of their mother tree, that, perhaps, the trees of my vineyard may bring forth again good fruit; and that I may have joy again in the fruit of my vineyard, and, perhaps, that I may rejoice exceedingly that I have preserved the roots and the branches of the first fruit—" (v. 60)

**Observations:**
- v. 47: "I have dunged it" — dunging is the most intimate and unglamorous labor. God gets his hands in the manure of history.
- v. 47: "I have stretched forth mine hand almost all the day long" — almost all the day. Not all the day — there is a limit, a closing time approaching. The urgency is real.
- v. 47: "Who is it that has corrupted my vineyard?" — the question is rhetorical AND diagnostic. The Lord already knows (v. 48: the servant answers — it is the loftiness, the branches overgrowing the roots, prosperity without covenant).
- v. 48: The servant's diagnosis — "the loftiness of thy vineyard" — prosperity/worldliness overwhelming covenant roots. The branches are not evil in themselves; they have grown faster than the root can support.
- v. 49: "I have done all" — the finality of divine effort. Not "I have done much." All.
- v. 50-51: The servant pleads "Spare it a little longer." The Lord yields. This is the pattern: judgment is just, mercy pleads for more time, and the Lord honors the plea — once more.
- v. 52: Now the gathered branches from nethermost parts are grafted BACK into the mother tree. Reversal of the original scattering. This is gathering.
- v. 54: "branches of their mother tree" — the scattered natural branches become the healing graft for the corrupted main tree. The hidden preserved Israel returns to restore covenant identity.
- v. 60: "that I may have joy again" — joy is the goal. Not just survival, not mere fruit. Joy.
- **Connects to:** Section V (Fourth Movement), Section VIII (The Servants)

### Jacob 5:61-77 — The Last Labor: Many Servants, One Final Grafting, Joy

> "Wherefore, go to, and call servants, that we may labor diligently with our might in the vineyard, that we may prepare the way, that I may bring forth again the natural fruit, which natural fruit is good and the most precious above all other fruit." (v. 61)

> "Wherefore, let us go to and labor with our might this last time, for behold the end draweth nigh, and this is for the last time that I shall prune my vineyard." (v. 62)

> "Graft in the branches; begin at the last that they may be first, and that the first may be last, and dig about the trees, both old and young, the first and the last; and the last and the first, that all may be nourished once again for the last time." (v. 63)

> "Wherefore, dig about them, and prune them, and dung them once more, for the last time, for the end draweth nigh. And if it be so that these last grafts shall grow, and bring forth the natural fruit, then shall ye prepare the way for them, that they may grow." (v. 64)

> "For it grieveth me that I should lose the trees of my vineyard; wherefore ye shall clear away the bad according as the good shall grow, that the root and the top may be equal in strength, until the good shall overcome the bad, and the bad be hewn down and cast into the fire, that they cumber not the ground of my vineyard; and thus will I sweep away the bad out of my vineyard." (v. 66)

> "And thus will I bring them together again, that they shall bring forth the natural fruit, and they shall be one." (v. 68)

> "And it came to pass that the Lord of the vineyard sent his servant; and the servant went and did as the Lord had commanded him, and brought other servants; and they were few." (v. 70)

> "And the Lord of the vineyard said unto them: Go to, and labor in the vineyard, with your might. For behold, this is the last time that I shall nourish my vineyard; for the end is nigh at hand, and the season speedily cometh; and if ye labor with your might with me ye shall have joy in the fruit which I shall lay up unto myself against the time which will soon come." (v. 71)

> "And it came to pass that the servants did go and labor with their mights; and the Lord of the vineyard labored also with them; and they did obey the commandments of the Lord of the vineyard in all things." (v. 72)

> "And it came to pass that when the Lord of the vineyard saw that his fruit was good, and that his vineyard was no more corrupt, he called up his servants, and said unto them: Behold, for this last time have we nourished my vineyard; and thou beholdest that I have done according to my will; and I have preserved the natural fruit, that it is good, even like as it was in the beginning. And blessed art thou; for because ye have been diligent in laboring with me in my vineyard, and have kept my commandments, and have brought unto me again the natural fruit, that my vineyard is no more corrupted, and the bad is cast away, behold ye shall have joy with me because of the fruit of my vineyard." (v. 75)

> "For behold, for a long time will I lay up of the fruit of my vineyard unto mine own self against the season, which speedily cometh; and for the last time have I nourished my vineyard, and pruned it, and dug about it, and dunged it; wherefore I will lay up unto mine own self of the fruit, for a long time, according to that which I have spoken." (v. 76)

> "And when the time cometh that evil fruit shall again come into my vineyard, then will I cause the good and the bad to be gathered; and the good will I preserve unto myself, and the bad will I cast away into its own place. And then cometh the season and the end; and my vineyard will I cause to be burned with fire." (v. 77)

**Observations:**
- VISIT 4 begins at v. 61 — the call for servants. This is structurally the Restoration / last days gathering. "Call servants" — not one servant, many. This is the first time the number expands.
- v. 70: "the servant went and did... and brought other servants; and they were few" — this is load-bearing. The servants are FEW in the last days. Not an army. A remnant that labors with the Lord himself.
- v. 62: "for the last time that I shall prune my vineyard" — "last time" appears in vv. 62, 63, 64, 71, 75, 76. Repetition is the text insisting this is not another cycle. Final.
- v. 63: "begin at the last that they may be first, and that the first may be last" — echoes Matthew 20:16, Matthew 19:30. The first/last reversal: scattered Lamanites, Gentiles brought in first, then covenant Israel.
- v. 65: incremental pruning — "ye shall not clear away the bad thereof all at once, lest the roots thereof should be too strong for the graft" — the gathering is careful, calibrated. Wisdom over speed.
- v. 68: "they shall be one" — unity is the end result of the gathering. One olive tree, not many.
- v. 71: "if ye labor with your might WITH ME" — the preposition is critical. The servants don't labor for the Lord; they labor alongside him. He descends into the vineyard.
- v. 72: "the Lord of the vineyard labored also with them" — direct confirmation. God is not directing from outside; he is pruning, digging, dunging beside the servants.
- v. 75: "ye shall have joy with me" — the covenant fruit is shared joy, not solo satisfaction.
- v. 77: the chapter ends with fire — the good laid up, the bad cast into its place, the vineyard burned. Not triumph in conquest but harvest and cleansing.
- **Connects to:** Section V (Fourth Movement), Section VIII (The Servants), Section IX (Becoming)

## Webster 1828

### "nourish"
- **Primary (1828):** "To feed and cause to grow; to supply a living or organized body, animal or vegetable, with matter which increases its bulk or supplies the waste occasioned by any of its functions; to supply with nutriment."
- **Also:** "To cherish; to comfort. James 5:5."
- **Study relevance:** "Nourish" in Jacob 5 is not passive watering. It is active cherishing — feeding, supplying, promoting health. The Lord nourishes the vineyard the way a parent nourishes a child. "Cherish; comfort" amplifies the grief when the nourishment fails to produce.

### "graft"
- **1828:** "A small shoot or cion of a tree, inserted in another tree as the stock which is to support and nourish it. These unite and become one tree, but the graft determines the kind of fruit."
- **Study relevance:** The graft determines the fruit, not the rootstock. This is why wild branches grafted into tame roots can bring forth tame fruit (Jacob 5:18) — the connection to the root transforms the branch. But also why the reverse fails: wild branches overgrow and overwhelm the root (5:37, 48).

## Visit / Return Count — VERIFIED

Tracing every "it came to pass that the Lord of the vineyard went/came/went down" through the chapter:

**Visit 1: vv. 4-14** — The initial going forth. "The master of the vineyard went forth, and he saw that his olive tree began to decay" (v. 4). He prunes, grafts, scatters natural branches to nethermost parts. Then "the Lord of the vineyard went his way" (v. 14). This is the *first period of covenant intervention* — maps to early Israel's prophetic era, the Abrahamic/Mosaic covenant structure. The natural branches are hidden across the world.

**Visit 2: vv. 15-28** — "A long time passed away, and the Lord of the vineyard said unto his servant: Come, let us go down into the vineyard" (v. 15). He checks the grafted wild branches (good fruit, vv. 17-18), then visits the four hidden natural-branch locations in the nethermost parts (vv. 19-27). At the end the Lord and servant nourish all the fruit together (v. 28). This maps to the meridian of time / Christ's own ministry (or possibly Nephite golden era — the sequence is ambiguous, but something is going right).

**Visit 3: vv. 29-49** — "A long time had passed away, and the Lord of the vineyard said unto his servant: Come, let us go down into the vineyard, that we may labor again in the vineyard" (v. 29). This visit finds universal corruption — wild branches overwhelming the root everywhere (vv. 30-40). The weeping (v. 41), the rhetorical question "What could I have done more?" (vv. 41, 49), and the servant's plea "Spare it a little longer" (v. 50). This maps to the Great Apostasy — when Christ returned (through early Christianity), then the branches overwhelmed the root.

**Visit 4: vv. 50-76** — The servant's plea is answered (vv. 50-51), and the final plan is announced (vv. 52-60). Then: "go to, and call servants, that we may labor diligently with our might" (v. 61). The servant brings "other servants" (v. 70), and they labor together — the Lord alongside them (v. 72). The fruit becomes good again (v. 73), the vineyard is cleansed (v. 74), the servants are blessed with shared joy (v. 75). This is the Restoration / last days gathering.

**CONFIRMED TOTAL: FOUR VISITS**
- Visit 1 (vv. 4–14): First intervention — decay seen, grafting and scattering executed
- Visit 2 (vv. 15–28): Return check — good fruit in most locations, one mixed
- Visit 3 (vv. 29–49): The crisis visit — universal corruption, weeping, "What could I have done more?"
- Visit 4 (vv. 50–76): The last labor — servant pleads, plan announced, servants multiply, gathering executed, joy shared

*Note: Visit 4 is arguably two phases — the plan (vv. 50-60) and the execution (vv. 61-76) — but there is no second departure and return between them. It is one continuous visit.*

### Moses 7:28-33 — God Weeps (PGP parallel to Jacob 5:41)

> "And it came to pass that the God of heaven looked upon the residue of the people, and he wept; and Enoch bore record of it, saying: How is it that the heavens weep, and shed forth their tears as the rain upon the mountains?" (v. 28)

> "And Enoch said unto the Lord: How is it that thou canst weep, seeing thou art holy, and from all eternity to all eternity?" (v. 29)

> "Were it possible that man could number the particles of the earth, yea, millions of earths like this, it would not be a beginning to the number of thy creations; and thy curtains are stretched out still; and yet thou art there, and thy bosom is there; and also thou art just; thou art merciful and kind forever; and how is it thou canst weep?" (v. 30-31)

> "The Lord said unto Enoch: Behold these thy brethren; they are the workmanship of mine own hands, and I gave unto them their knowledge, in the day I created them... And unto thy brethren have I said, and also given commandment, that they should love one another, and that they should choose me, their Father; but behold, they are without affection, and they hate their own blood;" (vv. 32-33)

**Observation:** Enoch's question is the same question the Restoration produces: how can an omnipotent God grieve? The Lord's answer is that the grief is real precisely because the relationship is real. He gave them knowledge. He gave them agency. They turned away. The same pattern underlies Jacob 5:41 — "What could I have done more?" is a real question, not rhetoric. The answer is: nothing. And nothing is the most grief-inducing answer an all-powerful being can give.

### Phase 3a — Critical Analysis

**Strongest Claims — Confirmed**

1. **Four visits** — verified from the text. Each marked by "it came to pass that... the Lord of the vineyard went/came down" with a "long time passed away" formula between visits 1→2 and 2→3.
2. **"What could I have done more?" as the central grief question** — verified verbatim in vv. 41 and 49, and echoed by Isaiah 5:4 (2 Ne 15:4). Both texts verified.
3. **"Labor ye for the last time" as a Restoration refrain** — verified in vv. 62, 63, 64, 71, 76 of the text AND in D&C 43:28.
4. **The Lord labors alongside the servants in the last visit** — v. 72 is unambiguous: "the Lord of the vineyard labored also with them."

**Claims Needing Qualification**

1. **Dispensational mapping** — the "visit 2 = meridian of time" reading is the standard LDS interpretation (via Nephi 1 Ne 15:13) and is coherent, but the text itself does not name these eras. The allegory works even without the dispensational key; the mapping is exegetically defensible but interpretive.
   - Adjustment: present the dispensational reading as the tradition's own interpretation (rooted in 1 Ne 15:13), not as the plain meaning of the text.
2. **"They were few" (v. 70)** — this verse is striking but I should not over-read it as a prophecy of small Church membership. It describes the servants in the last labor, and "few" may simply contrast with the need for many more — it is an invitation, not a census.

**Tensions to Name**

1. **Mercy vs. Judgment**: The servant repeatedly pleads for more time (vv. 27, 50). The Lord repeatedly grants it. But v. 49 says "I have done all." And v. 69: "only this once will I prune my vineyard." The text holds both: God is endlessly patient AND the end is real and approaching. The tension is not resolved; it is the tension that drives the final labor.
2. **Agency of the tree**: The tree does not speak. The allegory is entirely from the Lord's perspective — the tree's agency (Israel's choices) is described only as result (good fruit, bad fruit, corruption). This is not a failure of the allegory but a feature: we see the vineyard from the laborer's side, not the branch's side.
3. **"Grieveth me" five times** — is this anthropomorphism or literal divine emotion? The Moses 7 parallel (God actually weeps) supports literal. The study takes it as literal, in line with Restoration theology.

**Ring Check**: The binding question asks what the allegory reveals about the labor and grief of a God who keeps coming back AND why it ends with servants laboring "for the last time" alongside the master. The text answers both: the labor is driven by grief (the five "grieveth me" occurrences), and the ending is shaped by urgency AND invitation ("if ye labor with your might WITH ME ye shall have joy" — v. 71). The study's four movements answer the binding question directly.
