# Omni 1 — The Record Runs Dry, Then Flares Back Up

*(The whole Book of Omni — five writers. The small plates dwindle to their nadir, then close with Amaleki's rich account + soaring exhortation. Mini-study + small-plates synthesis.)*

**Binding idea:** Five keepers, each writing less than the last, until one reports "no revelation" of his own — and then the record recovers. What does the dwindling reveal, and what proves the record-keeping was never busywork?

**Read:** **Omni**: "I of myself am a wicked man, and I have not kept the statutes and the commandments of the Lord as I ought" (v2). **Amaron** (few words): by 320 years "the more wicked part of the Nephites were destroyed" — for "**Inasmuch as ye will not keep my commandments ye shall not prosper in the land**" (vv5-6). **Chemish** (fewer): notes the handoff custom (v9). **Abinadom** (the nadir): "**I know of no revelation save that which has been written, neither prophecy; wherefore, that which is sufficient is written**" (v11). **Amaleki** (the recovery): Mosiah, "warned of the Lord that he should flee out of the land of Nephi," led the hearkening through the wilderness to **Zarahemla** (vv12-13). There they found the people of Zarahemla, who "came out from Jerusalem at the time that Zedekiah... was carried away captive into Babylon" (v15) — but who "had brought no records with them; and **their language had become corrupted**; and **they denied the being of their Creator**" (v17). Mosiah taught them his language, united the peoples, was made king (vv18-19). A "large stone... with engravings" was interpreted "by the gift and power of God" — the account of Coriantumr and the Jaredites who "came out from the tower" (vv20-22). Amaleki, having no seed, delivers the plates to king Benjamin (v25), "**exhorting all men to come unto God, the Holy One of Israel**... for there is nothing which is good save it comes from the Lord" (v25): "**come unto Christ... and offer your whole souls as an offering unto him, and continue in fasting and praying, and endure to the end**" (v26). "And these plates are full" (v30) — the small plates end.

**Thoughts:**
- *The record dwindles to a nadir, then flares back (vv1-26).* The five writers shrink visibly — Omni confesses wickedness, Amaron and Chemish manage a few lines each, and Abinadom hits bottom: "I know of no revelation save that which has been written... that which is sufficient is written" (v11). This is the thinning I flagged in Jarom reaching its floor: a record-keeper with *nothing new from God to report*. A spiritual record can run dry. **But** the last writer, Amaleki, recovers it — a substantial historical account *and* one of the most beautiful Christ-exhortations in the book. Even at the small plates' end, after the dryness, the witness flares back up. The lesson is not despair at the thinning but the proof that one faithful voice (Amaleki) can carry more than four thin ones — and that the record's worth does not depend on its length.
- *What the Mulekites prove (vv14-17) — the record was never busywork.* The whole small-plates chain (Nephi → Jacob → Enos → Jarom → Omni's five) labored to keep and preserve a record, sometimes apologizing for how little they added. Omni 1 vindicates all of it by contrast: the people of Zarahemla left Jerusalem at the same time, "brought no records with them," and the result was catastrophic — "their language had become corrupted; and they denied the being of their Creator." A people without the record lost their *language* and their *God*. The record-keeping the whole chain treated as a sacred burden was the difference between a people who kept their Creator and a people who forgot He existed. The dwindling record still kept the Nephites tethered; the recordless Mulekites drifted into atheism.
- *"Offer your whole souls as an offering unto him" (v26).* Amaleki's closing exhortation is the small plates' final word, and it is a complete one. Not a portion — "**your whole souls** as an offering." In an era still under the law of Moses (animal offerings), Amaleki names the offering that fulfills the type: the self, given entirely (cf 3 Ne 9:20, the broken heart and contrite spirit that replaces the altar). The small plates, begun "for Christ's sake" (Jacob 1:4), end on the most Christ-centered ask: give him not your firstlings but your whole soul.
- *"That which is good comes from the Lord" (v25).* Amaleki's test for discernment — believe in prophecy, revelation, ministering of angels, gifts — rests on a simple criterion: "there is nothing which is good save it comes from the Lord; and that which is evil cometh from the devil." Moroni will make this the heart of his teaching on discernment (Moroni 7:15-17). The dwindling-record book sets down the rule for knowing the source of good.
- *The small plates close (v30).* "These plates are full." The record Nephi began (1 Ne 6), curated "for the more precious things," passes from Amaleki — who has no son — to a *king* (Benjamin) rather than a descendant. The lineage of keepers ends; the record is handed to the throne, and (via Words of Mormon) to Mormon's abridgment. The most spiritually concentrated section of the Book of Mormon closes, and the historical narrative (Mosiah onward) takes over.
