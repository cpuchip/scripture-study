# 1 Nephi 4 — The Slaying of Laban, the Records Won

**Binding idea:** How does Nephi obtain the plates, and what reasoning carries him through the killing? *(The deep ethical/legal/covenantal study is spun off → `studies/1ne4_slaying-of-laban.md`.)*

**Read:** Nephi rallies his brothers with the Exodus — be strong like Moses, who divided the Red Sea while Pharaoh's armies drowned; the Lord who delivered our fathers can "destroy Laban, even as the Egyptians"; "wherefore can ye doubt?" They follow, still wroth. By night Nephi creeps alone into Jerusalem, "led by the Spirit, not knowing beforehand the things which I should do." He finds Laban fallen drunk, draws his fine sword, and is constrained by the Spirit to kill him. Nephi shrinks — "Never at any time have I shed the blood of man." The Spirit presses three times: the Lord has delivered Laban; Laban sought your life, defied the Lord, stole your property; and "it is better that one man should perish than that a nation should dwindle and perish in unbelief." Nephi then *remembers* the covenant — his seed cannot keep the law of Moses without the law, and the law is engraved on the plates — and obeys, beheading Laban with his own sword. Disguised in Laban's garments and voice, he secures the brass plates from the servant Zoram, who, brought outside the walls, chooses by oath to become "a free man" and join Lehi's family. They depart with the plates and Zoram to the tent.

**Thoughts:**
- *The Exodus typology goes explicit (v2-3).* Nephi's whole rallying speech is the Exodus: Moses, the divided sea, Pharaoh drowned, "deliver us, even as our fathers... destroy Laban, even as the Egyptians." Laban is cast as Pharaoh; the errand is a mini-exodus. The pillar of fire (ch 1) and the Red Sea departure (ch 2) set it up; here Nephi *reasons* from the Exodus the way the whole Book of Mormon will later reason from him. He is a typological reader of his own life.
- *"Led by the Spirit, not knowing beforehand" (v6) is Abraham's faith.* The footnote is exact — Heb 11:8, Abraham "went out, not knowing whither." This is the lived form of 3:7: the way is prepared but not shown in advance; Nephi must walk into the dark city before the way appears. Faith as obedience prior to sight.
- *The killing — see the spin-off study.* The structure is worth marking: the Spirit constrains, Nephi *shrinks* (the text honors his reluctance), and the reasoning arrives in three strands — Laban delivered up, Laban's own guilt (murder-attempt, theft, defiance), and the covenantal necessity of the law. The capstone — "better that one man should perish than that a nation... dwindle and perish in unbelief" (v13) — is the one-for-many logic Caiaphas will later speak of Christ (John 11:50). Nephi beheads Laban with Laban's own sword: the David-and-Goliath move (1 Sam 17:51). Full ethical, legal, and covenantal treatment is in `studies/1ne4_slaying-of-laban.md`.
- *The hinge is remembering the covenant (v14).* What finally moves Nephi is not the command repeated louder but his *remembering* — "Inasmuch as thy seed shall keep my commandments, they shall prosper in the land of promise" (the 2:20 covenant, cited back) — and the deduction that the covenant is unkeepable without the law, and the law is on the plates. The three opening chapters interlock here: the covenant (ch 2) requires the word (ch 3), and the word is won at the cost of one life (ch 4). The killing is reasoned *from the covenant*.
- *Zoram is the chapter's redemptive counterweight.* It does not end on a death; it ends on a servant set free. Zoram, offered his liberty, swears to join, and his line runs a thousand years (the Zoramites — Jacob 1:13; Alma 54:23; 4 Ne 1:36). A man dies; a man is freed and grafted into the covenant family. The oath-form ("as the Lord liveth, and as I live") brackets the chapter with 3:15.
