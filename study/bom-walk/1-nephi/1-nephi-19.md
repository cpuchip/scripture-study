# 1 Nephi 19 — "Liken All Scriptures Unto Us" (and the God of Israel Suffers)

**Binding idea:** How does Nephi tell us to read scripture — and who does he say the suffering Messiah is?

**Read:** Nephi explains the two sets of plates again, notes he writes only "that which I think... sacred," and admits, "if I do err... because of the weakness which is in me, according to the flesh, I would excuse myself." He testifies that "the very God of Israel" — "the God of Abraham, and of Isaac, and the God of Jacob" — "yieldeth himself... as a man, into the hands of wicked men, to be lifted up... and to be crucified... and to be buried in a sepulchre," citing three prophets not in our Bible: Zenock, Neum, and Zenos. Zenos foretells "three days of darkness... a sign given of his death unto those who should inhabit the isles of the sea," and that at that day the Lord will visit Israel "some with his voice, because of their righteousness... and others with the thunderings and the lightnings of his power"; "the God of nature suffers." The Jews, because "they crucify the God of Israel," will be "scourged... and become a hiss and a byword" until "they no more turn aside their hearts," when He will "remember the covenants." Nephi reads to his brothers "many things... written by the prophet Isaiah; for I did liken all scriptures unto us, that it might be for our profit and learning" — and bids them, "liken them unto yourselves, that ye may have hope."

**Thoughts:**
- *"I did liken all scriptures unto us" (v23) — the reading-key for everything that follows.* The methodological hinge of the book. Nephi reads Isaiah not as ancient Judean history but as a mirror: "liken them unto yourselves, that ye may have hope as well as your brethren from whom ye have been broken off" (v24). Scripture is for "profit and learning" precisely when you put yourself inside it. Every Isaiah chapter ahead (20-21, and the great blocks of 2 Nephi) is meant to be read this way — *why did this prophet set it here, and how does it apply to us?* 19:23 is the license for the whole Nephite use of Isaiah.
- *The suffering Messiah is Jehovah, the God of Israel (v7-12).* Nephi's Christology is explicit and pre-Christian: "the very God of Israel" — "the God of Abraham, and of Isaac, and the God of Jacob" — "yieldeth himself... as a man... to be crucified." The God of the Old Testament is the premortal Christ, and He *yields himself* (the condescension of ch 11 again: God comes down to be "spit upon," and "he suffereth it"). The whole Book of Mormon holds this identification — the Creator and the Crucified are one, and His suffering is voluntary ("he suffereth it... because of his loving kindness").
- *Zenock, Neum, Zenos — the lost prophets the brass plates preserved (v10).* Nephi quotes three prophets absent from our Bible, each testifying of Christ's death — lifted up (Zenock), crucified (Neum), buried with three days of darkness (Zenos). This is ch 5's "plain and precious" payoff: the brass plates carried Christ-prophecies the surviving Old Testament lost. Zenos especially will loom large (the olive-tree allegory, Jacob 5). The plainness Nephi insists on (v3, "more plain and precious parts") is precisely this — a canon that named the crucified God centuries early.
- *"The God of nature suffers" (v12).* Zenos's phrase, spoken by "the kings of the isles" when the rocks rend at the crucifixion. The Creator's death convulses creation; the sign of His suffering is the earth itself groaning (fulfilled at 3 Ne 8). It is one of the most arresting four-word Christologies in scripture — the God of nature, suffering.
- *One event, two visitations (v11).* At the crucifixion's sign, the Lord visits "some with his voice, because of their righteousness, unto their great joy and salvation, and others with the thunderings and the lightnings... tempest... fire... vapor of darkness." The same moment is mercy to the righteous and judgment to the wicked (the marvelous-work-cuts-both-ways of 14:7; fulfilled 3 Ne 8-11). The cross sorts.
- *The honest prophet admits he might err (v6).* "If I do err... because of the weakness which is in me, according to the flesh, I would excuse myself." Nephi does not claim inerrancy in the writing; he claims sacredness of intent. The disclaimer recurs through the book (Morm 8:17; Ether 12:23-27, "thou hast made us mighty in word... but not in writing"). The record is true and the writers are weak — both held at once.
- *The cost of seeing (v20).* "I have workings in the spirit, which doth weary me even that all my joints are weak, for those who are at Jerusalem." Vision drains Nephi bodily (Dan 10:8; Moses 1:10) — grief for the doomed city plus the weight of revelation. The seer pays in his joints (continuing the ch-15 thread: the prophet wounded by what he sees).
