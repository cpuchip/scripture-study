# 1 Nephi 10 — Lehi Names the Messiah, and Nephi Wants to See for Himself

**Binding idea:** What does Lehi prophesy about Christ — and what does Nephi *do* with his father's vision that becomes the engine of the next four chapters?

**Read:** After the dream, Lehi prophesies the future of the Jews: destroyed and carried to Babylon, then returned to possess their land. Then the specifics — "six hundred years from the time that my father left Jerusalem," the Lord will raise up "a Messiah, or, in other words, a Savior of the world." Many prophets have testified of this Redeemer; all mankind are "in a lost and in a fallen state... save they should rely on this Redeemer." A forerunner will "prepare the way," crying "Prepare ye the way of the Lord, and make his paths straight," baptizing in Bethabara, and baptizing the Messiah himself — then bearing record of "the Lamb of God, who should take away the sins of the world." The Messiah will be slain, rise, and manifest by the Holy Ghost to the Gentiles. Lehi compares Israel to an olive tree whose branches are broken off and scattered "upon all the face of the earth" — his own family among them — and afterward gathered, the natural branches grafted back when the Gentiles receive "the fulness of the Gospel." Hearing all this, Nephi "was desirous also that I might see, and hear, and know of these things, by the power of the Holy Ghost, which is the gift of God unto all those who diligently seek him... For he is the same yesterday, today, and forever... he that diligently seeketh shall find... the course of the Lord is one eternal round."

**Thoughts:**
- *The first dated, named prophecy of Christ — startlingly specific.* Lehi gives the timing (600 years, fulfilled at 3 Ne 1:1), the forerunner crying Isaiah 40:3, baptism at Bethabara, and "the Lamb of God, who should take away the sins of the world" — the exact phrase John the Baptist will speak (John 1:29) six centuries later. This is what 1:19 meant by the book "manifested plainly of the coming of a Messiah." The Nephites do not await an unknown deliverer; they know his name, his forerunner, his baptism, his death and resurrection. The plainness is the point.
- *Nephi's desire is the hinge of the whole vision sequence (v17).* He hears his father's vision and does not merely believe it — he wants it *himself*: "I, Nephi, was desirous also that I might see, and hear, and know." This single desire drives chapters 11–14 (Nephi's own panoramic vision). And the doctrine that licenses it is radical: the Holy Ghost "is the gift of God unto *all* those who diligently seek him." Revelation is not the prophet's monopoly; a son may receive his father's vision by seeking. It is the exact inverse of Laman and Lemuel, who (15:8-9) "have not [inquired] of the Lord." The book's whole epistemology sits here: seekers see; the unasking do not.
- *"He that diligently seeketh shall find... one eternal round" (v18-19).* The seek-and-find promise (Matt 7:7) is grounded in God's constancy — "the same yesterday, today, and forever" (Heb 13:8) — so the gift is available "in times of old as in times to come." This is the doctrinal floor of the Restoration itself: modern revelation is possible because the God who spoke anciently has not changed. Nephi states the principle that makes his own — and later Joseph Smith's — visions thinkable.
- *"The way is prepared... from the foundation of the world" (v18) — prepare-a-way, a third deepening.* The thread keeps enlarging: a way to get the plates (3:7), a way across millennia (9:6), and now THE way of salvation "prepared for all men from the foundation of the world, if... they repent and come unto him." Same verb, ascending scale — errand, history, eternity.
- *The olive tree opens the covenant master-narrative (v12-14).* Israel scattered and gathered; the Gentiles receiving the fulness, then the remnants grafted back. Lehi's family is itself a scattered branch — and the footnote ties it to Gen 49:22, Joseph's "branches [that] run over the wall," the very lineage of ch 5. The scattering that exiled them is the same scattering that planted them in a promised land. This sets up Jacob 5 (the full allegory) and 1 Ne 15. The family's displacement is reframed as God's horticulture.
- *The Fall stated early (v6).* "All mankind were in a lost and in a fallen state... save they should rely on this Redeemer." The book's soteriology — the Fall plus the necessity of Christ — is on the table by chapter 10, long before 2 Ne 2 expands it.
