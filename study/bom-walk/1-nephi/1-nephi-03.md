# 1 Nephi 3 — "I Will Go and Do," and the Way That Runs Through Failure

**Binding idea:** What does Nephi actually mean by "the Lord prepares a way" — and what does the chapter show that way looks like in practice?

**Read:** Lehi, by dream-command, sends his sons back to Jerusalem for the brass plates Laban holds — the record of the Jews and their genealogy. The brothers murmur that it is "a hard thing"; Nephi answers (v7): "I will go and do the things which the Lord hath commanded, for I know that the Lord giveth no commandments unto the children of men, save he shall prepare a way for them that they may accomplish the thing which he commandeth them." First attempt: lots fall on Laman, who asks Laban for the plates; Laban calls him a robber and threatens to kill him. The brothers despair and are "about to return"; Nephi swears an oath — "As the Lord liveth, and as we live, we will not go down unto our father... until we have accomplished the thing which the Lord hath commanded." Second attempt: they fetch the family's abandoned gold and silver to buy the plates; Laban "did lust after it," seizes the property, and sends servants to kill them. They flee, losing everything, and hide "in the cavity of a rock." There Laman and Lemuel smite Nephi and Sam with a rod — until an angel stands before them: "Why do ye smite your younger brother...? Know ye not that the Lord hath chosen him to be a ruler over you...? the Lord will deliver Laban into your hands." The angel departs — and Laman and Lemuel immediately murmur again: "How is it possible...? he can slay fifty; then why not us?"

**Thoughts:**
- *v7 is a doctrine, not a mood.* Nephi's willingness rests on an inference about God's character: He commands, therefore He provides a way. Faith here is reasoned, not blind. The footnotes anchor it — Gen 18:14 ("Is any thing too hard for the Lord?"), Philip 4:13 — and Nephi himself restates it at 1 Ne 17:3. It is the most-quoted verse in the book for a reason: a portable theology of obedience.
- *But the chapter refuses the triumphalist reading.* If you stop at v7 you expect the plates to drop into their hands. Instead the chapter is two flat failures — Laban threatens, then robs them of everything and tries to murder them — ending with the brothers beaten and hiding in a rock. The "prepared way" is real, but it runs *through* the lost gold and the cave, not around them. God prepares a way to *accomplish*, not a way that is easy. That distinction is the whole spiritual content of the chapter.
- *Reason-from-God vs. reason-from-the-obstacle.* Nephi reasons from God's power (v7); Laman and Lemuel, even after seeing an angel, reason from Laban's: "he can slay fifty; then why not us?" (v31). Same data, opposite arithmetic. The sharpest line in the chapter is that an *angel* is not enough to change the arithmetic — the unwilling heart re-murmurs within a verse of ministry. Conversion is not an evidence problem.
- *Why the records are worth dying for (v19-20).* Nephi names the stakes: preserve "the language of our fathers" and "the words... spoken by the mouth of all the holy prophets... since the world began." This is the book's theology of scripture — the word must not be lost. The footnote's forward glance (Omni 1:17; Mosiah 1:3) is the proof by contrast: the people of Zarahemla, who carried *no* record, lost their language and denied their Creator. The errand is about preventing exactly that.
- *Nephi as a Joseph-of-Egypt type.* The angel's "the Lord hath chosen him to be a ruler over you" (v29) carries footnote → Gen 41:43: Joseph, the younger brother sold and smitten by his elders, raised to rule and to save the family. Lehi was the new Abraham (ch 2); Nephi is shaping up as the new Joseph — the smiting with a rod (v28) fits the type.
- *Laban's lust is the hinge of his own fall.* His coveting (v25) wins him the property but sets up ch 4 — the same greed will leave him drunk and exposed. The angel's promise ("deliver Laban into your hands") is already loading.
