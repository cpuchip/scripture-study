# Mosiah 28 — The Vilest Sinners Go to Heal the Hatred: The Mission, the Translation, the Chain

*(The sons of Mosiah beg to preach to the Lamanites; Mosiah translates the Jaredite plates with the interpreters; no son will take the throne; the records and interpreters conferred on Alma. ~92 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** The converted persecutors now ask for the hardest mission imaginable — to carry the gospel to the people of the eternal hatred. Three things converge here: a missionary motive aimed straight at the grievance tradition, the translation that becomes the Book of Ether, and a king with no heir. How do they connect?

**Read:** The sons of Mosiah ask leave to "go up to the land of Nephi... to preach... to their brethren, the Lamanites" (v1) — "that perhaps they might bring them to the knowledge of the Lord... and **convince them of the iniquity of their fathers; and that perhaps they might cure them of their hatred** towards the Nephites... that there should be no more contentions" (v2). "They were desirous that salvation should be declared to every creature, for **they could not bear that any human soul should perish**; yea, even the very thoughts that any soul should endure endless torment did cause them to quake and tremble" (v3). "Thus did the Spirit of the Lord work upon them, for they were the **very vilest of sinners**. And the Lord saw fit in his infinite mercy to spare them" (v4). Mosiah "**inquired of the Lord** if he should let his sons go" (v6); the Lord: "Let them go up, for **many shall believe on their words, and they shall have eternal life; and I will deliver thy sons**" (v7). They depart (v9, → Alma 17-26). "Now king Mosiah had **no one to confer the kingdom upon**, for there was not any of his sons who would accept of the kingdom" (v10). He took the records and, "after having **translated**... the records which were on the plates of gold which had been found by the people of Limhi" (v11) — "because of the great anxiety of his people... to know concerning those people who had been destroyed" (v12) — "he translated them by the means of **those two stones which were fastened into the two rims of a bow**" (v13). "These things were prepared from the beginning... for the purpose of interpreting languages" (v14), "kept... by the hand of the Lord, that he should **discover to every creature who should possess the land the iniquities and abominations of his people**" (v15); "whosoever has these things is called **seer**, after the manner of old times" (v16). The account ran "from the time that they were destroyed back to the building of the **great tower**... even from that time back until the **creation of Adam**" (v17); it caused the people "to mourn exceedingly... nevertheless it gave them much knowledge, in the which they did rejoice" (v18); "this account shall be written hereafter" (v19). Mosiah "**conferred** them upon Alma, who was the son of Alma; yea, all the records, and also the interpreters... handing them down... even as they had been handed down from the time that Lehi left Jerusalem" (v20).

**Thoughts:**
- *The missionary motive aimed straight at the grievance (vv1-2).* The sons of Mosiah's stated goal is, almost verbatim, the *healing of the Lamanite grievance tradition*. They go to "**convince them of the iniquity of their fathers**" — to name the false tradition as the wicked thing it is (the same "wicked... traditions of their fathers" Samuel will later describe, Hel 15:7) — and to "**cure them of their hatred**" — to heal the "eternal hatred" that the fathers taught their children (10:17, Jacob 7:24). This is the deliberate going-*to* the people of the inherited lie to re-teach the true story, exactly the cure the grievance-tradition study identified: the lie was taught in from the fathers, so the truth must be taught in by someone willing to carry it. The mission launched here is the answer to the wound diagnosed in chapter 10. (And it will take fourteen years and cost them everything — Alma 17-26.)
- *Could not bear that any soul should perish (v3) — the missionary impulse defined.* The deeper motive beneath the strategy: "they could not bear that any human soul should perish; yea, even the very thoughts that any soul should endure endless torment did cause them to quake and tremble." This is the seed sown in chapter 25 — the people's "anguish for the welfare of [the Lamanites'] souls" (25:11) — now grown into a consuming purpose. Missionary zeal at its root is not duty or conquest; it is the inability to bear the thought of a soul lost. The worth of souls (D&C 18:10) felt so acutely that comfort at home becomes impossible while others perish.
- *The vilest sinners become the most zealous missionaries (v4).* The record names it plainly: "thus did the Spirit work upon them, for they were the **very vilest of sinners**." The men most desperate to save others are the ones who were themselves nearly lost — "the Lord saw fit in his infinite mercy to spare them," and the spared cannot bear to see anyone else go down. This is the Paul pattern and the Luke 7:47 principle: the one forgiven much loves much. The depth of their former sin became the depth of their missionary fire. A man snatched from the burning (27:28) spends the rest of his life pulling others out. Redemption does not just rescue; it *conscripts* — and it often makes the worst former sinner the most relentless savior of others.
- *Mosiah inquires, and the promise (vv6-7).* Like Alma facing the unprecedented case (26:13), Mosiah does not decide his sons' fate alone — he "inquired of the Lord." And the Lord's answer is a fourfold promise that will govern the entire Alma 17-26 narrative: many will believe, those sons will have eternal life, and "**I will deliver thy sons out of the hands of the Lamanites**." Every near-death and rescue in their fourteen-year mission is the keeping of this one promise. The father sends his sons into mortal danger only after God guarantees their preservation — the answer, again, to a parent's inquiry about his children (cf 27:14, the father's prayers).
- *The Jaredite plates translated — the Book of Ether is born (vv11-19).* The twenty-four gold plates that Limhi's lost party found (ch 8) and that came to Mosiah with the escape (ch 22) are now *translated* by the interpreters — "two stones... fastened into the two rims of a bow" (v13; the Urim and Thummim, the same instrument by which Joseph Smith would later translate, JS-H 1:35). The record runs from the Jaredites' destruction "back to the building of the great tower" (Babel) and "back until the creation of Adam" — this is the textual origin of the **Book of Ether**, which Moroni will later abridge ("this account shall be written hereafter," v19). And the *purpose* is the one Limhi sought (8:12): the record exists "that he should discover... the iniquities and abominations of his people" — a destroyed people's record preserved as a *warning* to whoever next possesses the land. The people respond rightly — they "mourn exceedingly" at the destruction and "rejoice" in the knowledge (v18). A warning read in time produces both grief and gratitude.
- *No heir, and the records pass to the convert (vv10, 20).* Two endings converge that set up the book's climax. Mosiah "had no one to confer the kingdom upon, for there was not any of his sons who would accept of the kingdom" — the four heirs *chose the mission over the throne*, preferring to preach to enemies rather than rule at home. This refusal of the crown (the same instinct as Alma's, 23:7) is what forces the great question of chapter 29: if not a king's son, then what? And the sacred records and interpreters pass not to a political heir but to **Alma the younger** — the former persecutor, now the keeper of the chain that runs "from the time that Lehi left Jerusalem." The records follow the *prophetic* line, not the royal one — another deliberate severing of church from state, and a quiet vindication: the man who once sought to destroy the church is entrusted with its very memory.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off (the grievance healing is covered in the **mos10 spin-off**, whose "cure" arc *launches here*). The chapter's heart: **the mission to heal the grievance** — the sons go to "convince them of the iniquity of their fathers" and "**cure them of their hatred**" (v2), the deliberate going-*to* the people of the inherited lie to re-teach the true story (the answer to ch 10's wound). Beneath the strategy is the missionary impulse defined: **they could not bear that any soul should perish** (v3, the worth of souls grown from 25:11's anguish) — and the **vilest of sinners become the most zealous missionaries** (v4): the snatched spend their lives pulling others out (Luke 7:47; the Paul pattern). Mosiah leads by **inquiry** and receives the **deliverance promise** that governs all of Alma 17-26 (v7). Textually, **the Jaredite plates are translated into the origin of the Book of Ether** (vv11-19 — creation → Babel → destruction, by the interpreters; a record-as-warning), confirming the **seer doctrine** of ch 8. And two endings set up the climax: **no son will take the throne** (mission over crown, forcing the reign-of-judges question) and **the records pass to Alma** the convert — the *prophetic* line, not the royal one. Next: Mosiah 29 (Mosiah's masterstroke — the abolition of the monarchy and the institution of the reign of judges, chosen "by the voice of the people"; "it is not common that the voice of the people desireth anything contrary to that which is right"; the end of the book of Mosiah).
