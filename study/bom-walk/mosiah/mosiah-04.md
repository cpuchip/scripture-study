# Mosiah 4 — "Are We Not All Beggars?": Retaining the Mercy You Extend

*(Benjamin's sermon, day 1 close — the people receive a remission, then learn to retain it. Mini-study. Contains Mosiah 4:27, this project's pacing covenant.)*

**Binding idea:** The people beg God for mercy and receive it. Benjamin's next move is the surprise: the way to *keep* that mercy is to extend it — and the ground of charity is that "are we not all beggars?" What does it mean to retain a remission rather than merely receive one?

**Read:** The people had fallen to the earth, "viewed themselves in their own carnal state, even less than the dust of the earth" (v2), and cried, "O have mercy, and apply the atoning blood of Christ" (v2); the Spirit came and they "received a remission of their sins, and... peace of conscience" (v3). Benjamin teaches them to keep it: "always retain in remembrance, the greatness of God, and your own nothingness, and his goodness and long-suffering" (v11); do this and "ye shall always rejoice, and be filled with the love of God, and **always retain a remission of your sins**" (v12). The fruit: "ye will not have a mind to injure one another" (v13); "ye will not suffer that the beggar putteth up his petition to you in vain" (v16). Against the excuse "the man has brought upon himself his misery... I will stay my hand" (v17): "**are we not all beggars? Do we not all depend upon the same Being, even God, for all the substance which we have?**" (v19) — "ye have been... begging for a remission of your sins. And has he suffered that ye have begged in vain? Nay" (v20). The withheld substance "doth not belong to you but to God" (v22). To the poor who cannot give: "**I give not because I have not, but if I had I would give**" — and "ye remain guiltless" (v24). "**For the sake of retaining a remission of your sins from day to day**... impart of your substance to the poor... feeding the hungry, clothing the naked, visiting the sick" (v26). "**And see that all these things are done in wisdom and order; for it is not requisite that a man should run faster than he has strength**" (v27). Watch "your thoughts, and your words, and your deeds... O man, remember, and perish not" (v30).

**Thoughts:**
- *"Are we not all beggars?" (v19) — the leveling that makes charity unavoidable.* Benjamin disarms the oldest excuse for withholding — "he brought it on himself" — not with an argument about the poor man's worthiness but by collapsing the distance between giver and beggar. You just spent the chapter "begging for a remission of your sins" (v20), and God did not make you beg in vain. You depend on God for "all the substance which we have" (v19) — the food, the silver, the breath (the unprofitable-servant logic of ch 2). So the beggar at your door is not beneath you; he is *you*, at God's door. To despise him is to forget what you are. The substance you would withhold "doth not belong to you but to God" (v22) anyway. Charity is not generosity from the secure; it is one beggar sharing with another what neither of them owns.
- *Retaining a remission, not just receiving one (vv11-12, 26).* The chapter's structural insight: the remission received in v3 is not a one-time transaction but a state to be *retained* "from day to day" (v26). And the means of retaining are two — inward (remember your nothingness + God's goodness, humble yourself, call daily, v11) and outward (impart to the poor, v26). **Mercy is kept by mercy extended.** The remission you hold onto is the remission you pass along; stop giving it outward and you stop keeping it inward. This is the gospel's anti-stagnation principle (cf 2 Ne 31:19, "is all done? Nay") applied to forgiveness: grace is a current, not a deposit — it has to keep moving through you or it ceases to be yours.
- *Mosiah 4:27 — this project's pacing covenant.* "See that all these things are done in **wisdom and order; for it is not requisite that a man should run faster than he has strength.** And again, it is expedient that he should be diligent." This is the verse the workspace covenant invokes for pacing (paired with D&C 10:4) — the standing caution against running faster than strength. And it is balanced, not slack: *diligent* "that thereby he might win the prize," yet *ordered* so as not to overrun. Benjamin sets it right after the sweeping charge to feed the hungry and clothe the naked — precisely where zeal could burn a person out. The command to give generously comes with the command to pace; consecration without order is its own kind of failure. (A live word for a long day's work: diligence and order, not diligence alone.)
- *The poor's willingness counts (vv24-25).* Benjamin will not let "I have nothing to give" become guilt: the poor man who says in his heart "I would give if I had" "remain[s] guiltless." The charity God weighs is the disposition, not the dollar amount — which means the duty to give is universal (everyone can will to give) while the capacity is not. The widow's mite is already implied.
- *Belief that does (v10).* "If you believe all these things see that ye do them." Benjamin's belief is not assent; it is the kind that acts. The whole sermon turns belief immediately into doing — and the doing is, above all, caring for the poor.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The chapter's two pillars: **"are we not all beggars?"** (v19 — charity grounded in the giver's own beggary before God) and **retaining a remission "from day to day"** by extending mercy to the poor (vv12, 26) — *mercy is kept by mercy extended*, grace a current not a deposit. **Project connection surfaced: Mosiah 4:27 ("it is not requisite that a man should run faster than he has strength") is this workspace's pacing covenant** — and note that Benjamin places it *immediately after* the sweeping charge to feed the hungry and clothe the naked, where zeal could burn one out: diligence *and* order.
