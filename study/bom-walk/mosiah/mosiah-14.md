# Mosiah 14 — Isaiah 53 on Trial: The Suffering Servant as Abinadi's Evidence

*(Abinadi quotes Isaiah 53 entire before Noah's priests — the suffering servant. Isaiah-block protocol: full exegesis + Nephite framing + four likening questions + KJV-variant comparison. ~148 BC. Doctrinal synthesis in spin-off `mos13-16_abinadi-christology`.)*

**Binding idea:** Having said "salvation comes only by the Atonement God himself shall make" (13:28), Abinadi does not argue — he *reads*. He plants Isaiah 53 in the middle of his defense, the whole suffering-servant chapter, as the prophetic proof that the God who comes down will be the One despised, wounded, and slain. Why this text, here, in this courtroom?

**Read (the chapter is Isaiah 53 quoted whole):** "Who hath believed our report, and to whom is the arm of the Lord revealed?" (v1). "He shall grow up before him as a tender plant, and as a root out of dry ground... no beauty that we should desire him" (v2). "He is **despised and rejected of men; a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief**... we esteemed him not" (v3). "Surely he has borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows; yet we did esteem him stricken, smitten of God, and afflicted" (v4). "**But he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities; the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed**" (v5). "**All we, like sheep, have gone astray**... and the Lord hath laid on him the iniquities of us all" (v6). "He was oppressed, and he was afflicted, **yet he opened not his mouth**; he is brought as a lamb to the slaughter" (v7). "He was taken from prison and from judgment; and **who shall declare his generation?**... for the transgressions of my people was he stricken" (v8). "He made his grave with the wicked, and with the rich in his death; because he had done no evil, neither was any deceit in his mouth" (v9). "**Yet it pleased the Lord to bruise him**... when thou shalt make his soul an offering for sin he shall **see his seed**, he shall prolong his days" (v10). "He shall see the travail of his soul, and shall be satisfied; by his knowledge shall my righteous servant justify many; for he shall bear their iniquities" (v11). "**He hath poured out his soul unto death; and he was numbered with the transgressors; and he bore the sins of many, and made intercession for the transgressors**" (v12).

**Exegesis (the servant's arc):** Isaiah's fourth servant song moves in a single descending-then-rising arc. It opens with *disbelief* — "who hath believed our report?" (v1) — the report being so contrary to expectation that the arm of the Lord is revealed only to those who will see it. The servant comes *without* the markings of power: a tender plant from dry ground, "no form nor comeliness," nothing to make men desire him (v2). He is actively *rejected* — despised, a man of sorrows, a face men turn away from (v3). Then the hinge the whole chapter turns on, the substitution: the suffering men assumed was God's judgment *on him* ("we did esteem him stricken, smitten of God," v4) was in fact judgment borne *for them* — "he was wounded for **our** transgressions... with his stripes **we** are healed" (v5). The logic is vicarious exchange: we strayed, each to his own way, and "the Lord hath laid on him the iniquities of us all" (v6). He bears it *silently* — "he opened not his mouth," the lamb led to slaughter that does not cry out (v7) — and is killed unjustly, "cut off out of the land of the living," buried among the wicked and the rich though "he had done no evil" (vv8-9). Then the turn: this death "pleased the Lord" not as cruelty but as *offering* — "when thou shalt make his soul an offering for sin he shall **see his seed**" (v10). The death is fruitful; out of it comes posterity, satisfaction, the justification of "many" (v11), the dividing of spoil (v12). The servant who poured out his soul unto death and bore "the sins of many" *gains* by it — he is "numbered with the transgressors" precisely so he can make "intercession for the transgressors" (v12). Humiliation is the mechanism of exaltation; the offering is the means of the seed.

**KJV-variant comparison (Mosiah 14 vs Isaiah 53):** The variants are minor in wording but show one consistent *theological tilt* — Mosiah's text **broadens** both the sin borne and the servant's innocence:

**The meta-pattern:** In the 2 Nephi Isaiah block, the BoM variants clustered in *judgment* chapters and tilted toward *mercy* (softening doom). Here, in the *atonement* chapter, the tilt runs the same God-ward direction by a different mechanism: the variants **magnify the Atonement's scope**. Three times Mosiah pluralizes the sin the servant carries — "iniquit**ies** of us all" (v6), "transgress**ions** of my people" (v8), "**sins** of many" (v12) — widening the burden from a category to a totality. And v9 generalizes the servant's innocence from "no violence" (one kind of wrong) to "no **evil**" (all wrong). Where judgment-chapter variants made mercy wider, atonement-chapter variants make both the sin borne and the bearer's purity *more complete*. The Book of Mormon text consistently reads Isaiah in the direction of a larger redemption.

**Nephite framing — why Abinadi plants Isaiah 53 here:** The placement is the argument. Abinadi has just asserted (ch 13) that the law cannot save and that "all the prophets" testified the God who comes down would be "oppressed and afflicted" (13:35). The priests challenged him with Isaiah's *triumphant* text — "how beautiful upon the mountains... publisheth peace" (12:21). Abinadi answers with Isaiah's *suffering* text — the same prophet, the other face of the same Messiah. He is showing the priests that the God of glory they expect and the man of sorrows they would never desire (v2) are *one*; that "Thy God reigneth" (the text they quoted) and "he was numbered with the transgressors" (the text he quotes) describe the same person. Isaiah 53 is Abinadi's evidence that the condescension is not Abinadi's invention but the burden of the whole prophetic record. And then — crucial — he does not leave Isaiah 53 to speak for itself; in the next chapter he *expounds* it (15:1-13), telling the priests who the servant is, who "his seed" is, and how "who shall declare his generation?" (v8) is answered. He quotes the text, then teaches it, the way a prophet should and the way these priests never did (12:25-27).

**The four likening questions:**
1. *Why did Abinadi include it?* As courtroom evidence. The priests bet salvation on the law; Abinadi proves from their own scriptures that salvation was always meant to come through a *suffering, slain* servant whom the law only foreshadowed. Isaiah 53 is the prophetic ground under 13:28.
2. *Was it for last-dispensation readers?* Doubly. It was Isaiah's word to Israel, Abinadi's word to Noah's court — and Mormon's word to us, preserved because it shows the clearest pre-Christian portrait of the vicarious Atonement. The disbelief it opens with ("who hath believed our report?") is perennial; every generation must decide whether to believe a report this contrary to the world's idea of power.
3. *How does it apply to us?* The substitution is personal: *our* transgressions, *our* iniquities, *us* who strayed like sheep "every one to his own way" (v6). The chapter does not let the reader stand among the spectators who "esteemed him not"; it puts the reader among the strayed for whom the stripes were borne. To read Isaiah 53 rightly is to find your own sin in the "us all" the Lord laid on him.
4. *How do we read it better through Abinadi's framing?* Abinadi reads it Christologically and *vicariously* — not as a poem about an anonymous sufferer but as the God who comes down (15:1) bearing the sins of the people who would kill him. And he reads "he shall see his seed" (v10) as a promise of *spiritual posterity* (15:10-13) — the death is fruitful. Through Abinadi, Isaiah 53 stops being a lament and becomes the mechanism of the new birth (cf 5:7): the servant pours out his soul, and gains a family.
