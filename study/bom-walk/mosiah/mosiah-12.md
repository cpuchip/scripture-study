# Mosiah 12 — The Prisoner Who Tries the Court: Abinadi Before Noah's Priests

*(Abinadi returns in disguise; the harder prophecy; the trial; the priests' Isaiah 52 trap; the pivot to the law of Moses. ~148 BC. Mini-study — doctrinal core 13-15 flagged for spin-off.)*

**Binding idea:** Two years after his first escape, Abinadi walks back into a court rigged to kill him — and somehow it is the priests, not the prophet, who end up exposed. How does the accused become the judge? And why does Abinadi refuse to answer the one scripture they throw at him?

**Read:** "After the space of two years... Abinadi came among them **in disguise, that they knew him not**" (v1) and prophesied harder: this generation "shall be brought into bondage, and shall be smitten on the cheek... and the vultures of the air, and the dogs... shall devour their flesh" (v2); "the **life of king Noah shall be valued even as a garment in a hot furnace**" (v3); famine, pestilence (v4), "burdens lashed upon their backs... driven before like a dumb ass" (v5), hail, east wind, insects (v6). "Except they repent I will utterly destroy them... yet **they shall leave a record behind them, and I will preserve them for other nations... that I may discover the abominations of this people**" (v8). Arrested and hauled before Noah, accused of prophesying "evil concerning thy people" and "thy life" (vv9-12). The priests' defense: "we are **guiltless**, and thou, O king, hast not sinned; therefore, this man has **lied**" (v14); "we are strong, we shall not come into bondage... thou hast prospered... and **thou shalt also prosper**" (v15); "we deliver him into thy hands" (v16). Noah imprisons him and convenes a council. They question him "that they might **cross him**, that thereby they might have wherewith to accuse him; but he answered them boldly, and **withstood all their questions**... and did **confound them in all their words**" (v19). One priest quotes Isaiah: "**How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings; that publisheth peace**... that saith unto Zion, Thy God reigneth" (vv21-24). Abinadi turns it: "**Are you priests, and pretend to teach this people**... and yet desire to know of me what these things mean?" (v25) — "wo be unto you for **perverting the ways of the Lord! For if ye understand these things ye have not taught them**" (v26); "ye have **not applied your hearts to understanding**" (v27). "We teach the law of Moses" (v28). "**If ye teach the law of Moses why do ye not keep it?** Why do ye set your hearts upon riches? Why do ye commit whoredoms..." (v29). "**Ye know that I speak the truth; and you ought to tremble before God**" (v30). "**Doth salvation come by the law of Moses? What say ye?**" (v31). They answer yes (v32). Abinadi begins the Decalogue — "I am the Lord thy God, who hath brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the **house of bondage**" (v34), "no other God before me" (v35), "no graven image" (v36) — then: "**Have ye done all this? I say unto you, Nay, ye have not. And have ye taught this people that they should do all these things? Nay, ye have not**" (v37).

**Thoughts:**
- *The prisoner tries the court (vv19, 25-27).* Abinadi is the defendant in a proceeding designed to manufacture a charge — they question him "that they might cross him, that thereby they might have wherewith to accuse him" (the same maneuver the scribes ran on Jesus, John 8:6). But the roles invert: he "withstood all their questions... and did confound them in all their words." When they produce their Isaiah text expecting a trap, he turns it into an indictment of *them*: "Are you priests, and pretend to teach this people... and yet desire to know of me what these things mean?" The judged becomes the judge; the accused becomes the prosecutor. This is the prophet-before-the-tribunal pattern — Christ before the Sanhedrin, Paul before Agrippa, Joseph Smith before his accusers — the truth-teller on trial who, by simply telling the truth, puts the court on trial. A rigged proceeding cannot survive a witness who will not flinch.
- *The deferred answer (vv20-24, resolved in ch 15).* The priests quote Isaiah 52:7-10 — "How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings; that publisheth peace" — almost certainly as a gotcha: how dare you preach destruction when scripture promises peace and "Thy God reigneth"? Abinadi does *not* answer the question here. He defers it, builds the foundation first — the law cannot save without Christ (ch 13), the condescension Christology (ch 15) — and only then, in 15:14-18, returns to "how beautiful are the feet" and reveals who the good-tidings-bringer actually is: Christ, and those who publish his salvation. The structure is deliberate and patient. He will not let his enemies set the order of the argument; he refuses the trap, lays the groundwork, and then resolves on his own terms the very text they used against him. (This is why 13-15 reads as one sustained discourse — it is a single argument that bends back to close the loop the priests opened in v21.)
- *"Ye know that I speak the truth" (v30).* The hinge of the confrontation. Abinadi does not accuse the priests of ignorance — he accuses them of *suppression*: "ye know that I speak the truth; and you ought to tremble before God." Their wickedness is not a failure to recognize the truth; it is the deliberate rejection of a truth they fully recognize. This is the deepest indictment in the chapter, and the most uncomfortable: the difference between the priests and the prophet is not knowledge but will. They know. Noah's "who is the Lord?" (11:27) was never real ignorance; it was performance. The condemnation falls on those who see and refuse (cf 3:12, the knowing rebel).
- *The false prosperity gospel (v15).* The priests' theology in one line: "we are strong, we shall not come into bondage... thou hast prospered... and thou shalt also prosper." They invert the prosperity covenant — they read present prosperity as proof of divine approval, with no reference to righteousness. It is "all is well in Zion" (2 Ne 28:21) in its native habitat: the comfortable assume comfort is a verdict. Their prosperity, of course, came from taxing the people to fund vice — so the very evidence they cite for God's favor is the evidence of their guilt. Prosperity unhooked from obedience becomes the wicked man's proof-text.
- *The teachers who neither keep nor teach (vv28-37).* Abinadi's strategy is a pincer. The priests claim two things — "we teach the law of Moses" and "salvation comes by the law of Moses." He will dismantle both. First the hypocrisy: "if ye teach the law of Moses why do ye not keep it?" (v29) — and he proves it by reciting the first commandments (no other gods, no graven images) and asking, "Have ye done all this? Nay. And have ye taught this people that they should? Nay" (v37). They neither keep the law nor teach it; they profess a law they have abandoned. Then, having exposed the hypocrisy, he sets up the deeper point for ch 13: even the law *kept* does not save by itself — it points beyond itself to Christ (13:27-32). He starts with the Ten Commandments not to affirm the priests' law-religion but to show they have failed even the floor of it, before raising the ceiling they have never seen.
- *Even the destruction serves the record (v8).* A quiet, arresting line: God will preserve a record of this people "for other nations... that I may discover the abominations of this people to other nations." The record is not only a treasury of the righteous; it is a witness *against* the wicked, a preserved testimony of what abomination cost. Even in pronouncing utter destruction, God is keeping evidence — so that a later people might read these abominations and not repeat them. The warning function of the whole Book of Mormon, stated inside the doom itself.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off yet — but **the Abinadi discourse of 13-15 is one sustained argument** that this chapter opens (the priests' Isaiah 52:7 trap, deferred to 15:14-18) and the doctrinal high-point of the Zeniff record. **Decision point at ch 13: strongly consider a spin-off covering 13-15** (the law→Christ argument + the condescension Christology of 15 + the Isaiah-52 resolution). The chapter itself turns on **the prisoner trying the court** — Abinadi confounds the cross-examiners and flips their trap into an indictment ("Are you priests... and yet desire to know of me what these things mean?", v25). Its sharpest line is **"ye know that I speak the truth"** (v30) — their evil is suppression, not ignorance, the difference from the prophet being *will*, not knowledge. Plus the **false prosperity gospel** (v15 — comfort read as a verdict), the **teachers who neither keep nor teach** the law (vv28-37), and the quiet note that even destruction preserves a **record as witness against abomination** (v8).
