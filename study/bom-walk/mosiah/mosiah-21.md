# Mosiah 21 — Slow to Hear, but Hearing: The Burden Eased Before It Is Removed

*(The bondage deepens; three failed rebellions; the people humble themselves; God is "slow to hear" but eases the burden; Ammon arrives — the frame rejoined; Limhi's people covenant but cannot be baptized for lack of authority. ~122–121 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Abinadi prophesied that when this people cried, God would be "slow to hear" (11:24). This chapter is where that lands — and it lands with a nuance that may be the most pastorally important verse in Mosiah. What does it mean that God is "slow to hear," yet *hears*, yet does not deliver — but eases the burden?

**Read:** Bound by the oath not to slay them, the Lamanites instead "would **smite them on their cheeks**... and began to put **heavy burdens upon their backs, and drive them as they would a dumb ass**" (v3) — "all this was done that **the word of the Lord might be fulfilled**" (v4); surrounded, "there was no way that they could deliver themselves" (v5). The people murmur and demand battle; Limhi yields; they are beaten (vv6-8). Great mourning, "the widow mourning for her husband" (v9); the widows' cries stir a second battle, driven back (v11); "they went again even the **third time**," same result (v12). Then: "they did **humble themselves even to the dust**, subjecting themselves to the yoke of bondage" (v13); "they did humble themselves even in the **depths of humility**; and they did cry mightily to God... all the day long" (v14). "And now **the Lord was slow to hear their cry because of their iniquities; nevertheless the Lord did hear their cries, and began to soften the hearts of the Lamanites that they began to ease their burdens; yet the Lord did not see fit to deliver them out of bondage**" (v15). They "prosper by degrees" (v16); Limhi commands "that every man should **impart to the support of the widows** and their children" (v17). Then **Ammon** arrives; Limhi, supposing them priests of Noah, imprisons them, then finds they are from Zarahemla and "was filled with exceedingly great joy" (vv22-24). The lost search party had found the bone-covered land and brought "a record... engraven on plates of ore" (vv25-27); Limhi rejoices that Mosiah can interpret it (v28). Ammon's people sorrow over the slain, Noah's sins, "the death of Abinadi," and "the departure of Alma" (vv29-30). Limhi and many "entered into a covenant with God to serve him" (v32); "**desirous to be baptized; but there was none in the land that had authority from God. And Ammon declined doing this thing, considering himself an unworthy servant**" (v33); so "they did not at that time form themselves into a church, **waiting upon the Spirit of the Lord**" (v34). "All the study of Ammon... and king Limhi and his people, was to **deliver themselves**... from bondage" (v36).

**Thoughts:**
- *"Slow to hear, nevertheless did hear" — God's slow yes (v15).* This is the doctrinal heart of the chapter and one of the most important verses in Mosiah for anyone praying out of consequences they brought on themselves. Abinadi's prophecy (11:24, "I will be slow to hear their cries") is fulfilled — "the Lord was slow to hear their cry **because of their iniquities**." But the verse refuses to stop at "no." It moves through four beats: God is *slow* (the consequence is real), "**nevertheless** the Lord did hear" (mercy persists), He "began to **soften the hearts of the Lamanites that they began to ease their burdens**" (partial relief comes), "**yet** the Lord did not see fit to deliver them out of bondage" (full deliverance is withheld). This is not a flat refusal and not an instant rescue. It is the *eased burden before the removed burden* — God answering within the bondage before He answers the bondage. A people reaping what they sowed are not abandoned; they are heard slowly, helped partly, and carried inside the consequence even while the consequence remains. The burden eased is itself an answer to prayer — a real mercy short of rescue. (For the one who prays from a pit of their own digging, this is the shape of grace: not always out, but always *with*, and often lighter before it is over.)
- *Three rebellions, then the dust (vv6-14).* The order matters. The people try to fight their way out **three times** — by their own strength, by the arm of flesh — and are beaten back each time, before they "humble themselves even to the dust... in the depths of humility" and cry to God. Self-deliverance had to be exhausted before genuine submission could come. They had to fail at every version of saving themselves before they would actually cry out. The humbling that finally moves heaven is the one that comes *after* the strength of men is spent — and it comes "even to the dust," not as a posture but as the truth of their position. (Deliverance begins where self-deliverance ends.)
- *The deliverance pair — Limhi vs. Alma (set up here).* The record is deliberately constructing a contrast it will complete in chapters 23-24. Limhi's people, who had to be humbled the *hard* way (three defeats, then the dust), receive a *delayed, partial* mercy and will eventually escape by their own *stratagem* (ch 22). Alma's people, who were already humble, will be delivered *miraculously and swiftly* — even their burdens made supernaturally light (24:14-15). Two captive peoples, two modes of deliverance, each calibrated to where the people's hearts already were. The same God who is "slow to hear" the proud-then-humbled is quick to ease the burdens of the already-submissive. Both are delivered; the *manner* is the lesson.
- *Widow-care under bondage (v17).* Even crushed and oppressed, the people do not abandon their most vulnerable: Limhi "commanded that every man should impart to the support of the widows and their children." This is Benjamin's "impart of your substance to the poor" (4:26) lived out by a people in chains — and the widows are many "because of the greatness of their number that had been slain" in the failed rebellions. The covenant community carries its weakest even while the whole community is being carried as a burden itself. Bondage did not dissolve their care for each other; it concentrated it.
- *The frame rejoined (vv22-28).* Ammon's arrival is chapter 7 retold from the inside — the imprisonment, the joy at finding brethren from Zarahemla, the twenty-four plates. The back-fill that began in chapter 9 (Zeniff's "I, Zeniff") has now circled all the way back to the frame's present. The two ends of the embedded record meet here; from this point the story moves forward again toward the escape.
- *Ammon will not baptize without authority (vv33-34).* A quiet but pointed detail. Limhi's people want baptism, "but there was none in the land that had authority from God. And Ammon declined... considering himself an unworthy servant." Ammon is faithful, the leader of the rescue party, clearly a believer — and he will *not* presume to baptize without authority he does not have. The covenant of the heart is made now (v32); the ordinance *waits* for authority (fulfilled at Zarahemla, 25:18). The people "prolong the time" rather than do it wrongly — "waiting upon the Spirit of the Lord." It is a model of doing it *right* over doing it *now*: the desire is real, the willingness total, but the ordinance is not improvised. Authority is not a technicality to be waved past in zeal; even a good man waits for it.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The **doctrinal heart is v15** — God's *slow yes*. Abinadi's "slow to hear" (11:24) is fulfilled, but the verse moves through four beats: slow (the consequence is real), "**nevertheless** the Lord did hear," "began to **soften the hearts of the Lamanites that they began to ease their burdens**," "**yet**... did not see fit to deliver them." The **burden is eased before it is removed** — God answers *within* the bondage before He answers the bondage; for anyone praying out of a pit of their own digging, this is the shape of grace: not always out, but always *with*, and often lighter before it is over. The people reach it only after **three failed rebellions** exhaust self-deliverance and they humble themselves "even to the dust" (vv6-14) — *deliverance begins where self-deliverance ends*. This sets up the deliberate **deliverance pair**: Limhi (humbled the hard way → eased + escape by stratagem, ch 22) vs. Alma (already humble → swift miracle, 24) — same God, manner calibrated to the heart. Plus **widow-care under bondage** (v17, Benjamin's 4:26 in chains), the **embedded frame rejoined** (Ammon's arrival = ch 7 from inside), and the **covenant made but baptism withheld for lack of authority** — Ammon, faithful, will not improvise the ordinance (vv33-34): do it right over do it now.
