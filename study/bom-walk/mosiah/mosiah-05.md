# Mosiah 5 — The Mighty Change, and the Name Written in the Heart

*(Benjamin's sermon, culmination — the people's covenant response. The mighty change of heart; spiritually begotten as children of Christ; taking + retaining the name. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** Benjamin asks whether the people believe — and their answer is not "yes" but a description of what has happened *to* them: a mighty change, no more disposition to do evil. From that change comes a covenant, a new parentage, and a name. What is the relation between the change, the name, and *keeping* the name?

**Read:** Benjamin sends to learn if the people believe (v1). "They all cried with one voice... we believe all the words... and also, we know of their surety and truth, because of the Spirit of the Lord Omnipotent, which has **wrought a mighty change in us, or in our hearts, that we have no more disposition to do evil, but to do good continually**" (v2). They have "great views of that which is to come" (v3); "we are willing to **enter into a covenant** with our God to do his will... all the remainder of our days" (v5). Benjamin: "the covenant which ye have made is a righteous covenant" (v6) — "because of the covenant... ye shall be called the **children of Christ, his sons, and his daughters; for behold, this day he hath spiritually begotten you**... ye are **born of him**" (v7). "Under this head ye are made free, and there is no other head... no other name given whereby salvation cometh; therefore... **take upon you the name of Christ**" (v8); whoever does "shall be found at the right hand of God" (v9), and whoever does not "must be called by some other name; therefore, he findeth himself on the left hand" (v10). "This is the name... **that never should be blotted out, except it be through transgression**" (v11) — "**retain the name written always in your hearts**" (v12). "For **how knoweth a man the master whom he has not served, and who is a stranger unto him, and is far from the thoughts and intents of his heart?**" (v13). Therefore "be **steadfast and immovable, always abounding in good works, that Christ, the Lord God Omnipotent, may seal you his**" (v15).

**Thoughts:**
- *The mighty change — the desire itself reoriented (v2).* Asked if they believe, the people answer with an experience: the Spirit "has wrought a mighty change in us, or in our hearts, that we have **no more disposition to do evil, but to do good continually**." This is the fruit of Mosiah 3:19's yielding, now realized — not merely forgiveness of past sins but the transformation of *wanting*. The natural man's appetites are not just restrained; the disposition is replaced. This is sanctification (Ezek 36:26's new heart; Alma 5:14's "mighty change"), and Benjamin's people are the Book of Mormon's first collective instance of it. Note the epistemology: they *know* the doctrine is true "**because of** the Spirit... which has wrought a mighty change" — the change is itself the witness. They don't believe and then change; the change is the evidence they cite for believing. Transformation is its own proof.
- *Spiritually begotten — Christ becomes the father (v7).* The covenant produces a new parentage: "this day he hath spiritually begotten you... ye are born of him and have become his sons and his daughters." Christ, who in the angel's prophecy is "the Father of heaven and earth" (3:8), now becomes their father by the new birth. Abinadi will give this its fullest statement (Mosiah 15:10-13 — those who hearken are "his seed"): the suffering Servant who has no posterity of the body gains a spiritual posterity through the Atonement. To be born again is, precisely, to be begotten of Christ — to take his nature as a child takes a father's.
- *Taking the name — and the master-servant test (vv8-13).* The new birth comes with a new name: "take upon you the name of Christ." But the name is not a label affixed once; it is "written... in your hearts" (v12) and can be "blotted out... through transgression" (v11). Then the sharpest line: "how knoweth a man the master whom he has not served... and is far from the thoughts and intents of his heart?" (v13). You cannot bear a master's name if you've never served him — the name is known *by service*, not by profession. This is the exact inverse of "I never knew you; depart from me" (Matt 7:23): the name on the lips means nothing without the service that makes the master known. Bearing Christ's name is a relationship earned into knowledge, not a claim asserted.
- *The retain-thread binds the whole sermon.* Chapter 4: "**retain** a remission of your sins" (4:12, 26). Chapter 5: "**retain** the name written always in your hearts" (5:12). Benjamin's sermon's deep structure is *retention* — what God freely gives (a remission, a name, a new heart) must be *kept* by ongoing covenant living. Grace initiates; discipleship retains. The remission is kept by extending mercy to the poor; the name is kept by serving the Master whose name it is. Both can be lost not by a single failure but by ceasing to live the covenant that holds them. (This is why the sermon ends on "steadfast and immovable" — retention is the whole point.)
- *Sealed his (v15).* The end is not just being saved but being "sealed his" — Christ claiming the steadfast as his own (the making-sure of 2 Pet 1:10; D&C 88:4). The name taken in covenant becomes the name by which he claims you at the right hand. Identity given, retained, and finally ratified.
