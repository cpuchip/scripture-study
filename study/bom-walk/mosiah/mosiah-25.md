# Mosiah 25 — One People from Many: The Records Read, the Name Chosen

*(The great gathering in Zarahemla — Mosiah reads all the records; the peoples are made one; the children of Amulon choose a new name; Alma baptizes Limhi's people and organizes the church. ~120 BC. Mini-study.)*

**Binding idea:** All the scattered threads of Mosiah converge in one assembly. Mosiah reads the records aloud, and a people is formed by hearing its own story. What does the reading do to them — and what does it mean that the children of the wicked priests refuse their fathers' name?

**Read:** Mosiah gathers all the people (v1). The people of Zarahemla (descendants of **Mulek**) outnumber the Nephites, and the Lamanites outnumber both — "they were not half so numerous" (vv2-3); they assemble "in two bodies" (v4). Mosiah "did read... the records of Zeniff" and "the account of Alma and his brethren, and all their afflictions" (vv5-6). The people "were struck with wonder and amazement" (v7): "**filled with exceedingly great joy**" at those delivered (v8), "**filled with sorrow, and even shed many tears**" for the slain (v9), "raise their voices and give thanks" for God's goodness to Alma (v10), and "**filled with pain and anguish for the welfare of their souls**" when they thought on the Lamanites "of their sinful and polluted state" (v11). "The **children of Amulon**... were displeased with the conduct of their fathers, and they **would no longer be called by the names of their fathers**, therefore they took upon themselves the name of Nephi" (v12). All the people of Zarahemla "were numbered with the Nephites" (v13). Mosiah has Alma speak; Alma preaches "repentance and faith on the Lord," going "from one body to another" (vv14-15), exhorting Limhi's people "that it was the Lord that did deliver them" (v16). Limhi and all his people desire baptism; "Alma did go forth into the water and did baptize them... after the manner he did his brethren in the **waters of Mormon**" (vv17-18). Mosiah "granted unto Alma that he might **establish churches throughout all the land**" and ordain priests and teachers (v19), since the people were too many "to be governed by one teacher" (v20); "notwithstanding there being many churches they were **all one church**" (v22), preaching "nothing... except... repentance and faith in God." "There were seven churches in the land of Zarahemla" (v23); those who took the name of Christ "were called the **people of God**. And the Lord did pour out his Spirit upon them, and they were blessed, and prospered" (v24).

**Thoughts:**
- *One people from many (vv1-13).* This is the convergence the whole book of Mosiah has been moving toward. Five streams meet in one assembly: the Nephites who stayed in Zarahemla, the Mulekites (people of Zarahemla), Limhi's people just escaped from bondage, Alma's people delivered from Amulon, and the repentant children of the wicked priests — all gathered, all "numbered with the Nephites," all becoming "the people of God." The Book of Mormon's recurring vision of disparate peoples made one is enacted here. And the demographic note is sober and important: the covenant people are a *minority* — the Mulekites outnumber the Nephites, and the Lamanites outnumber everyone combined (vv2-3). The people of God are always the smaller number; their security is never in their numbers but in their God. (This sets the stage for the wars to come — the righteous are perpetually outnumbered.)
- *The records read — a people formed by hearing its own story (vv5-11).* The purpose of all the record-keeping is realized in this scene. Mosiah reads the Zeniff record and Alma's account *aloud to the gathered people*, and they are transformed by it — moved through the full human range: wonder, joy at the delivered, tears for the slain, thanksgiving for God's goodness, and "pain and anguish for the welfare of [the Lamanites'] souls." A community is *formed* by hearing its shared history — the deliverances and their costs, the mercy and the loss. The records are not archives; read aloud to the assembled people, they make a people. (This is how scripture works on a community even now: read together, it produces joy, grief, gratitude, and concern for the lost — the very emotions that bind a covenant people. And note the last one, v11: the anguish for the Lamanites' souls is the seed of the great missionary effort to come — Mosiah's own sons will carry it, ch 27-28.)
- *Identity by choice, not blood — the children of Amulon (v12).* The chapter's quiet hinge, and a direct answer to the grievance tradition. The children of Amulon's wicked priests, "**displeased with the conduct of their fathers**," refuse to be "called by the names of their fathers" and *take* the name of Nephi. This is the exact inverse of the Lamanite grievance, where hatred was *inherited* from the fathers and became "eternal" (10:17). Here, children break the chain: they refuse their fathers' identity and *choose* a righteous one. Identity is not fate; you can decline your fathers' name and take a better one. The lie taught in from the fathers (the grievance study's core finding) is broken precisely the way it was made — by the next generation's choice. And the language is the baptismal covenant's own: they "took upon themselves the name" (cf 5:8) — name-taking as the act of choosing who you will be. The wicked do not have to beget the wicked; a child can repudiate the inheritance.
- *The baptism that waited — authority arrives (vv17-18).* Limhi's people, who in chapter 21 desired baptism but had no one with authority (Ammon declined, "an unworthy servant," 21:33-34), are now baptized by Alma "after the manner... in the waters of Mormon." The authority problem is resolved: Alma *has* authority, and administers the ordinance that waited. The patience of chapter 21 — "waiting upon the Spirit of the Lord" rather than improvising — is rewarded. The ordinance is done *right*, by authority, when authority is present. Their willingness was counted from the moment they covenanted (21:32); the ordinance simply waited for the proper hand.
- *Many churches, one church (vv19-24).* The first organized church structure in the record. The people are too numerous for one teacher or one assembly, so Alma (authorized by Mosiah) establishes *seven churches* — yet "notwithstanding there being many churches they were **all one church**," held together not by a single building but by a single doctrine: "nothing... preached in all the churches except it were repentance and faith in God." Multiple congregations, one church, doctrinal unity — the pattern of the church in every age, in seed. Unity is not uniformity of location but uniformity of message.
- *King and prophet, distinguished (vv14, 19).* A structural detail with long consequences. Mosiah, the *king*, does not lead the church; he has Alma *speak*, and *grants* Alma the authority to organize it. The civil ruler empowers the religious leader but does not become him — Mosiah governs, Alma shepherds. This separation of the king's office from the high priest's is deliberate (and the opposite of Noah, who fused them and corrupted both). It prepares for the reign of judges (ch 29): a people learning to keep civil and religious authority distinct, so that a Noah can never again own both.

**Notable / flag:** No spin-off. The **convergence chapter** — five streams (Nephite, Mulekite, Limhi's people, Alma's people, the repentant children of the wicked priests) made **one people of God**, though the covenant people remain a *minority* (the Lamanites outnumber everyone, vv2-3 — security in God, not numbers). The **records read aloud form a people**: Mosiah reads the Zeniff and Alma accounts, and the assembly is moved through the full range — wonder, joy, tears, thanksgiving, and "**anguish for the welfare of [the Lamanites'] souls**" (v11, the seed of the coming missionary effort). The quiet hinge is **identity by choice**: the children of Amulon, "displeased with the conduct of their fathers," **refuse their fathers' name and take the name of Nephi** (v12) — the exact inverse of the inherited "eternal hatred" (10:17); the lie taught in from the fathers is broken the same way it was made, by the next generation's choice. Plus **the baptism that finally happens** (Limhi's people, who waited for authority in ch 21, baptized now by Alma, vv17-18 — patience rewarded), **many churches / one church** (seven congregations, one doctrine, v22), and **king and prophet distinguished** (Mosiah authorizes Alma but does not lead the church — the opposite of Noah, preparing the reign of judges).
