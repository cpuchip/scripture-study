# Mosiah 16 — "The Arms of Mercy Were Extended, and They Would Not"

*(Abinadi's close — universal redemption from the Fall, the carnal man "as though there was no redemption," the resurrection's victory, and the commission to teach Christ. The discourse ends. ~148 BC. Mini-study — full synthesis in spin-off `mos13-16_abinadi-christology`.)*

**Binding idea:** Abinadi has built from "you don't keep the law" up through the suffering servant and the Father-Son condescension. Now he lands it: the redemption is *universal* — it lifts all mankind from the Fall. So how is anyone lost? The answer is the chapter's most piercing line, and it is not about God withholding.

**Read:** Abinadi stretches forth his hand: "the time shall come when all shall see the salvation of the Lord; when **every nation, kindred, tongue, and people shall see eye to eye and shall confess before God that his judgments are just**" (v1). "Then shall the wicked be cast out... because they would not hearken... therefore the Lord redeemeth them not" (v2). "For they are **carnal and devilish**... even that old serpent that did beguile our first parents, which was the cause of their fall; which was the cause of all mankind becoming **carnal, sensual, devilish, knowing evil from good**" (v3). "Thus all mankind were lost; and... they would have been endlessly lost **were it not that God redeemed his people from their lost and fallen state**" (v4). "But... he that **persists in his own carnal nature**, and goes on in the ways of sin and rebellion... remaineth in his fallen state... Therefore he is **as though there was no redemption made**, being an enemy to God" (v5). "If Christ had not come into the world, speaking of things to come as though they had already come, there could have been no redemption" (v6); "if Christ had not risen from the dead... there could have been no resurrection" (v7). "But there is a resurrection, therefore **the grave hath no victory, and the sting of death is swallowed up in Christ**" (v8). "He is the **light and the life of the world**" (v9). "Even this mortal shall put on immortality... and shall be brought to stand before the **bar of God**, to be judged of him according to their works" (v10) — "if they be good, to the resurrection of endless life and happiness; and if they be evil, to the resurrection of endless damnation" (v11). "Having gone according to their own carnal wills and desires; having **never called upon the Lord while the arms of mercy were extended towards them; for the arms of mercy were extended towards them, and they would not**" (v12). "Ought ye not to tremble and repent... and remember that **only in and through Christ ye can be saved**?" (v13). "If ye teach the law of Moses, also teach that it is a **shadow** of those things which are to come — **Teach them that redemption cometh through Christ the Lord, who is the very Eternal Father**" (vv14-15).

**Thoughts:**
- *Universal redemption, conditional benefit (vv4-5) — the chapter's pivot.* Abinadi states the redemption's reach at full width: "all mankind were lost... they would have been endlessly lost **were it not that God redeemed his people from their lost and fallen state**" (v4). The Atonement answers the Fall *universally* — it is the reason anyone rises at all (vv7-8, 10). But then the precise qualification: "he that persists in his own carnal nature... is **as though there was no redemption made**" (v5). The redemption is unconditional in its reach over the Fall (everyone is resurrected, everyone stands at the bar) and conditional in its *saving* effect (life or damnation by response). The rebel does not cancel the redemption — it stands, paid in full — he simply lives *as if it had never happened*, locked in the fallen state Christ already opened. The tragedy is not an unpaid debt; it is a paid debt refused.
- *"The arms of mercy were extended... and they would not" (v12).* This is the most piercing sentence in the discourse, and it relocates the cause of damnation entirely. The lost are not lost because mercy ran short or God turned away. "The arms of mercy **were extended** towards them" — reaching, open, waiting — "and **they would not**." Three times the refusal: warned, "yet they would not depart"; commanded to repent, "yet they would not repent." Damnation is self-authored against an outstretched mercy. This is Christ's own lament over Jerusalem — "how often would I have gathered thy children... and ye would not" (Matt 23:37) — given its doctrinal root. God does not cast the wicked out so much as the wicked refuse the arms that reach for them. (And it lands as a warning to the priests, whose arms-of-mercy moment is *this*, Abinadi, standing before them.)
- *All shall confess his judgments are just (v1).* Before the warning, the strange comfort: at the end "every nation, kindred, tongue, and people shall see eye to eye and shall confess... that his judgments are just." Even the condemned will, in the end, agree. No one will stand at the bar with a grievance God cannot answer. The justice that excludes the willful rebel (15:27) is a justice the rebel himself will finally acknowledge — because the arms were extended and he saw them and would not. The verdict will be confessed even by the lost.
- *Carnal, sensual, devilish — and the resurrection that answers it (vv3, 8-10).* The Fall's effect is the triad "carnal, sensual, devilish" (v3 — the recurring Book of Mormon description of the fallen state, cf Alma 42:10, Moses 5:13), the condition the serpent loosed on "all mankind." Against it Abinadi sets the resurrection's victory in Paul's own words — "the grave hath no victory, and the sting of death is swallowed up in Christ" (v8; 1 Cor 15:54-55) — "this mortal shall put on immortality" (v10). The Fall made all flesh mortal and carnal; the risen Christ makes all flesh immortal and, for those who would, incorrupt. Death is universally conquered; carnality is conquered only in those who let the redemption take effect.
- *The commission — to the men about to kill him (vv14-15).* Abinadi's last words are not a curse but an *assignment*. He turns back to the priests' actual job: "if ye teach the law of Moses, also teach that it is a **shadow** of those things which are to come" (closing the law-as-type thesis of 13:31) — and "**teach them that redemption cometh through Christ the Lord, who is the very Eternal Father**" (closing the Father title of 15:4). The whole four-chapter discourse distills to a teaching-charge. And the poignancy is total: he is commissioning the men who will burn him within the hour. He dies handing them the very work they have failed — teach Christ — as though some of them might yet do it. (One will. Alma is listening, 17:2.)

**Notable / flag:** **Discourse COMPLETE (13-16); spin-off `mos13-16_abinadi-christology` RUNNING** — its full synthesis covers this close. The chapter's pivot: **redemption is universal in reach but conditional in benefit** (vv4-5) — Christ lifts all mankind from the Fall, yet the one who "persists in his own carnal nature... is as though there was no redemption made." The redemption is not undone by the rebel; it is *refused* — which the most piercing line names exactly: **"the arms of mercy were extended towards them, and they would not"** (v12), three times "they would not." Damnation is self-authored against an outstretched mercy (Matt 23:37), and in the end **all will confess God's judgments are just** (v1) — even the lost. The resurrection swallows death's sting (vv7-10; 1 Cor 15), all stand at the bar judged by works (vv10-11). And Abinadi closes by **commissioning the very men about to kill him** — teach the law as a shadow, teach Christ "the very Eternal Father" (vv14-15) — distilling the whole discourse into an assignment, with Alma listening (17:2).
