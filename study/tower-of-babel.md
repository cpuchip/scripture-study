# Tower of Babel, Languages, and the Jaredites

*From a Sunday School discussion on Genesis 37–41 (March 15, 2026), the conversation turned to how the Jaredites ended up in the promised land — and what was really happening at the Tower of Babel.*

---

**Binding Question:** Was there one language before Babel, or many? What does the "confounding" actually mean? And what was the brother of Jared really asking God to preserve?

---

## The Language of Adam — Pure and Undefiled

The story of language in scripture doesn't begin at Babel. It begins at the beginning.

> "And a book of remembrance was kept, in the which was recorded, in the language of Adam, for it was given unto as many as called upon God to write by the spirit of inspiration" ([Moses 6:5](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md))

Two things leap out. First, Adam's language was *given* — it had divine origin, not merely human invention. Second, writing in this language was done "by the spirit of inspiration." The language itself was a gift for people who "called upon God."

The very next verse names its quality:

> "And by them their children were taught to read and write, having a language which was pure and undefiled" ([Moses 6:6](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md))

"Pure and undefiled." Hold that phrase. It comes back.

This wasn't just a communication system. The Adamic language encoded theological truth in its vocabulary. Later in the same chapter:

> "for, in the language of Adam, Man of Holiness is his name, and the name of his Only Begotten is the Son of Man" ([Moses 6:57](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md))

God's name in the language of Adam: *Man of Holiness.* His Son: *Son of Man.* The language itself was revelatory — you couldn't speak it without declaring doctrine.

---

## Enoch and the Power of the Language

By Enoch's time — still well before the Flood, well before Babel — the language hadn't merely survived; it carried power:

> "so great was the faith of Enoch that he led the people of God, and their enemies came to battle against them; and he spake the word of the Lord, and the earth trembled, and the mountains fled, even according to his command; and the rivers of water were turned out of their course; and the roar of the lions was heard out of the wilderness; and all nations feared greatly, so powerful was the word of Enoch, and **so great was the power of the language which God had given him**." ([Moses 7:13](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/7.md))

Read that last line again: "the power of the language which God had given him." Enoch's language didn't just describe reality — it *moved* it. Mountains, rivers, the earth itself responded. This is more than vocabulary. This is covenant language. When someone who calls upon God speaks in the language God gave, creation listens.

So we know: from Adam through Enoch, through the city of Zion, there existed a pure, God-given language with divine power. The question is — what happened to it?

## The Table of Nations Puzzle

Genesis 10 is the "Table of Nations" — a genealogical catalog of Noah's descendants spreading across the earth. And it contains a puzzle that careful readers catch immediately.

Three times, it describes peoples divided "after their tongues":

> "By these were the isles of the Gentiles divided in their lands; every one after his tongue, after their families, in their nations." ([Gen. 10:5](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/gen/10.md))

> "These are the sons of Ham, after their families, after their tongues, in their countries, and in their nations." ([Gen. 10:20](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/gen/10.md))

> "These are the sons of Shem, after their families, after their tongues, in their lands, after their nations." ([Gen. 10:31](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/gen/10.md))

"After their tongues" — language diversity. And this is in chapter 10. But chapter 11 opens with:

> "And the whole earth was of one language, and of one speech." ([Gen. 11:1](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/gen/11.md))

So which is it? Many tongues in chapter 10, one language in chapter 11?

The resolution is literary, not chronological. Genesis 10 is a retrospective catalog — the writer looks back from a vantage point *after* the dispersion and describes where everyone ended up. Genesis 11 then tells the story of *how* it happened. The "tongues" in chapter 10 are the *result* of what chapter 11 describes.

But there's a figure embedded in chapter 10 who links the two narratives:

> "And Cush begat Nimrod: he began to be a mighty one in the earth. He was a mighty hunter before the Lord ... And the beginning of his kingdom was Babel, and Erech, and Accad, and Calneh, in the land of Shinar." ([Gen. 10:8-10](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/gen/10.md))

Nimrod. The first kingdom-builder after the Flood. His kingdom begins at Babel, in Shinar — the exact location of the tower in chapter 11. The Bible Dictionary confirms: "According to Gen. 10:8–10 it was founded by Nimrod and was one of the oldest cities of the land of Shinar" ([BD Babylon](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bd/babylon.md)).

And one more verse that keeps scholars busy:

> "And unto Eber were born two sons: the name of one was Peleg; for in his days was the earth divided" ([Gen. 10:25](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/gen/10.md))

"In his days was the earth divided." This may refer to the Babel event itself — the scattering — placed chronologically in the lineage of Shem. But it could also point to something geographical. Either way, it marks a rupture.

## The Tower and the Confounding

The narrative itself is nine verses. Every word counts.

> "And they said, Go to, let us build us a city and a tower, whose top may reach unto heaven; and let us make us a name, lest we be scattered abroad upon the face of the whole earth." ([Gen. 11:4](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/gen/11.md))

Notice: their stated fear is being *scattered*. The tower isn't primarily about reaching God — it's about preventing dispersion. They want to stay together "lest we be scattered." They want to "make us a name" — establish identity, permanence, collective power.

The irony is devastating. God decreed exactly what they feared:

> "And the Lord said, Behold, the people is one, and they have all one language; and this they begin to do: and now nothing will be restrained from them, which they have imagined to do. Go to, let us go down, and there confound their language, that they may not understand one another's speech." ([Gen. 11:6-7](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/gen/11.md))

And here, Webster 1828 lights up the key word.

### What Does "Confound" Actually Mean?

**CONFOUND** (Webster 1828): "To mingle and blend, so that different elements can not be distinguished; to confuse."

The primary meaning isn't "to bewilder" in the modern lightweight sense. It's *to mingle* — to mix different things together until you can't separate them back out. God didn't erase their language. He didn't replace it. He *blended* it. He took one thing and mixed it into many, so thoroughly that the original elements couldn't be recovered.

This reframes the entire event. The confounding wasn't destruction — it was fragmentation. The pure, undefiled language of Adam, passed down through Seth through Enoch through Noah, was mixed and scattered. Every human language on earth may carry fragments of it. None has it whole.

> "Therefore is the name of it called Babel; because the Lord did there confound the language of all the earth: and from thence did the Lord scatter them abroad upon the face of all the earth." ([Gen. 11:9](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/gen/11.md))

Two actions: confound (mix the language) and scatter (disperse the people). The mixing came first. The scattering followed — because once you can't understand each other, you migrate toward those you *can* understand.

And the name itself carries a double meaning. In Hebrew, "Babel" was connected to *bālal* ("to confuse/confound"). But in Akkadian, *Bāb-ilim* means "Gate of God." They called it the gate of God. God called it confusion. The irony is embedded in the very name.

## The Brother of Jared's Prayer

The Book of Mormon gives us a ground-level view of the confounding — not from the narrator's distance, but from inside the event. Jared and his brother are *there*, watching it happen around them.

> "Which Jared came forth with his brother and their families, with some others and their families, from the great tower, at the time the Lord confounded the language of the people, and swore in his wrath that they should be scattered upon all the face of the earth" ([Ether 1:33](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/1.md))

And Jared's response is immediate:

> "Jared, his brother, said unto him: Cry unto the Lord, that he will not confound us that we may not understand our words." ([Ether 1:34](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/1.md))

Look at the specific wording: "that we may not understand our words." The fear isn't about losing a language in the abstract — it's about losing *mutual comprehension*. The ability to understand one another. To think together. To be one.

And the Lord answered:

> "And it came to pass that the brother of Jared did cry unto the Lord, and the Lord had compassion upon Jared; therefore he did not confound the language of Jared; and Jared and his brother were not confounded." ([Ether 1:35](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/1.md))

Then Jared extends it: ask for our *friends* too.

> "Cry again unto the Lord, and it may be that he will turn away his anger from them who are our friends, that he confound not their language." ([Ether 1:36](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/1.md))

> "And it came to pass that the brother of Jared did cry unto the Lord, and the Lord had compassion upon their friends and their families also, that they were not confounded." ([Ether 1:37](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/1.md))

Three prayers, three answers — each expanding the circle of mercy. First for themselves. Then for their friends and families.

**What language did they preserve?** The text doesn't say explicitly. It says their language "was not confounded" — meaning it wasn't mixed, blended, fragmented. Whatever they spoke before the event, they continued speaking after it. Was it the Adamic language itself — the pure and undefiled tongue of Adam, passed faithfully from Seth through Enoch through Noah? The scriptures don't confirm this directly, but there's a compelling inference: if one language existed before Babel, and the Jaredites' language was specifically *exempted* from the confounding, then what they preserved was the pre-Babel tongue — which traces through the Adamic line.

And then God led them to a promised land — with language, families, flocks, and seed. They carried forward an unbroken cultural and spiritual inheritance. The parallel to Noah is unmistakable.

## The Mulekite Contrast

What happens when you *don't* preserve language and records? We have a case study.

> "And at the time that Mosiah discovered them, they had become exceedingly numerous. Nevertheless, they had had many wars and serious contentions, and had fallen by the sword from time to time; and their language had become corrupted; and they had brought no records with them; and they denied the being of their Creator" ([Omni 1:17](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/omni/1.md))

The people of Zarahemla (Mulekites) left Jerusalem with no scriptures. Within a few generations: corrupted language, lost faith, wars, denial of God. Language and record-keeping aren't just cultural niceties — they're the infrastructure of covenant identity. Lose the language, lose the faith.

## Historical Context — Around 2200 BC

Placing the Tower of Babel in history is complex and contested, but the traditional dating puts it roughly around 2200–2100 BC. Here's what was happening in the ancient Near East:

**The Akkadian Empire (c. 2334–2154 BC)** — Under Sargon of Akkad, Mesopotamia was unified under one empire. Akkadian became the lingua franca while Sumerian was preserved as a literary and sacred language. This is the closest the ancient Near East came to "one language" in the political sense — and its collapse around 2154 BC scattered peoples across the region.

**Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta** — A Sumerian literary text (ca. 21st century BC) that describes a "confusion of tongues." Since Samuel Kramer's analysis, this has been compared with the Genesis Babel narrative. Enmerkar, king of Uruk, constructs temples and contends with the king of Aratta — and the text references a time when languages were unified and then divided.

**The "Gate of God"** — The most widely cited physical inspiration for the Tower is Etemenanki, a ziggurat dedicated to the god Marduk in Babylon. The Akkadian name *Bāb-ilim* ("Gate of God") is the source of "Babylon." Whether or not Etemenanki *is* the Tower of Babel, the connection between ziggurats ("towers whose top may reach unto heaven") and the Genesis narrative is architecturally and culturally direct.

**Writing vs. Speaking** — Marc Van De Mieroop (ANE Today, 2023) makes a fascinating observation: in the 3rd and 2nd millennia BCE, Sumerian served as a universal *written* language even as *spoken* languages diversified. The transition from universal Sumerian literacy to multilingual writing systems mirrors the Babel narrative in an unexpected way — a kind of "confounding" of the written word as well.

None of this "proves" the Babel event in a secular-historical sense. But the ancient Near East around 2200 BC was characterized by linguistic diversity, imperial collapse, population dispersal, and cultural remembered a time of prior unity. The world the biblical authors inhabited remembered Babel — not just as myth, but as explanation for the world they lived in.

## Zion and Babel — Inverse Images

One of the most striking patterns in this study is the mirror between Zion and Babel.

| | **Zion** (Moses 7:18) | **Babel** (Gen 11:1-4) |
|---|---|---|
| Unity | "one heart and one mind" | "one language and one speech" |
| Basis | Righteousness | Pride ("make us a name") |
| Direction | God dwells with them (Moses 7:16) | They build toward heaven (Gen 11:4) |
| Fear | None — "Zion shall dwell in safety" | "Lest we be scattered" |
| Outcome | Translated/taken up | Confounded and scattered |

Both stories are about oneness. Zion achieves it through voluntary righteousness — and God preserves it. Babel attempts it through collective ambition — and God shatters it. The lesson isn't that unity is bad. The lesson is that unity without righteousness becomes dangerous. "Nothing will be restrained from them, which they have imagined to do" — and what they *imagined* was their own glory, not God's.

## The Full Arc: Pure → Confounded → Restored

The story of language in scripture isn't a tragedy. It's an arc:

1. **Adam:** A "pure and undefiled" language, given by God, carried divine power ([Moses 6:5-6](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/6.md))
2. **Enoch:** The language moves mountains, sustains Zion ([Moses 7:13](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/7.md))
3. **Babel:** The language is confounded — mingled, scattered, fragmented ([Gen. 11:7-9](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/gen/11.md))
4. **Jaredites:** One group's language is preserved through prayer and faith ([Ether 1:35-37](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/1.md))
5. **Zephaniah's promise:** God will one day "turn to the people a **pure language**, that they may all call upon the name of the Lord, to serve him with one consent" ([Zeph. 3:9](gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/zeph/3.md))

The same word — *pure* — bookends the arc. Moses 6:6's "pure and undefiled" language and Zephaniah 3:9's "pure language." What was fragmented will be restored. What was mingled will be made whole. Babel isn't the end of the story.

## Becoming

Language is identity. It's how we think, how we pray, how we understand each other. When God confounded the language at Babel, He wasn't just changing phonemes — He was breaking the ability to *understand one another*, to be of one mind.

The brother of Jared's prayer is a prayer I can pray: *Don't let me lose the ability to understand.* Not just languages in the linguistic sense, but the deeper language of the Spirit — the capacity to hear God, to comprehend truth, to speak in ways that build Zion rather than towers.

Every generation faces its own Babel. Every generation has to decide: are we building toward heaven to make a name for ourselves, or are we building Zion because God dwells with us? The language we speak — not just English or Hebrew, but the language of our hearts — reveals the answer.

And Zephaniah's promise says the arc bends toward restoration. A pure language is coming. A day when we'll all "call upon the name of the Lord, to serve him with one consent." Every tongue on earth may carry a fragment of what Adam knew. One day, we'll have it whole again.
