# Is Jesus Christ God?

> *"Behold, I am Jesus Christ. I am the Father and the Son. In me shall all mankind have life, and that eternally, even they who shall believe on my name; and they shall become my sons and my daughters."*
> — [Ether 3:14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/3.md)

**Binding question:** From an LDS standpoint, is Jesus Christ God?

---

## The shape of the question

The Restoration's first job was to undo a centuries-old fusion. The First Vision opens with two Personages, standing in the air, distinct and separate; one of them points to the other and says, *"This is My Beloved Son. Hear Him!"* ([JS—H 1:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/js-h/1.md)). The historic creeds had insisted for fifteen centuries that the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost are one God in substance even while being three Persons; Joseph was told that those creeds were an abomination ([JS—H 1:19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/js-h/1.md)). The Saints who took that vision seriously learned to distinguish the Father from the Son carefully and never to collapse them.

That habit can quietly invert. Once you have learned to say "the Son is not the Father," it is short work to start saying it as if the Son were *less*: a glorified servant, an honored intermediary, a Person we worship the Father *through* rather than a Person we worship *as God*. The instinct is well-meaning. It also misreads almost every load-bearing passage on the question.

The scriptures answer differently, and the most direct answers come from the two volumes Joseph translated last: the Pearl of Great Price and the Book of Mormon. Look at Moses and look at Ether. The Son is God. Distinct from the Father, yes, and *fully God*. Both true. Both required.

---

## The sighting in Ether 3

The strongest single witness in the entire canon for this is the brother of Jared on Mount Shelem, because what happens there happens before the incarnation. There is no body of flesh in the room. There is a man with sixteen stones and a Being who is about to identify himself.

The Being touches the stones, the veil drops, and the brother of Jared sees a finger like flesh and blood and falls down. The Being asks him to rise. He says he feared because "I knew not that the Lord had flesh and blood" ([Ether 3:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/3.md)). The answer is precise: "Because of thy faith thou hast seen that I shall take upon me flesh and blood" (v. 9). *Shall take*, future tense. The flesh is still ahead. The body the prophet is about to see is not Mary's son. It is the body of the spirit who will become Mary's son.

Then the Lord shows himself fully, and the sentence comes out in the first person:

> *"Behold, I am he who was prepared from the foundation of the world to redeem my people. Behold, I am Jesus Christ. I am the Father and the Son. In me shall all mankind have life, and that eternally."*
> — [Ether 3:14](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/3.md)

The Father and the Son. In one mouth. Spoken by a glorified Being in spirit form, identifying himself by the name *Jesus Christ* before he had ever taken the flesh that name belongs to. There are technical readings of this. The [father-and-son study](plan-of-salvation/father-and-son.md) walks through the 1916 First Presidency exposition's three senses in which Christ bears the Father title, and those readings are right; investiture and creatorhood and spiritual rebirth do explain how the Son speaks in the Father's name. But they do not exhaust what is happening here. Because immediately after the speech, the narrator's voice rises out of the chapter:

> *"And he ministered unto him even as he ministered unto the Nephites; and all this, that this man might know that he was God, because of the many great works which the Lord had showed unto him."*
> — [Ether 3:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/3.md)

That is Moroni. A thousand years removed from the brother of Jared, abridging the record, supplying the doctrinal frame. And the frame he supplies is not *that this man might know that he was the Father's spokesman*. It is *that this man might know that he was God*. Not by investiture. Not by office. Outright. The premortal Christ is the God the brother of Jared saw. The same Being then says one more sentence that puts the matter beyond rescue:

> *"Behold, this body, which ye now behold, is the body of my spirit; and man have I created after the body of my spirit; and even as I appear unto thee to be in the spirit will I appear unto my people in the flesh."*
> — [Ether 3:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/ether/3.md)

The body of my spirit. *Created*, said by the speaker, in the first person, of Adam and of every man. This is the God who made us, in the body he had before he was born. There is no way to read this as a polite assist to a senior God. The Being in the room is God.

---

## The Word who creates, in Moses 1

Moses 1 puts the same claim on a wider stage. The chapter opens with God speaking, and the speaker names himself: *"I am the Lord God Almighty, and Endless is my name"* ([Moses 1:3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/1.md)). Six verses later he distinguishes himself from his Son: *"mine Only Begotten is and shall be the Savior… but there is no God beside me"* (v. 6). The Father speaks; the Father exists; the Father is the one calling Moses *my son*. So far so clear.

Then, near the end, the Father describes the architecture of creation in a single sentence that this whole tradition organizes itself around:

> *"And by the word of my power, have I created them, which is mine Only Begotten Son… and worlds without number have I created; and I also created them for mine own purpose; and by the Son I created them, which is mine Only Begotten."*
> — [Moses 1:32–33](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/moses/1.md)

The Father's *word of power* is the Son. The Son is the one *by whom* the worlds are made. John's gospel, written by a different hand a half-world away, says the same thing in its first three sentences: *"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God… All things were made by him; and without him was not any thing made that was made"* ([John 1:1, 3](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/1.md)). With God, and *was God*. Two persons; one divinity; the second one is the one who actually makes things. The Greek New Testament and the Restoration's translation of an ancient Egyptian record converge on the same sentence: the Word is God, and the Word makes the worlds.

This is also where the question of who the Old Testament Jehovah is finally settles. The voice that names itself "the Lord God Almighty" in Moses 1 and the voice that says "by the Son I created them" cannot be entirely distinct. By v. 25 the Father is again speaking ("I, the Almighty, have chosen thee"), and Moses is told to "Call upon God in the name of mine Only Begotten" (v. 17), language that already presupposes the modern pattern of praying to the Father in the name of the Son. The Father speaks; the Son acts; the two voices interleave because the Son speaks for the Father and acts in his stead. And every voice you can hear at Sinai, at the burning bush, at the parting of the sea, every "I am the Lord" through the whole Old Testament: that voice was the Word, and the Word was God, and the Word was, premortally, the One we now know as Jesus Christ.

To create as God is to be God. The Son does both.

## The named-God language of the Restoration

If Moses and Ether do most of the heavy lifting, the Doctrine and Covenants nails the door shut. In D&C 19, three years before the Church was organized, the Lord opens the revelation to Martin Harris with a one-sentence self-introduction:

> *"I am Alpha and Omega, Christ the Lord; yea, even I am he, the beginning and the end, the Redeemer of the world."*
> — [D&C 19:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/19.md)

Christ the Lord. Alpha and Omega, the title given at the throne in [Revelation 1:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/rev/1.md). The same revelation, fifteen verses later, contains the line that has done more pastoral work than any other in modern Restoration scripture:

> *"For behold, I, God, have suffered these things for all, that they might not suffer if they would repent… which suffering caused myself, even God, the greatest of all, to tremble because of pain, and to bleed at every pore, and to suffer both body and spirit."*
> — [D&C 19:16, 18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/19.md)

The speaker is Christ. The speaker calls himself *God, the greatest of all*. There is no daylight in the grammar. The Being who bled in Gethsemane is, in his own first-person testimony, named God in superlative terms; and he keeps the relationship to the Father intact in the very next verse: *"Nevertheless, glory be to the Father, and I partook"* (v. 19). Both at once. Christ is God. The Father is God. The Father is glorified by what the Son does. Two persons. Both fully God.

Three sections later in the same body of revelation the architecture is given physical form:

> *"The Father has a body of flesh and bones as tangible as man's; the Son also; but the Holy Ghost has not a body of flesh and bones, but is a personage of Spirit."*
> — [D&C 130:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/130.md)

Two glorified bodies. Both belonging to Persons we know as God. And the New Testament adds the line they were always converging toward, said by Christ himself to Jews who heard it correctly and tried to stone him for it: *"Before Abraham was, I am"* ([John 8:58](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/8.md)). The *I am* of Exodus 3 in the voice of a Galilean carpenter who had not yet turned forty. They were not confused. Neither should we be.

---

## How both claims hold

The reflex that worries about Trinitarianism is a real worry; the creeds did collapse three Persons into one substance and fail to know either Person well. The Restoration's correction was to insist that they are two, and one of them is bodily, and one of them is older, and the older introduces the younger. None of that correction requires demoting the Son. It is the same correction that *enabled* knowing the Son: when you can see him distinctly, you can see what he is.

Abinadi puts both halves in one breath: *"I would that ye should understand that God himself shall come down among the children of men, and shall redeem his people"* ([Mosiah 15:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/15.md)). God himself. Not a deputy. Not a representative. God. And in the same passage, the distinction stays intact: *"the will of the Son being swallowed up in the will of the Father"* (v. 7). Amulek answers Zeezrom with the same construction: *"Yea, he is the very Eternal Father of heaven and of earth, and all things which in them are; he is the beginning and the end, the first and the last"* ([Alma 11:39](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/11.md)). Five verses later, the wicked stand before *"the bar of Christ the Son, and God the Father, and the Holy Spirit, which is one Eternal God"* ([Alma 11:44](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/11.md)). Three Persons. One Eternal God. Both true.

Isaiah gives the language we still sing at Christmas: a child to be born whose name shall be called *"Wonderful, Counsellor, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace"* ([Isaiah 9:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/9.md)). The mighty God. The everlasting Father. Both titles, on the same Person, in the same line, eight centuries before Bethlehem. The Hebrew prophet was not asking us to choose between *Son* and *God*. He was naming a Person to whom both belong.

The technical apparatus that lets these passages cohere (the three senses in which the Son is called Father: Creator, spiritual rebirth, divine investiture; and the conjugate three ways the Father's name and authority rest upon the Son) is laid out at length in the 1916 First Presidency exposition and walked through scripture-by-scripture in the [father-and-son study](plan-of-salvation/father-and-son.md). That is the precision tool; this study is the structural claim that requires the tool. The claim, said as plainly as we can: there are two Persons, both fully God, distinct in office and order; the Son is not the Father, and the Son is God.

---

## What changes when the answer is yes

The shape of worship reorganizes around this.

The sacrament: when we covenant to "always remember him" and partake of bread that represents his body and water that represents his blood, we are not remembering a really impressive servant who suffered on behalf of a distant God. We are remembering, in Mosiah's exact words, *God himself*, come down. The matter on the tray is the body and blood of God. The prayer of approach is a prayer to a Being who is fully divine and fully ours.

The Old Testament reads differently. Every "the word of the Lord came unto" is the word of Jehovah, and Jehovah is Christ. The Lord who walked with Adam in the cool of the day; the Lord who closed the door of the ark; the Lord who appeared to Abraham at Mamre; the Lord who wrestled with Jacob; the Lord on Sinai with thunder and trumpet; the Lord whom Isaiah saw "high and lifted up" with seraphim around him crying *holy* ([Isaiah 6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/isa/6.md)). Every one of these is the premortal Christ. The Old Testament is not a Father's book and a New Testament a Son's book. They are both his books. He was on every page before he was on every cross.

Prayer keeps its shape. We pray to the Father, in the name of the Son, through the Spirit. That order is not because the Son is not God; it is because the Father presides, and the Son glorifies the Father, and the Spirit testifies of both. The Son's full divinity is what *makes* the path through him valid. *"No man cometh unto the Father, but by me"* ([John 14:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/14.md)) is a sentence said by a Person with the standing to say it.

And the language of "we worship the Father in the name of the Son" (true and right, used by Joseph as the corrective against creedal confusion) also expands. Worship of the Father runs through the Son because the Son is also God; we are not bypassing him on the way to the only one who counts. We are passing through one Person of the Godhead to reach another, with the second testifying of the first the whole way.

---

## Becoming

The thing I want to carry from this is small and embarrassingly practical. I find that when I think clinically about the Godhead, the Father becomes the only one I quite think of as *God* in the unqualified sense, and the Son becomes the one I think of as my brother, my elder brother, my redeemer, my friend. There is something right about that. The Son made himself accessible at exactly that human level. But the cost, if I am not careful, is that the Person I take the sacrament for becomes someone I love rather than Someone I worship.

The fix is not to stop loving him. The fix is to let the love and the worship both be true. The Being whose body broke on Friday is the Being whose voice spoke to Moses on Sinai and whose finger touched the stones for the brother of Jared and whose word framed the worlds. *He* is the one I take the sacrament for. The intimacy is not threatened by the divinity; the intimacy is *more astonishing* because of the divinity. The God of all worlds is the one who learned my name and remembered it.

Next time the bread comes by, that is what I want to be holding. Not a respectful memorial for a great servant. A piece of bread that stands for the body of God, who in the flesh I knew not, who has flesh and blood, who was prepared from the foundation of the world to redeem his people, and whose first-person introduction at Shelem was a sentence that had to be a thousand years in storage before anyone outside that mountaintop ever heard it: *Behold, I am Jesus Christ.*
