# The Gift of Aaron

> "Now this is not all thy gift; for you have another gift, which is the gift of Aaron; behold, it has told you many things." — [D&C 8:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/8.md)

> **Binding question:** What is "the gift of Aaron" in D&C 8:6, and does it relate to the Aaronic Priesthood?

## I. The verse no one quotes

The BYU Scripture Citation Index records zero citations of D&C 8:6, 8:7, 8:8, or 8:9 across the entire general conference corpus. Verse 11, the closing promise about translating "ancient records," picks up two hits, both from the 1870s (Wilford Woodruff and Orson Pratt in the *Journal of Discourses*). Otherwise, the passage that names the gift of Aaron is, in modern conference preaching, silent.

The Come Follow Me Doctrine and Covenants curriculum present in this library does not exposit the verse either. The children's *Doctrine and Covenants Stories* tells the Oliver Cowdery story straight through without mentioning the gift of Aaron. The phrase has dropped out of our shared vocabulary.

That is the gap this study is trying to read into.

## II. April 1829, not May 15

D&C 8 is dated April 1829, given through Joseph Smith to Oliver Cowdery at Harmony, Pennsylvania. The Aaronic Priesthood was restored on May 15, 1829, when "this same John was sent from heaven as a resurrected being to confer the Aaronic Priesthood upon Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery" ([BD Aaronic Priesthood](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bd/aaronic-priesthood.md)).

The dates do the first piece of the work. When the Lord said to Oliver, "you have another gift, which is the gift of Aaron," neither Joseph nor Oliver had yet received any priesthood office in this dispensation. The Aaronic Priesthood as an office was not yet on the earth. Whatever the gift of Aaron is in v.6, it cannot be the office Oliver would be ordained to a few weeks later.

The verse therefore names something Oliver already had. D&C 6, given earlier that same month, confirms the past tense: "Behold thou *hast* a gift, and blessed art thou because of thy gift. Remember it is sacred and cometh from above" ([D&C 6:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/6.md)). D&C 8 specifies what that pre-existing gift was.

## III. "Sprout," then "rod," then "Aaron"

The 1835 published reading "gift of Aaron" is a revision. The original 1829 manuscript, recorded in Revelation Book 1 in Oliver Cowdery's own handwriting, reads differently. The phrase in the manuscript is "the gift of working with the *sprout*," and the parallel phrase in v.7 is "this *thing of Nature*" (Joseph Smith Papers, [Revelation, April 1829–B, Revelation Book 1, p. 13](https://www.josephsmithpapers.org/paper-summary/revelation-april-1829-b-dc-8/1)).

Preparing the text for the 1833 *Book of Commandments*, Sidney Rigdon replaced "sprout" with "rod." BoC 7:3 reads, "you have another gift, which is the gift of working with the *rod*: behold it has told you things: behold there is no other power save God, that can cause this *rod of nature*, to work in your hands."

For the 1835 *Doctrine and Covenants*, the wording was revised once more to the canonical reading we now have: "the gift of Aaron."

The textual progression is therefore *sprout → rod → Aaron*. All three name the same gift. The revisions are interpretive; they are reaching for a more theologically legible name for what Oliver already had. By 1835 Oliver had been ordained to the Aaronic Priesthood, had assisted in opening the dispensation, had been called Second Elder, had stood as one of the Three Witnesses, and had received with Joseph the keys spoken of in [D&C 110](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/110.md). The revision is naming Oliver's personal revelatory gift after the biblical figure whose calling Oliver's own life had come to typify.

This is not concealment; it is interpretation in the light of what had happened in the intervening six years. But to read the verse honestly, both the original and the canonical form have to stay in view. The 1835 text is the canon. The 1829 text is what was first spoken. Both are saying the same thing about Oliver, and the bridge between them is what kind of figure Aaron actually is in scripture.

## IV. Aaron in the Old Testament — what kind of figure is being named?

Aaron's call comes in Exodus 4. Moses pleads, "I am not eloquent… I am slow of speech, and of a slow tongue" ([Ex. 4:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/4.md)). The Lord answers first with a promise, "I will be with thy mouth, and teach thee what thou shalt say," and Moses still resists. Then ([v.14-16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/4.md)):

> "the anger of the Lord was kindled against Moses, and he said, Is not Aaron the Levite thy brother? I know that he can speak well… And thou shalt speak unto him, and put words in his mouth: and I will be with thy mouth, and with his mouth, and will teach you what ye shall do. And he shall be thy spokesman unto the people: and he shall be, even he shall be to thee instead of a mouth, and thou shalt be to him instead of God."

The footnote to v.16 makes the structure explicit: "A prophet is a spokesman; hence he speaks for, or instead of, God." Aaron is a prophet's prophet, the visible mediator who delivers in the people's hearing what the prophet himself cannot deliver alone.

The rod is given in [v.17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/4.md): "And thou shalt take this rod in thine hand, wherewith thou shalt do signs." It is given to Moses. In [v.20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/4.md) it is called "the rod of God." In Exodus 7 it migrates to Aaron's hand to do the signs before Pharaoh, but it is the same instrument, and the power is never the instrument's own. The rod and the words alike originate beyond Aaron. His gift is to carry them faithfully into the people's sight.

When Aaron meets Moses in the wilderness ([Ex. 4:27-31](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/4.md)):

> "Moses told Aaron all the words of the Lord who had sent him, and all the signs which he had commanded him… And Aaron spake all the words which the Lord had spoken unto Moses, and did the signs in the sight of the people. And the people believed."

The people believed because Aaron stood out front and delivered what Moses had received. That is Aaron's gift. He is the second man, the visible one, the one who makes the prophet's word public.

Numbers 17 finishes the portrait. Twelve tribal rods are placed overnight in the tabernacle, indistinguishable from each other; "the rod of Aaron was among their rods" ([Num. 17:6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/17.md)). In the morning Aaron's rod alone "was budded, and brought forth buds, and bloomed blossoms, and yielded almonds" ([v.8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/17.md)). The Lord then commands that the rod be kept in the ark "for a token against the rebels" ([v.10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/17.md)).

Aaron's authority is shown by what God causes to *live* in his rod. The instrument is ordinary. The life is divine. The rod is preserved as a permanent witness that the calling was given by God, not assumed by the man.

This is the figure the 1835 revision is naming: a spokesman who carries his brother-prophet's word; a sign-bearer whose instrument has no power of its own; a chosen one whose calling is authenticated by what God causes to grow in his hand.

## V. Cowdery as the Aaron of this dispensation

Joseph Fielding Smith, in *Church History and Modern Revelation*, reads D&C 8:6 directly through this typology:

> "There was another gift bestowed upon Oliver Cowdery, and that was the gift of Aaron. Like Aaron with his rod in his hand going before Moses as a spokesman, so Oliver Cowdery was to go before Joseph Smith. Whatever he should ask the Lord by power of this gift should be granted if asked in faith and in wisdom. Oliver was blessed with the great honor of holding the keys of this dispensation with Joseph Smith, and, like Aaron, did become a spokesman on numerous occasions. It was Oliver who delivered the first public discourse in this dispensation."

Hyrum M. Smith and Janne M. Sjodahl, in their *Commentary*, land the same connection: "In some respects Oliver Cowdery was the Aaron of the new and last dispensation."

The biography fits the type at every point. Oliver came to Joseph in April 1829 when Joseph was alone, behind in the work, without a scribe. Oliver wrote what Joseph translated. Oliver was the second man kneeling beside Joseph when John the Baptist came on May 15, 1829, and the priesthood that bears Aaron's name was placed jointly on both of them. Oliver was ordained Second Elder of the Church. Oliver was one of the Three Witnesses and signed his testimony to the world. Oliver received with Joseph the keys returned by Moses, Elias, and Elijah at Kirtland ([D&C 110](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/110.md)). Oliver delivered the first public discourse of the Restoration.

He was the visible second who let the prophet's word land in the people's hearing. He carried what was properly his brother's. He went out front and the people believed.

What about the rod? Jeffrey G. Cannon's 2012 essay "Oliver Cowdery's Gift" in *Revelations in Context*, an official Church publication, handles this directly. Oliver came to Joseph already practicing a form of rod-revelation that was unremarkable in his frontier religious culture; many sincere Christians of the period believed God could speak through such instruments. The April 1829-B revelation does not condemn the practice. It legitimizes it: "there is no other power save God that can cause this thing of Nature to work in your hands." But it immediately disciplines and redirects it: "trifle not with these sacred things" ([D&C 8:10](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/8.md)), the same warning given two weeks earlier in [D&C 6:12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/6.md). And the gift's function is widened from its private use into something canonical: "whatsoever you shall ask of me to tell you by the Spirit of revelation, you shall have knowledge of, that you may translate and receive knowledge from all those ancient records which have been hid up" ([v.11](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/8.md)).

Elder Dallin H. Oaks, in his October 1987 *Ensign* article on Church history and forged documents, gave the right pastoral framing for the whole question of revelatory instruments:

> "It should be recognized that such tools as the Urim and Thummim, the Liahona, seerstones, and other articles have been used appropriately in biblical, Book of Mormon, and modern times by those who have the gift and authority to obtain revelation from God in connection with their use."

The criterion is not the instrument. It is the person and their authority. Aaron's rod was an ordinary staff until God budded it. Moses's rod was a shepherd's stick until God turned it into a serpent. What makes the instrument lawful is the calling of the hand that holds it. This is the substance D&C 8:8 is teaching when it says, "you shall hold it in your hands, and do marvelous works; and no power shall be able to take it away out of your hands, for it is the work of God."

## VI. The gift, the office, and the relationship between them

Does the gift of Aaron in D&C 8:6 relate to the Aaronic Priesthood?

Not as identity. The office was not yet on the earth when the verse was spoken. The two are separable. The gift was Oliver's personal revelatory capacity, which he brought to the work and which the Lord both legitimized and redirected. The priesthood was a conferred authority that came down weeks later from a resurrected messenger and was given to Joseph and Oliver jointly. D&C 8:8 says of the gift, "no power shall be able to take it away out of your hands." D&C 9:5 says of the translation gift Oliver received in D&C 6 and then failed to exercise, "I have taken away this privilege from you." Two gifts, two destinies. The gift of Aaron stayed with Oliver. The translation privilege did not.

Yet they are bound by type. The Lord called Oliver's revelatory gift "the gift of Aaron" in the same season that He was preparing to give Oliver and Joseph the priesthood that also bears Aaron's name. The 1835 revision, made after Oliver had lived out the Aaron-typology in office and witness, reaches across both, naming the gift and the office after the same biblical figure because the same biblical figure illuminates them both. Aaron is the brother who speaks for the prophet, carries the prophet's instrument, stands out front so the people will believe, and whose authority God authenticates by what He causes to live in the man's hand.

That figure was given to Oliver as a name for what he already had. That figure was then conferred on him as the office he would hold. The gift typified the office. The office completed the gift.

For us, the verse is doing something it can still do. It is naming the possibility that the Lord may take the unconsecrated thing a person already carries — a habit of mind, an instinct, a folk practice, a way of asking — and not throw it away but legitimize, discipline, and rename it. The Lord met Oliver in his rod and called it Aaron's. He did not require Oliver to come empty-handed. He required only that Oliver let the gift be set under priesthood, set under the Spirit of revelation, set under the warning not to trifle.

## VII. Becoming

What do I carry to Joseph?

Not nothing. The Lord does not call us out of nothing. Oliver came with a practice, a method of asking, something he had been doing before the revelation found him. The Lord's response was not "set that down." It was "I know what that is, and I can name it more truly than you have named it. Bring it under My Spirit, do not trifle with it, and what you have already learned to do with your hands you will now do for the work of God."

The application is not to take up a divining rod. The application is to notice what I already do, the capacities I brought to this work before anyone called them gifts, and to ask whether I have let the Lord rename them. The instinct I have for finding the right question. The patience that came from a job I no longer have. The way I think out loud with another person until something clicks. These are not nothing. They are rods. The question is whether I am holding them as the work of God, or holding them as mine.

The discipline Oliver received was twofold. *Trifle not*: do not exercise the gift for show, for novelty, for self. *Ask in faith*: exercise it precisely so the work of God can move through it. The gift was kept; the casualness about it was not.

I want to ask, this week, of one capacity I know I have: have I asked the Lord to name it? Have I held it in my hand as His work, or as mine? Have I trifled with it? And the Aaron piece: have I been willing to use it not for myself but in the service of someone else whose work depends on me being out front, speaking what they cannot yet make heard alone?
