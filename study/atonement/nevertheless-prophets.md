# Nevertheless — Part 2: The Apostolic Witness

*A companion to [Nevertheless Part 1](nevertheless.md), which traced the word through scripture. This study asks: do modern prophets reinforce, extend, or reframe the "nevertheless" pattern?*

---

## The Binding Question

The [first nevertheless study](nevertheless.md) traced three voices — Christ's, God's toward us, ours toward God — entirely through scripture. But in tracing the word through Genesis to Doctrine and Covenants, we never asked what the *living* voices are doing with it. Michael pointed out the gap: the original study has almost no conference talks. That's not a minor omission — it means we traced the ancient pattern but never checked whether the modern witnesses carry it forward or add to it.

The question now: **Do modern apostles and prophets teach the "nevertheless" pattern, and if so, what do they add to what scripture already says?**

Specifically: Is Elder Holland's use of "nevertheless" a personal rhetorical habit, or does it reflect a broader apostolic emphasis? Do others teach it? Does the modern prophetic witness bring anything new — or does it simply confirm what the text already shows?

---

## What We Found

Short answer: it's not Holland's habit. It's the apostolic voice doing what it has always done — standing at the hinge between suffering and hope, between cost and choice, and saying *nevertheless.*

Longer answer: the pattern appears across decades (Maxwell in 2001, Holland in 1989 and 2009 and 2021, Eyring in 2021 and 2025, Nelson in 2021, Stevenson in 2020, Hales in 2024, and a chorus of others). But modern prophets don't just echo the scripture. They do at least five things the text alone doesn't do.

---

## Holland's "Nevertheless" — Three Decades of One Theme

If anyone owns this word in modern discourse, it's Jeffrey R. Holland. But it's not a tic. It's a theological commitment that spans his entire ministry.

### "He Loved Them unto the End" (October 1989)

Holland's first general conference talk as a newly called Seventy. He walks through the final hours of Christ's life — the washing of feet, the Last Supper, the Garden, the cross — and arrives at the darkest moment:

> "Because he must ultimately tread this winepress of redemption unaided, can he endure the darkest moment of them all, the shock of the greatest pain? This comes not with thorns and with nails, but with the terror of feeling utterly alone: 'Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani? … My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?' Can he bear all of our sins *and* our fear and loneliness too? He did and he does and he will." — [Elder Jeffrey R. Holland, "He Loved Them unto the End," October 1989](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1989/10/he-loved-them-unto-the-end.md)

He doesn't use the word *nevertheless* here. But the pattern is fully present: "Can he?" is the cost. "He did and he does and he will" is the nevertheless. The question-and-answer structure carries the same grammatical weight: doubt → affirmation. Everything before the question mark is the agony. Everything after is the choice that survived it.

The talk centers on [John 13:1](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/john/13.md) — "Having loved his own which were in the world, he loved them unto the end." That sentence IS a nevertheless structure: *having faced* all of this, *having known* what was coming, he loved them *to the end.* The love is not diminished by the cost. Not the less.

Holland closes with Moroni and the pure love of Christ: "one thing only never faileth — and that is the pure love of Christ." Charity as the ultimate nevertheless. Love that never fails IS the never-the-less of God.

### "None Were with Him" (April 2009)

Twenty years later, Holland returns to the same night — but now he tracks something specific: the *shrinking circle of support.* The crowds abandon Him, the priests condemn Him, Pilate washes his hands, even the disciples flee. The circle tightens and tightens until it's Christ alone.

Then Holland reaches the theological crux and uses the word:

> "Nevertheless, that the supreme sacrifice of His Son might be as complete as it was voluntary and solitary, the Father briefly withdrew from Jesus the comfort of His Spirit, the support of His personal presence." — [Elder Jeffrey R. Holland, "None Were with Him," April 2009](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2009/04/none-were-with-him.md)

This is not a scripture quote. This is Holland's own apostolic commentary. He *chose* this word himself, at the moment he believes was hardest — the withdrawal of the Father's presence. And look at how it works: the Father was never closer (Holland says, "it is my personal belief that in all of Christ's mortal ministry the Father may never have been closer to His Son than in these agonizing final moments"), *nevertheless* He withdrew His comfort.

This is a different kind of nevertheless than scripture's. In [Luke 22:42](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/22.md) and [D&C 19](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/19.md), Christ speaks the "nevertheless." In Holland's commentary, it's the *Father's* "nevertheless" — the choice to withdraw comfort from the Son He loves most, because the sacrifice required it.

Then Holland inverts it. Christ's solitary suffering → *our* never being alone:

> "Because Jesus walked such a long, lonely path utterly alone, *we* do not have to do so." — [Elder Jeffrey R. Holland, "None Were with Him," April 2009](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2009/04/none-were-with-him.md)

This is the apostolic addition Part 1 didn't capture: "nevertheless" as the bridge between Christ's abandonment and our consolation. He was alone *so that* we aren't. His solitude IS our companionship. That's not stated anywhere in scripture that directly — Holland draws the connection.

### "Not as the World Giveth" (April 2021)

Holland ties Christ's "nevertheless" to peace:

> "He would nevertheless rise, as the psalmist said, to be 'our refuge and strength, [our] very present help in [times of] trouble.'" — [Elder Jeffrey R. Holland, "Not as the World Giveth," April 2021](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2021/04/23holland.md)

Slaughtered like a sheep → *nevertheless* rose to be our refuge. And then Holland does something remarkable: he connects Joseph Smith's Liberty Jail cry — "O God, where art thou?" — to Christ's "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?", calling it Joseph's "own version of the cry." Liberty Jail as the Restoration's Gethsemane. The "nevertheless" of [D&C 121–122](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/121.md) parallels the Savior's — both cry out, both endure, both are given future consolation.

Across three decades, Holland's pattern is consistent: he always frames the full cost first. He makes you feel the abandonment, the betrayal, the slaughter. He does not rush to the comfort. And then — *nevertheless.* The word lands harder because you've been made to wait for it.

## Eyring's "Nevertheless" — Proving and Revelation

Henry B. Eyring brings a different angle. If Holland's "nevertheless" is rhetorical and emotional, Eyring's is practical and experiential.

### Proving as Evidence of Love (October 2025)

In [Proved and Strengthened in Christ](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2025/10/47eyring.md), Eyring builds his entire talk on the idea that trials don't just test — they *strengthen*. He weaves four witnesses: Moroni alone but writing for us, Jacob suffering but consecrated, Joseph in Liberty Jail but exalted, Christ in Gethsemane but finishing His work.

At the center sits [2 Nephi 2:1–3](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md), Lehi speaking to young Jacob:

> "Nevertheless, Jacob, my firstborn in the wilderness, thou knowest the greatness of God; and he shall consecrate thine afflictions for thy gain." — [2 Nephi 2:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md)

This verse appeared in Part 1's catalogue, but Eyring *adds* something the scripture doesn't say explicitly:

> "Proving moments are not evidence that the Lord has abandoned you. Rather, they are evidence that He loves you enough to refine and strengthen you." — [President Henry B. Eyring, "Proved and Strengthened in Christ," October 2025](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2025/10/47eyring.md)

That's the apostolic contribution. Scripture says God will *consecrate* your afflictions. Eyring says the trial itself is *evidence of love.* The "nevertheless" isn't just survival despite the trial — the trial IS the "nevertheless" mechanism. It's how God loves you into strength. Proving is not the barrier to the blessing; it's the path to it.

### The Posture of Revelation (October 2021)

In [The Faith to Ask and Then to Act](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2021/10/38eyring.md), Eyring takes Christ's Gethsemane prayer and turns it inward — into the *posture we need for revelation:*

> "My personal experience is that the still, small voice—which is real—is clear and discernible in my mind when I feel an internal quiet and submission to the Lord's will. That feeling of humility can be best described as 'Not my will, but thine, be done.'" — [President Henry B. Eyring, "The Faith to Ask and Then to Act," October 2021](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2021/10/38eyring.md)

In scripture, "not my will, but thine" is what Christ says in [Luke 22:42](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/22.md) under the weight of the Atonement. Eyring says it's also what *we* need to say — not in extremity, but every time we seek the Spirit. The Gethsemane "nevertheless" becomes daily practice. It's not rare courage reserved for crisis. It's the orientation we need to hear God at all.

This is one of the most unexpected findings in the study: Eyring democratizes the "nevertheless." He takes the word that holds eternity together and says, *you need this too, every day, every prayer.* The posture of revelation and the posture of Gethsemane are the same posture.

## Nelson's "Nevertheless" — The Prophet's Present Tense

When a prophet speaks, he doesn't just teach doctrine — he speaks it into the specific moment his people are living through. That's exactly what President Nelson does in [What We Are Learning and Will Never Forget](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2021/04/36nelson.md), delivered during COVID-19:

> "Ye are little children, and ye have not as yet understood how great blessings the Father hath … prepared for you; And ye cannot bear all things now; nevertheless, be of good cheer, for I will lead you along." — [D&C 78:17–18](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/78.md)

Then Nelson adds his prophetic witness: "My dear brothers, I testify that He has been, and *is*, indeed leading us along."

The structure of [D&C 78:18](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/78.md) is unique among the "nevertheless" scriptures: it begins not with sin or rebellion or suffering, but with *limitation.* "Ye cannot bear all things now." You're not ready yet. You don't understand yet. And then: *nevertheless, be of good cheer.* The "nevertheless" here says: your unreadiness doesn't cancel the blessing. It doesn't cancel the leading. Not the less.

Nelson makes it present tense. That's what prophets do. They take the ancient word and say: this is *now.* This is *you.* The "nevertheless" isn't a historical observation — it's a living promise spoken into a pandemic-stricken priesthood session. The gap between "ye cannot bear" and "I will lead you along" is the gap every person in that audience was sitting in. Nelson puts the "nevertheless" bridge right across it.

## The Broader Chorus

Holland, Eyring, and Nelson are the heavyweight voices. But the pattern appears far more broadly than three speakers.

### The D&C 19 Cluster — "Nevertheless, Glory Be to the Father"

The most-quoted "nevertheless" in modern conference talks may be [D&C 19:18–19](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/19.md): "would that I might not drink the bitter cup, and shrink — Nevertheless, glory be to the Father, and I partook and finished my preparations unto the children of men."

At least five conference speakers in recent years have built on this exact passage:
- [Elder Marcos A. Aidukaitis, "Lift Up Your Heart and Rejoice," April 2022](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2022/04/25aidukaitis.md) — applies Christ's "nevertheless" to the sacrifice of missionary service
- [Elder W. Christopher Waddell, "Just as He Did," April 2019](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2019/04/15waddell.md)
- [Elder Christopher H. Kim, "Harden Not Your Heart," April 2025](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2025/04/54kim.md)
- [Elder Ulisses Soares, "Adorned with the Virtue of Temperance," October 2025](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2025/10/43soares.md)

These speakers reach for this verse because D&C 19 is the *only* place in scripture where Christ Himself narrates His own "nevertheless" in first person. Luke reports it. Matthew paraphrases it. But in D&C 19, Jesus tells Joseph Smith directly — *I* trembled, *I* bled, *I* would that I might not — *nevertheless, glory be to the Father, and I partook.* The modern speakers aren't just quoting a verse. They're standing with Christ's own first-person account of what "nevertheless" cost.

Aidukaitis does something particularly interesting: he applies it to youth facing mission calls. Christ didn't want to drink the cup — *nevertheless.* Youth don't want to leave school, romance, career — *nevertheless.* He scales the pattern from cosmic to personal. The same word that holds the Atonement together holds a nineteen-year-old's decision together.

### The Nephi 1:1 Cluster — "Nevertheless, Having Been Highly Favored"

[1 Nephi 1:1](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/1-ne/1.md) — "having seen many afflictions in the course of my days, nevertheless, having been highly favored of the Lord in all my days" — appears in at least four conference talks:
- [Elder Gary E. Stevenson, "Highly Favored of the Lord," October 2020](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2020/10/53stevenson.md) — builds the entire talk around it
- [Elder Moisés Villanueva, "Favored of the Lord in All My Days," October 2021](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2021/10/28villanueva.md)
- [Elder Jorge F. Zeballos, "Building a Life Resistant to the Adversary," October 2022](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2022/10/27zeballos.md)
- [Elder Aroldo B. Cavalcante, "The Wind Did Never Cease to Blow," October 2024](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2024/10/27cavalcante.md)

Stevenson built his entire COVID-era talk around Nephi's "nevertheless." His argument: "Times of affliction and disappointment do not change the watchful eye of the Lord as He favorably looks upon us, blessing us." The "nevertheless" becomes a doctrinal claim about God's constancy — the afflictions don't change the favor. Not the less.

But Stevenson adds something the scripture doesn't explicitly say: the "nevertheless" is *prospective,* not just retrospective. God didn't just help Nephi look back and see favor. He prepared tools — home-centered church, ministering adjustments — *before* the pandemic trial arrived. The "nevertheless" was already in place when the affliction hit. That's a prophet-seer framing the pattern forward.

### The Personal Witness — Hales's "Nevertheless"

Perhaps the most striking use comes from [Elder Brook P. Hales, "Mortality Works!" October 2024](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2024/10/43hales.md). After quoting both [2 Nephi 2:2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/2.md) ("Nevertheless, [God] shall consecrate thine afflictions for thy gain") and [Mosiah 23:21–22](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/23.md) ("Nevertheless—whosoever putteth his trust in [the Lord] the same shall be lifted up at the last day"), Hales makes the word *personal:*

> "As a youth, I personally experienced great emotional pain and shame that came as the result of the unrighteous actions of another, which for many years affected my self-worth and my sense of worthiness before the Lord. Nevertheless, I bear personal witness that the Lord can strengthen us and bear us up in whatever difficulties we are called upon to experience." — [Elder Brook P. Hales, "Mortality Works!" October 2024](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2024/10/43hales.md)

This is a General Authority standing at the conference pulpit and using "nevertheless" as his own hinge — not quoting Nephi, not citing D&C 19, but speaking his own pain and his own testimony with the word that bridges them. Childhood trauma → *nevertheless* → personal witness. The scriptural pattern becomes autobiographical.

His mother's story carries the same shape: a lifetime of physical pain, financial hardship, raising an orphaned granddaughter — and after death, a message from the spirit world: "I want you to know that mortality works, and I want you to know that I now understand why everything happened the way it did—and it is all OK." That's the "nevertheless" that only becomes visible after the mortal trial is complete. [Hebrews 12:11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/heb/12.md) — "nevertheless afterward it yieldeth the peaceable fruit of righteousness" — given in plain English from a mother's spirit.

### The Surrender Witnesses

Several speakers approach "nevertheless" not through the word itself but through the Gethsemane surrender it captures:

[Neill F. Marriott, "Yielding Our Hearts to God," October 2015](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2015/10/yielding-our-hearts-to-god.md) embodies the pattern without ever using the word. After her 21-year-old daughter Georgia's death, the family's motto — "It will all work out" — was tested to its limit. Marriott specifies: the motto "doesn't say, 'It will all work out *now.*' It speaks of our hope in the eternal outcome—not necessarily of present results." This IS the temporal "nevertheless" — [Hebrews 12:11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/heb/12.md)'s "nevertheless *afterward.*" The present suffering doesn't determine the end. You hold on through the gap you can't see past.

[Elder Hugo Montoya, "The Eternal Principle of Love," October 2022](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2022/10/16montoya.md) places [Matthew 26:39](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/26.md) — "nevertheless not as I will, but as thou wilt" — at the center of a talk about love. His apostolic contribution: "the supreme demonstration of God's love for us happened in Gethsemane." The "nevertheless" isn't just obedience or endurance. It's the language of love. Christ's surrender was an act of love before it was an act of will.

[Elder Neal A. Maxwell, "The Seventh Commandment: A Shield," October 2001](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2001/10/the-seventh-commandment-a-shield.md) connects moral courage — standing alone against a declining culture — to Christ's Gethsemane aloneness: "Our willingness to [stand alone], here and now, is consistent with Christ's kneeling alone, there and then, in Gethsemane. In the final atoning process, 'none were with [Him].'" Maxwell preceded Holland's "None Were with Him" by eight years. The theme of Christ's costly aloneness as model for our costly discipleship flows across apostles and across decades. It's not one man's insight. It's the apostolic witness.

## The Fourth Voice

Part 1 identified three "nevertheless" voices in scripture:
1. **Christ's** — Luke 22:42, D&C 19:18–19, Alma 7:13. The choice to proceed despite the cost.
2. **God's toward us** — Psalm 89, Psalm 106, Judges, Ezekiel, Jeremiah, the D&C recommissions. The refusal to let our rebellion diminish His covenant.
3. **Ours** — 2 Nephi 4:19, Psalm 73, Alma 5:7. The trust that survives our own wretchedness.

The conference talks map to all three. But they also reveal a **fourth voice**: the apostolic witness itself.

When Holland writes, "Nevertheless, that the supreme sacrifice of His Son might be as complete as it was voluntary and solitary, the Father briefly withdrew from Jesus the comfort of His Spirit" — he is not quoting scripture. He's saying *nevertheless* in his own voice about what he has seen and believed as a witness.

When Eyring says, "Proving moments are not evidence that the Lord has abandoned you. Rather, they are evidence that He loves you enough to refine and strengthen you" — that's not a paraphrase of any verse. It's Eyring's apostolic interpretation. He's *adding* to what scripture says.

When Nelson quotes D&C 78:18 and then adds, "He has been, and *is*, indeed leading us along" — the "is" is Nelson's prophetic claim about *right now.* Scripture said "I will lead you along" (future). Nelson says "He *is* leading" (present).

When Hales says, "Nevertheless, I bear personal witness" — that's not Nephi's testimony or Christ's or Paul's. It's *his.*

This fourth voice — the apostolic witness — doesn't replace the scriptural three. It *activates* them. It takes the ancient "nevertheless" and speaks it into specific modern moments: a pandemic, a moral crisis, a season of loneliness, a young person's decision about a mission. The prophet stands at the same hinge scripture stands at — between suffering and hope — and says, *I am a witness that this is true. Nevertheless.*

This may be what Eddy was getting at when he said "there is virtue in the words of ancient and modern prophets *precisely* because their words are the Lord's words" ([Elder Mark D. Eddy, "The Virtue of the Word," October 2022](../../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/2022/10/54eddy.md)). The apostolic "nevertheless" carries the same virtue because it comes from the same source.

## What the Prophets Add

Five things scripture alone doesn't say:

**1. The Inversion.** Holland: Christ was alone *so that* we aren't. His "nevertheless" (choosing to proceed) inverts into our never-ending companionship. Scripture records Christ's choice; Holland draws the line from His solitude to our comfort. "Because Jesus walked such a long, lonely path utterly alone, *we* do not have to do so."

**2. "Nevertheless" as the Posture of Revelation.** Eyring: "Not my will, but thine, be done" isn't just Gethsemane language — it's how we hear the Spirit. The "nevertheless" is daily, not just cosmic. Every prayer that truly listens requires the same surrender Christ made in the Garden.

**3. Proving as Evidence of Love.** Eyring: the trial isn't the barrier to the blessing — it IS the blessing's mechanism. When God proves us, that's the "nevertheless" at work — not evidence of abandonment, but evidence that He loves us enough to refine us.

**4. The Prospective "Nevertheless."** Stevenson: God doesn't just redeem us after the trial. He prepares the "nevertheless" tools *before* the affliction arrives. The prophet's adjustments (home-centered church, ministering) were the "nevertheless" infrastructure pre-positioned for a pandemic no one saw coming. The "nevertheless" is forward-looking.

**5. The Present Tense.** Nelson: the prophet takes scripture's "nevertheless" — which exists in the past tense of completed history — and speaks it into the present moment. "He has been, and *is*, indeed leading us along." This is what prophets do. They close the gap between ancient text and living audience. The "nevertheless" isn't just about what God did. It's about what He is doing *now.*

## An Absence Worth Noting

Part 1 identified a powerful use of "nevertheless" as *commandment* — [D&C 98:41–42](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/98.md): "Nevertheless thou shalt forgive him. And if he trespass against thee the second time, and repent not, nevertheless thou shalt forgive him." The offense repeats, the forgiveness repeats. "Nevertheless" becomes a refusal to let someone else's sin determine your response.

This usage doesn't appear in the conference talks we found. None of the speakers we studied build on "nevertheless thou shalt forgive him" as a teaching. That's a gap in the apostolic discourse — or perhaps it's a topic so personal and so costly that it doesn't lend itself to twenty-minute conference talks. The "nevertheless" of forgiveness may be the one that's taught less and lived more.

---

## Becoming

The [first study](nevertheless.md) ended with three "neverthelesses" — Christ's, God's, ours — and called them "a circuit, a closed loop of mercy." Christ's makes possible God's toward us, which enables ours back to Him.

This second study adds a dimension: the loop isn't just textual. It's *spoken.* Living prophets and apostles stand at the pulpit and say *nevertheless* not because they've memorized a pattern, but because they've witnessed one. Holland saw something about Christ's lonely sacrifice that compelled him to say "nevertheless" in his own voice, not quoting anyone. Eyring felt something about prayer's posture that made him reach for "Not my will, but thine" as a daily description, not just a Gethsemane reference. Hales lived through childhood trauma and found that "nevertheless, I bear personal witness" was the only honest hinge between the pain and the testimony.

What does this mean for how you live?

Maybe this: you have a "nevertheless" to speak too. Not just Nephi's or Paul's or Holland's — yours. The pattern isn't only found in scriptures or in conference. It's found every time someone looks at the weight of their own failure, weakness, suffering, or loss and says — *nevertheless, I trust. Nevertheless, I go forward. Nevertheless, I know in whom I have trusted.*

Part 1 said the word that holds the Atonement together is *nevertheless.* Part 2 says: the witnesses keep saying it because they keep seeing it. And they need you to see it too.
