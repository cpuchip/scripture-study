# Nevertheless

*Not the less; notwithstanding; in spite of that.* — Webster 1828

---

## The Binding Question

What does "nevertheless" actually mean, and why does scripture use it at every hinge point between ruin and rescue?

---

## The Word Itself

We don't use this word much anymore. Modern English has "but" and "however" and "still." They're fine words. They're not this word.

"Nevertheless" is a compound: **never** + **the** + **less.** Literally: *not ever to a lesser degree.* When scripture says "nevertheless," it's making a specific claim: the thing that came before — the pain, the rebellion, the cost, the darkness — subtracts *nothing* from what comes after. Not diminished. Not even a little.

"But" introduces contrast. "Nevertheless" introduces *survival.* Something endures despite what should have destroyed it.

Webster's 1828 dictionary defines it simply: *"Not the less; notwithstanding; in spite of that; yet."* And then he does something remarkable — he illustrates it with [Hebrews 12:11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/heb/12.md):

> "No chastening for the present seemeth to be joyous, but grievous: nevertheless afterward it yieldeth the peaceable fruit of righteousness."

Present grief → *nevertheless* → future fruit. Webster, in 1828, reached for this word and landed in the middle of the Atonement. Because that's where this word lives.

---

## Christ's Nevertheless

When Jesus speaks this word, He is choosing. The cost is real. The resistance is real. And the word that holds eternity together is *nevertheless.*

### Gethsemane

> "Father, if thou be willing, remove this cup from me: nevertheless not my will, but thine, be done." — [Luke 22:42](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/22.md)

He wanted it to stop. He asked for it to stop. And then: *nevertheless.* Not my will. Thine. Everything before that word is the cost. Everything after it is the choice.

### D&C 19

The Savior Himself tells us what Gethsemane was:

> "Which suffering caused myself, even God, the greatest of all, to tremble because of pain, and to bleed at every pore, and to suffer both body and spirit — and would that I might not drink the bitter cup, and shrink — Nevertheless, glory be to the Father, and I partook and finished my preparations unto the children of men." — [D&C 19:18-19](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/19.md)

Look at the emphasis on identity: "myself, even God, the greatest of all." He wants us to know *who* is trembling. And then the dash — "and shrink —" — followed by *nevertheless.* The dash is the pause between the agony and the choice. The "nevertheless" is the bridge across it.

The suffering didn't make the choice lesser. Not the less.

(See the [How Is It Done?](how-is-it-done.md) study and [Eternity Paused](../plan-of-salvation/eternity-paused.md) for the full exploration of this moment.)

### Alma 7:13

> "Now the Spirit knoweth all things; nevertheless the Son of God suffereth according to the flesh." — [Alma 7:13](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/7.md)

This one moves in a different direction. Omniscience → *nevertheless* → chosen suffering. He already knew everything. He could have succored us from perfect knowledge alone. He chose to know by experience. The omniscience didn't make the suffering unnecessary. Not the less.

## God's Nevertheless Toward Us

This is the one that wrecked me when I started counting.

When we read the Old Testament, we see a cycle that repeats so many times it almost becomes wallpaper: Israel rebels, God punishes, Israel cries out, God delivers. Over and over and over. The word that holds that cycle together is *nevertheless.* And it's God's word — spoken about a people who keep breaking His heart.

### Psalm 89

God lays out the scenario with complete clarity:

> "If his children forsake my law, and walk not in my judgments; if they break my statutes, and keep not my commandments; then will I visit their transgression with the rod, and their iniquity with stripes. Nevertheless my lovingkindness will I not utterly take from him, nor suffer my faithfulness to fail. My covenant will I not break, nor alter the thing that is gone out of my lips." — [Psalm 89:30-34](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/89.md)

Children forsake, break, refuse. Consequences come. And then: *nevertheless my lovingkindness will I not utterly take from him.* The rebellion doesn't diminish the love. Not the less. "My covenant will I not break." Our breaking doesn't trigger His breaking.

### Psalm 106 — The "Nevertheless" Psalm

This entire psalm is one long "nevertheless." It catalogs Israel's rebellion — provoking God at the Red Sea, forgetting His works, making the golden calf, sacrificing children to idols, polluting the land with blood — and then:

> "Nevertheless he saved them for his name's sake, that he might make his mighty power to be known." — [Psalm 106:8](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/106.md)

Forty verses later, after the rebellion has gotten *worse*:

> "Many times did he deliver them; but they provoked him with their counsel, and were brought low for their iniquity. Nevertheless he regarded their affliction, when he heard their cry: And he remembered for them his covenant." — [Psalm 106:43-45](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/106.md)

*Nevertheless he regarded their affliction.* After everything. After the idols, the whoring, the sacrificed children. He heard their cry and He remembered His covenant. Not the less. Not one bit less.

### Judges, Ezekiel, Jeremiah

The pattern repeats across voices and centuries:

Israel does evil, serves Baal, abandons God — "Nevertheless the Lord raised up judges, which delivered them out of the hand of those that spoiled them" ([Judges 2:16](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/judg/2.md)). Not because they deserved it. Because He is who He is.

Israel rebels in the wilderness, pollutes the sabbaths — "then I said, I would pour out my fury upon them... Nevertheless mine eye spared them from destroying them, neither did I make an end of them" ([Ezekiel 20:13,17](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ezek/20.md)). Twice in one chapter. Rebellion → fury intended → *nevertheless* → hand withdrawn. The anger is real. The restraint is also real. "Nevertheless" is the space between justice and mercy where God makes His choice. And He keeps choosing mercy.

Destruction is coming for Judah — "Nevertheless in those days, saith the Lord, I will not make a full end with you" ([Jeremiah 5:18](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/jer/5.md)). Not a full end. Even in judgment, preservation. The "not a full end" is the nevertheless of divine restraint.

### The Restoration's Nevertheless

In the Doctrine and Covenants, the Lord speaks this word to imperfect people with stunning directness:

To Joseph Smith, less than four months after the Church's organization: "I have lifted thee up out of thine afflictions, and have counseled thee, that thou hast been delivered from all thine enemies, and thou hast been delivered from the powers of Satan and from darkness! Nevertheless, thou art not excusable in thy transgressions; nevertheless, go thy way and sin no more" ([D&C 24:1-2](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/24.md)).

*Two* neverthelessses in one verse. The first: deliverance does not erase accountability. The second: accountability does not prevent forward motion. God holds both truths at once. You are not excused. *And* you are not dismissed. Go.

To William McLellin: "And he sinned; nevertheless, I forgive him and say unto him again, Go ye into the south countries" ([D&C 75:8](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/75.md)). The sin is named directly. The forgiveness is given directly. And then — a recommission. The sin didn't revoke the calling.

And then the Lord commands *us* to become His "nevertheless" for others:

> "And if he trespass against thee and repent not the first time, nevertheless thou shalt forgive him. And if he trespass against thee the second time, and repent not, nevertheless thou shalt forgive him." — [D&C 98:41-42](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/98.md)

The offense repeats. The forgiveness repeats. "Nevertheless" becomes a commandment — a refusal to let someone else's sin determine your response. We are told to *be* the nevertheless.

## Our Nevertheless

And then there's the nevertheless we speak — when we look at our own wretchedness, our own darkness, our own smallness, and choose trust anyway.

### Nephi's Psalm

> "Nevertheless, notwithstanding the great goodness of the Lord, in showing me his great and marvelous works, my heart exclaimeth: O wretched man that I am! Yea, my heart sorroweth because of my flesh; my soul grieveth because of mine iniquities." — [2 Nephi 4:17](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/4.md)

Here "nevertheless" carries the crash. Gratitude → *nevertheless* → wretchedness. He knows God's goodness and he's *still* devastated by his own weakness. Two verses later:

> "And when I desire to rejoice, my heart groaneth because of my sins; nevertheless, I know in whom I have trusted." — [2 Nephi 4:19](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/2-ne/4.md)

The second "nevertheless" overcomes the first. Verse 17: *nevertheless* I am wretched. Verse 19: *nevertheless* I know in whom I have trusted. Two neverthelessses, two verses apart, and the second one wins. This is the Atonement working in real time in a real soul. The wretchedness was real. The trust was realer.

### Psalm 73

Asaph writes his own version:

> "So foolish was I, and ignorant: I was as a beast before thee. Nevertheless I am continually with thee: thou hast holden me by my right hand. Thou shalt guide me with thy counsel, and afterward receive me to glory." — [Psalm 73:22-24](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ps/73.md)

Foolish. Ignorant. Beast-like. *Nevertheless* — continually held by God's right hand. My worst view of myself doesn't change His grip. The self-knowledge doesn't diminish the hold. Not the less.

### Alma 5:7

> "They were in the midst of darkness; nevertheless, their souls were illuminated by the light of the everlasting word." — [Alma 5:7](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/alma/5.md)

Darkness → *nevertheless* → illumination. The darkness didn't diminish the light. The light didn't wait for the darkness to clear. It broke through. Not the less.

### Alma Himself

Alma, who had been "caught in a snare" and done abominable things, testifies:

> "I myself was caught in a snare, and did many things which were abominable in the sight of the Lord, which caused me sore repentance; Nevertheless, after much tribulation, the Lord did hear my cries, and did answer my prayers, and has made me an instrument in his hands... Nevertheless, in this I do not glory, for I am unworthy to glory of myself." — [Mosiah 23:9-11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/23.md)

Three neverthelessses in three verses: sin → *nevertheless* → God heard → instrument → *nevertheless* → no personal glory. The word keeps Alma honest in *both* directions — neither trapped in his past nor puffed up by his present. "Nevertheless" doesn't just rescue. It calibrates.

### Hebrews 12:11

> "Now no chastening for the present seemeth to be joyous, but grievous: nevertheless afterward it yieldeth the peaceable fruit of righteousness." — [Hebrews 12:11](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/heb/12.md)

The "nevertheless" of *time.* Present grief → *nevertheless* → future fruit. The present doesn't determine the end. What seems grievous *now* yields peace *afterward.* The word asks us to trust across a gap we can't see past.

## The Double Nevertheless

This pattern appeared in the previous study and it became even more visible here. In several passages, scripture places two neverthelessses close together — and the pair creates a bridge. You enter from one side, cross the valley, and emerge on the other.

**2 Nephi 4:17, 19** — Gratitude → *nevertheless* → wretchedness → *nevertheless* → trust. The second redeems the first.

**Mosiah 23:21-22** — Prosperity → "*Nevertheless* the Lord seeth fit to chasten his people" → *Nevertheless* → "whosoever putteth his trust in him the same shall be lifted up at the last day." Chastening is real. The promise is realer.

**D&C 24:2** — Deliverance → "*Nevertheless*, thou art not excusable" → "*nevertheless*, go thy way and sin no more." Accountability and forward motion, held in one breath.

In every case, the first "nevertheless" names the hard truth. The second "nevertheless" names the way through. Together they refuse to collapse reality into either despair or denial. Both things are true. Both things stand. The bridge is built from two neverthelessses.

## Proverbs 19:21

One more. Almost a capstone:

> "There are many devices in a man's heart; nevertheless the counsel of the Lord, that shall stand." — [Proverbs 19:21](../../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/prov/19.md)

Human plans scatter. Intentions fragment. Hearts are full of devices. *Nevertheless* — the counsel of the Lord stands. Our instability doesn't diminish His constancy. Not the less.

---

## Becoming

You said this word hit you two ways, and I think it's actually three.

The first is Christ's: He looks at the cost and says *nevertheless.* The agony, the blood, the loneliness — nevertheless, glory be to the Father, and I partook and finished. His "nevertheless" is what holds eternity together. Without it, there is no Atonement.

The second is God's toward us: He looks at our rebellion — the cycles, the forgetting, the golden calves we keep building — and says *nevertheless.* My lovingkindness will I not utterly take from him. Nevertheless he regarded their affliction when he heard their cry. His "nevertheless" is why we aren't consumed.

The third is ours: We look at our own wretchedness — the things we can't fix, the sins that keep recurring, the distance between who we are and who we want to be — and say *nevertheless, I know in whom I have trusted.* Our "nevertheless" is faith. It's the choice to trust despite the evidence of our own failure.

And here's the thing that stops me cold: all three are the same grace. Christ's nevertheless *makes possible* God's nevertheless toward us, which *enables* our nevertheless back to Him. The word is a circuit. A closed loop of mercy.

When you kneel and it's hard — when the weight of your own inadequacy makes you wonder why you bother — the word is *nevertheless.* Not a denial that the weight is real. Not a promise that it will vanish. Just: *not the less.* God's love is not diminished by your failure. His covenant is not broken by your breaking. His grip on your hand is not loosened by your stumbling.

And you are asked — commanded, even — to be that word for others. *Nevertheless* thou shalt forgive him. *Nevertheless,* go thy way. The trespasses of the people around you do not diminish the call to love them. Not the less.

The word that holds the Atonement together is not *atonement.* It's *nevertheless.*
