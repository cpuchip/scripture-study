# The Stewardship Pattern — How God Delegates

*Study date: March 22, 2026*
*Born from: the March 22 Sabbath session, where the delegation irony surfaced — effective at delegating to AI agents, failing at delegating to human councilors*
*Study mode: study (phased writing with externalized memory)*

---

## The Binding Question

How does God delegate, and what does His pattern teach about presiding over human stewardships — especially when you're better at delegating to machines than to people?

---

## I. The Problem — Why Delegation to Humans Is Hard

Elder Ballard once described what he called "the one-cylinder ward":

> "The one-cylinder ward is the ward where the bishop handles all of the problems, makes all of the decisions, and follows through on all of the assignments. Then, like an overworked cylinder in a car engine, he is soon burned out." ([Counseling with Our Councils](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1994/04/counseling-with-our-councils.md), Apr 1994)

That description landed because it's a mirror. Sunday School president with two counselors and a secretary, and the presidency meetings aren't happening. The plans aren't being made. The assignments aren't going out. Not because the councilors are unwilling — but because the president never asks. He's running on one cylinder.

Meanwhile, on the other side of the desk, the same person delegates to AI agents with crisp efficiency. Clear specifications. Immediate feedback loops. No relational ambiguity. Machines execute what you tell them. They don't have bad weeks. They don't need to be asked how they're doing. They don't require trust-building before they'll do their best work.

The irony is sharp enough to cut: delegating to machines is easy because machines don't require covenant. They don't require relationship. They don't require you to be vulnerable. Human delegation requires all three — and that's exactly why it's harder, and exactly why God keeps commanding it.

The Lord put the pacing principle right next to the delegation principle. D&C 10:4: "Do not run faster or labor more than you have strength and means provided to enable you to translate; but be diligent unto the end" ([D&C 10:4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/10.md)). The Exodus 18 footnotes cross-reference this to [Mosiah 4:27](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/4.md): "It is not requisite that a man should run faster than he has strength." The stewardship crisis and the pacing crisis are the same crisis. If you're running too fast, you're probably not delegating. If you're not delegating, you will surely run too fast.

So: how does God delegate? The scriptures give us not one model but several — each adding a dimension the others lack.


## II. Jethro and Moses — "The Thing That Thou Doest Is Not Good" (Exodus 18)

Moses is doing everything himself, from morning to evening. The people stand around him all day waiting for judgment. Then Jethro arrives — Moses' father-in-law, a Midianite priest who, according to the JST, held Melchizedek priesthood authority ([Ex 18:1b](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md); cf. [D&C 84:6-16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/84.md)). Jethro is an outsider with priesthood authority who sees what Moses can't see from inside the work.

His diagnosis is immediate and blunt: "The thing that thou doest is not good" ([Ex 18:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)).

Not "you should consider adjusting your approach." Not "have you thought about maybe..." The thing you're doing is not good. And then the reason — which has two victims:

> "Thou wilt surely wear away, both thou, and this people that is with thee: for this thing is too heavy for thee; thou art not able to perform it thyself alone." ([Ex 18:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md))

Both *thou* and *this people*. Burnout doesn't just destroy the leader. It starves the people who need more than one exhausted man can provide.

Jethro's solution is structural: "provide out of all the people able men, such as fear God, men of truth, hating covetousness; and place such over them, to be rulers of thousands, and rulers of hundreds, rulers of fifties, and rulers of tens" ([Ex 18:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)). The selection criteria are character-first: able, God-fearing, truth-loving, covetousness-hating. Competence is implied. Character is stated.

The escalation protocol is simple: "every great matter they shall bring unto thee, but every small matter they shall judge" ([Ex 18:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)). Small things handled locally. Big things go up.

But Jethro doesn't strip Moses of his prophetic function. Verses 19-20 preserve what only Moses can do: "Be thou for the people to God-ward, that thou mayest bring the causes unto God" and "thou shalt teach them ordinances and laws." The prophet still teaches and mediates with God. He just stops trying to judge every dispute himself.

One detail that's easy to miss: Jethro conditions his advice. "If thou shalt do this thing, *and God command thee so*, then thou shalt be able to endure" ([Ex 18:23](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)). Good counsel still requires divine confirmation. Even the right organizational pattern needs God's endorsement.


## III. Christ and the Twelve — Delegation as Empowerment, Not Offloading

When Christ sends the Twelve, the pattern is different from Jethro's. Jethro reorganized. Christ empowers.

> "And when he had called unto him his twelve disciples, he gave them power against unclean spirits, to cast them out, and to heal all manner of sickness and all manner of disease." ([Matt 10:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/10.md))

Power first. Then boundaries — "Go not into the way of the Gentiles... but go rather to the lost sheep of the house of Israel" ([Matt 10:5-6](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/10.md)). Then tools — "Heal the sick, cleanse the lepers, raise the dead, cast out devils: freely ye have received, freely give" ([Matt 10:8](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/10.md)). Then identity framing — "I send you forth as sheep in the midst of wolves: be ye therefore wise as serpents, and harmless as doves" ([Matt 10:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/10.md)).

The sequence matters. Authority → scope → capacity → identity. He doesn't start with the task list. He starts with the authority to do it.

Christ also warns them of failure. They will be persecuted, delivered up, hated ([Matt 10:17-18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/10.md)). Delegation in God's pattern isn't protecting the delegate from hardship. It's equipping them for it. And the provision comes not from the delegator managing from the back, but from the Spirit: "It is not ye that speak, but the Spirit of your Father which speaketh in you" ([Matt 10:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/10.md)).

Then the authority chain: "He that receiveth you receiveth me, and he that receiveth me receiveth him that sent me" ([Matt 10:40](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/matt/10.md)). The delegate carries the delegator's authority. The people's response to the delegate IS their response to Christ. Luke's version makes this even more explicit: "He that heareth you heareth me; and he that despiseth you despiseth me" ([Luke 10:16](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/10.md)).

When Christ sends the Seventy, He adds two more elements. First, partnership: they go "two and two" ([Luke 10:1](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/10.md)). Delegation into isolation is not the Lord's way. Second, the motivation reframe. The Seventy return joyful — "Lord, even the devils are subject unto us through thy name!" ([Luke 10:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/10.md)). Christ's correction is immediate: "Notwithstanding in this rejoice not, that the spirits are subject unto you; but rather rejoice, because your names are written in heaven" ([Luke 10:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/nt/luke/10.md)).

Performance-based delegation is fragile. Identity-based delegation is durable. Don't rejoice that the task went well. Rejoice that you belong to God.


## IV. Abraham 4-5 — The Gods Watched Until They Obeyed

Abraham's creation account uses language that none of the other creation accounts use. The Gods *counsel*. They *watch*. They *order* and then verify.

> "And the Gods took counsel among themselves and said: Let us go down and form man in our image, after our likeness" ([Abr 4:26](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/4.md))

Delegation begins with shared deliberation. "Took counsel among themselves" — Webster 1828 defines counsel as "interchange of opinions; mutual advising; consultation." Not one God directing and others executing. An interchange. The Gods planned together before they acted.

Then the execution pattern:

> "And the Gods watched those things which they had ordered until they obeyed." ([Abr 4:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/4.md))

This is oversight without micromanagement. They ordered. They watched. They waited for obedience. They didn't step in and do the work themselves. Webster 1828 defines *preside* as "to exercise superintendence; to watch over." Presiding IS watching. Abraham 4:18 is a definition of presiding.

> "And the Gods saw that they would be obeyed, and that their plan was good." ([Abr 4:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/4.md))

Evaluation has two criteria: obedience AND goodness. Not just "did they comply?" but "is the direction right?" Compliance without goodness is bureaucracy. Goodness without compliance is disorder.

Abraham 5 is the execution phase: "The Gods came down and formed these the generations of the heavens and of the earth" ([Abr 5:4](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/5.md)). Everything executed "according to all that which they had said concerning every plant of the field before it was in the earth" ([Abr 5:5](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/5.md)). Spiritual creation precedes physical creation. Planning precedes execution. Specification before implementation.

And then a moment of genuine delegation of creative authority:

> "And out of the ground the Gods formed every beast of the field, and every fowl of the air, and brought them unto Adam to see what he would call them; and whatsoever Adam called every living creature, that should be the name thereof." ([Abr 5:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/pgp/abr/5.md))

The Gods don't name the animals. Adam does. And whatever Adam calls them — that's the name. This isn't task delegation. It's creative authority. Adam's naming sticks. The Gods honor his choices.


## V. Alma's Organization — One Priest to Every Fifty

Alma organizes a church in the wilderness, at the waters of Mormon, near a fountain of pure water and a thicket of small trees. What he builds there is remarkable for its simplicity and its constraints.

> "Alma, having authority from God, ordained priests; even one priest to every fifty of their number did he ordain to preach unto them, and to teach them concerning the things pertaining to the kingdom of God." ([Mosiah 18:18](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18.md))

One to fifty. The same ratio Jethro prescribed for "rulers of fifties" in Exodus 18:21 — same ratio, same delegation function, different dispensation. Whether that's coincidence or revealed structure, the scale principle is clear: one person cannot hold fifty people's spiritual needs alone, and fifty people is about the limit of what one person can serve.

Alma constrains what the priests may teach:

> "He commanded them that they should teach nothing save it were the things which he had taught, and which had been spoken by the mouth of the holy prophets." ([Mosiah 18:19](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18.md))

Delegation with doctrinal guardrails. Not creative freedom — faithful transmission. "Nothing preached in all the churches except it were repentance and faith in God" ([Mosiah 25:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/25.md)). The message is constrained so the structure can multiply without fragmenting.

But the structure isn't bureaucratic. It's covenantal:

> "He commanded them that there should be no contention one with another, but that they should look forward with one eye, having one faith and one baptism, having their hearts knit together in unity and in love one towards another." ([Mosiah 18:21](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18.md))

Hearts knit in unity and love. The organizational structure is embedded in covenant relationship. And the priests are self-supporting: "the priests whom he had ordained should labor with their own hands for their support" ([Mosiah 18:24](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18.md)). No clergy class. No dependent hierarchy. And their compensation is spiritual: "for their labor they were to receive the grace of God, that they might wax strong in the Spirit" ([Mosiah 18:26](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/18.md)).

When the church scales, the same principles hold. Mosiah 25 records the moment it becomes structurally unavoidable:

> "There were so many people that they could not all be governed by one teacher; neither could they all hear the word of God in one assembly." ([Mosiah 25:20](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/25.md))

The plainest rationale for delegation in all of scripture. One person cannot hold it. You must multiply. But structural multiplicity doesn't break unity: "notwithstanding there being many churches they were all one church" ([Mosiah 25:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/25.md)).

Then Mosiah 26 gives us the most beautiful escalation chain in scripture. Sinners are "brought before the priests, and delivered up unto the priests by the teachers; and the priests brought them before Alma, who was the high priest" ([Mosiah 26:7](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/26.md)). And Alma, facing a case he'd never seen before, takes it to king Mosiah. Mosiah sends it back: "I judge them not; therefore I deliver them into thy hands to be judged" ([Mosiah 26:12](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/26.md)). And Alma, still uncertain, takes it to God.

Teachers → priests → Alma → king → back to Alma → God. Every level handles what it can and escalates what exceeds its capacity. Same as Jethro's "every great matter they shall bring unto thee" — but with the added dimension that when human authority reaches its limit, the escalation continues to God Himself, who gives the standard for judgment ([Mosiah 26:29](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/26.md)).

And at the end: "Alma and his fellow laborers" ([Mosiah 26:38](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/26.md)). Not Alma alone. The delegation worked.


## VI. D&C 107 — The Delegation Charter

D&C 107 formalizes what all the previous patterns demonstrate. The phrase that appears at every quorum level is "sit in council with them":

> "The duty of the president over the office of the Aaronic Priesthood, is to preside over forty-eight priests, and sit in council with them, to teach them the duties of their office" ([D&C 107:72](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107.md))

> "The duty of the president over the office of elders is to preside over ninety-six elders, and to sit in council with them, and to teach them according to the covenants." ([D&C 107:78](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107.md))

Twelve deacons, twenty-four teachers, forty-eight priests, ninety-six elders — all with a president who "sits in council with them" ([D&C 107:85-89](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107.md)). The president doesn't direct from above. He councils from within. Webster's 1828 *counsel* — "interchange of opinions; mutual advising" — not one-directional instruction.

The ratios scale with quorum maturity. Deacons get closer supervision (1:12). Elders operate with more independence (1:96). The structure trusts people progressively.

And then the direct connection to Moses: "The duty of the President of the office of the High Priesthood is to preside over the whole church, and to be like unto Moses" ([D&C 107:91](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107.md)). The D&C doesn't just describe a pattern parallel to Moses. It invokes Moses by name, closing the loop: Exodus 18 → Numbers 11 → D&C 107.

The section ends with what might be the most operationally clear verse in all of revelation:

> "Wherefore, now let every man learn his duty, and to act in the office in which he is appointed, in all diligence." ([D&C 107:99](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107.md))

Not "let the president do every man's duty." Let every man learn *his own*. The president's job is to teach the duty, not perform it. And the consequence is real: "He that is slothful shall not be counted worthy to stand, and he that learns not his duty and shows himself not approved shall not be counted worthy to stand" ([D&C 107:100](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/107.md)).

President Hinckley described the presidency relationship from personal experience:

> "The president, if he is wise, will assign to these chosen assistants particular duties and then leave them free to perform, requiring from them accountability for what happens." (["In … Counsellors There Is Safety"](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1990/10/in-counsellors-there-is-safety.md), Oct 1990)

Assign. Leave free. Require accountability. Not assign-and-hover. Not assign-and-do-it-yourself-anyway.

> "When three kneel together in prayer, discuss every aspect of the problem which is before them, and under the impressions of the Spirit reach a united conclusion, then we may have the assurance that the decision is in harmony with the will of the Lord." (["In … Counsellors There Is Safety"](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1990/10/in-counsellors-there-is-safety.md))

And Ballard's observation is the diagnostic for when this isn't happening:

> "Without exception, the bishop took charge of the situation immediately and said, 'Here's the problem, and here's what I think we should do to solve it.' Then he made assignments... This was a good exercise in delegation, I suppose, but it did not even begin to use the experience and wisdom of council members to address the problem." ([Counseling with Our Councils](../gospel-library/eng/general-conference/1994/04/counseling-with-our-councils.md), Apr 1994)

The bishop dictated a solution and distributed tasks. That's delegation as offloading. When he opened the floor and invited the council to counsel together, "the effect was like opening the floodgates of heaven."


## VII. The Asymmetry — Why Machines Are Easier

Now the binding question cycles back. How does God delegate? The pattern is clear: organizational structure (Jethro), spiritual empowerment (Numbers 11), authority with identity (Christ/Twelve), counsel and watching (Abraham 4), covenantal relationship (Alma), council and mutual advising (D&C 107).

But the pattern reveals something uncomfortable about why delegating to machines is easier. Consider each element:

| Pattern Element | With Machines | With Humans |
|---|---|---|
| **Structure** | Defined by specs and APIs | Requires negotiation and shared understanding |
| **Empowerment** | Not needed — machines execute | Requires sharing the Spirit (Num 11:17) |
| **Authority** | Granted by access controls | Requires trust built over time |
| **Watching** | Logs, metrics, immediate feedback | Requires patience, presence, vulnerability |
| **Covenant** | None — transactional | "Hearts knit together in unity and love" |
| **Council** | Not applicable — machines don't advise | "Interchange of opinions; mutual advising" |

Machines don't need empowerment because they don't have agency. They don't need covenant because they can't choose. They don't need council because they can't contribute insight you haven't already provided. Every element that makes human delegation hard is an element that makes it *holy*.

Numbers 11 illuminates this most sharply. When Moses is dying under the weight of undelegated responsibility — "kill me, I pray thee, out of hand" ([Num 11:15](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/11.md)) — God's solution isn't Jethro's organizational restructuring. It's spiritual transfer:

> "I will take of the spirit which is upon thee, and will put it upon them; and they shall bear the burden of the people with thee, that thou bear it not thyself alone." ([Num 11:17](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/11.md))

God doesn't redistribute Moses' workload. He redistributes Moses' *spirit*. The seventy elders don't just handle logistics — they prophesy ([Num 11:25](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/11.md)). They receive the same spiritual gift Moses has. This is empowerment, not employment.

And then Eldad and Medad — two of the seventy who stayed in camp, didn't come to the tabernacle, and prophesied anyway ([Num 11:26](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/11.md)). Joshua's reaction is the control instinct: "My lord Moses, forbid them" ([Num 11:28](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/11.md)). Moses' response is the vision:

> "Enviest thou for my sake? would God that all the Lord's people were prophets, and that the Lord would put his spirit upon them!" ([Num 11:29](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/num/11.md))

The ideal isn't managed control. It's universal empowerment. Moses doesn't want seventy delegates he can manage — he wants a people who are *all* prophets. The delegation pattern, at its best, works toward a world where it's no longer needed because everyone has become what the leader is.

You can't delegate to a machine and have it become anything. You can only delegate to a human and watch them grow. The relational cost IS the spiritual mechanism.

A machine will never say "the effect was like opening the floodgates of heaven." That only happens when Bishop opens the floor to people who have agency, experience, and the Spirit — and trusts them enough to listen.


## VIII. Becoming — What This Means for Monday Morning

The pattern is clear enough. The application is uncomfortably specific.

**Webster 1828 defines *steward*:** "A man employed in a large family, or on a large estate, to manage the domestic concerns, supervise other servants, collect the rents or income, keep accounts." A steward manages what belongs to someone else. The Sunday School presidency, the teachers, the curriculum, the classes — none of it is mine. It belongs to the Lord. I'm the steward. And a steward who tries to do everything himself is a steward who has forgotten whose house it is.

**Webster 1828 defines *ordain*:** "To set in order; to arrange according to rule; to regulate." Before it means "to set apart for an office," it means to bring order. The calling isn't just a title. It's a command to organize.

The concrete steps are small and the pattern already exists:

1. **Hold presidency meeting.** Not to assign tasks — to counsel. "Interchange of opinions; mutual advising." Ask the counselors what they see that I don't. Jethro saw what Moses couldn't see from inside the work. My counselors can see what I can't see from inside the doing.

2. **Assign and leave free.** Hinckley: "assign to these chosen assistants particular duties and then leave them free to perform, requiring from them accountability for what happens." Not assign-and-hover. Not assign-and-do-it-yourself-when-they're-slow.

3. **Watch, don't do.** Abraham 4:18: "the Gods watched those things which they had ordered until they obeyed." Webster: preside means "to watch over." The president's job is to watch and teach, not to perform every duty.

4. **Trust the Spirit's work in them.** Numbers 11:17: God puts His spirit on the delegates. They don't need my spirit. They need God's. When I delegate to a counselor, I'm not just distributing tasks — I'm creating space for the Spirit to work in someone else.

5. **Let the escalation protocol work.** Not every problem needs the president. Mosiah 26 shows the chain: teachers handle what they can, priests handle what teachers can't, the president handles what exceeds everyone else. The goal is to handle the "great matters" ([Ex 18:22](../gospel-library/eng/scriptures/ot/ex/18.md)) and let the small matters be judged at their proper level.

D&C 107:99 holds the final word: "Let every man learn his duty, and to act in the office in which he is appointed, in all diligence." That verse is directed at every man — including the president who hasn't yet learned that his duty is to preside, not to perform. And the counselors who haven't been given the chance to learn theirs.

The machines will still get their clear specs. The AI agents will still execute with precision. But the calling — the human calling, with human councilors and human teachers and human students — that requires what no machine can give: covenant, trust, the Spirit shared, and the willingness to watch someone else grow into their duty.
